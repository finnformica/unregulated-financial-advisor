# Tractor Supply Co (TSCO) Deep Dive Analysis

*Published March 1, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report discusses the recent market sell-off, dives into some economic data, looks at an interesting ETF that took a beating late last week, and then analyzes a fast-growing retailer with a strong balance sheet.

## The Fastest Correction

The market sell-off over the past week and a half was the fastest S&P 500 and Dow correction from all-time highs in history.

A correction in this case is defined as a -10% or greater move down from all time highs. There have been faster sell-offs (including more than -20% in an infamous single day in 1987) from periods that were not all-time highs, but this is specifically the fastest 10% down move directly from all-time highs in 90 years of data.

![SPX Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-spx-chart.png)

The broad market is now below its 200-day moving average, has a bearish MACD crossover on the weekly chart, and is now not much higher than where it peaked in September 2018, a year and a half ago.

And it was a disorderly sell-off as well:

* The first third of the sell-off focused more on the sectors you would expect, like travel stocks, cyclical stocks, and so forth.
* The middle third of the sell-off was extremely broad-based, with basically everything selling off.
* The final third of the sell-off, on Thursday and Friday, was the weird part, where traditionally *defensive* things sold off more heavily than the broad market, like gold and utility stocks. Travel stocks were up.

Speculators likely liquidated some of their defensive positions to fund margin calls on their other long positions. Similar things happened in 2008; gold and gold stocks sold off sharply back then as investors needed to sell anything to meet margin calls and liquidity requirements, but rebounded well before the S&P 500 bottomed. We may have had a smaller taste of that during the tail end of last week. When volatility gets high enough, liquidity dries up, the market breaks down, and disorderly sell-offs occur.

This was the type of week where Treasuries were particularly useful. Treasuries protected purchasing power. While I [don’t like Treasuries for the long-term](https://www.lynalden.com/treasury-bonds-risks/) with their yields below the inflation rate, I still use them tactically at the moment in most of my portfolios for disorderly sell-off weeks just like this.

Interestingly, the dollar weakened by almost 2% vs a basket of other currencies (the DXY index) during the sell-off, as the bond market quickly priced in multiple Federal Reserve interest rate cuts this year. Specifically, the dollar hit a local peak on February 20th, and has fallen since then:

**Risk Management, and Next Moves**

The coronavirus that originated in Wuhan continues to spread internationally, including into the United States. Global travel is slowing considerably, Japan has closed their schools, Saudi Arabia has closed Mecca for pilgrims, Italy locked down infected towns, France closed the Louvre Museum, etc.

I sent out an [email alert back on January 26](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-1-26/), near all-time S&P 500 highs, between normally-scheduled semi-monthly premium reports, warning of elevated risk conditions due to a combination of an overbought stock market and early-stage coronavirus concerns, to ensure that readers had a chance to make sure their portfolios were risk-appropriate. I also sent out a [second email on February 25](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-2-25/) in the middle of the sell-off, updating readers and providing another warning to ensure that investors’ portfolio risk levels matches their risk tolerance and unique situation. Many readers rode through it with their existing asset allocation, including me. I was satisfied with my existing level of defensiveness for my age, and although I took a slightly bigger hit than I would have preferred, it was smaller than the S&P 500 decline, and acceptable overall.

In the near term, the market is extremely oversold in terms of the daily relative strength index (RSI), and I would expect a bounce to happen at some point. How big that bounce is, and when exactly it comes, is anyone’s guess. When we look at a multi-month timeline, however, there is certainly still a lot of risk for further downside as we continue into 2020, as recession risk continues to grow. It’s still entirely possible that we could have more disorderly selling this month.

The market has certainly opened up some bargains, and we can start poking around for them, but we don’t have any sort of “all clear” to dive in without risk management. **Central Bank Response**

In the coming weeks and months, look for multiple central bank interventions to try to prop up asset prices and provide liquidity. During the Q4 2018 sharp sell-off in the United States, no junk bonds were issued for six weeks straight, which is problematic to say the least. The same thing happened last week; credit markets froze. Credit markets stop functioning in highly-volatile markets with scarce liquidity and uncertain monetary policy. Left unchecked, this would ensure a recession as leveraged companies fail to get any sort of capital, so central bankers will be paying attention to it, and try to facilitate an operational credit market.

The U.S. Treasury market is now pricing in multiple rate cuts by the Federal Reserve this year, with most bond yields currently well below the federal funds rate. In addition, we may get some quantitative easing announcements. This could help the stock market bounce a bit, at least for a time, but even more likely could re-ignite the gold market.

Over the next several months, stock markets will likely be in a tug-of-war between problematic economic fundamentals pulling asset prices down, and central bank monetary stimulus attempting to push asset prices up.

![Business Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years (20 quarters), and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation, I now use a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation is not necessarily “good” or “bad” like the rest of the heat map. Blue indicates higher inflation, white indicates medium, and red is lower inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme now.

As you can see, we had a significant slowdown in the 2015/2016 timeframe, re-accelerated during 2017/2018, and since late 2018 and 2019 we have been in another slowing phase.

So, nobody should be truly shocked by this equity sell-off, as it merely closed a gap that was basically begging to be closed. It was fascinating due to its record speed, but not surprising that it happened.

**USA vs China: Market Performance**

An interesting fact is that China’s stock market has outperformed the U.S. market year-to-date, over the past three months, and over the past six months. Not many people would guess that this is true, given coronavirus headlines centered on China over the past two months. Here is the year-to-date performance:

![China vs US Markets YTD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-china-us.png)

This is because market performance is about more than just outcomes. It also includes policy response (in this case, China’s aggressive intervention to keep their market from crashing so far), and the divide between expectations and outcomes.

In other words, the U.S. stock market was considered untouchable. It was expensive and riding on high momentum, detached from a slowing economy (S&P 500 corporate earnings were completely flat in 2019), so all it took was an unexpected catalyst to bring it back down to Earth, like Apple, Microsoft, Mastercard, and other mega-cap stock darlings coming out and saying they are going to miss guidance due to supply chain disruptions and virus impacts. The Chinese market, on the other hand, was already cheap and beaten down from the trade war and its local attempts at deleveraging. When the market expects perfection and gets something bad, prices can crash. When the market expects bad things and gets bad things, prices can just grind around.

I’m not sure which country’s market will outperform in 2020, but the main takeaway is to always compare expectations (particularly valuations) to possible outcomes.

What seems invincible, but might surprise to the downside? What seems terrible, but might surprise to the upside? That sort of thinking can be very good for long-term investors, and is why I tend to poke around to find opportunities in out-of-favor sectors and countries even though it can be painful in the short-term.

## ETF Spotlight: GDX & SGOL

I added various gold stocks to the newsletter back in October 2018. These were the ones initially added, and all have outperformed the S&P 500, which has made it one of the better-performing segments in the portfolio:

![Gold Stock Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-gold-stocks-performance.png)

During this past year and a half, I have added several more, and have dollar-cost averaged in along the way through dips and bounces. I own some of them in my larger accounts as well, such as Franco Nevada (FNV) and Sandstorm (SAND).

The simplest way for most investors to get exposure is to own the VanEck Vectors Gold Miners ETF (ticker: GDX), which focuses on large and medium gold miners and royalty companies.

Gold mining is a very rough business, and many of them have destroyed capital for the long term. That is why individual miner selection is very challenging, and the best thing to do when it comes to individual stocks is select companies with the absolute top-tier of managers with proven serially-successful track records. However, in recent years, there has been a promising industry shift towards consolidation, balance sheet improvements, and cash flow production, so I expect a bit better capital utilization in the industry going forward. I also like gold streaming and royalty companies for their superior business model.

I don’t intend to hold gold stocks forever, and would never make them an outsized portfolio position, but I do like them for the multi-year long term from 2018 levels, including from today’s levels, as part of a diversified portfolio.

Despite their strong performance for the past couple years, however, gold stocks took a beating last week. This is particularly interesting, because the material that they mine and sell, gold, is now more expensive than it was at the beginning of the year (good for the miners), while one of their primary expenses, energy, is cheaper than it was at the beginning of the year (good for the miners). And yet, gold mining stocks themselves are down for the year:

Gold and gold stocks sometimes get sold off during sharp liquidity events like in 2008 or this past week, but they tend to do very well when central banks ease and reflate, with interest rate cuts, quantitative easing programs, and so forth.

There is plenty of volatile downside risk, so it’s important to size the position appropriately, but I like it as a rather uncorrelated asset that generally does very well when central banks stimulate and devalue their currencies.

I also continue to think it’s a good time to have precious metals directly as part of a diversified portfolio. The metals themselves, especially gold, are less risky than the miners, and can be maintained as a larger portfolio segment for those that prefer to do so. Read my [full guide on precious metals](https://www.lynalden.com/precious-metals-investing/) if you want to learn more.

## Tractor Supply Co (TSCO) Deep Dive Analysis

Tractor Supply Co is a highly-efficient and Amazon-resistant retailer that continues to grow strongly, and has recently come down in price. They focus on a customer base that is rural and suburban property owners, tradesman, and business owners.

Here is their description from their 10-k:

> Tractor Supply Company (the “Company” or “we” or “our” or “us”) is the largest rural lifestyle retailer in the United States (“U.S.”). The Company is focused on supplying the needs of recreational farmers, ranchers, and all those who enjoy living the rural lifestyle (which we refer to as the “Out Here” lifestyle), as well as tradesmen and small businesses. We operate retail stores under the names Tractor Supply Company, Del’s Feed & Farm Supply, and Petsense and operate websites under the names TractorSupply.com and Petsense.com. Our stores are located primarily in towns outlying major metropolitan markets and in rural communities.
>
> The Company has one reportable industry segment which is the retail sale of products that support the rural lifestyle. At December 28, 2019, we operated 2,024 retail stores in 49 states (1,844 Tractor Supply and Del’s retail stores and 180 Petsense retail stores). Our Tractor Supply stores typically range in size from 15,000 to 20,000 square feet of inside selling space, along with additional outside selling space, and our Petsense stores have approximately 5,500 square feet of inside selling space. For Tractor Supply retail locations, we use a standard design for most new built-to-suit locations that includes approximately 15,500 square feet of inside selling space. Our online selling websites are positioned to offer an extended assortment of products beyond those offered in-store and drive traffic into our stores through our buy online and pickup in-store and ship to store programs.
>
> Market Niche: We have identified a specialized market niche: supplying the lifestyle needs of recreational farmers, ranchers, and all those who enjoy living the rural lifestyle, as well as tradesmen and small businesses. By focusing our product assortment on these core customers, we believe we are differentiated from general merchandise, home center, and other specialty retailers. We cater to the rural lifestyle and often serve a market by being a trip consolidator for many basic maintenance needs for farm, ranch, and rural customers through convenient shopping options both in-store and online.
>
> Customers: Our target customers are home, land, pet, and livestock owners who generally have above average income and below average cost of living. We seek to serve a customer base that primarily lives in towns outlying major metropolitan markets and in rural communities. This customer base includes recreational farmers, ranchers, and all those who enjoy living the rural lifestyle, as well as tradesmen and small businesses.

It’s a medium-sized company, with a market capitalization of a little over $10 billion and annual revenue of a little over $8 billion. For reference, Home Depot has a market capitalization of over $230 billion and annual sales of $111 billion, and Lowe’s has a market capitalization of $81 billion and annual sales of $71 billion.

Compared to Home Depot and Lowe’s, Tractor Supply stores are smaller and more rural. In addition, a key difference between Tractor Supply and those big hardware chains, is that Tractor Supply’s biggest sales segment is “Livestock and Pets”, which accounts for 47% of sales. So, they are specifically more oriented towards farmers and ranchers, as well as pet owners (hence why owning a separate pet store chain, Petsense, is likely a good synergy due to the product overlap). Tractor Supply gets 21% of its revenue from hardware and tools, 20% from seasonal items, 8% from clothes and shoes, and 4% from agriculture products. Nearly a third of their total sales across categories come from exclusive in-house brands.

Tractor Supply focuses on hiring sales associates that have this rural lifestyle background, meaning they are informed on many of the products. The company has proven to be very Amazon-resistant, due to the nature (and physical size) of their products, and the expertise that comes when selecting the products. In addition, they do have an online presence, and they own and operate a distribution facility network to get products to their stores and to their online customers.

**Management and Performance**

Tractor Supply has, for lack of a better word, *crushed* the S&P 500 over the long run:

![TSCO Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-tsco-performance.png)

Going forward, however, expectations need to be more realistic. The company went from a small-cap to a mid-cap, and did extraordinarily well by finding and dominating a profitable niche. Over the next decade, they are starting as a mid-cap with stores in most states, so the goal is to provide a combination of moderate growth and shareholder capital returns, to hopefully continue outperforming the broader market.

Since 2010, they became a dividend growth stock as well. Rather than just requiring top line growth to provide returns, they spend all of their free cash flow on dividends and share buybacks. They have rapidly grown dividends per share, and substantially reduced the total share count.

![TSCO Buybacks and Dividends](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-dividends-buybacks.png)

They are able to do this because they have historically high returns on invested capital. It’s a very efficient business, where it doesn’t require a lot of capital to open a new store, so they can grow very cheaply and quickly while simultaneously giving extra cash back to shareholders.

![TSCO ROIC ROE ROA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-1-tsco-roic-roe-roa.png)

Tractor Supply maintains a very strong balance sheet. They have about $400 million in debt, along with $80 million in cash. That means they have less net debt than one year’s worth of net income or free cash flow (currently over $500 million/year). In addition, the company’s debt/equity ratio is low, and very little of their balance sheet space is made up of goodwill or intangible assets. They are rock solid.

The company recently put in place a new CEO, Hal Lawton. Lawton has executive retail experience with Home Depot, Ebay, and Macy’s. In particular, he is credited for starting Home Depot’s internet business, and now Home Depot is one of the largest online retailers in the country.

Overall, the business model is very successful, the company has been a good steward of capital, and the new CEO appears to be a logical choice for succession.

**Risks**

Tractor Supply is not one of the riskier stocks out there, but they do have specific risks to be aware of.

*Recession Risk*

When we’re this late into a business cycle with rising recession risk, investors need to prepare for the possibility of poor stock returns over the next few years. This risk can be mitigated by holding defensive assets, and by focusing on companies that might dip but are unlikely to be permanently impaired.

Tractor Supply remained profitable through the 2008/2009 financial crisis, although it did have a reduction in profit margins. Its earnings per share declined in 2008, but rebounded to new all-time highs as early as 2009. So, within two years, it was back to being as strong as ever.

Tractor Supply stock prices, on the other hand, reached a peak in 2005 and took until 2010 to reach new highs, or about 5 years. This isn’t unexpected; they were overvalued in 2005.

In this cycle, Tractor Supply stock reached an expensive peak in 2019, and they are already down almost 20% from there. So, I’m happy to start buying here. If the U.S. economy has a rough few years coming up, it could take some time for this stock (and most stocks for that matter) to reach new all-time highs. The company itself will also naturally be impacted, but should weather it rather well.

As long as investors manage their expectations, dollar-cost average to take advantage of dips, and hedge with defensive assets, this stock appears to offer a good long-term opportunity.

*Saturation Risk*

The company has 1,844 Tractor Supply locations, and have identified markets for an additional 600 of them. They have 180 Petsense stores and believe they can open another 820 of them. Beyond that, the company may need to enter other markets or other countries, if they wish to continue their high topline growth rates.

As a mid-sized company, that still leaves at least a decade of growth before reaching market saturation. However, a risk is that management expectations could be off, and they might get closer to market saturation earlier than they expect.

Remember, however, that the company gives all of its free cash flow back to shareholders as dividends and buybacks, so those that buy, hold, and reinvest dividends will own a larger and larger percentage of the company over time. The total share count keeps declining, and dividend reinvests will build a larger number of shares over time. Therefore, investor returns come not just from topline growth, but from management’s total use of capital, with the investor’s stake of earnings being reinvested into a larger and larger ownership stake in the company.

In the future, the company may acquire or start another brand, or may expand into another region, and that will open up some risks of niche dilution, lower returns on capital, and so forth. However, there’s a lot of time between now and then to see what management does and what industries they move into, if any.

*Competitor Risk*

So far, Tractor Supply has warded off competitors, carving out a profitable niche. However, investors should monitor margins to ensure that the company is not being squeezed by an emergent competitor. Margins will likely diminish in a recession, so the key is to see how margins perform in a strong market.

Down the line, there’s the possibility that Tractor Supply could even be acquired by a larger competitor, which would be a nice boost for shareholders and a good exit point. Otherwise, the company itself may grow into a large retailer itself, and expand into other industries.

**Conclusions**

Overall, in this uncertain time, I think Tractor Supply Co is likely a good long-term investment, after its nearly 20% decline from 2019 highs.

The company has solid revenue growth, a strong balance sheet, and great profitability metrics. They generate nice free cash flow and return it all to shareholders as dividends and buybacks along the way.

Investors should remain vigilant and well-diversified, because although we may get asset price bounces, there is considerable risk in 2020 and beyond from current broad equity levels.
