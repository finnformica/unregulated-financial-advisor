# Investment Update: High Yielders

*September 15, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report looks at liquidity conditions, the U.S. yield curve, and the state of the Chinese economy.

The digital asset section looks at BTC and ETH price action.

The investment analysis section looks at a number of high-yielding companies, and explores which investment themes might hold up even if we get a recession next year.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to trend almost perfectly sideways, as both the Fed and Treasury Department indirectly work together to maintain some degree of tightness in the financial system, tempered by financial stability ever since the March 2023 regional banking incident.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-domestic-liquidity.png)

I expect this trend to continue until the reverse repo facility is drained. Once that occurs, likely by mid or late 2025, we’ll likely see the Fed go back to gradual balance sheet increases in order to maintain what they define as ample reserves in the banking system.

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-reverse-repos.png)

Global liquidity has fared a bit better, mainly because the dollar index continues to ease.

![Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-global-liquidity.png)

The dollar index (i.e. the strength of the dollar relative to other major currencies) has been chopping along in a band, and is currently on the low end of its two-year range:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-dollar-index.png)

The price of gold touched a new high at over $2,600 per ounce, amid dollar weakness and heightened geopolitical tensions as potential catalysts. However, I continue to have some intermediate-term concerns that the the dollar is a bit oversold and that gold is a bit overbought, and thus both could consolidate for a few months. This is not a concern that I’m actively trading around, but rather something I am making note of to set expectations even as a gold bull.

**The Yield Curve**

The Treasury market has rallied, likely from a combination of U.S. economic slowdown concerns and the ongoing Chinese economic malaise.

The 2-year Treasury rate is now implicitly forecasting major cuts by the Federal Reserve. This chart shows the relationship between the 2-year yield and the short-term rate set by the Fed:

![Yields Differential](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-yields.png)

In fact, the gap between the 2-year rate and the Fed Funds rate, shown on the chart below, is about as deep as it historically gets, aside from the late 1970s and early 1980s:

![Yield Differential](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-yield-differential.png)

There is an FOMC meeting this week, and the Fed will likely cut interest rates. The futures market is currently torn 50/50 about whether it will be 25 basis points or 50 basis points. And the futures market is pricing in upwards of 200 basis points of cuts by mid-2025.

This is despite the fact that the majority of economic indicators suggest economic softness but not necessarily recession. The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator, which predicts real GDP growth with reasonable accuracy based on a variety of incoming data points, is currently pointing to mediocre but positive growth in the current quarter:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-gdpnow.png)

I don’t have a strong view on what the Fed will do next week, although I have concerns that the market is pricing in rate cuts too aggressively for late 2024 and into 2025. This ties back to my concerns about the dollar being oversold. I would not be surprised if the bond, futures, and currency markets digest the current move a bit, and factor out a couple rate hikes. This could bounce the dollar for a period of time. The catalyst for this type scenario could be from some hawkish verbal pushback from the Fed, and/or from a handful of not-as-weak-as-expected economic data points.

And as I’ll describe in the next section, I think the market is putting too much emphasis on the Fed and not enough emphasis on China.

**China’s Economy: The Global Lynchpin**

Many analysts are highly focused on the question of whether the United States is going to enter a recession or not, and how quickly the Fed will cut interest rates, but I think the bigger question for the global economy is to ask how long will China remain in its economic malaise until officials decide to take more aggressive action.

That’s because China is a more “physical” economy than the United States, with more steel production, electricity production, infrastructure build-out capacity, and manufacturing output. And it’s the biggest trade partner with the majority of countries in the world currently (for which it gradually replaced the United States over the past twenty years).

What China is going through now is similar to what the United States went through after the subprime mortgage crisis. China’s real estate sector was incredibly overvalued and levered, and for years it has been deleveraging and dropping in price, which has negatively impacted consumer spending via a negative wealth effect and reduced consumer confidence. Government officials in China generally want that to happen; they want to deflate bubbles rather than keep them propped up. On the exports front, China is still doing amazing, and spent the last few years becoming the world’s largest car exporter, for example.

However, China’s central authorities are less willing to stimulate than U.S. officials, because they have a different set of priorities. China is currently trying to reduce its dollar dependency and internationalize its currency and bond market, meaning that it wants more of its trade partners to accept Chinese yuan for payment, and to be willing to hold some yuan bonds in their reserves, knowing that they can buy all sorts of Chinese goods with it when needed and that it will be among the top tier of fiat currencies in terms of retaining value. But in order for that to continue gaining adoption as it has been (from a low starting point), they need to maintain the perception that those bonds will hold up reasonably well in terms of purchasing power. In recent years, Chinese bonds have indeed outperformed dollar bonds, but it has come with a cost.

*Note #1: Garbage Time*

Back during COVID-19, China adopted the “Zero COVID” policy, and framed their initial success as an example of the superiority of their top-down system versus the more decentralized system that the United States and “the West” in general has.

But then as repeated waves of major lockdowns in China dragged on into 2022 while the rest of the world started moving on, there started to be widespread protests in China, which are rare. Central authorities backed down rather quickly at that point, ripped the bandage off, and moved toward opening up. The cost/benefit analysis was strongly toward that direction, given the high priority placed on social stability there.

I reference this because China’s current malaise might play out similarly. There is increasing social media noise from unsatisfied Chinese citizens about the state of their economy. And many of them have been very well articulated.

In timed sports games, if there is very one-sided matchup and the game is effectively over due to a big points gap while there’s still time left to play, both teams tend to switch out the best players to avoid the risk of injury, and let their “benchwarmers” play. And so it becomes a weaker game with no stakes. This is known as “garbage time” in sports terminology. In recent months, Chinese citizens on social media have been referring to their economy as being in garbage time. It started late last year, became widely reported in July 2024 when Chinese central authorities were more aware of it and began [cracking down on it to censor it](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-07-18/china-moves-to-quash-viral-phrase-to-describe-economic-malaise), and yet the sentiment persists.

The NYT [offered a recent long-form piece](https://www.nytimes.com/2024/09/13/world/asia/china-economy-garbage-time.html) around China’s social media garbage time meme, but a lot of media outlets ranging from Reuters to the Guardian have been reporting on it since July.

And as many of these types of pieces describe, with specific examples, many Chinese social media users are adept at spreading their message even amid widespread censorship. Rather than criticize the current non-democratic government or President Xi, they write posts about prior Chinese dynasties, and how they made similar mistakes that resulted in economic malaise, and draw clear parallels to the present state but with plausible deniability in case they get in trouble, and/or to make the critical content harder to identify and censor.

So, under the surface, tensions are simmering. The youth unemployment rate is high, and China just raised the retirement age for its citizens, albeit from a younger age than many other countries.

China is currently providing record low domestic interest rates to assist with the deleveraging, but beyond a certain point, monetary stimulus doesn’t cut it. Fiscal stimulus or other economic reforms can be more impactful, but they require more political levers to initiate, which President Xi has been unwilling to pull.

However, President Xi recently called on government officials in multiple layers of the country’s government to keep striving to reach their 5% annual GDP growth target. As Bloomberg [reports](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-09-12/xi-urges-efforts-to-hit-annual-growth-target-amid-rising-doubt):

> “All regions and departments should studiously implement all the major economic initiatives and measures introduced by the Central Committee and deliver on the economic tasks for the third and fourth quarters,” Xi said Thursday.

The increasing dissatisfaction with the economy by Chinese citizens, and calls by the president and other government officials to meet their growth targets, is reaching an interesting point.

This has direct relevance for Chinese equity market performance. Their stocks in aggregate are currently unusually cheap relative to their fundamentals, but are fraught with tail risk that international investors don’t have the appetite for. But even for investors that don’t want to invest in China directly, the state of China’s economy and the performance of their real estate and equity markets have all sorts of butterfly-effect implications for the global economy.

I view China as the #1 variable to watch regarding global macro cycles, even more-so than the U.S. Federal Reserve. Whether the Fed cuts 25 basis points or 50 basis points is somewhat meaningful, but how long China’s leaders consider the cost/benefit analysis of remaining in a malaise of deleveraging versus performing a pivot and stimulating out of it, is likely more important.

*Note #2: Not Like Japan*

Many analysts have drawn analogies between China and Japan, as though China may enter a multi-decade lost economic period. China has similarly shrinking demographics, and arguably even more-so. They grew their economy like a raging fire, and are now running into headwinds. There are clear parallels to Japan there.

However, there are two key differences.

Firstly, China hit its demographics wall at an earlier stage of per-capita development than Japan, and so there is still a lot of development to do. The average Chinese citizen consumes less energy and has less overall purchasing power than the average Japanese citizen. They still have plenty of room to “emerge”, in other words, as it pertains to the modern definition of an advanced society on a per capita basis. Some analysts have argued, more accurately in my view, that China’s slowdown is perhaps more analogous to South Korea’s slowdown from decades ago. That is, a less extreme version of what Japan went through because the slower-growth period happened at an earlier stage of development.

Secondly, Chinese assets in aggregate don’t have nearly the valuation problems that Japan had at the height of their late-1980s bubble. Japan’s imperial palace land alone was valued at more than all the land in California in the late 1980s. Japan’s equity market was twice as expensive on a cyclically-adjusted price-to-earnings basis as the U.S. dotcom equity bubble was. In relation to one country’s total economy, there has arguably never been a bubble than what Japan experienced in the late 1980s.

China had a huge real estate bubble, similar to what Canada and Australia currently have, if not worse. However, China’s equity market entered this malaise at reasonable valuations, and is now at extremely cheap valuations. The valuations are basically priced for war and a decent probability of confiscation at this point, which some analysts such as Russel Napier have argued will likely be the case (similar to Russian equities in 2022). And that’s a possibility. However, what makes China different is that it’s far more intertwined with the global economy than Russia.

If I were to discount JD.com (NASDAQ: JD) and argue that it’s not worth even 7x earnings as a growing e-commerce company due to significant tail risk, then that might be a fair point.

![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-jd-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

However, it would then be hard for me to argue that it’s fine to own Apple (AAPL) at 33x earnings, Tesla (TSLA) at 90x earnings, or Nvidia (NVDA) at 53x earnings, which would be heavily disrupted from certain geopolitical outcomes in China and/or Taiwan. And so I’d argue there’s a risk/reward imbalance there that needs to be managed appropriately in both directions.

Overall, my view is that China is more likely to enter a slower-growth period than a lost-decade type of environment. The asset valuation and per-capita development variables point more in that direction.

However, timing is important, and so as long as China continues its economic malaise, we shouldn’t expect much strength out of commodities or global growth. And the pain point to watch is the broad Chinese sentiment that shifts the central authority’s cost/benefit analysis in the more bullish direction. If they fail to reach certain growth targets, and if a growing percentage of people don’t feel that their lives are better off than 3-5 years ago, then we would likely see more of a pivot.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Bitcoin has been consolidating since March of this year. I think the spot ETFs that were launched early this year pulled forward a lot of bullishness and expectations, and the market has had to digest that.

![BTC Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-btc.png)

In addition, a lot of low-quality assets like meme tokens and NFTs rallied significantly earlier this year, and most of that has been deflating for months. Whenever bitcoin rallies, the whole ecosystem tends to rally, and capital starts getting diluted among a lot of different things. And then the market has to work through that, and drain the value out of low-quality assets and back to bitcoin. It’s like how a bodybuilder goes through bulking and cutting cycles, where he builds muscle and fat together, and then cuts away some of the fat, and then goes back to building muscle and fat again.

**ETHBTC Ratio: Ongoing Deterioration**

The price of ETH continues to deteriorate versus BTC:

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-ethbtc.png)

The Ethereum spot ETFs launched in July, and they were lackluster compared to the launch of the bitcoin spot ETFs. I continue to be neutral-to-bearish on Ethereum, in the sense that I don’t see a compelling reason to hold it, and instead primarily focus on bitcoin.

> I plan to continue holding Bitcoin ETFs in the model portfolios, but don’t intend to add a position in the Ethereum ETFs.
>
> -July 21 premium report

Very few coins ever make a higher-high in bitcoin-denominated terms in their second or third cycle. Only two or three coins have ever done that, and even Ethereum with its substantial network effect thus far has not done so. The ETH/BTC ratio hit its peak back in 2017, and still hasn’t surpassed it.

Back in May 2022, I wrote a [public article](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/) discussing this phenomenon of lower-highs:

> When each new cycle comes, bitcoin’s network effect and investment thesis have historically remained intact, while most other projects become stagnant and discarded by developers and investors. Bitcoin has gone on to make higher highs in four separate major cycles, with greater levels of adoption and development each time, while the majority of altcoins only go through one or two cycles before rolling over for good.
>
> Even many of the most notable coins eventually stagnate. If we look at the top 10 coins near the end of the 2017 bull market, for example, every single one of them went on to underperform bitcoin from those highs in the subsequent bear market and bull market. ETH, BCH, XRP, LTC, ADA, MIOTA, DASH, NEM, and XMR all reached lower highs as denominated in BTC during the 2021 bull run compared to the 2017 bull run.
>
> ![2017 Crypto Snapshot](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/digital-alchemy-2017-crypto-snapshot.png)
>
> *Chart Source: Coin Market Cap*
>
> The same is basically true if we look back to the 2013 bull run as well; people today haven’t even heard of most of those top ten coins by now. Of this list, only XRP went on to make higher highs in 2017 as denominated in BTC compared to 2013, but then it failed to do so in the 2021 cycle. None of the others managed even one more cycle of bigger BTC-denominated gains.
>
> ![2013 Crypto Snapshot](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/digital-alchemy-2013-crypto-snapshot.png)
>
> *Chart Source: Coin Market Cap*
>
> I think this pattern will continue; there’s a high probability that most of the crypto darlings of the 2021 bull run have already seen their all-time highs as denominated in BTC. Maybe one or two will go on to get a higher high at some point, but most will not.
>
> That doesn’t mean that bitcoin is without risks, but it has by far the best track record in the space, and is the one designed with a specific purpose in mind. Block after block, it continues to operate as intended over time.

Since then, Bitcoin has touched a fifth-cycle higher high at $73k, but most other coins are far off their highs of 2021, including Doge, Ethereum, and others.

Among altcoins, Solana is the one that has shown decent strength here in 2024, but even that one hasn’t touched new all-time highs versus 2021 yet in either dollar-denominated terms or bitcoin-denominated terms. I suspect that it might touch new dollar highs, but I doubt that it will reach new bitcoin-denominated highs.

## Investment Update: High-Yielders

The S&P 500 has been rangebound since its high in July of 2024:

![S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-spx.png)

Underneath the surface, however, there has been a decent rally in beaten-down stocks. And so, as the Fed likely cuts rates significantly over the next 12 months, I figured it would be good to review some of the higher-yielding stocks out there, for investors seeking income.

When we think about rate cuts, it’s important to ask which types of entities rate cuts would help or hurt. A very cash-rich corporation like Alphabet (GOOGL) is unlikely to benefit from rate cuts. They have more cash than they have debt, and so if anything, the high rates have been *good* for them since they earn extra interest income.

On the other hand, very asset-heavy businesses that make use of a lot of debt, can greatly benefit by rate cuts, as well as the rally in bond prices that has already occurred. And stocks with high dividend yields can be further differentiated from Treasuries if rates go back to 3% or 4%. Indeed the 10-year Treasury rate has already fallen to below 3.7%, which makes it less compelling as an income source than many equities. And so those equities have caught a bid.

**Altria**

Tobacco stocks in aggregate became overvalued in 2017, and then for a variety of reasons including regulatory actions, they sold off sharply and became undervalued in my view. Altria (MO) is a good example of this:

![MO FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-mo-fastgraph.png)

And then in aggregate, here in 2024 they have rallied hard off of their lows. My expectation is that their long-term lows are in, and that they’ll grind sideways or up relative to those lows while paying out very high yields.

I don’t expect overall tobacco use to diminish much from here, but there is an ongoing change in how people consume it. Initially it was from cigarettes to vapes, and more recently it’s toward newer oral products like mint-flavored pouches. And as tobacco stocks are priced for no growth, they continue to plow their free cash flows into dividends and share buybacks. And they’re generally isolated from economic cycles.

I personally don’t hold tobacco stocks in my discretionary “Other holdings” category, simply because I don’t like the social impacts of the products. However, for the model portfolios, I don’t apply my ethical lens to them since they are for other people to consider as investments, and so I have them in there as sources of growing income.

**Realty Income**

Commercial real estate has been in an outright depression in the United States, from a combination of high interest rates and the rise of remote work. The office sector has been hit the hardest, but other areas have been under moderate pressure as well.

Realty Income (O) is generally considered a leading blue-chip real estate investment trust. They own a large portfolio of real estate that is rather resistant to pressure from e-commerce competition and from the remote work trend. Specifically, they own real estate that’s rented by fitness centers, convenience stores, casinos, restaurants, Walmart, Tesco, FedEx, Tractor Supply, CVS, and similar types of property.

![O FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-o-fastgraph.png)

Due to their broad diversification and defensiveness, they have historically been able to issue low-cost debt and use it to buy more properties. The higher-rate environment has therefore pressured them somewhat. But as short-term and long-term rates and rate expectations drift back down, it has been helping stocks like Realty Income get off their lows.

I view Realty Income’s lows as likely being in, and expect the stock to drift sideways to up while paying a 5% dividend yield that grows by 2-4% per year. In my view, this is more attractive than owning a 3.7% yielding ten-year Treasury with no income growth, but it comes with more volatility overall.

**Enterprise Products Partners**

My favorite high-yielder continues to be Enterprise Products Partners (EPD). On this list, they’re the only ones that issue a K-1 during tax season since they’re a publicly traded partnership rather than a c-corp, so investors should be aware of that.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-epd-fastgraph.png)

The units pay a 7% distribution yield, and have grown distributions for 25 consecutive years. They have the highest credit rating in the midstream sector, have large inside ownership, and are largely isolated from the price of oil and gas and various petrochemicals, since they focus on transporting and processing all of it rather than producing it.

There has been some price resistance around $30/unit, but both the fundamentals and the price continue to grind up over time, and I expect it to eventually break over $30 while continuing to pay high and growing distributions to owners.

**Chevron**

The energy sector has been disappointing lately, and I view a lot of that as being associated with China’s economic malaise.

The way that I’ve been approaching the energy sector is to own the producers rather than the underlying commodities. For other sectors, like gold and uranium, I have weighted my exposure more toward the underlying commodities themselves compared to the producers, but in the oil and gas sector I’ve stuck with the producers.

This is because, even at current oil prices as they fluctuate around significantly in the $60-$90 range, many producers are highly profitable, and are using those profits to pay hefty dividends and buy back cheap shares.

![CVX FastGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-cvx-fastgraph.png)

I view the 2015-2020 bear market in oil and gas to have been the structural bottom for the industry. There was a fast rise in U.S. shale oil, and many producers were willing to drill unprofitably for the sake of growth, since they had so much capital pouring into them.

However, now that investors are not interested in the sector, most producers have become more conservative. They have focused on drilling more profitably, and have generally put profits toward improving their balance sheet strength and paying dividends. Many of them have also pursued acquisitions of inexpensive companies to increase their production and reserves without new drilling.

Right now the oil price and the energy sector stocks are bit of a falling knife. We might have seen the bottom last week, but it’s too early to say. Investor positioning and sentiment are quite bearish, and I’m a buyer of the dip, especially if we start to see some stabilization in prices and some semblance of a low being in for now.

This ties back to my view on China. I think investors are a bit too bearish on China, because their authorities have a lot of stimulus levers to pull if they determine that their economic malaise is becoming too unpopular among the citizenry. And as the Fed cuts interest rates, I think several emerging markets with dollar-denominated debt will be beneficiaries. And that’s where marginal oil demand growth is: developing countries with lower per-capita usage.

Specifically, China’s exports of inexpensive vehicles should have bullish impacts on global oil demand, as many people can upgrade from motorbikes to $15-$20k automobiles.

![Chinese Auto Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-chinese-auto-exports.png)

The majority of those cars at low price points have internal combustion engines, rather than being electric. And even as people trend toward using electric vehicles, they still use energy. Electrical grids around the world continue to rely on a lot of natural gas, coal, and uranium for baseload power.

## Final Thoughts: It’s All About Specifics

Many investors are understandably concerned about the prospects of a U.S. recession and bear market in equities.

And I think in many cases, investors are right to be worried. For example, I’m somewhat concerned for Apple stock’s prospects as it trades for 33x earnings with a saturated phone market and a softening consumer economy.

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-15-aapl-fastgraph.png)

But for both the economy and the stock market, I think it’s better to look at specifics. Which sectors of the economy have *already* gone through a recession or malaise? Right now, the answer is real estate, manufacturing, banks, and several emerging markets. They’re the areas that have more to gain from Fed rate cuts than the mega-cap cash-rich defensive stocks. And they’re the areas that few investors are excited to own.

Investors are currently fleeing risk into overpriced defensive stocks, and ironically that’s where I think a lot of risk is, mainly due to the  high valuations. I’m more willing to take on volatility to pursue lower-priced out-of-favor areas, since in an era of fiscal dominance, I think investors in aggregate are too bearish on them. And many of those areas pay decent dividend yields, so if the stock market chops around for a while, at least investors get paid for the wait.
