# Current News and Portfolio Changes

*Published August 23, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This is a brief update during a week between normal reports to inform readers about current market situation and recent/upcoming portfolio changes.

My most recent unscheduled note between normal reports was [back on June 9th](https://www.lynalden.com/risk-mitigation-update/), when I discussed risk mitigation and potentially overbought equity conditions, and consequently shifted 5% of my newsletter portfolio from equities to Treasuries.

We indeed had a correction and consolidation from that level:

![Warning Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-23-warning-chart.png)

The market-weight S&P 500 consolidated for about a month and then eventually broke out to new highs. The equal-weight S&P 500, however, still hasn’t recovered to those mid-June highs. So, there still is an ongoing consolidation among many economically-sensitive stocks, even as the mega-cap tech stocks pulled the primary index back up due to their large weightings in the index.

That June update was mostly technical in nature, focused on overbought conditions and record low put/call ratios. We still have notably overbought conditions in those metrics, but due to the consolidation since then, they are less extreme.

However, the current concern is with more fundamental reasons. As I have detailed in multiple reports, the economy and the market are currently heavily reliant on fiscal spending, because unemployment remains high and economic indicators remain weak. As I outlined recently, personal income and retail spending are \*up\* year-to-date, due to government transfer payments that kept many consumers and businesses solvent, while just about everything else is down (GDP, employment, industrial production, exports, imports, capital spending, etc).

This year’s fiscal stimulus filled the economic hole for more than four months, but mostly ended three weeks ago, and the timing and magnitude of the next round of fiscal stimulus remains an open question, as Congress remains in gridlock. So, the market is shifting more and more into recession-resistant tech stocks at any price, and we’re seeing downward pressure on economically-sensitive assets.

Although my longer-term outlook is for reflation, a weaker dollar, and move up in some of these economically-sensitive industries, in the near term there is a decent possibility of disinflation and a partially W-shaped economic outcome, higher downside stock market volatility, as well as a firm dollar, until the next round of fiscal stimulus is passed.

Both “small businesses open” and “small business revenue” on [tracktherecovery.org](https://tracktherecovery.org/), for example, are starting to roll back over. Here is small business revenue:

![Small Business Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-23-small-business-revenue.png)

*Chart Source: Opportunity Insights Economic Tracker*

Small business revenue fell to about -40% compared to January levels, then recovered into the summer and at its peak was -10% from where it started the year, and now it’s down to -19% again.

Money supply growth has been flat for the past month, and so has the Fed’s balance sheet. The stock market can’t point to aggressive money-printing as a bullish catalyst at the moment.

And yet, the Citigroup Euphoria/Panic model is at highs not seen since the Dotcom bubble twenty years ago:

![Panic Euphoria Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-23-panic-euphoria.png)

*Chart Source: [@hmeisler](https://twitter.com/hmeisler/status/1297163815411425281)*

This model historically makes for a decent contrarian signal. It signaled mild euphoria early this year, then panic in March and April, and now it’s up to even more extreme euphoria levels.

Apple, the largest stock in the S&P 500, recently hit a $2 trillion market capitalization, and went up over 5% on Friday alone (a gain of over $100 billion in market capitalization that day) without any specific reason other than a bullish monthly options-expiration day.

Lately, there has been a trend where companies like Apple and Tesla announce stock splits (which theoretically have no impact on fundamentals, like cutting an 8-slice pizza into 16 half-slices, and giving everyone two mini-slices instead of one normal slice) and then their share prices immediately soar, which is market behavior that happened a lot in the late 1990’s tech mania.

In addition, the U.S. dollar is near-term oversold, and could potentially bounce here.

I’m not making any top-level asset allocation changes at this time but I am considering another small sale of equities into bonds. Each investor has their own unique risk profile so I’m alerting readers to current risks until the next round of fiscal spending happens.

I don’t focus on short-term calls and am not making a specific call here, other than to point out that the rubber band is stretched pretty far in a few directions. It’s as good a time as any to review your asset allocation, rebalance if appropriate, and not be surprised if we do get higher volatility going forward than we’ve had lately.

## Recent and Upcoming Portfolio Moves

Although I’m not making top-level asset allocation changes at this particular time, I did make some small moves and plan to make a few more.

**Recent MicroStrategy Purchase**

On August 17th, I mentioned in the member’s area and Google Drive that I am selling Cerner (CERN) and buying MicroStrategy (MSTR) in the growth stocks pie of the newsletter portfolio. I went ahead and made that switch on August 18th. This represents a small 0.8% portfolio change (10% of the growth stocks pie, which is 8% of the portfolio).

The reason mainly has to do with calculated speculation. MSTR is a cash-rich and profitable software company with no debt, but that has been experiencing significant growth problems over the past five years. Due to concerns about their big cash hoard (which was about 40% of their market cap) against potential currency devaluation, they shifted $250 million (half of their cash) into Bitcoin. Now, the stock is an indirect Bitcoin play, with a profitable company wrapper. I took a small and speculative position in that stock in the newsletter portfolio with a 16-month expected holding period, after which point I plan to sell it.

**Recent Tesla Cover**

I also covered my TSLA short with a small loss a while back because it hit my pre-determined stop point at $1700 that I listed when I initiated the short. The short was profitable for a while but then the stock turned sharply back up to new highs starting from when Tesla announced a share split.

I consider Tesla stock to be in bubble territory, because their revenue has been flat-to-down for the past six quarters while their stock has soared roughly 10x over that period from $200 to $2,000 per share, based on sentiment. Now it is one of the most expensive stocks in the market.

However, it’s important to protect losses and not fight price action too much. I am very patient for my long stock positions (usually with multi-year holding periods), but shorting is riskier and requires timing, so I do it in smaller bursts with pre-determined exit points to keep risk low. I may look for another shorting opportunity for Tesla in the future.

**Upcoming Tencent Purchase**

Next, I plan to buy a small position in Tencent (TCEHY) in the No Limits IB portfolio. I plan to publish an analysis of this stock in an upcoming deep-dive, but in simplistic terms, here’s the F.A.S.T. Graph:

![TCEHY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-23-tcehy.png)

Tencent has an extremely strong balance sheet, diverse exposure to online gaming, payment services, social networks, online advertising, and a host of investments in other digital companies both in China and around the world. Their valuation is reasonable compared to their growth rate, especially vs US-based “FAAANG” stocks with slower growth rates.

For example, I like Tencent over Apple here (and both of them have “US vs China trade dispute” risks):

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-23-aapl.png)

I don’t like investing in China too heavily, because reported financial numbers have to be taken cautiously, but Tencent has broken out of a significant 3-year consolidation and the reported fundamentals are good, so I like it for a small position with an intended long-term holding period. Independent third-party data, such as flight data, car traffic data, commodity sales, and so forth suggest that China’s economy is rebounding reasonably well, and many of their tech titans have plenty of runway to grow, similar to the big period of FAAANG outperformance that happened over the past several years in the United States vs the rest of the market.

I am also switching out Booking Holdings (BKNG) from the aggressive category in the Top Twelve stocks list for Tencent. BKNG has mostly recovered its stock price this year but their growth remains challenged over the next 1-2 years, so I like the risk/reward of Tencent for the aggressive category in the Top Twelve list.

**Selling BBL**

My No Limits IB portfolio has a significant exposure to commodity producers. Most of them (except the oil producers) had a nice run recently, with the rebound in copper and other commodities. I continue to like BBL overall, but am selling it here at a gain to lock in a bit of the recent commodity rebound, and use that capital to buy TCEHY.
