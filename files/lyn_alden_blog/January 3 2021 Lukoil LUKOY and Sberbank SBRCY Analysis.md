**Bitcoin Supply Cycle**

As a reminder, or for investors new to the service, here’s an overview of Bitcoin price action.

Bitcoin is a distributed open-source public ledger that has up to 21 million units (of which 18.5 million are already mined), which are divisible into fractions. No central authority can change this number; it requires a majority of decentralized network participants to agree to a change. So far, network participants only allowed for minor changes and security updates, because there is an in-built incentive not to change anything that would disrupt the scarcity of the tokens. One of the biggest updates was in 2017, which allowed for second-layer scaling solutions like the Lightning Network to connect with the base-layer blockchain.

Some subnetworks have decided to make a bigger change, fork the Bitcoin protocol, and take their version in a new direction. These include Bitcoin Cash, Bitcoin Satoshi Vision, and other less well-known ones. So far, all of those forks failed to capture market capitalization from core Bitcoin itself, and lost value compared to Bitcoin. They are consistently unable to maintain the mining hash rate and large node network that Bitcoin maintains.

Looking at the long-term logarithmic Bitcoin price chart, with instances of “halvings” annotated, we can see that Bitcoin is just doing what it has typically done at this stage of its supply cycle:

![Bitcoin Supply Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-bitcoin-supply-cycle.png)

A halving is a pre-programmed instance where the supply rate of new Bitcoin generated every 10 minutes gets cut in half, and occurs approximately every 4 years.

Between halvings, there tends to be a period of price consolidation as the market reaches some degree of supply/demand equilibrium. Then, a pre-programmed halving occurs, new supply flow gets cut in half, the existing supply/demand balance gets disrupted, and a bull price run occurs as demand outpaces supply. This attracts more buyers and media attention (resulting in even higher demand vs reduced supply), momentum traders jump on board, and it encounters a huge blow-off top followed by a big correction. It then partially rebounds and eventually finds another consolidation range at a much higher level than the previous cycle. Then, another halving occurs and the fun starts all over…

Many investors or financial media folks have been baffled by this year’s Bitcoin bull run, but doing homework on the protocol shows that, really, this bullishness was the higher-probability outcome all along after the halving event in May 2020, and lasting for about 18 months on average. I outlined this in detail in my [public July 2020 article](https://www.lynalden.com/invest-in-bitcoin/) when Bitcoin was a little over $9,000.

The sample size is small due to only having 12 years of price history, so there is never a guarantee of what it will do in any given cycle, but there are a variety of fundamental indicators we can watch to see how the current cycle is playing out compared to previous cycles.

**Bitcoin 1+ Year HODL Wave**

Analyzing the blockchain to see what percentage of Bitcoin haven’t moved in at least 1 year is helpful.

For this chart, the blue line is price, and the yellow line is the percentage of Bitcoin that haven’t moved for at least one year:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-hodl-wave.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/1-year-hodl-wave/)*

During bear markets, Bitcoin investors tend to accumulate and hold, and the percentage of coins that haven’t moved in 1 year or more increases. This effectively reduces liquid supply, since these coins are tightly held. During post-halving major bull markets, some of these holders slowly begin to sell their coins to lock in 3x, 5x, or 10x+ gains on their position.

In the previous cycle, the percent of coins that hadn’t moved in 1-year or more hit a peak level of 61%, and then during the epic 2016-2017 bull run, this number gradually went down to 41%.

Similarly, in 2020, this number reached as high as 63%, and is now down to 59%. This still seems relatively early; the price became high enough to encourage some long-term holders to cash out, but not high enough to get a significant amount of them to cash out. The majority of coins remain tightly held, even as institutional demand has picked up. That’s bullish.

**Bitcoin MVRV Z-Score**

The market value of Bitcoin (blue line below) is simply the current price of Bitcoin multiplied by the number of coins. The realized value of Bitcoin (yellow line below), on the other hand, is the average price at which all individual coins were last moved, multiplied by the number of coins. The Z-Score (red line below) is the standard deviation of how much higher the market value is vs the realized value:

![MVRV Z Score](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-mvrv.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/mvrv-zscore/)*

This metric is looking a little frothy, probably due for a correction or consolidation, but nowhere near as high as previous multi-year blow-off tops. It’s firmly mid-cycle as far as a post-halving bull run historically goes.

**Bitcoin on Exchanges**

This is the metric that stands out. Over time since inception, the number of coins held on exchanges has continually gone up. There have occasionally been single months where the number of coins on exchanges went down, and there was one 5-month period during the second half of 2016 where the number went down. Other than that, the number of coins on exchanges has been constantly going up.

The year 2020 was the exception. After topping in Q1 of 2020 at just under 3 million coins on exchanges, the number has gone down all year and is now below 2.4 million. It has never gone down this long. Even with the huge bull market that happened this autumn, coins are coming off of exchanges on net, rather than going to them.

For a more detailed look, Glassnode has three classifications of Bitcoin: illiquid, liquid, and highly liquid. The number of coins classified as liquid or highly liquid spent most of 2020 going down:

![Glassnode Illiquid Bitcoin](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-glassnode-illiquidity.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

This seems to be because there was a different group of buyers in 2020 than in previous years.

Historically it has been retail investors buying, but 2020 was the year of institutions getting interested. MicroStrategy bought over 70,000 coins with no plans to sell. MassMutual bought $100 million worth. One River Asset Management started a $1 billion Bitcoin and Ethereum fund. Skybridge Capital launched a $25 million Bitcoin fund that they can scale up. Fidelity is providing deep cold storage custody services for various institutions and funds.

And then, for both retail and institutional investors, Grayscale has been the massive buyer this year. They own more than 500,000 coins and have $17+ billion in assets under management.

Plus, there has been a strong culture of dollar-cost averaging and holding among retail buyers this year, rather than trading. Square’s Cash App and Paypal have been major buyers on behalf of retail clients in a non-exchange setting, and other places like Swan Bitcoin have been buying many millions of dollars worth of coins for clients as well.

Many people are just buying every week through automatic recurring purchases and transferring to cold storage, with no plans to sell anytime soon. So, the amount of liquid Bitcoin available keeps decreasing.

No wonder the price action is so bullish; either demand needs to reach a point of exhaustion, or the price has to go up substantially to encourage some more cold-storage holders to bring their coins back to exchanges to sell.

**Recent Action**

Every time Bitcoin had an opportunity or likelihood to correct in this bull run, it instead mostly just consolidated sideways for a bit and then moved up again. Corrections have been shallow and brief. This seems to be due to the institutional interest, and retail holding culture.

With Bitcoin liquidity going down, it makes it somewhat challenging for sustained corrections to occur unless demand takes a big hit. Many people, including institutions, want to invest and feel like they missed the boat, so if there’s a 10-15% pullback, a bunch of them buy. There will most likely be a big correction eventually, but the question is, from what price level?

So far, in terms of percent gain after a halving event, Bitcoin is currently behaving a bit more bullishly at 8 months after the halving than it did 8 months after its July 2016 halving, and not as bullishly as it did 8 months after its November 2012 halving. In fact, it’s right in the middle of those two previous cycles in terms of performance.

![Bitcoin Performance Post-Halving](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-post-halving.png)

*Chart Source: [PlanB](https://twitter.com/100trillionUSD)*

My base case expectation from this summer was that each halving cycle would have a smaller percent price gain than the previous one, since it’s starting from a larger base each time. So, I expected a ton of bullishness in this cycle, but likely less than the post-2016 halving, and way less bullish than the post-2012 halving. So far, particularly in the past 2-3 months, it is outperforming my base case expectation, since it is doing better than the post-2016 halving.

A serious correction could get the red line back down to the blue line, in the chart above. I would encourage prudence, meaning that you make sure your position size is appropriate for your financial situation and goals. Bitcoin is near-term overbought, and is “due” for a correction within the context of a bull market to clear out some overleveraged folks and keep the market honest, but just because something is overbought doesn’t mean it can’t get even more overbought first.

I remain bullish into the second half of 2021, and haven’t sold any of my directly-held coins.

## Lukoil (LUKOY) Deep Dive Analysis

Some folks don’t like to invest in Russian companies, and that’s okay. I personally don’t mind investing in them, especially for established Russian shares that already exist as ADRs. A few of the members of this research service are Russian investors as well.

I covered Lukoil about a year ago. As an energy stock, it ended up being one of the worst-performers of the year among stocks I analyzed, so from a value/contrarian perspective, it’s worth another look in this new environment. The biggest-ever reduction in global energy usage from a global pandemic was certainly not part of the base case of the original Lukoil investment analysis; that was a far tail risk.

The stock is at a much lower price now, but usage for some of their products is lower as well, and the tax situation is less favorable.

Our tech stocks, copper stocks, gold stocks, uranium stocks, and general equity positions have for the most part done very well this year, so the subset of industries that got absolutely decimated, including the energy sector, continue to be of interest to me from a long-term value perspective.

However, I prefer to get energy exposure with caution. In other words, I like companies with strong balance sheets, low costs of production, and many years of reserves with low decline rates. Lukoil continues to be one of my preferred energy producers.

I also like Texas Pacific Land Trust (TPL), which I own in the Fortress Income portfolio, since that’s a debt-free royalty company with large acreage in the Permian basin without much operating leverage. Among other majors, I also like Total SA (TOT) of France which has some leverage but maintains rather low production costs and solid management.

While many people believe that renewable energy sources will rapidly overtake oil and gas, I think the market has overshot that assumption this year. There will indeed be a continued roll-out of electric vehicles and renewable energy, but there are some very interesting dynamics happening in the oil and gas market.

US oil production reached a peak of 13 million barrels in early 2020. This collapsed to 10 million, and has since rebounded to about 11 million and chopping along sideways. Shale oil accounts for about 7 million barrels per day of this total (down from a peak of 9 million), and has very high decline rates, so unless new capital is continually put into production, US oil supply can deteriorate quickly.

![US Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-us-oil-field-production.png)

If we look at recent rig counts and well completions, there is no sharp rebound that should push this number back up. It’s quite possible that looking back from years in the future, 13 million was the all-time peak US production, but time will tell.

I like a handful of US and Canadian energy transportation companies, but for producers, I generally prefer international producers that don’t have a ton of shale exposure, like the oil sands producers of Canada, Total of France, and Lukoil of Russia. The Texas Pacific Land Trust as a Permian play is also good in my view, especially due to its cost structure.

Lukoil is a Moscow-based energy major that has primary operations throughout Russia, central Asia, eastern Europe, and some assets in Africa and North America:

![Lukoil Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-lukoil-map.png)

*Chart Source: [Lukoil December 2020 Investor Presentation](https://www.lukoil.com/InvestorAndShareholderCenter/ReportsAndPresentations/Presentations)*

The company produces oil and gas, operates refineries, and sells gasoline at stations.

With 18 years of hydrocarbon reserve life, they have more years worth of production at current production rates than any other integrated oil and gas major. They are also one of the lowest-cost producers.

For the first nine months of 2020, their revenue was down 30% from a combination of OPEC+ cuts and the reduction in oil prices. EBITDA was down 48%, and free cash flow was down 62%. That’s how their operating leverage works; their fixed costs naturally make it so that even a moderate revenue decline can cause a major profitability decline, and vice versa.

However, Q3 2020 saw a massive jump from their bottom in Q2 2020. Revenue was up 47%, EBITDA was up 40%, and free cash flow was up 350%.

![Lukoil Results](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-lukoil-results.png)

*Chart Source: Lukoil December 2020 Investor Presentation*

Going forward, oil prices and overall production levels will play a key role in Lukoil’s profitability. Many energy companies performed massive capex cuts in 2020, so as the virus goes away and travel behavior starts normalizing over the next few years (except some percentage of business travel, which is likely permanently “Zoomed” to some extent), energy markets could get a bit tight. In addition, with significant currency debasement this year and extending into next year, the dollar prices of commodities are likely to do reasonably well across the board.

**Management Quality**

Investors in developed markets are often rightfully concerned about the quality of governance for emerging market companies.

Lukoil, however, has been an exceptionally well-managed energy company. They haven’t done financially-destructive acquisitions like many of their international peers have, and they operate with much less financial leverage.

As a result, they have outperformed all other oil majors over the past 3-year, 5-year, 10-year, and 20-year periods, which is impressive. Here’s the 5-year performance chart:

![Lukoil Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-oil-producer-returns.png)

![Lukoil vs Exxon Debt and Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-lukoy-vs-xom-debt.png)

Lukoil’s founder and CEO is Vagit Alekperov, an Azerbaijani/Russian billionaire. He initially worked on oil platforms in the Caspian sea, and then worked his way up the ladder in the Soviet Union’s energy industry. In 1990, he became the Soviet Union’s deputy minister of oil and gas, and as the Soviet Union dissolved and was replaced by the Russian Federation, he founded the company that would become Lukoil, and has been its CEO for three decades, amassing his fortune.

Although it was founded as one, Lukoil is not a state-owned corporation, and is instead one of Russia’s largest publicly-traded private enterprises.

Lukoil has an independent chairman of the board of directors, Ravil Maganov. Mark Mobius, the famous German-American emerging markets investor who in some ways pioneered emerging markets investing over the past 40+ years, used to be on Lukoil’s board as well. Continuing this approach, their board currently has some non-Russian members. Lukoil has a long history of operating with an emphasis on prudent capital allocation.

Nearly 40% of the company is owned by management and the board of directors, while the other 60% is owned by the public. So, management is strongly aligned with shareholder interest. Lukoil places a top priority on the dividend, committing to return virtually all free cash flow to investors primarily in the form of dividends.

**Tax Increases**

Many countries including the United States paid for government budget shortfalls this year by basically printing money and running deficits of 10-20% of GDP. They issued a bunch of bonds, and the central bank created new bank reserves to buy nearly as many bonds as were issued on the secondary market.

Russia went into this year with a government surplus, and tends to be rather austere in terms of government budget and central bank management. With projected fiscal deficits during 2020-2022 due to the pandemic, economic shock, and fiscal aid, Russia has attempted to keep the funding gap as narrow as possible. As part of this approach, they raised taxes on many energy and mineral producers.

Specifically, the tax scheme is progressive, in the sense that the higher the price of oil, the bigger percentage the government takes. This presents a sort of natural hedge; it reduces the profitability of companies like Lukoil during spikes in the oil price, but also softens the blow a bit when oil prices collapse.

**Risks**

*Oil and Gas Price Volatility*

As an oil and gas producer, Lukoil’s fate is of course tied to oil and gas prices, which are volatile and economically-sensitive. There are three reasons why I think oil is headed up in the longer-term, however.

Firstly, there is less investment capital available for US shale oil producers, which have been the disrupters over the past decade for global energy markets. With shale producers starting to be forced into profitability by investors rather than being allowed to continue capital-destructive drilling year after year, the global energy market could normalize, especially after the shock of 2020 on the oil sector. Many companies in the US and elsewhere have slashed capital expenditures, which should keep production tight unless OPEC+ purposely floods the market.

Secondly, by 2022, several components of oil consumption should be back near normal. This could be quite a reflationary rebound, although the timing is hard to pin down.

Thirdly, rapid increases in the broad US money supply should, over time, put upward pressure on commodity prices, especially for ones where there is no supply glut. Gold, silver, copper, and uranium all saw nice price boosts this year. Oil and gas are slower because they came into 2020 with a big oversupply problem (largely due to the growth of unprofitable US shale oil), but that seems to gradually be sorting itself out.

*Electric Vehicles and Renewable Energy*

The longest-term risk for major energy companies like Lukoil is the technological advancement of electric vehicles and renewable energy. As someone with an electrical engineering background, I’ve always been interested in renewable energy and watching its development.

Electric vehicles by themselves are not really a huge disruption to fossil fuels, because electricity itself is not an energy source; it’s just a transmission medium for energy. Coal, natural gas, oil, nuclear reactors, hydroelectric dams, wind farms, solar utilities, and other forms of energy like geothermal or tidal mechanisms need to produce that usable energy so that it can be sent over electrical transmission lines and into someone’s Tesla. With coal being phased out in many areas, that puts more emphasis on natural gas for core electrical generation.

However, as costs for solar and wind energy continue to decrease, these sources of energy may become an increasing competitor to oil and gas. Right now, solar and wind market share of electric generation is low, but with a substantial growth rate. If they become a significant enough contributor, and electric vehicles take off, then the electrical grids in most countries will need to be substantially modified and expanded, which is a huge capital expenditure.

Right now, most of us get household energy from electricity, and vehicle energy from gasoline. If gasoline-powered cars are largely replaced by electric vehicles, then it means both our household and vehicle energy would come over the electrical grid instead of through those two separate mediums, and the current electrical grid in most places is not prepared for that higher level of electrical throughput.

Oil and gas are likely to remain a significant part of the global energy mix for a while, especially in emerging markets where price per unit of energy is very important to their standard of living. And as long as oil stocks stay cheap with high shareholder yields like Lukoil, they can be quite lucrative for shareholders.

Investors often overestimate how quickly something will be disrupted, or assume that government opposition necessarily leads to poor investment returns. That’s not always the case.

Emerging markets, including China and India, are still increasing their oil and gas consumption. In fact, they’re still increasing their coal consumption.

Historically, whenever technology introduced a new energy source, previous energy sources didn’t go down. They just flatlined, and it became additive:

![Global Energy Consumption](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-global-energy.png)

*Chart Source: [Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/energy)*

Coal didn’t replace biomass; it added to it. Oil didn’t replace coal; it added to it. Natural gas didn’t replace oil; it added to it. Nuclear, hydro, and other non-carbon sources didn’t replace gas; they added to it.

This could change at some point. Coal in particular has a lot of pressure on it to wind down over time, since it’s the dirtiest for air quality and other environmental impacts. But overall, global oil and gas demand is still mildly going up, especially in emerging markets, even as non-carbon sources go up faster.

*The “Russian Discount”*

Russia is currently and historically one of the cheapest equity markets, and is still classified as an emerging market despite being one of the world’s top military and economic forces.

The country ranks near the top of international corruption rankings, the population levels are stagnant, GDP growth is slow, and there are more limits to freedom of expression than most developed countries have. Ever since the annexation of Crimea, Russia has been hit with sanctions that have hurt its currency strength and economic growth, although with time this has generally subsided. In fact, a weaker currency makes several Russian commodity exports (energy, metals, and agriculture products) more competitive.

The serious and prolonged hacks of several US federal agencies, evidenced to have come from Russia, could put further geopolitical strain between the nations in 2021 and beyond.

There is always the possibility that certain countries could ban their citizens from owning Russian stocks, which would likely hurt the equity prices of those stocks and may force investors to exit at an inopportune time. This is a low risk for Lukoil in my opinion, but worth noting, and the risk certainly rose a bit after the hacks. Canada already dabbled in doing this for its citizens; Canada has limited some of its citizens’ investment options into Russia since 2014, although it hasn’t affected Lukoil specifically.

## Sberbank (SBRCY) Update

I’m more bullish on Sberbank than Lukoil, but two of my last three deep dive reports were on bank stocks (Scotiabank of Canada and DBS Group of Singapore), and [I covered Sberbank back in July](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-7-5/), so I figured I would make this an add-on rather than a feature analysis.

Sberbank, as a diversified large bank, has a 30-50% market share of the major banking services in Russia:

![Sberbank Market Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-sberbank-market-share.png)

*Chart Source: [Sberbank August 2020 Presentation](https://www.sberbank.com/investor-relations/reports-and-publications/investor-presentation)*

Despite doing well fundamentally, remaining profitable this year, running at a higher ROE than most other banks in the world, and paying high dividends through the biggest economic shock in modern history, Sberbank continues to trade for a single-digit price/earnings ratio, and consensus analyst expectations are for earnings growth. There’s a ton of value here:

![SBRCY F.A.S.T. Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-sbrcy-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

With the banking industry rather saturated (slow-growing but pumping off massive cashflows), Sberbank is increasingly targeting non-financial sectors as well. Sberbank has a huge number of clients and maintains an atypically high-rate of digital adoption (i.e. currently at 60 million monthly active users for their app), so they are increasingly interested in broadening their network effect in other industries.

Kind of like how Amazon used its excess server space to launch a cloud platform for clients, Sberbank has also used the fact that it requires extensive server/IT systems to launch its own cloud platform for clients as well. Plus, they’re involved in e-commerce, e-education, ride-sharing, streaming, machine-learning digital assistants, and more. They’re also looking to get into digital asset trading and custody in 2021.

Non-financial revenue streams are currently a small fraction of Sberbank’s revenue, but the company ambitiously aims for them to reach 50% of revenue and 30% of operating income by 2030. Whether this comes to pass or not, Sberbank is a super-cheap and highly-profitable bank stock that pays a 5.5% growing dividend, and that basically has a free long-term tech “call option” sitting on the shares as well in the event that they are successful in these non-financial endeavors.

**Key Risk: Sanctions**

Sberbank, overall, is one of my most bullish stock picks with a 3-5 year view. Aside from the normal collection of risks associated with any company, the only risk that I consider a very significant potential problem would be regulations or sanctions that could stop Americans or folks from certain other countries from investing in it, and therefore potentially forcing a sale at an inopportune time. If it weren’t for that possibility, I’d double my position.

Due to the combination of being involved in financing, and being directly tied to the Russian government, Sberbank is a bigger sanction target than Lukoil. Unlike Lukoil, Sberbank is owned 50% by the Russian government, making it a state-controlled company. For the remaining half, 45% is owned by foreign investors and 5% is owned by domestic investors.

The Russian public is increasingly investing in equities compared to previous years (and in fact, retail buying jumped a lot this year, just like in many other countries), but as an emerging market with rather low median net worth, and with most of that net worth tied up in real estate, a significant chunk of large-cap Russian equities are owned by the foreign sector.

Despite being state-controlled, Sberbank historically has very successful management. In fact one of the Russian government’s key pushes on Sberbank is to make the dividend a full 50% payout ratio to shareholders, because as their largest shareholder, the Russian government wants robust dividend income for their budget.

Overall, I’m not concerned with any sanctions directly hurting Sberbank much, although I am concerned about US investors being disallowed to hold its shares and potentially having to sell at an inopportune time.

So, risk management is key. My approach with emerging markets, including Russia, is to invest in well-managed companies, but manage position sizes carefully.

## Summary: Across the Spectrum

As I finish this report, I’m reminded of the broad spectrum of investments we go into. This report looked at some of the cheapest beaten-down value stocks in the world, while also examining why I’m remaining long Bitcoin even after its 4x rise from our initial coverage back in April, as one of our key tech/momentum plays.

To leave you with one last chart, I’ll highlight this one from Bank of America and Bloomberg. The top 5 members of the S&P 500 reached a 30+ year record of 25% of the 500-company index in 2020, and this concentration has since turned down sharply:

![S&P 500 Concentration](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-3-spx-concentration.jpg)

With folks stuck at home, and interest rates near zero, technology/growth stocks had a great year. However, investors also piled into them at any price. As a consequence, for example, Apple’s earnings were up about 20% this year while their stock price went up 80%, meaning that valuation rather than earnings fundamentals made up most of the outperformance. Consensus analyst expectations are for high-single digit earnings growth over the next two years for the company, while the P/E ratio is nearly 40x.

My base case for 2021 and 2022 is to see a reversal of that trend, with some of the more cyclical stocks playing catch-up with the tech giants in terms of investment returns. A steeper yield curve tends to be good for financials, rapid increases in the broad money supply tend to be good for commodities, and the post-pandemic environment should favor a continued rebound in some of the higher-quality cyclical companies out there.
