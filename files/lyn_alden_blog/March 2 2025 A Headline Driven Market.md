**A Headline Driven Market**

Back during 2018 and 2019, the market and overall financial conditions were moving in a relatively predictable pattern. Trends tended to persist for a while. One thing would lead to another with pretty reliable likelihood.

Then, when the pandemic hit in 2020, the market was more headline driven, with more binary outcomes. Congress and the Fed performed a hyper-stimulus, there were multiple waves of the pandemic, multiple waves of China locking down hard and then easing, and so forth. And then in early 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine, which among other things contributed to instability in global energy markets. So, 2020-2022 was more of a headline driven market.

By around late 2022, and then through most of 2023 and 2024, the market was back to being more predictable. A lot of it was about watching liquidity conditions, and seeing fiscal dominance keep inflation and nominal GDP on the higher side of average even as the private sector had weak spots (commercial real estate, manufacturing PMIs, and low-income consumer spending).

Thus far, 2025 is firmly back into being a headline driven market. One day, 25% tariffs are announced on Mexico and Canada, and 10% on China. Then, the Mexico and Canada tariffs are delayed or walked back. Then reciprocal tariffs are announced. And then, the tariffs on Mexico and Canada are announced to go into effect in early March, and the tariffs on China are boosted to 20%.

In my prior report, I discussed how the reciprocal tariff policy was a milder and more predictable approach, and generally a de-escalation:

> President Trump has recently shifted toward a [public policy of reciprocal tariffs](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/02/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-announces-fair-and-reciprocal-plan-on-trade/). That is to say, if other countries place tariffs on American goods, the United States would put the same level of tariffs against them in return. If those countries raise or lower their tariffs, the United States would do the same.
>
> Fox business [reports](https://www.foxbusiness.com/politics/trump-signs-reciprocal-tariff-plan-countries-tax-us-goods):
>
> *“On trade, I have decided for purposes of fairness, that I will charge a reciprocal tariff — meaning whatever countries charge the United States of America, we will charge them no more, no less. In other words, they charge us a tax or tariff and we charge them the exact same tax or tariff. Very simple,” he said at the White House.*
>
> Critics of this plan ([such as this WSJ opinion editorial](https://www.wsj.com/opinion/reciprocal-tariffs-make-no-sense-duties-trade-economy-policy-china-d682c356)) argue that it’s complex, prone to frequent change, and basically gives tariff decisions to foreign countries, then. In addition, there is some debate around what counts as a tariff, such as VAT taxes.
>
> However, I view it as a milder and more predictable approach than the tariff policy that started to take shape back in late January. It gives somewhat more clarity on likely tariff amounts for businesses and investors, reduces some of the tail risks, and if anything might put some downward pressure on global tariffs.
>
> This is obviously subject to potential change and needs to be monitored, but overall I view this as a potential de-escalation on tariffs.
>
> -February 16th, 2025 premium report

However, my last line about this being subject to potential change is already kicking in. Based on these latest tariff announcements to put tariffs on Canada and Mexico in March and double the Chinese tariffs, we’re not really in a reciprocal tariffs environment. We’re still in a geopolitical bargaining environment. A headline driven market.

All else being equal, neither businesses nor equity markets tend to like unpredictability. A business trying to plan its capital spending or hiring over the next 12 months has a higher degree of uncertainty going forward, especially if they have any significant international supply chains (which includes a lot of them). An investor trying to value a company generally needs several years of visibility into what is likely to happen with their revenues and expenses.

The market ran in to volatility during the last week of February, as some of this kicked in. The Nasdaq 100’s close on Thursday, February 27th, marked the fastest 5% decline from all-time highs since September 2020. Friday saw a bit of a recovery off those highs.

Now, a 5% decline is nothing to write home about, and many of those stocks were expensive and frothy. But the point is, that’s the kind of move that is more likely to happen in a headline driven market. Volatility is back.

On a recent interview, I was asked if headline driven markets are mainly a Trump administration thing. However, my answer is that I don’t think it’s that simple. For example, the years 2018 and 2019 were in the middle of Trump’s first term, and that was more of a predictable, trending environment rather than a headline driven one.

So, while the administration is certainly a factor, and this second Trump administration is starting out with more of a shock-and-awe approach than his first one, it’s not as simple as categorizing markets by 4-year terms. External events matter, and what politicians are doing within a given period of time also matters. A headline driven market doesn’t necessarily need to persist for four years, and instead has to be taken one step at a time.

Right now, things are rather headline driven, and will likely be for some time to come.

**Fundamental Restructuring**

It’s important to note that President Trump nominated Stephen Miran to be the chairman of the Council of Economic Advisors. After Trump was elected, Miran published a research piece during November 2024 called [A User’s Guide to Restructuring the Global Trading System](https://www.hudsonbaycapital.com/documents/FG/hudsonbay/research/638199_A_Users_Guide_to_Restructuring_the_Global_Trading_System.pdf).

The paper discussed the imbalance of U.S. trade due to the Triffin Dilemma, the dollar being structurally overvalued relative to the currencies of its trading pairs, and how various things may begin to address that imbalance.

Here were the opening lines:

> The desire to reform the global trading system and put American industry on fairer ground vis-à-vis the rest of the world has been a consistent theme for President Trump for decades. We may be on the cusp of generational change in the international trade and financial systems.
>
> The root of the economic imbalances lies in persistent dollar overvaluation that prevents the balancing of international trade, and this overvaluation is driven by inelastic demand for reserve assets. As global GDP grows, it becomes increasingly burdensome for the United States to finance the provision of reserve assets and the defense umbrella, as the manufacturing and tradeable sectors bear the brunt of the costs.
>
> In this essay I attempt to catalogue some of the available tools for reshaping these systems, the tradeoffs that accompany the use of those tools, and policy options for minimizing side effects. This is not policy advocacy, but an attempt to understand the financial market consequences of potential significant changes in trade or financial policy.

For those who have read my book [Broken Money](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR/), a lot of what Miran discusses relates to Chapter 13: *Heavy is the Head that Wears the Crown*, which explores the costs of having the world reserve currency, mainly as it negatively affects the country’s trade balance.

For context, here’s the U.S. trade balance:

![Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-trade-balance.png)

And during that time, our industrial base has flatlined after decades of growth. On a per capita basis, it has decreased:

![Industrial Base](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-industrial-base.png)

Miran goes into the various mechanisms of addressing this, including tariff policies as well as measures that prevent the excess purchasing of U.S. financial assets by certain foreign entities. The huge appetite that the world has for U.S. financial assets (stocks and bonds, and to a lesser extent private equity and real estate) ironically makes it hard for the U.S. to manufacture things or otherwise maintain a strong industrial base, because it drives up the costs and basically forces a structural trade deficit to persist.

In the research piece, Miran discusses tools that may begin to address that, as well as the challenges of trying to tackle such a difficult decades-long imbalance, and the associated volatility and risks that come from doing so.

This further contributes to my prior point about being in a headline driven market, at the moment. Attempts at structural system change don’t come easy, and it remains to be seen how much this will be prioritized even as some of the harder or more painful aspects of reversing long-term structural trends start to emerge.

I’d add that the situation has persisted long enough that a lot of other issues have built up around it, too. For example, since the U.S. hasn’t grown its manufacturing, fewer people have gone into that kind of work, and the social capital (i.e. generations of experience) instead built up where that manufacturing is growing. As another example, the U.S. has the highest healthcare costs in the world, which is a cost center for employers, and puts a lot of competitive pressure against putting jobs in the U.S. that could be elsewhere.

So, those sorts of issues have to be addressed as well, and overall it’s a very complicated process.

There are, however, a lot of other sudden changes in consumer expectations and other areas of the market. While I would fade one brief spike low of data, it’s nonetheless notable. What it mainly helps quantify is my prior point about headline risk. Volatility is higher now, not just for asset prices but also for businesses trying to plan their next 12 months of capex and hiring.

**U.S. Business Cycle Summary**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy was decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, entered stall speed for a while, began to show signs of stabilization, and is now kind of stuck in that phase. The majority of the data is still from Q4 2024, since a lot of this is reported with a lag, which is why I also look at things like the Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow calculator and the Conference Board Leading Index, and similar measures.

CPI peaked in Q2 2022, steadily declined, and has become relatively sticky above the target level for a while.

![Heat Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-business-cycle-heat-map.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

## Digital Assets Note

I had to tweak this section of the report shortly before sending it out, reaffirming my prior point about this being a headline driven market.

I had originally written this, which is mostly still relevant but now comes with an additional caveat below:

> Amid the recent volatility in equity markets, bitcoin tumbled out of its recent range, and currently rests at around $86k. There are some initial signs of a bottom being in, but of course one can never know for sure if it’s “the” bottom or a temporary bottom.
>
> The good news is it reset a lot of the mini-euphoria that was in the market.
>
> Back in March 2024, bitcoin surged to a new high of around $73k, and things got a little frothy. It then consolidated for seven months, falling to around $53k in September, before eventually breaking out to the highs it reached late in the year at around $108k, and it’s been consolidating for a few months. So far, this drawdown to $85k has been smaller drawdown on a percentage basis than the one from $73k to $53k.
>
> One of my favorite on-chain methods is to look at market value relative to realized value. Realized value is basically the average on-chain cost basis. When market value gets way ahead of cost basis, it’s generally a more dangerous or euphoric period.
>
> March 2024 and November 2024 both had similar levels of medium-euphoria, meaning they didn’t hit extremes that are historically associated with multi-year tops, but they did reach substantial levels. And this recent pullback helped drain much of that:
>
> ![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

And then President Trump announced an executive order to establish a strategic crypto reserve, with BTC, ETH, XRP, SOL, and ADA:

![Trump Post](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-trump-post.jpg)

The result as of this writing is that bitcoin quickly surged back up to $94k, and other crypto assets surged as well. Some of that is a rational move based on the news, and then some of that is also short-covering from such a headline.

It remains to be seen how large these positions will be, or under what timeframe they will occur.

I view most cryptocurrencies outside of bitcoin to basically be digital equities, meaning they have an issuer. In that sense, this would be similar to the United States buying certain equities.

I have a similar view as what Naval Ravikant expressed about it on Twitter/X (which he clarified in comments refers to most of them except bitcoin):

![Naval Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-naval-tweet.png)

In general, these types of announcements don’t significantly affect my view on bitcoin, because I base my view on the assumption that such events will not occur. That is, I gear my expectations around long-term adoption and continually re-assess whether it remains the best-in-class network at what it does.

The reason for this is that I’d rather be surprised to the upside, than surprised to the downside. I’m not a fan of basing an investment thesis around the question of whether the United States government will buy the asset I hold or not, and in what amounts, and so I only buy an asset (bitcoin in this case) if I think its fundamentals are sound and reasonably priced for its current level of adoption and expected future range of adoption.

I think its properties are likely to lead to nation states buying it and mining it (and several smaller ones already have), but I prefer not to base decisions around the timing of when a particularly large nation state will buy it, and how much.

Thus, I remain long-term bullish on bitcoin, and will monitor any purchase announcements by the U.S. government, but will consider them to be potential positive upside surprises.

## Investment Analysis: S&P 500 and MSTR

When we look at the broad S&P 500, we can see that it’s rather richly-priced, which is concerning given the headline driven market that we’re now in. The F.A.S.T. Graphs tool that I tend to like for individual stocks normally doesn’t have much use for indices, but they do have a data set for the S&P 500, via the SPY ETF:

![SPY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-spy-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

According to the way they measure it, the S&P 500 has traded for an average of 19.80x adjusted earnings over this period, but is currently trading at 24.76x adjusted earnings.

We can also look at the free cash flow version:

![SPY FASTGraph FCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-spy-fastgraph-fcf.png)

According to the way they measure it, the S&P 500 has traded for an average of 20.45x adjusted earnings over this period, but is currently trading at 29.49 adjusted earnings.

In many ways, this market looks a lot like how it looked at the end of 2021. The stimulus held helped pump asset values during the year, but by 2022 the hangover from that stimulus started to hit, with high consumer price inflation and rapid central bank tightening of financial conditions.

While I do not know if that will occur again, the market is generally priced for perfection, and so I think it’s a time to be mindful of risks. It could be that the market corrects a bit, or perhaps it just trends sideways in a range for a while until fundamentals catch back up. It’s also possible that fundamentals could accelerate more quickly than Wall Street consensus analysts expect.

Now, to be clear, that doesn’t mean I suggest to avoid owning stocks. A lot of that high valuation is concentrated in a handful of companies. I have received emails for many years going back to 2017 from people being concerned about high equity prices, and asking if they should go to cash and wait for a better buying opportunity. And along that time, stocks have generally kept grinding higher, despite some notable corrections in late 2018, early 2020, and 2022.

My approach with these types of markets tends to be to 1) make sure the stocks I own aren’t as frothy on average as the market, 2) diversify to other equity areas that have less froth, 3) sell covered calls or cash-secured puts to get paid for volatility in a choppy market, and 4) and own other assets like precious metals, bitcoin, and cash-equivalents.

As a base case, I’m not bullish on the S&P 500 for 2025. There are events that could change that, but in general I view this as a more selective investment environment.

**MSTR**

I also want to take a moment to highlight MSTR here, given the recent volatility.

MicroStrategy (MSTR) has changed its name to Strategy. Back in the 1990s, the co-founder and executive chairman Michael Saylor bought up a lot of one-word URLs, such as hope.com, michael.com, strategy.com, etc. And so for this purpose, they do already own strategy.com.

I like to monitor the premium that MSTR trades relative to the value of its bitcoin.

In November 2024, they reached a premium to NAV of well over 3x, meaning that the stock price was trading for more than 3x the value of the bitcoin. It’s now down to around 1.5x which is pretty healthy (via [mstr-tracker.com](https://www.mstr-tracker.com/)).

![MSTR Tracker](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-mstr-tracker.png)

For the No Limits portfolio in this research service, I trimmed part of the MSTR position in mid-March, and another portion in mid-November, within days of both of those two local tops:

![MSTR Timings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-mstr-timings.png)

I normally don’t time things that well, but the premium-to-NAV metric is just so visible, and then I also closely monitor Twitter/X chatter and see anecdotally (but pretty broadly) the level of euphoria that exists in those moments, not just for bitcoin but for MSTR specifically.

Thus, this big MSTR drawdown hasn’t affected the No Limits portfolio much, because we already took a ton of profits from the position and made sure not to be overly tied to any one security during moments of euphoria.

Now that the euphoria is washed out, it’s interesting again. I’m not exactly ready to jump back in with both feet, especially since it’s likely to surge higher in the open on Monday if current bitcoin price levels hold, but it’s back on my radar as potentially interesting.

They have about $9.2 billion in convertible debt with an average maturity of 5.1 years, which is pretty reasonable relative to the market capitalization of over $64 billion.

For the most part, I recommend buying bitcoin rather than MSTR. However, for select portfolios in brokerage environments, MSTR can make for an interesting position when it’s out of favor. I don’t have a strong conviction that this is the bottom, but I do see that a lot of the froth has been washed out of it, so I’m willing to monitor it more closely as a potential long again. I still have a small position in the No Limits portfolio.

## Final Thoughts: Cash-Secured Puts

I expect that as headline volatility remains elevated, and as there are more headwinds to the buy-the-dip trend given the macro themes described earlier in this report, I’ll be focusing more on selling cash-secured put options. It’s a strategy to get paid to wait to enter positions that you like the fundamentals of.

Literally eight years ago (how time flies), I posted [an article on selling put options](https://www.lynalden.com/selling-put-options/). Some examples might be dated, but the core article content itself was written to be evergreen.

![Selling Put Options Graphic](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-Selling-Put-Options-Rate-of-Return.jpg)

There are plenty of equities on the market that I’d love to own for the next five years, but would prefer to buy a bit lower, and get paid to wait to do so. Selling cash-secured puts is one of my preferred ways to do that, when market conditions are right for it.

That way, I either buy them at a lower price amid a sell-off (having done a lot of fundamental analysis on their long-term bullish potential), or I get paid despite the stock never falling to my target price. The more volatility there is, the higher option premiums tend to be.

During 2023 and 2024 in particular, I was focused on other aspects of the market, and equities were relatively docile, so selling options wasn’t my focus. However, here in 2025 we’re entering a market that I think is more conducive to emphasizing that area a bit more.
