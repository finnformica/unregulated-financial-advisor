## Macro View

“Stagflation” continues to be the general theme in global markets, with recessionary indicators flashing red while inflation remains persistent.

**US Economic Snapshot**

The US economy continues to decelerate into recession-level conditions.

The ERCI weekly leading index, for example, looks grim:

![ECRI WLI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-ecri-wli.png)

*Chart Source: [Advisor Perspectives](https://www.advisorperspectives.com/dshort/updates/2022/07/15/ecri-weekly-leading-index-update)*

GDP numbers for Q2 (April through June) will be reported this upcoming week. The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow estimator has a -1.6% annualized real rate as its current baseline estimate for Q2:

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-gdp-now.png)

*Chart Source: [Atlanta Fed](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow)*

Multiple regional assessments continue to be concerning here in Q3 as well. For example the Philly Fed manufacturing data for mid-July came in very weak:

![Philly Fed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-philly-fed.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Weekly jobless claims continue their upward trend, indicating a softer labor market:

![Initial Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-initial-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

A number of analysts proposed going into this year that financial assets would be weak due to Fed tightening, but that the underlying economy would be strong. The problem with that analysis is that the US is so financialized, that financial assets doing poorly contributes to weak economic growth. In other words, the tail is so big that the tail can wag the dog. We’re a very consumer-oriented economy, and so when stocks and bonds go down in price, it often curtails spending by people, which curtails economic growth, which results in labor market weakness, which results in more spending curtailment, which then results in weaker tax revenues.

To quantify that a bit, US household net worth reached 6.2x as high as GDP late last year, which is much higher than the 4.9x it reached in 2007 and much higher than the 4.4x it reached in 2000.

![Net Worth to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-net-worth-to-gdp.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As interest rates went steadily downward from the early 1980s, debt levels and asset valuations grinded upward. With rates having bounced around zero for a while and inflation starting to become an issue, it’s very hard to maintain those asset valuations. But at the same time, when those asset prices fall, it causes a negative wealth effect and pushes the US towards recession.

**Treasury and Fed Policy Analysis**

The 10yr-2year Treasury curve has firmly inverted, and the 10yr-3mo yield curve is now catching down to it:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-yield-curve.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Historically, the 2-year Treasury yield gives us a good idea of how high the Fed Funds rate will get during a hiking cycle. Here’s a zoomed-out and zoomed-in set of charts for the 2-year Treasury yield and the Fed funds rate:

![2 Year Treasury vs Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-2yr-vs-fed-funds.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Momentum on the 2-year Treasury yield has already rolled over and suggests that the top in the 2-year Treasury yield is in or nearly in (especially when we also consider that 10yr-2yr yield curve inversion has already occurred):

![2 Year Yield Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-2yr-yield-technicals.png)

The Fed is widely expected to raise rates by 75 basis points next week, which will bring their rate up to 2.25%. If they manage another 50 basis points after that during their September meeting, followed by 25 basis points during their November meeting, followed by a pause, then that would top out at around 3%.

Hiking to 3% or a bit higher is still well below the inflation rate, but with so much debt in the system and a recession forming, it’ll be hard for the Fed to keep pushing beyond that point.

![Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-inflation.png)

On one hand, if one is unfamiliar with history, it’s surprising that the yield curve inverted while the Fed’s interest rate is still 7.5% below the prevailing inflation rate. The Fed is still the most accommodative they have been in most of our lifetimes on an inflation-adjusted basis, and yet the market is already telling them that they’re nearing the stopping point of what has been a rather feeble hiking cycle thus far.

On the other hand, this environment of high inflation yet low interest rates is precisely what those who are familiar with history would expect in the “inflate the debt away” stage of a [long-term debt cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/).

![Total Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-total-debt.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Short-term interest rates have spent most of their time below the prevailing inflation rate for over a decade now, which is basically the only realistic outcome once total debt (public and private) is this high relative to economic output. Maintaining over 300% total debt to GDP becomes untenable at structurally positive real rates, and so that debt load either starts to default nominally or starts to default in real terms via persistently negative inflation-adjusted rates on the short end of the curve (and maybe the long end too, like currently). It’s financial repression 101.

Policymakers can push back on that trend and try to harden things from time to time when inflation gets disorderly, but it’s very difficult to maintain that tightness, since tightening to any significant degree will likely result in recession and falling tax revenue.

Caution is warranted as we see how far the Fed tries to push it in this cycle, but the market is starting to signal that it’ll be hard to go more than a few more meetings worth of hikes from here.

**European Economic Snapshot**

Europe as a whole is in worse shape economically than the US for the most part.

![Euro Area PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-euro-area-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

The purchasing manager’s index and other indicators are suggesting that the continent is in a period of economic contraction now. The leading components of those indicators suggest further weakness ahead as well, rather than recovery any time soon.

European natural gas prices spiked in late 2021, and remain elevated at several times higher than American natural gas prices.

![Europe Natural Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-europe-natural-gas.png)

Electricity prices are also very high in several European countries, with charts that look similar to that natural gas price chart. This makes it very hard to manufacture things at competitive prices.

The European Central Bank performed their first rate hike in about a decade, going up 50 basis points. However, much like the US, this remains well below inflation levels (about 8% below in Europe’s case). In addition, they launched a tool that will allow them to continue to monetize Italian sovereign debt as needed, to avoid a fiscal spiral. Italy’s working age population is outright shrinking, which makes it nearly impossible for the country to grow its way out of its 150% debt-to-GDP problem.

This all puts the Euro Area in a very tight spot; they have raging energy insecurity, and are stuck in checkmate for southern European debt monetization in a period of high inflation. This will likely challenge their internal politics for quite a while, as they work to bring on additional power sources and gas supplies where possible.

Recently, Europe dealt with heat waves which put pressure on their power systems. As the continent heads towards winter later this year, which is an even more vulnerable situation for them, it remains unclear to what extent Russia may or may not try to leverage the supply of its natural gas exports to try to obtain geopolitical concessions from European leaders.

## Digital Assets Note

Things have been relatively quiet in the cryptocurrency space, with no more major liquidations recently.

**Tether Update**

I’ve been monitoring Tether or “USDT” in recent months as a liquidity bellwether for the digital assets ecosystem. USDT and USDC generally serve as units of account in offshore trading environments, so expansion or contraction in the market capitalization of these fiat-collateralized stablecoins tends to be a measure of whether capital is flowing into or out of the ecosystem as a whole.

Tether saw huge redemptions during the Terra/Luna fiasco in May, and then a another huge round of redemptions during the Three Arrows Capital fiasco in June, but since then has been pretty quiet:

![USDT Market Cap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-usdt-marketcap.png)

*Chart Source: [CoinMarketCap](https://coinmarketcap.com/currencies/tether/)*

Altogether, Tether had nearly $18 billion in redemptions. Some of the value went over to USDC, while the majority of it simply left the ecosystem as a whole. Since USDC is now 100% in cash and Treasuries, while Tether uses a portfolio model of multiple types of collateral assets, USDC is often perceived as more transparent and less risky than Tether. However, Tether is also increasingly shifting its corporate paper over to Treasuries as well, now that interest rates are above zero for T-bills.

Similar to a bank, fully-collateralized stablecoin issuers make money on the spread between assets and liabilities. Their liabilities are their tokens, which yield zero but offer crypto-native dollar liquidity for users. Their assets are their collateral, which include things like Treasuries and corporate paper.

Back when T-bills yielded zero, USDT and USDC used a diversified portfolio mix to get some corporate yield. However, now they can profitably run with all T-bills and bank cash as collateral. For example, Tether has $65 billion in assets under management, and for every 1% they earn on their collateral, that represents $650 million in annual revenue.

During recent months, Tether was attacked by certain market participants to see if they could break the peg (along with DDoS attacks as well), but so far they have been unsuccessful, and Tether has been able to meet all redemptions with no issue. The peg has been ranging around $0.999 for a while but in recent days has moved back up to being worth a full $1.00.

I’m not particularly concerned about either USDC or Tether in general, but due to lack of regulatory clarity and due to nonzero counterparty risk, I wouldn’t hold a ton of value in either of them long-term.

Overall, the fact that Tether is no longer being redeemed and that it reclaimed its full $1.00 peg is a decent sign that liquidity is not leaving the ecosystem anymore, but liquidity is also not yet coming back in for the most part either. It’s something I plan to continue monitoring.

**Bitcoin Update**

Bitcoin has enjoyed a bit of a bounce off its lows. I have no idea if the “bottom is in” for this cycle or not, but it’s in a pretty significant historical value range.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-hodl-wave.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/1-year-hodl-wave/)*

Addresses that are estimated to belong to small holders continue to add to their holdings, while big corporate or institutional-scale players have generally been selling. Tesla announced that in Q2 of this year they reduced their bitcoin position by 75%, for example.

Small holders that buy their own hardware wallets and bring their coins into on-chain cold storage tend to be pretty knowledgeable and convicted about the asset, so it’s always interesting when addresses that are mostly estimated to belong to them are going up quickly.

I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into bitcoin as being the best way to get exposure to the industry for most people, rather than trying to trade it around too frequently. And when it’s in a rather cheap and low sentiment period as it is now, I like to lean into it a bit with some extra purchases.

For the No Limits portfolio, I’m holding the GBTC exposure at current levels, and it is now up a bit to $14.48 since I made this graphic last month.

![GBTC Trades](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-gbtc-trades.png)

From the original position, I sold two 100-share tranches during its euphoric spike upward, bought back a 50-share tranche after Luna/Terra blew up and capitulated their coins to the market, and bought back another 50-share tranche after Three Arrows Capital “3AC” blew up and capitulated their coins to the market. This is less about precisely timing the market, and more about rebalancing and managing overall risk exposure.

I would be willing to add a final 50-100 share tranche if we see even deeper capitulation later this year, but either way, I continue to risk-manage that GBTC position in a counter-cyclical way where possible, without trying to specifically call tops or bottoms. GBTC currently trades at a 30% discount to its NAV.

**Lightning Update**

The Lightning network that runs on the bitcoin base layer continues to grow despite the bear market:

![Lightning Capacity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-lightning-capacity.png)

*Chart Source: Look Into Bitcoin*

![Arcane Research](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-arcane-research.png)

*Chart Source: [Arcane Research, The State of Lightning Volume 2](https://arcane.no/research/reports/the-state-of-lightning-volume-2)*

Lightning has a pretty clear virtuous cycle in place. The more liquidity that joins the network, the easier it becomes to route bigger and bigger payments (since it’s all based on finding paths between any two points with adequate liquidity to transfer a given transaction size). The easier it becomes to route payments, the more useful the network is and the more liquidity that will likely come to it. It’s a growing network effect.

However, it’s still very small, it doesn’t have a lot of quick gamified incentives to onboard people other than those who want to use it for actual payments utility, and so it’s not on a lot of peoples’ radar yet. Nobody is going to get rich quickly from Lightning; to the extent that it will be successful, it’ll be about the long grind. The 2018-2020 period consisted of initial launches and initial liquidity bootstrapping, while the 2021-2022 period is really its first era as a truly usable network.

Despite it not being a “get rich quick scheme”, I’ve been optimistic on what I’ve seen in the Lightning startup scene as well, when looking at businesses with 5-10 year investment time horizons like any normal venture. A significant amount of capital has been raised over the past couple years to bring better infrastructure and applications to the network by small startup companies.

A new startup called Fedi Inc recently [raised $4.2 million](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/technical/how-fedimint-scales-bitcoin-custody) in a seed round to create a mobile wallet application by next year that plans to make use of federated chaumian mints. This is a custodial solution that is more private than existing custodial solutions, which therefore may be able to increase fungibility and decentralization across the network. It relies on open source software that is currently being developed, which combines older chaumian mint technology with newer Lightning network technology. The seed round was led by Kingsway Capital, ego death capital ([the VC firm that I advise](https://egodeath.capital/blog/nuq1mgrplvok9pzy11wnjaxxangmd9)), and Ten31.

**Ethereum Update**

Ethereum developers have recently proposed mid-September 2022 as the rescheduled date for the Merge, which refers to the network’s transition from proof-of-work to proof-of-stake. This will make Ethereum more like most other smart contract blockchains, since many of them already use proof-of-stake.

The developers have delayed their timeframe before, but this renewed call for a specific date has been decent for price action, likely contributing to a sizable bounce for ETHUSD and ETHBTC trading pairs. ETH continues to come off of exchanges and get locked up in one-way staking contracts, and about a third of all staked ETH is in Lido.

Due to the way Ethereum operates, including its intended transition towards proof-of-stake and its difficulty bombs that give developers the power to push updates onto users, I continue to categorize Ethereum as a digital equity rather than as a digital commodity. It’s a developer-run smart contract ecosystem that competes with other smart contract ecosystems as an environment for creating, leveraging, and trading various coins and tokens.

I’m agnostic about the price action in the near term, but my base case is to view the Merge as a “sell the news” event. Renewed marketing activity and optimism can support the price leading up to it and shortly after it, but the question then becomes who is the marginal new buyer/holder after the merge. I expect that marketing attacks against proof-of-work will heat up as well, once Ethereum makes the switch to proof-of-stake.

For Ethereum bulls, I would continue to monitor competition like Avalanche to see to what extent Ethereum maintains or loses market share to these sorts of cheaper platforms. Ethereum maintains dominance in art-focused NFTs thus far, while DeFi and crypto gaming have been more fluid in moving to other blockchains to minimize fee pressure.

Ethereum has network effects from its high liquidity and developer activity (Anthony Hopkins has “.eth” in his Twitter name, as do a number of other celebrities, which is a testament to how far into culture the Ethereum network reached), but also has some anti-network effects from high fees that cause users to spill out onto other platforms. Developers try to solve this problem with second-layer Ethereum solutions, but those second-layer solutions compete with various layer-one solutions like Avalanche.

![Hopkins](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-hopkins.png)

NFT volumes have been rather low lately, however, and OpenSea announced layoffs for 20% of its staff here in July.

![OpenSea Volume](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-opensea-volume.png)

*Source: [punk9059](https://twitter.com/punk9059)*

It remains to be seen of course to what extent NFT enthusiasm will bounce back in the next crypto bull cycle. People like being part of shared online communities, or finding ways to support their favorite artists. However, because more and more art sets were created over time, it eventually resulted in buyer and price exhaustion.

## Dividend Stocks Update

There’s an old adage that goes, “sometimes the best thing to do is nothing”. When it comes to investing, people always feel that they need to be “doing something”.

I continue to view energy pipelines (like EPD and ET), healthcare stocks (like BMY, CI, and CVS), various dividend growth stocks in general, and gold to be among the best defensive assets in this environment. Dividend compounders in particular are well-positioned for a 5+ year outlook in my view. On the offensive side, banks (like BAC and HBD), homebuilders, industrials, and bitcoin tend to go down in risk-off scenarios but remain fundamentally attractive with 3-5 year views as well.

During stagflation, I want profitable dividend-paying businesses, commodities, and scarce monies at the core of my liquid portfolio, along with my illiquid real estate exposure with low long-term fixed-rate mortgage debt attached. Cash and Treasuries can round out the anti-volatility portion for opportunistic rebalancing, and a small percentage of select growth stocks can round out the more speculative side, especially when they are oversold and rates seem to be topping.

I’m looking at some restaurant stocks and other potential investments, but due to where we are in the cycle, those would perhaps best be left for a later report.

In short, I am treating this as an inflationary recession, and those historically tend to look more like a malaise than a bust. Many analysts seem to be treating this like a massive deflationary recession, such as 2008 or 2020, but I view such comparisons as fighting the last battle. This is more like one of the recessions that occurred within the 1940s or 1970s decades.

If you expect a sharp downturn in asset prices, then a 10-year Treasury note yielding 2.8% with zero growth makes sense. It can be a useful trading asset, which is why I bought some last month as they started to hit around 3% yields. Even as a long-term bond bear, I began finding them somewhat attractive for the intermediate term.

However, there are plenty of dividend stocks that yield 3% or more, that also grow their dividends each year, and have a multi-decade track record of raising their dividends through recessions. And some of the banks that ran into trouble in 2008 are in much better shape today.

Bank of America (BAC) remains attractive in my view for the next 3-5 years.

The stock is starting to show signs of a momentum change right when everyone seems to be bearish on banks, but frankly there’s no price action, bullish or bearish, that would surprise me over the next six months in this challenging macro environment:

![BAC Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-bac-technicals.png)

As I mentioned in the prior report, the financial situation is almost the opposite of 2008. Banks on average have a high allocation to cash and Treasuries, and have very conservative loan books.

![Bank Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-bank-assets.png)

I’m bearish on quite a significant number of things, but widespread bank loan losses in the US aren’t particularly one of those things. I think there are plenty of problems that would emerge and force a policy change before it gets to that point. Will loan losses tick up a bit? Yes I think so. But I think that scenario is already quite discounted.

Banks are indeed facing pressure in 2022 and likely 2023 but 1) their balance sheets are prepared for that and 2) they are valued for that. Does that mean bank stocks have to go up over the next six months? No. They could go up or sideways or down. But as a long-term investor, I think many of them are attractive as buy-and-holds here, especially when paired with more defensive investments like healthcare stocks.

If we focus on more defensive stocks, then companies like Amgen (AMGN) look attractive:

With AMGN, investors get a yield that is slightly higher than a 10-year Treasury yield, along with a decade of consecutive annual dividend growth that of course a Treasury does not offer. Normally I would say that the equity investor also has to put up with higher volatility in exchange for these better expected returns, but in recent years long-duration Treasuries have had as much volatility as a basket of large cap dividend-growing healthcare stocks.

One of the biggest background risks for healthcare stocks is the possibility of drug pricing reform, but given the rather split US government that is likely going to get more split in November, I don’t expect any major drug price reform for at least the next 2+ years and probably a lot longer than that. They can potentially pass rather mild reforms.

If we look at some of the more cyclical names, then Stanley Black and Decker (SWK) looks interesting:

They’re the big maker of hand tools and power tools, like their namesake brands as well as Craftsman and DeWalt. They generally grow through acquisitions, and absorb them over time, which is common among large-cap industrial stocks. And after this recent drop in price, they are back down to the same stock price they were at in 2016.

SWK has 55 years of consecutive dividend increases. They have been increasing their dividend every year since the late 1960s. After they made it through 2008 and 2020, I think it’s unlikely that here in 2022 or 2023 will be the recession that breaks their streak. Of course, in an extreme scenario, the share price can still fall further such as it did in 2008, with a yield that goes well over 4%.

## Final Thoughts

Going into 2022, I was pretty cautious about the stock market and wrote about that quite a bit. After it fell substantially, I’ve been very slowly leaning into a bit more stock and bitcoin exposure around the margins, without necessarily calling any sort of “bottom”.

The bullish case for the stock market going forward is that many stocks have already fallen tremendously, sentiment is pretty washed out, everyone is talking about a recession, and long-term interest rates are no longer going up, which means one of the valuation pressures on equities is gone. If this is a stagflationary malaise rather than a deflationary bust, then a lot of bears could be quite offsides.

![Put Call Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-putcall.png)

*Source: [SentimenTrader](https://twitter.com/sentimentrader)*

![Google Trends](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-24-google-trends.jpg)

*Source: [FusionptCapital](https://twitter.com/FusionptCapital)*

The bearish case for the stock market is that as the recession hits, earnings on many companies will disappoint, and the Fed has just started quantitative tightening. From that perspective, what could happen now is a bear market rally at best, before further pain ahead as a recession invariably brings down asset prices much further.

To me, it’s about a stock-by-stock and industry-by-industry view. Defensive stocks are still a better bet than many others in my view, but many cyclical stocks have already been pretty decimated. Bank of America stock fell 36% from its high. Stanley Black and Decker fell 53%. While these companies will have a challenging 2022/2023 period, I don’t think they are going away anytime soon, and the stock market tends to front-run earnings to the upside and downside.

As I covered in a report a month ago, inflationary spikes due tend to result in weak earnings, but those weak earnings tend to be sideways and choppy in nominal terms due to so much inflation occurring, while being negative in real terms.

![Earnings vs Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-earnings-vs-inflation.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

My base case, which could change as more data come in, is to expect a very choppy and rangebound market for many types of risk assets. Many assets could easily go up or down 10-20% without it being surprising. My guiding star continues to be to focus fundamentally on high-quality assets, with an awareness towards business cycles and investor sentiment.
