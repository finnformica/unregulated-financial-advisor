## Macro View

The US business cycle continues to show economic deceleration in most areas, but a sticky labor market.

The ISM manufacturing purchasing manager’s index has been rolling over since 2021, and as of November and December of 2022 it crossed down into mild contraction (sub-50) territory. The services PMI is less cyclical overall, but in December it had an unusually strong downward print:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-pmi.png)

The [Conference Board Leading Economic Index](https://www.conference-board.org/topics/us-leading-indicators) continues to point towards significant deceleration and potential recession:

![Leading Economic Indicator](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-leading-economic-indicator.png)

The yield curve continues to get even more inverted, suggesting a high likelihood of a recession:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-yield-curve.png)

Meanwhile, the Atlanta Fed [estimates](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow) that the economy grew at a 3.8% annualized real pace in Q4, which has not yet been officially reported.

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-gdp-now.png)

Economic growth is not evenly distributed. The St. Louis Fed recently [cited data](https://www.stlouisfed.org/on-the-economy/2022/dec/are-state-economic-conditions-harbinger-national-recession) from the Philadelphia Fed showing that on average at the eve of a recession, 26 out of 50 states have a shrinking economy. As of Q4 2022, they estimated that the US has crossed that threshold:

![State Recessions](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-state-recessions.png)

The strongest area of the economy continues to be the labor market, which so far is still holding up strong (but tends to be a lagging indicator). The unemployment rate remains at 3.5%, which is a multi-decade low:

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-unemployment-rate.png)

People are more likely to quit their job when they perceive the economy to be strong, and fewer people quit when they feel like it would be hard to get another job. The quit rate currently is quite high in absolute terms, but is negative in year-over-year percent change terms:

![Job Quits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-job-quits.png)

Employers tend to reduce temporary help before they lay off full time employees, and so temporary help jobs have historically been a leading indicator of broad employment. They just touched negative on a year-over-year basis:

![Temporary Employees](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-temporary-employees.png)

If we focus on the labor participation rate, it’s not good either. After reaching a small local peak in March 2022 from the March 2020 bottom, it has been flat, and it has a bigger structural demographics headwind working against it:

![Labor Force Participation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-labor-force-participation.png)

From the 1960s into the early 2000s, the US labor force participation rate had two massive forces behind it.

The first big force was culturally that women entered the workforce in significant numbers, leading to the rise of double-income households. The labor force participation rate for women roughly doubled from the 1950s to the 1990s, until it eventually peaked at around 60% and rolled over a bit from there:

![Labor Force Participation by Gender](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-labor-force-participation-by-gender.png)

And the other big force was that the very large baby boomer generation, born from the mid-1940s to the mid-1960s, entered their working-age years. The older members of the generation reached their 20s in the mid-1960s, and the younger members of the generation reached their 20s in the mid-1980s. They then began reaching their 60s in the mid-2000s through the mid-2020s. This was an unusually large generation, and so the period where they were virtually all in the workforce in the late 1990s was the peak for US demographics.

As the US workforce began aging, the labor force participation rate began to roll over. However, it was offset by globalization. China entered the World Trade Organization in 2001, and for the next two decades they supplied a massive amount of labor and manufacturing capacity to the United States and the rest of the world.

Here are monthly Chinese exports (in billions of USD):

![China Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-china-exports.png)

And here is their trade balance (in billions of USD):

![China Balance of Trade](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-china-balance-of-trade.png)

Now, China has also reached peak demographics, and may also be running into some geographical constraints regarding water usage, which is an important input to electrical generation including for coal and nuclear power generation facilities. China generates about twice as much electricity as the United States, and uses a lot of it for their enormous industrial base that supplies the rest of the world with manufactured goods.

So, the US and the rest of the developed world collectively have a rather top-heavy population, with a large need for goods and services and not necessarily enough people to comfortably supply them. The release valve for the past two decades has been Chinese and other emerging market manufacturing, but the low-hanging fruit from those regions have likely already been picked.

I think we’ll see continued globalization in certain services, thanks to all the recent advances in remote work technology. And AI should offer a solid productivity boost for a number of industries as well. However, globalization in manufacturing may be reaching its peak and rolling over, leaving developed countries with more issues related to their top-heavy populations to deal with, at least around the margins. All else being equal, with more geopolitical frictions around globalization, this should be a background structural inflationary force for a while.

The way that I’ve phrased it before is that “if one country ages alone (e.g. Japan), that’s deflationary, but if all countries age together, that’s inflationary”. I think the developed world is generally entering that second type of environment.

**Chinese Re-Opening**

China has abruptly pivoted out of its zero-COVID policy that it maintained for three years.

I’ve been observing and reporting on some of the extreme and concerning social controls in China for a while, and they pulled the plug on a number of them very quickly. A sharp drop in economic output combined with protests across multiple cities, seem to have convinced officials to reframe the narrative.

In fact, they’ve pivoted out of the policy so quickly, that other countries are [temporarily putting restrictions on travelers from China](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/01/04/world/europe/china-travelers-covid.html), because many people in China have the virus all of a sudden after not being exposed to it for so long.

Back in my November 27th report, I analyzed the Taiwan pivot out of its zero-covid policy, including the sharp uptick in cases that occurred when they did so.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin continues to chop along with no major rises or falls recently.

Although I view this as a great accumulation zone for a 3-5 year holding period, I remain rather uncertain about its price action for Q1 2023. Macro liquidity dynamics are still not great, and so demand for bitcoin continues to be weak.

Historically, bitcoin has done well in risk-on, rising liquidity environments and has done poorly in risk-off, falling liquidity environments. Liquidity dynamics tend to lead economic activity by a couple quarters, and bitcoin tends to be coincident with those liquidity dynamics and therefore leading of that economic activity. For example, bitcoin has been rather correlated with (and a leading indicator for) the US manufacturing PMI:

![Bitcoin vs PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-bitcoin-pmi.png)

*Source: [YCharts](https://ycharts.com/store/start_trial_register?utm_source=lyn+alden)*

As the HODL wave chart shows, during raging bull markets the percentage of coins held for over a year falls significantly. During bear markets, the percentage of coins held for over a year rises significantly. Right now, the percentage of coins held for over a year is at all-time highs:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-hodl-wave.png)

If price starts to shows evidence of a bottom and the HODL wave starts to roll over, I would be more confident that a bottom is in from a trading perspective. Until then, I continue to separate timeframes as long-term bullish but intermediate-term cautious.

For most people who want some bitcoin as part of their asset mix, I think [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) or rebalancing is the optimal way to do it rather than trying to trade it too much.

I also continue to work with [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/) in terms of venture financing of bitcoin-related startups, including startups that are building out better custody, payments, and backend infrastructure solutions.

**Digital Currency Group**

Digital Currency Group, which is a crypto “empire” that owns Grayscale, Genesis, and a number of other subsidiaries, continues to have legal and financial questions around it.

Genesis is a major crypto trading desk and lender stretching back to its founding in 2013, and it was heavily impaired during the insolvency of Three Arrows Capital in mid-2022, since Genesis was one of Three Arrows Capital’s main lenders. The parent company of Genesis, DCG, absorbed that hit, and owes over $1.6 billion because of it. The FTX/Alameda collapse in November 2022 put another big hole in Genesis/DCG, and Genesis/DCG has been accused of a number of major industry entities for stalling and lack of transparency as it faces liquidity and solvency issues. Now, there are reports that the [SEC is investigating them](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-01-07/crypto-empire-dcg-faces-us-investigation-over-internal-transfers) over their internal transfers.

Their cash cow product is the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC), which is currently trading at a 45% discount to net asset value. GBTC has over $10 billion in assets under management, and at a 2% annual fee (which is too high) it earns over $200 million per year. If sold to another asset manager at a 3x multiple, that could bring in $600 million in liquidity. A new owner could then reduce the fee, restructure and improve it, etc.

David Bailey, the CEO of Bitcoin Magazine, has been a vocal activist proponent of redeeming GBTC. Valkyrie, a registered investment advisor and operator of a futures-based bitcoin ETF, has proposed themselves to become the new sponsor and manager of GBTC.

A lot of this is due to the lack of a spot bitcoin ETF in the US. There are spot bitcoin ETFs in Canada and other countries that work fine, but the US has been slow to allow one. And so, the main ways to get bitcoin price exposure in a typical brokerage account are either through Grayscale (which fluctuates wildly around net asset value), MicroStrategy (which has an operating company and leverage associated with its bitcoin and thus offers a different investment profile), bitcoin miners (which have operating and financial leverage) or futures-based ETFs (which over long periods of time can substantially underperform their underlying commodity exposure). If GBTC could be converted into an ETF, it would do so at 100% of net asset value, thus unlocking a lot of value.

Overall, GBTC continues to have uncertainty around it. In the long run, a redemption, sale, or ETF conversion would be a good thing, but the path between now and then is uncertain and comes with counterparty risk. Buying bitcoin directly is preferable when possible, but is not always possible in brokerage accounts.

## Banking Sector and Truist Financial Deep Dive Analysis

My outlook for the banking sector is mixed, but seems to differ considerably from the consensus.

I’m rather uncertain about the pricing of most risk assets in 2023, including bank stocks, due to recession risk.  However, if a recession does occur, I don’t think the banking sector will be at the epicenter of it like it was in the 2008 recession. I think a lot of macro analysts are “fighting the last battle” and assuming that if a recession occurs, it would devastate the banking system. I think the banking system is one of the stronger areas of the economy currently, and is priced inexpensively.

In other words, I would say that I am “less bearish” on banks than the market seems to be, even though I’m not exactly pounding the table on them yet. I like them for diversification and generally think they’ll outperform the S&P 500 over the next five years, inclusive of dividends.

The summary is that I’m bullish on bank stocks with a 3-5 year view but uncertain about them for the next 6-12 months, and I’ll walk through the various parts of that thesis.

**Bank Balance Sheets**

US bank balance sheets are a lot more conservatively-positioned here in 2023 than they were in the 2006-2008 period.

For example, the percentage of total bank assets that is in cash and Treasuries is historically high, whereas in 2008 it reached generational lows:

![Bank Cash and Treasuries](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-bank-cash-treasuries.png)

In the 2006-2008 period leading up to the financial crisis, banks had an unusually low allocation to nominally risk-free assets like cash and Treasuries. Instead, they were very aggressively making loans and buying riskier securities, and much of that blew up.

The US government and Federal Reserve recapitalized them during and after the crisis, and imposed new regulations. Banks became more conservative in general, and specifically have been less aggressive in lending. These days, most of them are pretty conservatively-positioned.

Another way of looking at this is to chart the monetary base as a % of total bank loans. The smaller the number is, the higher the domestic money multiplier is. It reached generational lows of a little over 12% in 2008 and then a sea change occurred:

![Monetary Base to Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-monetary-base-to-bank-loans.png)

It eerily reached around the same number in 1930 after the 1929 crash, followed by a similar sea change:

![Monetary Base vs Bank Loans 1930s](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-monetary-base-1930s.png)

Long-term readers will of course know what I think about the 2010s to 1930s comparison (and by extension, the 2020s to 1940s comparison). And if you don’t, my [May 2021 Public Newsletter: Fiscal-Driven Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/) (and this chart below from that newsletter issue) summarizes it:

![Long-Term Debt Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-longterm-debt-cycle.png)

In 1929 and 2008, banks were at the epicenter of the crisis, and were subsequently recapitalized. In the 1940s and 2020s, banks were/are *not* at the epicenter of the crisis.

Now, that doesn’t mean banks wouldn’t be negatively-affected by a recession. Rather, it means they’re not going to be at the epicenter of it, and they’re in pretty good shape leading up to it.

**Bank Lending**

Banks are currently tightening lending standards, which tends to coincide with heightened recession risk:

![Bank Tightening](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-bank-tightening.png)

However, unlike the 2006-2008 tightening of lending standards, banks are tightening lending standards from a position of having strong balance sheets this time.

Meanwhile, year-over-year loan growth is strong, but is likely getting ready to roll over in rate of change terms. This chart shows the year-over-year % change in bank loans in blue, and shows the absolute amount of bank loans outstanding in red:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-bank-loans.png)

The brief spike in early 2020 can be discarded for historical analysis purposes; that was not actual bank “lending” but rather just consisted of various corporations tapping into their existing revolving credit facilities to get cash on their balance sheets to prepare for the COVID-19 lockdowns, and so it was technically bank loans, but not the same thing as an active decision to extend credit.

**The Fed “Doom Chart”**

The Fed has both assets and liabilities. The assets mainly consist of Treasures and mortgage-backed securities. The liabilities mainly consist of bank notes, bank reserves, and reverse repos.

Historically, their assets yielded more than their liabilities, and thus they had a positive net interest margin. They earned a profit, and used that money to pay for their operations, pay their owners a dividend, and then they remit the rest back to the US Treasury. However, in 2022 they raised interest rates so quickly from such a low base, that for the first time in modern Fed history they’re not “profitable” to the Treasury. Instead, they are collecting a big cumulative unrealized paper “loss” and sending that to the Treasury:

![Fed Remittances](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-fed-remittances.png)

The problem with this chart is that they switched how they account for it. When it was positive, they accounted for it as a weekly gain, but now that it’s negative, they count it as a cumulative negative amount. That’s why it looks so “doomy”.

[Jim Bianco](https://twitter.com/biancoresearch) produced and publicly shared a chart that makes the whole calculation cumulative since 2011. So the Fed spent a little over a decade sending a trillion dollars to the US Treasury (averaging a bit over $100 billion per year) and now they are starting to reverse course. Currently they are running maybe a $60-$70 billion annualized loss to the Treasury, and thus are remitting none to the Treasury.

![Bianco Remittance Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-bianco-remittance-chart.jpg)

In this case of a negative remittance, the Treasury does not pay the Fed. Instead, the Fed ironically books this loss as a “deferred asset”, and if they ever become profitable again, then rather than immediately beginning to pay the Treasury again, they first get to pay themselves back for all these recent losses, and only when that’s all paid back would they begin remitting money back to the Treasury.

So, the US Treasury just lost a $100B/year source of income for the foreseeable future. The question is, who is on the other side of that? If the Treasury/Fed are “losing” money, who is making it? The answer is found by looking at the Fed’s high-yielding liabilities, specifically the bank reserves and the reverse repos that pay interest.

As part of its way of setting and enforcing short-term interest rates, the Fed pays interest on reserve balances “IORB” to banks. Currently this rate is 4.40%, which is slightly higher than the Fed Funds rate. Reserve balances are the cash balances that the various banks hold at the Fed.

![Interest on Reserve Balances](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-iorb.png)

Currently, US banks collectively have about $3.1 trillion in reserves, so at 4.40% they collectively earn an annualized $136 billion or so in risk-free income at current rate levels.

Some analysts have phrased this by saying “the Fed is paying banks to not lend”. A more precise way to phrase it is that the Fed is incentivizing banks not to lend money for less than this rate, since banks can earn this rate risk-free by doing nothing and taking no risk. If they want to lend money for a mortgage, auto loan, credit card, personal loan, business loan, or margin loan, then they’ll do so at a higher rate, and with some risk of loan loss.

The theory behind this part of the Fed’s tightening approach is that consumers will be less likely to borrow at those higher rates, and so the money multiplier will stagnate and money creation will slow down, reducing consumer demand and price inflation.

Here’s the thing in the meantime- banks in aggregate are paying almost nothing on their deposits to customers. Banks are not deposit-starved at this time, and so rather than optimizing to get as many deposits as possible by offering high rates, they are optimizing to have the widest possible spread between interest rates on their asset and deposits (which are the primary liabilities for banks).

Truist Financial (TFC) for example paid a 0.31% annualized rate on average for its customer deposits in Q3 2022. Some instruments like CDs were higher, but the average deposit base was just 0.31%, and this type of rate was pretty normal for US banks. When the Q4 numbers are reported they will be somewhat higher, but still very low, and IORB went way higher between Q3 and Q4.

Truist’s net interest margin began doing very well in Q2 and Q3 of 2022 as the Fed raised rates (which only really began happening in Q2). This should be even better when they report Q4 numbers:

![TFC Net Interest Margin](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-truist-nim.png)

*Chart Source: Truist Q3 2022 Earnings Presentation*

So, banks are borrowing from customers at a fraction of 1%, holding some of it at the Fed for 4.40%, and collecting that spread as profit. That’s a pretty good deal for banks and a pretty bad deal for depositors. So, I want to own some banks.

**Bank Risks**

The biggest risk for banks is loan losses. If the US economy enters a major recession, as many people are worried about, then bank assets will be impaired as some significant percentage of their loans are defaulted on. While this risk is material, I think it’s already well-accounted for in bank stock prices. In my view, too many investors are fighting the last battle and assuming every recession will be a repeat of 2008, even if conditions (e.g. bank balance sheets) are completely opposite.

If the US enters a severe recession with massive loan losses, and the Fed sharply cuts rates, then owning cash and gold would be preferable to owning bank stocks. On the other hand, if the US enters a stagflationary malaise, with slow growth, some negative quarters, and recurring inflation after a brief disinflationary cycle, then bank stocks are likely great buys here. Bank stocks are a very uncrowded, out-of-consensus, inexpensive position at this time.

Milder bank risks, which are more likely to occur in my view, mainly revolve around slowdowns and mild recessions rather than financial crises. Bank lending standards are tightening, and the rate of change of loan growth may be rolling over. The combination of less loan growth and an uptick in loan losses could result in a couple years of earnings stagnation for banks, which could partially be offset by a decent spread between interest on customer deposits and interest on reserve balances as I discussed above.

**Truist Financial Valuation Analysis**

Truist Financial (TFC) is a result of the 2019 merger of equals between BB&T and SunTrust, although technically BB&T acquired SunTrust.

It’s the 7th largest bank in the United States as ranked by total assets. It’s far below the “Big Four” banks in size, and is similar to PNC (PNC) and US Bank (USB) in size.

Truist has both regional operations and national operations, and the regional operations are located primarily in the southeast United States. This region of the country has good population growth compared to other regions.

![Truist Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-truist-map.png)

*Chart Source: Truist Presentation, Goldman Sachs December 2022 US Financial Services Conference*

The merger activities of the two banks took longer than initially expected, in part because it occurred through the COVID years. Going forward, there will be a reduction in merger-related costs and a high likelihood of more cost synergies.

Analysts are pretty bullish on bank earnings in general over the next two years, and Truist is no exception:

![TFC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-tfc-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

If 1) consensus analysts are right about forward earnings and 2) the stock reverts to its 20-year average earnings multiple of 14.3x (from currently 10.2x), then the stock could jump from $46 to $77 in two years, while paying over $4 in dividends as well. That would be a 76% two-year gain:

![TFC Analysts Annotation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-tfc-fastgraph-analysts.png)

However, I think that is too rosy of an assumption. I do think the US will probably have at least a mild recession or near-recession in 2023.

If the US ends up having a severe and disinflationary recession, with significant loan losses, then TFC could face quite a hit. Here’s a hypothetical example:

![TFC Recession Scenario](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-tfc-fastgraph-recession.png)

In this case where it is modeled to go down to $30/share, the stock would have a 55% decline from all-time highs, which would be the third worst drawdown in its (Truist/BB&T’s) history:

![Truist Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-tfc-drawdown.png)

My base case is for something in the middle to happen. I think bank loan growth is likely to disappoint, loan losses are likely to tick up, and that this is already pretty much accounted for in the stock price. Meanwhile, net interest margins are likely to remain strong for a while. The risk/reward situation looks attractive for a 3-5 year position, although I would leave room to add in more tranches at lower prices if some of the more negative scenarios materialize.

I do have concerns about the broad economy in 2023, so I’m not making an “all in” call on bank stocks here. But I’m optimistic enough on bank stocks at these price levels to allocate to them in a diversified portfolio. I also plan to keep watching the sector, to see if it presents better bargains later this year.

Along with them, I want some short-term Treasuries, precious metals, energy pipelines, emerging markets, bitcoin, and a bunch of other assets.

The general problem with forecasting what will happen in 2023 is that the economy is rather actively managed at the moment. Massive stimulus was performed, and now the Fed is purposely trying to engineer an economic slowdown to suppress wage growth and contain inflation. Between fiscal policymakers and monetary policymakers, there is a lot of centralized active management happening here, and so a lot of what will happen in 2023 will depend on human decisions.

## Final Thoughts: Sentiment and Liquidity

In the long term, fundamentals matter. In the near term, it’s mostly about sentiment and liquidity.

And there are mixed signals everywhere at the moment. Domestic liquidity indicators in the US continue to suggest downward pressure on equities, while the negative level of sentiment is a contrarian bullish indicator.

This chart shows the 20-day rolling average CBOE equity put/call ratio vs the S&P 500:

![S&P 500 vs Put/Call Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-put-call-ratio.png)

And this chart is the same, except the S&P 500 is expressed in year-over-year percent change terms:

![S&P 500 YOY vs Put/Call Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-put-call-ratio-yoy.png)

As you can see, investors have been buying a lot of downside protection lately, which is usually a contrarian signal. While that doesn’t necessarily mark the exact bottom of bear markets, forward returns are usually better if you buy stocks during those fear spikes than when you buy during periods of euphoria. The big exception to that rule was mid-2008, when the put/call ratio was bearish and then the market indeed crashed anyway.

I wouldn’t read too much into the *magnitude* for that chart, but I think it’s useful to be aware of it *directionally*. This is because the CBOE recently introduced daily options, which likely skews the magnitude data compared to its history.

Meanwhile, the domestic US liquidity situation continues to worsen, suggesting lackluster performance for large cap equities in general:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-8-domestic-liquidity.png)

This liquidity calculation is derived from taking the Fed’s balance sheet, subtracting the Treasury General Account, and then subtracting reverse repos.

I described the mechanism for this back in November:

> As more bonds are issued and the Fed isn’t buying (or is even a net seller like they are now), then that means the private sector has to absorb more of the bond issuance, which crowds out potential equity investment. It’s also generally strong for the dollar, which slows down global growth.
>
> The Fed controls the size of its balance sheet, and the Treasury Department controls the size of the Treasury General Account.
>
> When the Fed expands its balance sheet by creating reserves and buying bonds, it is injecting liquidity into financial markets. When it reduces its balance sheet, by being a net seller of bonds and destroyer of reserves, it is removing liquidity from financial markets.
>
> When the Treasury expands the TGA, by raising debt and bringing in taxes faster than it spends money, it is sucking liquidity out of the system into a void. When it reduces the TGA, it is spending more money into the economy than it is currently withdrawing, or in other words putting liquidity from that void back into the economy. So, the smaller the TGA, the smaller the void of dead capital. There are some months where the Treasury can be injecting liquidity while the Fed removes it, or vice versa.
>
> -November 27th, 2022 premium report

The Fed plans to drain its balance sheet all year, providing a downward bias to liquidity unless or until they find a reason to pivot or pause. That doesn’t mean stocks have to go down, but all else equal it’s a net negative, mainly for the S&P 500 index as a whole but potentially less-so for individual value stocks.

This year looks very murky, with ongoing liquidity drainage and recession risk. But, traders may be too bearish on the wrong areas, and setting up some assets for nice 3-5 year returns, for investors that can navigate through the next 6-12 months.
