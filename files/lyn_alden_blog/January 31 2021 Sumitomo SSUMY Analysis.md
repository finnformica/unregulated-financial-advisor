## ETF Highlight: Japanese Value

This week’s stock analysis, later in this report, is for a Japanese value/commodity/industrial company.

While it looks good on its own, it’s also part of a broader theme of how increasingly attractive the Japanese industrial/value equity area is at the moment, with a long-term view through the 2020s.

The Japanese equity market has been dead money for 30 years, but under the surface, a lot has improved.

For starters, markets aren’t linear. This three-decade Japanese bear market is no longer ongoing. Instead, it has become the quietest bull market in the world since late 2012.

The Nikkei 225 bottomed during the 2009-2012 period, and since then has been in a near-decade-long bull market, as one of the top-performing equity markets for that period after the United States.

![Nikkei 225](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-nikkei.png)

*Chart Source: [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/)*

Since that 2009-2011 period, Japan’s Nikkei has outperformed the indices of emerging markets, have outperformed Europe’s Stoxx 600, and have outperformed most other markets except for the United States.

Indeed, the Bank of Japan’s balance sheet going vertical since 2012 seems to have played a sizable role:

![BOJ Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-boj-balance-sheet.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

The Bank of Japan owns a large chunk of Japanese government bonds, but they buy equities as well.

And yet, Japanese equities as a whole remain very reasonably priced compared to the rest of the world when you compare their stocks on a sector-by-sector basis. Plus, the yen has been one of the more stable and strong currencies despite the Bank of Japan’s intervention, which isn’t surprising considering that Japan continues to run a structural current account surplus and has the largest net international investment position in the world (i.e. they own more foreign assets than foreigners own of their assets), and is growing its broad money supply at a slower pace than the United States and Europe.

And, although Japan has a government debt/GDP ratio that is higher than the United States, their government debt/money-supply ratio is actually a bit lower.

This is occurring alongside the fact that Japanese corporate governance has improved over time. A series of reforms and gradually changing business culture have shifted more towards focusing on higher margins, better returns on capital, more independent board directors, debt deleveraging, non-core business unit divestitures to streamline operations, etc.

As an example of why the Japanese equity market and economy has been in a three-decade bear market, besides their structural demographics decline and bubble-like valuations that occurred at the peak (the two main culprits), look at the following period of deleveraging. Japan’s trading companies, back a couple decades, had insane leverage with 5x or 10x debt to equity ratios, which they’ve grinded down for years to more normal levels:

![Trading Company Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-trading-company-debt-equity.png)

That was part of the massive Japanese equity and real estate bubble from the 1980s and 1990s, which deflated slowly and took decades to deleverage. Debt in the country gradually shifted off of private balance sheets and onto public balance sheets, indirectly.

Now, with their debt/equity ratios back down to earth, the Japanese trading company balance sheets are a lot healthier. Itochu’s balance sheet is pretty good, while the others range from decent to solid.

It’s important to realize that Japanese companies don’t just serve Japan. Many of them have customers all over the world. So, even though Japan has stagnant GDP, it doesn’t mean that a given individual company has to perform poorly, especially if you take into account extreme cheapness (profitable companies trading below tangible book value with single-digit price/earnings ratios) along with a structural shift leaning towards some inflation, with the 2020s decade looking to be more commodity-and-industrial-driven than the past decade.

For those wanting fund exposure rather than individual stock exposure, there are some simple ETF and CEF picks.

The Vanguard FTSE Pacific ETF (VPL) has just under 60% exposure to Japan, with Australia and South Korea making up another 30%, and Hong Kong, Singapore, and New Zealand bringing up the last 10%.

The iShares MSCI Japan ETF (EWJ) provides overall passive exposure to large and mid-sized companies. The average P/E is 19.11 and the average P/B ratio is 1.53.

The iShares MSCI Japan Value ETF (EWJV) has a passive value tilt, and actually has a lower expense ratio than EWJ. The average P/E is 12.53 and the average P/B is 0.96.

Nomura’s Japan Smaller Cap CEF (JOF) is a closed-end fund that focuses on smaller companies and is actively managed. It currently trades at a 13% discount to NAV, and although small caps have struggled recently, the fund has outperformed EWJ over a multi-decade period.

## Sumitomo Corporation Deep Dive Analysis

Back in November 2020, I analyzed Itochu Corporation (ITOCY) as part of a broader look into the five big Japanese trading companies.

The Japanese trading companies are commodity/industrial conglomerates that have interests in an extremely broad and vertically integrated array of businesses, and is a structure that is rather unique to Japan. As a general theme, the trading companies own interests in natural resource projects around the world, provide logistics and supply chain services for customers, perform infrastructure projects domestically and in many other countries, are involved in industrial manufacturing, and then typically have some real estate and retail businesses such as supermarkets, convenience stores, or drug stores.

In other words, they are involved from the stage of getting commodities out of the ground, transported to where they need to be, manufactured into what they need to be, and organized into their finished state in their finished location. Some of it is done in-house, and some of it is done through businesses that they have a partial stake in. They also make loans to various businesses.

They made headlines last year because Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B) disclosed a 5% stake in each of the main five trading companies, worth over $6 billion at the time. The press release stated that Berkshire intends to hold for the long-term and showed that Buffett was personally involved in the decision.

This was a rather notable deal for Berkshire, which normally doesn’t do too many big international investments, although considering that the stocks were very cheap while simultaneously improving their governance, it actually made a lot of sense for the billionaire value investor to do at this point.

In November, [I highlighted Itochu](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-11-8/), the least-leveraged and historically best-performing one of the five. This, in my view, was the lowest-risk of them. I added it to the No Limits portfolio and the Top 12 Stock List. If you haven’t read it, that one is worth a read along with this report, since it’s about the same industry.

Indeed, all five trading companies have done very well since November, outperforming the S&P 500, and Itochu was the second-best performing one:

![Trading Company Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-trading-company-performance.png)

However, I’m bullish on all five with a 5+ year view.

Compared to Itochu, Sumitomo is a bit riskier and more leveraged, but also cheaper and with potentially more upward potential in a commodity bull market.

The reason I’m bullish on the trading companies on a risk-adjusted basis is that besides being cheap and increasingly well-managed, they tie into some big themes over the next 5+ years including global infrastructure stimulus, a dollar bear cycle, and a commodity bull cycle, without taking too much risk or being “all in” on those themes if they take longer to play out as expected.

They pay dividend yields that range from about 3% to 5%, with Sumitomo being at 5%.

**Sumitomo Corporation Overview**

Sumitomo Corporation (OTC: SSUMY, TYO:8053) is a large Japanese trading company, with the following six business segments:

* **Metal Products:** steel, aluminum, titanium, and other metal products
* **Transportation and Construction Systems:** shipbuilding, vessel leasing
* **Infrastructure:** water systems, rail systems, logistics
* **Media and Digital:** telecommunications and IT
* **Living Related and Real Estate:** supermarkets, drug stores, real estate, agricultural products
* **Mineral, Energy, Chemical, Electronics:** natural resources, electronics manufacturing

![Sumitomo Businesses](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-sumitomo-business.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Sumitomo](https://www.sumitomocorp.com/en/jp/about)*

The stock trades on the Tokyo Stock Exchange under the ticker 8053, or can be bought over-the-counter as a dollar-based ADR with the ticker SSUMY.

Over-the-counter stocks are not available on certain platforms like M1 Finance, but are available at places like Fidelity, Charles Schwab, Interactive Brokers, and other full brokerage accounts.

For investors that have access to the OTC market and don’t intend to trade (just buy and hold, with four-figure or five-figure position sizes), the lower liquidity of the OTC market isn’t necessarily a problem. However, for investors that need higher liquidity, buying directly on the Tokyo Stock Exchange is preferable.

The scope of Sumimoto’s business is extremely broad, with employees, clients, locations, and business interests spread around the globe:

![Sumitomo Overview](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-sumitomo-overview.jpg)

*Chart Source: Sumitomo*

As of their most recent quarterly report, they have 500 billion yen ($5 billion USD) worth of investments in commodity production, including 100 billion yen in copper, 90 billion in nickel (after a big write-down), 80 billion in iron, 80 billion in silver, zinc, and lead combined, 70 billion in oil and gas, and 70 billion in coal. Recently, they announced plans to stop new investment into fossil fuels, while maintaining their existing investments, and shift more towards the metals over time.

This year, fiscal 2020 that they’re currently still in, has been a year for asset write-downs. Their biggest one was for a nickel project in Madagascar that they’ve been involved with since 2005. Covid-19 shutdowns and a mixed outlook for nickel prices led them to reduce their valuation of the project.

Because the issues this year are mostly non-cash items (i.e. cash generation remains strong), the company expects to maintain its current dividend of 70 yen per year.

**Valuation Analysis**

Four out of five of the Japanese trading companies trade below book value:

![Trading Company Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-trading-company-book-value.png)

Itochu, with the least leverage, trades for a higher premium, but even that one is cheap by most metrics.

In prior decades, they used to trade above book value like most companies, but in this period of commodity and industrial stagnation, they’ve traded very cheaply.

Sumitomo is currently trading for less than 6x the annual earnings it had from fiscal years 2018 and 2019. It’s trading at less than 11x their partly recession-impacted earnings from fiscal year 2020. The fiscal year 2021 (which is already partly over; their fiscal years don’t align with calendar years) is expected to be zero due to some non-cash impairments, with a big piece coming from their valuation write-down of a nickel mine. For fiscal years 2022 and 2023 (which overlap with calendar years 2021 and 2022), the stock is currently trading at about 10x or 11x analyst-estimated earnings from those future years.

To some extent, this is fair, especially in a decade-long commodity/industrial stagnation. The trading companies are conglomerates and don’t exactly have the best return on invested capital metrics. However, the market adjusts for that by trading the stocks below book value, so that the earnings yield and the dividend yield are significant enough to compensate for slower growth.

The company targets a roughly 30% dividend payout ratio, and using some remaining capital to continue to strengthen the balance sheet. At 70 yen per share in dividends and about 1400 yen for the share price on the Tokyo Stock Exchange, that comes out to a dividend yield of 5% which the company expects to maintain this year, since their cash flows are able to cover it despite headwinds.

If we look back a few decades, here’s the CRB commodity index, which measures the price levels of a basket of commodities:

![CRB Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-crb.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Looking back in history, there was an absolutely massive commodity boom from the early 1970s to the early 1980s, in part due to a reset of the global monetary system and the associated currency devaluation that followed. Then, from the early 1980s to the late 1990s, there was a long commodity bear market from those high levels, where the index ranged sideways and slightly down.

Throughout the 2000s, there was another big commodity boom, largely due to the rise of China and other emerging markets. The global financial crisis, a stronger dollar, and a slowing emerging market growth trend all contributed to this.

We’ve been in a 13-year commodity bear market since then, which may have bottomed in early 2020 during the initial pandemic shutdowns. This follows capex boom/bust cycles; high prices lead developers to spend money on exploration and development, whereas low prices discourage new exploration and development until prices rise enough to make it economical.

The majority of commodities are now in deficit, meaning that demand is outpacing new supply coming online, and drawing down existing inventories and locations. Worldwide, after such a rough decade, commodity producers in aggregate are focusing on free cash flow rather than on growth through new development. Combined with very aggressive fiscal and monetary policy by governments, likely extending well into the 2020s, this should be beneficial for commodities and their producers.

If we look at the yen-denominated earnings per share and dividends per share for Sumitomo, we see a similar shape as the CRB commodity index:

![Sumitomo Earnings and Dividends](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-sumitomo-earnings.png)

*Data Source: Company Filings & Yahoo Finance*

The 2000-2008 commodity bull market was great for Sumitomo earnings. Their earnings went up 3x-5x during that period, depending on what time frame you start with.

Then, as commodities spent the next 12 years crashing, Sumitomo’s earnings went sideways in a choppy way (and had a rough 2015 in particular as commodity prices had a sharp fall).

However, the company used that rough decade well. Despite being in a structural bear market for a large chunk of their business operations, they improved corporate governance and shifted more towards capital returns, notably increasing the dividend over that time. And importantly, they continued their multi-decade deleveraging process:

![Sumitomo Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-31-sumitomo-debt.png)

*Data Source: Company Filings & Yahoo Finance*

Sumitomo is in much better financial shape now, and at lower valuations, compared to what it was at in the early 2000s when the previous commodity bull cycle occurred.

The bull case for Sumitomo and other Japanese trading companies is that the 2020s decade will have another commodity bull cycle, because a lack of investment (especially in ex-energy commodities), a weaker dollar, and global infrastructure spending should drive up prices. Sumitomo has a decent chance to end this decade-long sideways consolidation and enjoy another leg higher in terms of earnings and valuations, while also paying sizable dividends along the way.

The bear case is that commodities and the industrial sector will continue this lukewarm trend for several more years, and there will be no big reflation or commodities boom. In all but the most extreme environments, the company should be able to maintain or grow its dividend over time, and thus still offer 5% or more annualized returns from dividend income.

Normally, the conglomerate business model is not very attractive, but for the commodity industry it’s actually a smart move, because commodities are so boom/bust in nature, so having some stable balancing factors helps a company retain cash flow, dividends, capital, and good credit ratings through multiple cycles.

In other words, when Sumitomo’s commodities or manufacturing businesses are hurting, their grocery stores and drug stores and television operations are likely doing okay, and this gives the company a ballast to avoid the major ups and downs that are associated with pure-play commodity producers. Pure commodity stocks are generally better to trade for couple quarters or a couple years than to hold, whereas these diversified companies are suitable for holding longer-term while maintaining that bullish exposure to commodities.

**Company Risks**

Although it’s a rather diversified business, Sumitomo has a variety of risks.

Overall, a diverse assortment of trading companies in a portfolio comes with a low probability of major permanent loss, since it reduces single-company risk, but some of the risks having a decent probability of causing a period of industry-wide stagnation and underperformance compared to other investments.

*Commodity Prices*

Sumitomo is reliant on reasonable commodity prices. Lower-than-expected commodity prices would hurt profitability, potentially result in more asset valuation impairments, and if severe enough and prolonged enough, could force the company to cut its dividend to retain capital.

*Global Infrascture Spending*

Sumitomo has business interests throughout the world. Their metal products, their manufacturing capabilities, and their infrastructure projects are generally tied to global growth. Continued global economic stagnation due to the virus and shutdowns in response to the virus, would significantly weigh on the company.

*Financial Risk*

Although the company has deleveraged significantly, they do have a very large base of assets, and thus a large absolute debt load, in relation to their income. Its financial profile is therefore similar to a utility company or infrastructure business; a large asset base requires significant debt, and steady cashflows allow the company to comfortably sustain that debt.

In the prior year, taking into account the company’s total annual interest expense and the amount of debt they had, their cost of debt was about 1.5%.

An inflationary event would likely be very beneficial for the company, as their commodity and industrial segments should do reasonably well, while their debt liabilities lose purchasing power and thus get partially inflated away. Over time, the company may have to refinance at a higher interest rate, but their asset base and income should be large enough to accommodate that shift.

All five of the large Japanese trading companies have investment-grade credit ratings from S&P. Itochu, Mitsubishi, and Mitsui have “A” credit ratings. Sumitomo has “BBB+” and Marubeni has “BBB”.

This makes Sumitomo a bit riskier than some of the stronger ones, in exchange for perhaps more upside potential in a commodity bull scenario. However, all of them have deleveraged over time, and have significant liquidity and financial resources as evidenced by their ability to sustain their dividends in this environment.

## Summary: Quality Cyclical Stocks

While I remain bullish on commodities and cyclicals in general, I’m not leaning into the most aggressive picks.

This is a good environment for companies with low production costs, strong balance sheets, and smart management, because those are the companies that can thrive in a full-cycle environment.

The Japanese trading companies, for example, give a nice combination of cheapness, commodity diversification, industrial operations, and stable cash-cow businesses that serve as ballasts for their more cyclical operations. Similarly, I like companies such as Lukoil, Chevron, and Total for oil plays.

Given the sharp underperformance of financials and energy stocks this year (along with positive tailwinds like a steepening yield curve and a big global reduction in energy capex), I certainly like having some exposure to them.

People often think in terms of “growth vs value”. However, value is a very diverse set of sectors. It typically includes cyclical sectors like energy and financials, and more defensive sectors like healthcare, consumer staples, and utilities.

However, the risks can be managed with reasonable position sizing, and by emphasizing quality within the cyclical sectors rather than taking on the most speculative boom/bust stocks. In addition, I like maintaining some cash-equivalents in portfolios in this environment to take advantage of any liquidity shocks that may occur.
