## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Overall liquidity within the United States continues to be flat overall, but over the past month has enjoyed an upward move:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-domestic-liquidity-annotated.png)

At the end of October, the Treasury released its details on how it would fund the U.S. government for Q4, and they surprised market participants with more short-duration T-bill issuance than expected, and more than usual compared to the typical ratio.

Issuing more T-bills allows the Treasury to draw more money out of the reverse repo facility, which pulls liquidity *back into the financial system*. And indeed, that is what has been happening, with the reverse repo facility down to $865 billion.

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-reverse-repo.png)

The Treasury market and/or banking system may run into liquidity issues when the reverse repo is drawn all the way down, but until then, it’s generally a medium-to-positive liquidity situation. Fed balance sheet reduction is negative for liquidity, but it is being offset by the reverse repo drawdown that is positive for liquidity.

If we look more broadly, global liquidity is holding up too. For this metric, I use global M2 (as measured from eight large countries with the dataset I’m using) in dollar-denominated terms. It is therefore affected by both money/credit creation and by the strength of the dollar relative to other major currencies.

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-global-m2-2.png)

The big drop that global liquidity had in autumn of 2022 was from two related things. First, the Fed was tightening its monetary policy in terms of both interest rates and balance sheet reduction, and liquidity was not yet coming out of the reverse repo facility to offset it. Secondly, largely because of this (as well as due to war in Europe), the dollar was very strong relative to other currencies. Here is the dollar chart, with that big spike peaking out in autumn 2022:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-dxy.png)

At that time in autumn 2022, the U.K. sovereign bond market broke (i.e. went illiquid) and required temporary intervention by the Bank of England. the U.S. sovereign bond market was also getting very illiquid, but didn’t get as bad as the U.K. market did. The U.S. Treasury then began drawing down its Treasury general account, which was pro-liquidity, and then after that they began shifting more toward T-bill issuance, which again was pro-liquidity. Asset prices rebounded from there, and economic indicators began to stabilize in general rather than continue down.

Another area that I’ve been analyzing lately is the quality of monetary aggregate data. The M2 indicator (broad money) includes currency in circulation, most types of bank deposits (excluding some large time deposits) and includes retail money market funds (but not certain other types of money market funds). If I instead just sum up three mostly-separate types of money (currency in circulation, bank deposits, and money market funds), then the trend has been different:

![Near Money Metric](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-near-money-metric.png)

Which metric is better? I’m not sure, and I suspect probably something akin to the middle ground between them is ideal. For example, I think business money market funds should count when analyzing money supply. But on the other hand, I believe there is a slight bit of double-counting in the red line, specifically in the form of certain certificates of deposit that might be counted as both within money market funds and as part of bank deposits. From analyzing large money market funds, for the most part that double-counting is rather small and it’s a pretty clean metric.

The reason I think this is relevant is that ultra-low volatile “near-money” assets are increasingly used like money, at least when it comes to savings. Money markets, ultra-short-duration T-bills, ultra-short-duration commercial paper, and reverse repos are all “money like” or “near money” types of assets.

If we zoom out and look at a bigger but laggier data source, we can just look at all U.S. household net worth, which bounced back to new all-time highs in Q2 2023:

![Household Net Worth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-household-net-worth.png)

The United States is running enormous fiscal deficits, and the Treasury has been doing very pro-liquidity actions since autumn 2022, offsetting some of the Fed’s anti-liquidity things. As a result, early liquidity metrics turned up, and asset prices followed. This seems set to continue for the next few months at least.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy was decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, and has been in stall speed for a while. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as most of them are now except for construction spending), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms. Construction spending has been getting a big fiscal boost from the Inflation Reduction Act.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index is showing signs of being recessionary… but potentially stabilizing and turning back up:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-lei.png)

The year-over-year figure looks similar:

![Leading Economic Index Year over Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-lei-yoy.png)

Basically, the leading economic index is already at levels that would normally be a recession. This shows the unusual nature of the economy, with huge fiscal spending that is stimulatory for some sectors, and the aggressive moves in interest rates that have been suppressive for some sectors that have more interest rate exposure.

TSA flight numbers, for example, continue to be very strong. People have been flying as much or more than in 2019 lately. Overall, it’s a tale of two economies as I have been writing about. For any particular industry, it depends on whether they or their customers are on the receiving side of the fiscal deficits, or if they’re on the receiving side of the monetary tightening.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin briefly touched new cycle highs above $38k and then corrected back down to sub-$38k.

From paying attention to the prior bull market, this looks like large entrants accumulating positions over time. Big buyers use automated mini-purchases over time and when they start to move the price too quickly they pull back. They’re looking at likely upcoming ETF catalysts, a supply-halving in April 2024, and decent global liquidity. Many institutional capital allocators are looking at the chart and thinking, “wait, is this thing actually not dead? What if it does a huge price run for a fifth time?”

![BTC Price Annotations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-price-annotations.png)

As the chart above shows with annotations, the prior Bitcoin cycle had some unusual effects. In late 2018 and early 2019, Bitcoin price was influenced by the PlusToken Ponzi scheme. This was an altcoin Ponzi scheme centered around bitcoin within China and other parts of Asia. It likely inflated the price temporarily, causing a mini-bubble, and then likely affected the price negatively as it unwound. And then in early 2020 during the lockdowns the price crashed (which is when I first recommended a bitcoin allocation at $6,900/coin).

The other bull markets tended to be more continuously up, particularly the prior 2016/2017 bull run. I think too many traders try to “get too clever by half” with this asset and wait for super-low entry points, never get them, and then have to chase.

For that reason, my approach has generally been to buy a stake in bitcoin and then [dollar-cost average into it](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) from there. If you research it a ton and want more, you can buy more. Otherwise, just leave it as a small position. I’ve been structurally bullish on Bitcoin since around $6,900/coin and continue to be. I find that people who try to trade it too much tend to overthink it. People should consider gradually trimming on euphoria and gradually adding on despair, but as soon as people get specific notions in their head for a specific price target that bitcoin will reach for them to sell against or buy against, is where they tend to lose money.

**Industry News: Binance Plea Deal**

Binance is by far the largest cryptocurrency exchange in the world, and is used globally by people in dozens of countries. The U.S. Justice Department charged them with various monetary crimes, mainly in terms of interacting with sanctioned entities such as Iran. The CEO Changpeng Zhao plead guilty and stepped down as CEO. Binance will be paying over $4 billion in fines as well. The DOJ would have reviewed their finances pretty closely in the run-up to this plea, and going forward the DOJ will be maintaining a closer look at their finances.

Binance has its own coin, which is the fourth-largest cryptocurrency after Bitcoin, Ethereum, and Tether. Perhaps surprisingly, the coin is remaining above the “support floor” price of around $200 for the time being:

![BNB Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-bnb.png)

**Industry News: Argentinian Elections**

Javier Milei won the presidential election in Argentina. As far as I know, he’s the first self-identified anarcho-capitalist to be elected as a head of state in any country. And if there is some other tiny example out there, this is certainly by far the biggest, seeing as how it’s a G20 nation.

Part of his economic view is that the state shouldn’t issue money, and that instead the private sector should determine what money it uses naturally, and he has vowed close the central bank. In practice at the current time this generally would mean dollarization, and if that happens, it would be the biggest country to dollarize in terms of economic size. He has also said positive things toward bitcoin. He will be part of a ruling coalition so it’s not 100% clear what he will or won’t be able to change, especially from my perspective as someone who is not well-versed in Argentinian politics. But similar to how El Salvador’s decision to make bitcoin legal tender had some relevant impact on the market, this (much) larger country could have a similar impact as well if they move forward with even a moderate set of changes to their monetary policies and allowances.

Obviously there are a lot of other aspects regarding Milei’s upcoming presidency as well, but within this section the scope that I’m discussing is on how it may impact bitcoin or stablecoin/cryptocurrency markets rather than Argentina specifically.

## Energy Sector Checkup

Energy stocks have been hit hard in recent weeks but my general baseline view is that I continue to find them to be constructive within the context of a diversified portfolio.

For years, bonds were a key ballast for equities in a portfolio. If equities went down, bonds likely went up. If equities went up, bonds struggled but that was fine because equities were going up.

In inflationary recent times, it is a bit different. And arguably, broad U.S. stocks to energy stocks have had a better inverse correlation. In other words, hedging your various non-energy equity positions has been useful by having a long-energy position.

The divergence was sharpest in the first half of 2022, but has cooled down since then. Still, if oil is steadily ripping up, the broad stock market tends to not do great. They begin pricing in higher interest rates and potentially margin pressure. On the other hand, if oil cools off, the broad stock market tends to relax its forward expectations of interest rates.

![SPY vs XLE](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-spy-vs-xle.png)

A severe recession would likely bring both energy stocks and the broad stock market down together.

Almost all of the world’s marginal growth in energy production over the past decade came from U.S. shale oil. These are smaller oil wells with faster time to market, but that also deplete faster.

The following chart is an update on how many oil rigs there are active in the United States. The absolute magnitude is not very informative because the technology changes over time, but the intermediate-term direction matters. Big declines in rig counts tends to precede tops in overall production.

![US Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-us-production.png)

One of that factors that is currently elevating production even as drilling activity stalls, is that companies are tapping into drilled-but-uncompleted wells. These are wells that have been drilled, but then are stopped just shy of completion, so that they can be completed when the time is right. We can think of them as a type of inventory- sources of oil that can be brought online with little money, because the money has already been partially spent on them. The total number of these is now back down to levels not seen since 2014:

![DUCs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-ducs.png)

The U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve was partially drained in order to bring down oil prices in 2022, and they’ve not yet refilled it.

![Strategic Petroleum Reserve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-spr.png)

So, various U.S. resources have tapped into a lot of inventory and reserves. In addition, the war between Israel and Hamas has fortunately not broadened to directly include Iran or other regional entities, and so there has been little supply disruption thus far. Much of the “war premium” seems to have been removed from oil now, which de-risks it from a long perspective.

Oil saw a huge bull run during the 2000s decade, largely due to a weaker dollar index and the rise of the BRICS nations and their associated increase in oil consumption. It crashed during the global financial crisis, but rebounded, and then crashed again during the 2010s decade as shale-based U.S. oil production rapidly ramped up, with early 2020 during pandemic lockdowns being the low point. Since then, I think we’ve returned to a more normal baseline of $70-$90 oil, with a potential to break out of that the next time the global economy has a big upswing in terms of activity and liquidity.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-wti-crude.png)

Currently, many energy stocks trade at under 12x earnings at current oil prices. They generally have strong balance sheets, and are basically positive-carry hedges for a diversified portfolio in my opinion. I continue to view them favorably with a 3-5 year view. The past decade was rough for many of them due to the fast rise of shale oil, but much of that low-hanging fruit has been picked, and now the global oil market is back into equilibrium with U.S. shale oil included.

![XOM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-xom-fastgraph.png)

I also continue to view select pipeline companies, such as Enterprise Products Partners (EPD), as being strong income investments going forward. Their risk/reward compares favorably with 10-year Treasury notes, in the sense that you can get a 7.5% yield from a distribution that grows slightly each year (providing perhaps 10% total returns in a constant-valuation scenario) vs 4.5% yield from Treasury notes with less tail risk. That’s a decent equity risk premium in my opinion.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-epd-fastgraph.png)

## Final Thoughts

I still think gold is worth watching pretty closely at these levels. It has been banging its head against the approximately $2,000 level, and is currently near the high end of its recent three-year price range.

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-gold.png)

I expect the yellow metal to run pretty significantly if it firmly breaks above this recent zone. Historically, gold generally does well after the Federal Reserve stops raising rates, so it’s worth monitoring.

I continue to use gold as a small portfolio diversifier position. This paid off very well in the 2018-2020 period, has stalled in the 2020-2023 period, and I suspect the 2024-2026 period will be better again for that portfolio slice. We’ll see.
