## Macro View

This is one of the most bullish charts out there in my view. South African cases almost completely disconnected from deaths during this Omicron wave, even when we account for the typical lag between cases and deaths:

![South Africa Omicron](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-south-africa-omicron.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

The same thing appears to be happening so far in the United Kingdom, which was also one of the early Omicron-infected countries. They already had a big disconnect between cases and deaths for the entirety of 2021 due to various treatments, vaccines, and natural immunity, and that country seems to be holding up well to the Omicron spike as well:

![UK Omicron](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-uk-omicron.png)

*Chart Source: Worldometer*

South Africa has a young population on average, and only 27% of people have received two vaccine doses. The UK is an older population on average, with a 70% two-dose vaccination rate and a 45% boosted-vaccination rate. So, this is a rather diverse sample of data in terms of age and vaccine status.

The numbers so far continue to suggest that the Omicron variant itself is less deadly than prior variants. Hopefully this remains the case.

**Inflation**

The US is currently growing its broad money supply at a 12.7% annualized rate, which is below the mid-2020 spike of 27% year-over-year, but above the long-term average. The blue line is the absolute level and the red line is the year-over-year percent change:

![M2 Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-m2.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Consumer price inflation continues to run hot, with a lag after money supply growth. However, the headline year-over-year consumer price index in the United States appears as though it may reach a local peak in Q1 of 2022, due to monthly base effects. Month-over-month consumer price index increases would have to *accelerate* from current levels in order for that not to be the case.

This next chart shows the 5-year cumulative change in money supply and consumer price index. Even when prices potentially top out in rate-of-change terms, the 5-year cumulative growth rate is still trending up and will probably continue that way into at least 2023:

![M2 vs CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-m2-cpi.png)

*Data Source: [Macrohistory.net](https://www.macrohistory.net/), plus author’s calculations*

Housing should be the main positive contributor to year-over-year CPI for another couple quarters, since it’s operating on a lag compared to actual home prices. Other areas are up or down, due to complex and specific supply chain limitations.

A big source of price inflation has come from new and used vehicles, due to semiconductor shortages that have forced automakers to reduce production, resulting in vehicle scarcity. Auto dealers have record low inventories (measured in units on the chart below), while auto manufacturers have record high inventories (measured in inflation-adjusted dollar value on the chart below).

![Auto Inventories](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-auto-inventories.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Usually those two numbers are rather correlated, but a combination of fiscal stimulus and supply reductions have caused them to go in different directions this time. Auto manufacturers have record high inventory because they have a lot of mostly-finished cars sitting there that can’t be completed until they get key semiconductors. Auto dealers have record low inventories because they are held up at the manufacturer, awaiting those semiconductors. Along with a rapid increase in the broad money supply, the result is auto price inflation, which is a lot different than a normal business cycle.

When this resolves, it should cause a disinflationary or probably outright deflationary period for autos. That will likely weigh on headline CPI rather noticeably when it occurs. But we’re not there yet.

Meanwhile, Europe and some other places are in an outright energy crisis, as they have natural gas shortages that affect heating, electricity generation, fertilizer production, and other things. Some manufacturing facilities have had to shut down operations due to the spike in electricity prices, which can contribute to ongoing supply chain issues in the things those facilities produce.

![European Natural Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-euro-natural-gas.jpeg)

The European natural gas price spike became crazy enough, with European natural gas prices being 14x as high as in the United States, that US LNG export facilities began redirecting shipments to Europe. [As reported by Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-12-22/a-flotilla-of-u-s-lng-cargoes-is-headed-to-fuel-starved-europe):

> U.S. LNG export terminals are operating at or above capacity after reaching record flows on Sunday. Asia is typically the top destination for U.S. LNG cargoes, but that has changed this winter with the significant premium for gas in Europe.

I would generally fade any price spikes like this, which was the same view I had when lumber prices had a big spike. Those extremes eventually work themselves out, although the European natural gas situation is far more serious than the lumber situation was. Whether it hits a higher high again or not will depend in significant part on how cold Europe’s winter is over the next couple months.

**Bank Lending**

US bank lending is turning up:

![Bank Credit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-bank-credit.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The positive spike in 2020 was from companies drawing on their existing revolving credit facilities (aka “revolvers”) with banks to get as much cash as possible directly on their balance sheets. When companies faced massive uncertainty as the pandemic lockdowns occurred, their understandable reaction was to stock up on cash so that they didn’t run into liquidity issues.

So, that little spike wasn’t “real” lending for the most part; it was an emergency cash grab from existing revolvers. During late 2020 and into 2021 as the economy normalized to some extent, that revolver spike unwound, and so that quick decline in loans wasn’t “real” either. We seem to be past that stage into more normal loan growth again.

Meanwhile, banks did tighten up normal lending standards during the recession, as they always do. In recent quarters, they relaxed their lending standards, which is historically a sign of being early in an economic expansion cycle:

![Bank Lending Standards](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-bank-standards.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

All else being equal, bank lending is a reflationary sign. Economic growth and credit growth appear to be entering a new expansionary period in the US, as fiscal and monetary stimulus pull back.

My base case is for the credit growth to be relatively sluggish overall, but to resume an upward trend. Ongoing millennial home-buying is expansionary for credit but other areas of demographics are generally more neutral or contractive for credit.

**China Credit Cycle**

Continued evidence from some of the firms who are doing good analysis on the Chinese credit impulse is showing that the Chinese credit cycle is probably turning back up, or at least no longer falling:

![China Credit Impulse](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-china-credit-impulse.png)

China had a mild debt reduction as a percentage of GDP over the past twelve months as part of their recent crackdowns. As they go into 2022 (which by Q4 2022 will include their politically sensitive 20th National Congress where President Xi seems to be aiming for an unprecedented third term), they seem to be leaning more stimulatory again.

I expect China’s real estate sector to continue to be sluggish for years as it deleverages from so much speculation, but the rest of their economy should probably start to stabilize and heat back up by late in the year. The combination of China’s aging demographics and deflating housing bubble are likely to make their future economic expansions more muted in magnitude compared to the past couple decades.

Currently, Americans have more wealth in the stock market than real estate, and our stock market is historically expensive compared to our real estate market (even though our real estate market is also rather frothy). Meanwhile, Chinese citizens have more wealth in their real estate market than in stocks, and their stock market is relatively cheap while their real estate market looks like a classic bubble.

Ironically, I think the US real estate market can outperform the US stock market over the next decade, while the Chinese stock market can outperform the Chinese real estate market over the next decade. In other words, the areas where people are already overweight will probably be lackluster, while the areas where people are underweight will probably do better in comparison.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin has been in a correction since November 8th. During that time, it made constant lower-highs and lower-lows in terms of daily closes with no exceptions.

However, in recent days, it made a higher high, and overall has a decent bullish setup in place:

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-btcusd-chart.png)

Of course, that is by no means a perfect signal. In June 2021 for example, bitcoin made a higher high and looked like it may have bottomed after a sharp sell-off, but then the price rolled over and grinded lower for another month until it really bottomed. But this higher high is a signal that I notice, nonetheless.

A lot of the selling in this downcycle was from Asia, while Americans and Europeans have been buying the dip. Due to China’s ongoing crackdown on mainland Chinese people owning bitcoins and other cryptocurrencies, a number of exchanges are closing their business to Chinese customers by December 31st.

![Asia Bitcoin Selling](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-asia-btc-selling.png)

The Huobi exchange, founded in China, had over 400,000 bitcoins on it early in 2021. In September 2021 they announced they would stop serving mainland Chinese customers by the end of the year. There has been massive drawdowns in coins on that exchange throughout the year, and now they have under 40,000 bitcoins on the platform.

![Huobi Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-huobi-drawdown.png)

On the other side of this, Americans and Europeans have been accumulating bitcoins.

China hasn’t banned the ownership of bitcoin or cryptocurrencies, like they are not going door-to-door and such, but by banning large-scale mining and by banning exchange access, they have sharply reduced the involvement that their country has with the global Bitcoin network. China has capital controls, as well as extensive financial surveillance and social credit scoring, and cryptocurrencies have been a leak for those capital controls and surveillance/control methods. They don’t want their citizens to be able to make unknown purchases or move money out of the country without permission.

Dedicated users or “cypherpunks” in China can still find ways to use bitcoins and [even to mine bitcoins](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/12/18/chinas-underground-bitcoin-miners-.html), but for casual users it is increasingly hard to buy bitcoins or other cryptocurrencies there.

Overall, on-chain indicators continue to suggest that Bitcoin is in a consolidation phase. Euphoria in the Bitcoin market did not reach as high of a level in 2021 as it did in the 2017 or 2013 bull market tops, which suggests a shorter and shallower bear market as well. A lot of the euphoria in 2021 was instead centered around meme coins and smart contract blockchains.

Bitcoin miners are increasingly holding the coins they mine rather than selling them to market, and long-term holders are accumulating again rather than selling into strength, which sets up a certain amount of supply tightness to the market. It wouldn’t take a very large increase in demand to send the price up from here.

I monitor sentiment about Bitcoin on social media and it had been pretty bearish in recent weeks. People in the industry were bracing for a long bear market, and a lot of the Bitcoin critics were coming out with victory laps about Bitcoin’s recent price weakness from $69k down to $46k. When people get too bullish or too bearish on social media, I’ve noticed that price tends to shift in the opposite way pretty regularly.

A deceleration in Chinese selling as Huobi finishes its wind-down, a spent output profit ratio back above one, supply tightness, and higher highs in terms of price all look bullish. On the other hand, less fiscal and monetary stimulus by many countries including the United States in 2022 could provide some downward pressure on risk assets broadly, including on the price of bitcoins. I am neutral in the intermediate-term and bullish in the long-term.

**Jack Dorsey Took the Gloves Off**

Perhaps the most interesting news in the space, both for Bitcoin and the broader cryptocurrency ecosystem, centers around Jack Dorsey’s recent heated exchanges with various cryptocurrency venture capitalists.

Jack Dorsey recently resigned as CEO of Twitter to focus full time on Square (SQ), for which he is also the CEO and for which he renamed The Block. He re-affirmed Square/Block’s commitment to Bitcoin-only or at least Bitcoin-primary development within its suite of financial services, rather than focusing on multiple blockchains.

And then he got rather outspoken on Twitter over the past week. He has long since been somewhat critical of Ethereum and other blockchains outside of Bitcoin, but recently turned his attention to Web3 and VC funding. Web3 refers to a set of cryptocurrency platforms that aim to make decentralized social media and other web content, in a push against the current Web 2.0 internet mega-caps that often violate privacy and have monopoly-like aspects to them.

Dorsey said, however, that Web3 is shaping up to be rather centralized and that it primarily benefits the VCs that fund it.

![Web3 Centralization](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-web3-centralized.png)

Dorsey prefers focusing on building on top of Bitcoin, which is rather decentralized at the base layer. It’s a lot less sexy for VCs to build there though, because there is no custom token with fast exit liquidity in it for them. Companies specifically building on the Bitcoin network have been able to raise plenty of VC funding, but it’s dwarfed by altcoin funding. Bitcoin-only companies are not exactly naming stadiums after themselves, in other words.

A long-term critique from many Bitcoiners is that the broader cryptocurrency space is often about exit liquidity. Historically, when VCs invest in a private startup, they don’t get exit liquidity for 5-10 years, and are therefore tied to the success of the project. However, in the crypto/altcoin VC ecosystem, which exists in a regulatory arbitrage period and gets around existing security laws to some extent, the VCs can get exit liquidity via tokens much more quickly after a couple years, even before there is a viable product.

Dorsey supported that view:

![VC Exit Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-vc-exit-liquidity.png)

There was a lot of back and forth in various threads.

Dorsey unfollowed Tyler Winklevoss after Tyler tweeted critically about Dorsey, and Dorsey also unfollowed a number of other big names in the cryptocurrency space. Marc Andreessen ironically blocked Dorsey on Twitter, and Dorsey sarcastically tweeted that fact out.

This may seem like just a lot of nerd drama but these are all among the biggest billionaire financiers in the crypto ecosystem. Bitcoin and the rest of the cryptocurrency space have been increasingly at odds with each other in terms of design philosophy, sources of funding, and so forth, and this has kind of come to a head here.

I mostly agree with Jack Dorsey on this topic in the sense that most blockchains outside of Bitcoin have centralized areas of control, and there is currently a lot of aggressive VC marketing similar to the 2017 cryptocurrency bubble, from which very few things did well. That’s not to say that all projects will do poorly but I think there is a high rate of overpromising, underdelivering, and a lot of self-dealing by VCs and insiders, and disproportionate risks for retail buyers of tokens.

For that reason, I often suggest a lot of caution when it comes to investing in or trading cryptocurrencies. Some of them could do well, especially as intermediate-term trades, but so far the track record for their success throughout boom and bust cycles is very low outside of Bitcoin, with a high rate of scams, frauds, bad designs, insufficient disclosures, and weak investor protection. Investors or speculators should make sure they understand a project at a deep level before putting any serious capital into it.

## FedEx

FedEx Corporation (FDX) was founded in 1971 as Federal Express, which was the company’s original air division.

The current structure of the company dates back to 1997, and encompasses FedEx Express, FedEx Ground, FedEx Freight, FedEx Services, and a number of smaller groups. Along with the United Parcel Service (UPS) and the government-operated United States Postal Service, FedEx is one of three primary parcel delivery services in the US. They serve 220 countries internationally as well.

The weekly technical chart for FedEx’s stock price looks decent here. The stock was overbought in late 2020, consolidated for a while, became oversold a few months ago, and is now bouncing up with positive momentum from those oversold conditions.

![FedEx Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-fdx-technicals.png)

**Economic Moat Analysis**

Major logistics companies have wide economic moats. The cost to deliver a package if you don’t have massive infrastructure is very high, but once you build out that infrastructure for decades, the per-unit cost of delivery is low. It’s an industry that naturally trends towards oligopolies.

In 2002, the world’s largest shipping company, Deutsche Post (DPSGY) of Germany, tried to break into the lucrative US shipping market. This company was independently larger than either UPS or FedEx, but its center of network density was in Europe.  So, this was the most well-capitalized possible competitor in the industry to compete against them.

But by 2008, Deutsche Post failed to enter the market:

> Deutsche Post, owner of DHL Express, said it would close the parcel carrier’s U.S. operations after an unsuccessful five-year, $10 billion attempt to crack into a $61.1 billion market dominated by UPS Inc. and FedEx Corp.
>
> The express unit will shut down Jan. 30, but DHL International will maintain its freight forwarding, contract logistics and international parcel shipping operations in the U.S., for which it will still try to complete an agreement with UPS by year’s end to handle airlift.
>
> Deutsche Post, the German post office, bought DHL International in 2002 and acquired Airborne Express in 2003 as a platform for U.S. operations. But it never was able to rise above fourth place in market share, behind UPS, FedEx and the U.S. Postal Service, and by the third quarter of 2008, it actually had lost market share.
>
> “The U.S. market is a highly concentrated duopoly,” John Mullen, global chief executive officer of DHL Express, said Nov. 10. “In spite of our investing massive amounts of money to be a credible third choice, the reality of the lack of scale [for DHL], the productivity that [UPS and FedEx] have, the market reach and the brand awareness make it impossible for us to make it economically viable,” Mullen said in a conference call with reporters.
>
> –[Jonathan Reisken, Transport Topics, Nov 2008](https://www.ttnews.com/articles/dhl-end-us-parcel-service-after-5-years-nearly-10-bln)

For a while, DHL stuck around for the US as an international courier, having basically given up on the domestic market. Ten years later, Deutsche Post tried to find another niche and began offering ecommerce deliveries within certain US cities.

So, it’s possible to find niches that the big networks aren’t servicing completely, but it’s extremely hard to dislodge the leading networks from their core operations, even if you’re a well-capitalized competitor.

In recent years, UPS surpassed Deutsche Post in terms of global revenue, and FedEx has caught up and seems likely to surpass them within a few years as well. Being a US-based global logistics company is a very powerful position to have.

**Bullish Trends and Catalysts**

FedEx benefits from ongoing e-commerce growth. As businesses and residences perform a larger share of their purchases online and get those products delivered rather than driving them back from the store in their own car, the total market for last-mile parcel delivery increases.

The company has been focusing on remaining competitive in that industry, with optimizations for last-mile delivery and the introduction of residential delivery on Sundays, so that they can deliver on all seven days of the week.

Two decades ago, e-commerce sales represented 1% of total retail sales in the United States. Currently they represent 13% of total retail sales:

![Ecommerce Percentage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-ecommerce-percentage.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The pandemic spiked that percentage to over 15% briefly and now it’s cooling off, but the structural trend is clear. This trend will probably bottom out above 12% and then continue moving up.

In comparison, China and South Korea both have over 25% e-commerce penetration as a percentage of retail sales, and their numbers are still rising. The US will probably at least reach over 20% in the future, and far higher numbers than that are possible. It’s unclear at this point how high this will reach before leveling off. Speaking for my personal household, we buy over half of our items online, except for certain categories such as groceries, gasoline, large furniture, and restaurant outings.

FedEx has also been investing overseas in order to expand globally rather than limit itself to the United States. They acquired TNT (a global logistics company based in Europe) in 2016, and have been gradually integrating their logistics with FedEx’s logistics since then. By 2022, they plan to have that integration almost entirely complete, which is beneficial for profit margins and operational efficiency.

Over the past decade or so, FedEx established key hubs in China and Japan to serve the Asia-Pacific market as well.

Here in December, the company announced a $5 billion share repurchase plan, which is over 7% of the company’s market capitalization. After years of aggressive investment, the company is reaching enough critical mass that they can start returning capital to shareholders at an accelerated rate.

**Valuation Analysis**

FedEx is currently trading at a blended price/earnings ratio of under 13x, which is less than its historical average of over 17x:

![FDX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-fdx-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

FedEx pays a small 1.18% dividend, with a low payout ratio and historically fast dividend growth. Dividend growth stalled during the pandemic years, but then jumped 15% over the past year.

When valuing it based on sales, we can see that FedEx typically has strong sales growth and is currently trading in-line with its historically average price/sales ratio:

![FedEx YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-fdx-ychart.png)

FedEx persistently trades at a valuation discount to UPS based on most valuation metrics, which makes sense. FedEx has a BBB credit rating and is the smaller company, while UPS has an A- credit rating and is the larger company. UPS has a higher operating margin due to a more complete network effect. However, that valuation gap is above average at the moment, and FedEx has been gaining on UPS in terms of revenue.

This chart shows the cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio for UPS and FedEx, with the spread between them shown on the bottom panel:

![FDX UPS PE 5](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-26-pe-5.png)

Most other valuation metrics suggest the same unusually wide valuation gap. This is interesting, because consensus analyst expectations are for FedEx to have faster earnings growth than UPS over the next two years.

Overall, both in absolute and relative terms, FedEx offers an interesting value. I’m relatively bullish on UPS but a bit more bullish on FedEx from current levels over a 5+ year period.

**Balance Sheet Analysis**

As previously mentioned, FedEx has a BBB credit rating. This is investment-grade, but two steps below UPS at A-.

Specifically, FedEx has $13.7 billion in net debt, which is a 1.5x multiple compared to their trailing twelve months of operating cash flow. In comparison, UPS has $11.5 billion in net debt which is a 0.9x multiple compared to their operating cash flow. Both balance sheets are reasonable, but UPS has the notably stronger one. FedEx also has a larger share of capital leases.

I don’t foresee FedEx or UPS having trouble paying their bills any time in the foreseeable future. Considering that corporate bond yields are currently below the inflation rate, I don’t mind the fact that FedEx has more debt than UPS, because this is reflected in FedEx’s notably lower equity valuation. However, if they had the same equity valuation, I would buy UPS instead.

**Risk Analysis**

Like any company, FedEx has a variety of risks.

Some of them are tail risks, such as major cyberattacks or other issues that could seriously jeopardize their operations but are hard to account for. Other risks are more specific and monitorable, and I have particular focus on these two that I’ll describe below.

*Competition from Amazon and Others*

FedEx has enjoyed a corporate duopoly within the United States with UPS for a while, or a triopoly when you include the US Postal Service. As previously mentioned, even a company as large as Deutsche Post was unable to break into it.

However, Amazon has built out massive logistics infrastructure and has been gradually entering the last-mile delivery market since 2018, so they are now a fourth major competitor to some extent. If there’s a company that has a reasonable shot of breaking into this industry, the juggernaut that is Amazon would be it. They’ve been building out warehouse infrastructure for two decades now and are a $1.7 trillion company, which is more than 6x the value of UPS and FedEx combined.

However, I often observe that competition from Amazon is overblown outside of their core retail sales area. CVS for example was weighed down heavily by the news of Amazon entering the prescription drugs business, but so far it has had minimal impact. And Microsoft Azure is gaining on Amazon’s AWS in terms of market share. Amazon might be Superman, but even Superman has to deal with kryptonite.

Amazon is now so large, and moving in so many directions at the same time, that I’m not too concerned with its effect on wide-moat companies that already dominate niche industries. CVS is a dominant player in the pharmacy business. UPS and FedEx dominate the parcel delivery business. Microsoft is catching up on Amazon in cloud computing, drawing from its existing dominance in operating systems ever since Satya Nadella took over as CEO from Steve Ballmer. Amazon is the underdog in some of these areas despite being the superpower in the e-commerce market.

Still, it’s something to monitor for FedEx, because Amazon does have a history of physical retail disruption and has one of the strongest foundations for entering the space in a serious way. I don’t foresee them disrupting UPS and FedEx to a large extent because I view the overall pie likely growing fast enough to absorb competition without hurting the existing leaders, but we must monitor the market to see if trends change.

*Wage Pressure*

Labor-intensive businesses are facing pressures in the US at this time, and FedEx has hundreds of thousands of employees. FedEx, UPS, and Amazon have all faced some degree of employee organization and requests for higher compensation.

US corporate profit margins have been near record highs for years, as wages have been rather suppressed. I expect the overall 2020s decade to be more labor-friendly, and a bit harder for capital to maintain their share. There are persistent labor shortages, and wages haven’t kept up with inflation.

This is an issue I expect to be a problem for the majority of US companies, and I don’t view FedEx as being more vulnerable than average. Combined with their reasonable valuation, I am comfortable with FedEx’s margin risks as it compares to the broader market. Still, it could easily catch the market by surprise with a bad quarter under certain conditions, resulting in a negative volatility event for the stock.

## Final Thoughts

The Democrats’ Build Back Better bill was recently derailed for 2021 because it didn’t get support from Senator Joe Manchin, and it needs every Democrat vote to get through. They are going to attempt a revised bill in 2022 but it’s unclear if it will pass or not. My base case is that some smaller version of it will pass prior to the 2022 midterm election but I don’t have high conviction on that view.

Things like the child tax credit extension in that bill would be relevant for nominal GDP and inflation forecasts. In recent news however, President Biden did extend the pause on student loan repayments through May 2022. All else being equal, that’s a minor stimulus around the margins for that period because it leaves more cash in borrowers’ hands.

Overall, due to less monetary support and fiscal support for markets as I described in my prior report, I am not as bullish on the S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 in 2022 compared to the performance of these indices in 2020 and 2021.

That doesn’t mean that I necessarily think markets will crash. They might even grind up for a positive year. However, I don’t expect it to keep going up at this rapid of a rate, and instead expect more volatility in general.

However, I’m not particularly bearish on the US economy itself for 2022; the labor market remains tight, credit growth is expanding mildly, and the Omicron variant seems to be highly transmissible but not very deadly.

In other words, after 2020 and 2021 were years where the stock market did better than we might otherwise expect from the state of the economy, I suspect 2022 might be a year where the state of the economy does better than what the stock market will do. I’ll continue to monitor data on that idea.

I continue to have a relatively defensive outlook, but I do like a lot of value stocks and global stocks, including healthcare, energy pipelines, homebuilders, and consumer staples. For the growth side of things, my favorite growth play remains Bitcoin itself, although some individual growth equities like Roku are starting to look interesting again at current levels.
