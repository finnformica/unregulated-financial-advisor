# Portfolio Pruning

*September 20, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This is an extra research note between normal reports, to highlight some trades I plan to make on Monday. I’m doing some pruning for the various portfolios.

The turnover rate for the portfolios was pretty low this year, despite the extreme amount of volatility that happened.

In early/mid March, I sold some stock positions that were likely to face permanent capital impairment from the pandemic, such as Carnival (CCL) and Oneok (OKE), and put that capital into other beaten-down stocks that were facing big temporary challenges but that would likely recover on a more reasonable timeframe, like American Express (AXP) and Nucor (NUE). I also reduced Treasury exposure and increased precious metal exposure, which has done well with the reflationary outcome. I kept the portfolios mostly equity-focused, and they enjoyed the strong rebound.

Beginning in June with overbought conditions and high investor sentiment, I began de-risking the portfolios slightly. For example, I reduced equity index exposure and added a bit more short-term Treasury exposure in June, and sold high-flying Nvidia (NVDA) in September.

Now that we’ve had a very strong rally from March into September and are indeed experiencing some volatility and an ongoing correction/consolidation in the market, it’s a good opportunity to prune the portfolios, to make sure weaker or overvalued companies are removed, and better values are placed in. It also makes sense to simplify some areas where it makes sense to do so.

As a housekeeping note, I published a new public article called “[A Century of Fiscal and Monetary Policy: Inflation vs Deflation](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/)” that is worth a read for a long-term macro picture.

**Newsletter M1 Portfolio**

*Top Level*

There has been an increasing number of foreign stocks in the dividend stock pie and the growth stock pie, so I can reduce the international ETF section a bit, even though my overall approach remains to be very inclusive of quality foreign stocks.

Therefore, I am reducing the international ETF section by 2%, and increasing the growth pie by 1% and PHYS by 1%.

*International Equities*

The multi-asset newsletter portfolio has the most positions out of all the model portfolios. To simplify it a bit, I am consolidating some of the single-country ETFs in the international equity section into a few regional ETFs. This will keep a lot of the overall geographic exposure, while reducing the number of tiny positions.

In terms of the number of securities being replaced, this international ETF consolidation is my biggest change this week, but in terms of the underlying fundamental change, it’s not very big due to the replacements that significantly overlap with the securities that are removed.

I’ll be selling EWS, THD, EWY, EWZ, EWM, ECH, TUR, EIDO, and VNM. Most of these are single-country Asian and Latin American countries. In their place, I will buy ASEA, which consists of several southeast Asian countries, and ILF, which consists of several Latin American countries.

For consistency with this approach, I am replacing VEA and VIGI with VPL and VGK.

The international section will be as follows:

* 40% VWO (Emerging equities)
* 16% VPL (Pacific equities)
* 16% VGK (European equities)
* 7% RSX (Russian equities)
* 7% INDA (Indian equities)
* 7% ASEA (Southeast Asian equities)
* 7% ILF (Latin American equities)

I will dial those around over time based on what areas seem to present the best investment opportunities. Right now, the focus is on emerging Eurasia, with some western Europe, Japan, and Latin America.

*Dividend Stocks*

I’m consolidating some of the stocks here, by selling stocks that have largely done well and their prices have gone up faster than fundamentals.

Specifically, I’ll be selling SYY, DG, TSCO, and DIS.

In their place, I’ll be buying EBAY, buying AYI (which I’m selling from the growth stocks pie, so it’s a lateral move) and adding a percentage each to some existing positions such as BTI, BLK, BNS, ABC, and LRCX.

Individual stocks could always surprise to the upside or downside. For example, maybe DG does great and EBAY drops the ball next year, so DG ends up outperforming EBAY despite the swap. That could very well happen. However, for my stock selection process, I approach things in terms of probabilities over the long run, so it’s about a process that keeps emphasizing companies with good fundamentals (growth, ROIC, balance sheet, and so forth) relative to the stock price, and applying a pretty comfortable margin so that turnover remains relatively low.

*Growth Stocks*

In the growth stocks pie, I’ll be selling PYPL, ADBE, SWKS, and FSLR for sizable gains, and buying JD, BABA, and FB in their place. I’m also selling AYI to move it to the dividend stocks pie. Since I am buying JD and BABA (Chinese internet companies), I’m selling KWEB (Chinese internet ETF).

I’ve been holding ADBE and PYPL since the beginning of 2019, right after the Q4 2018 sell-off that mostly affected growth stocks. They both doubled from there in less than two years, and now I consider them a bit pricey. Here’s ADBE for example:

**Fortress Income M1 Portfolio**

*Individual Stocks*

I’ll be selling ITW, TSM, TSCO and WY from the individual stock section of the Fortress Income portfolio.

These have all done quite well this year. TSCO and TSM have performed incredibly, ITW performed above average, and WY performed decently. I’m not bearish on these stocks; they just aren’t among my more bullish picks at the moment after such solid performance relative to fundamentals, and their yields are low in a portfolio that emphasizes a combination of yield and growth.

I’ll be buying EBAY and NVS for the portfolio. I’ll also be adding to existing positions like BTI, AMGN, BLK. This collectively will increase the portfolio dividend yield a bit.

**ETF M1 Portfolio**

I’m performing slight asset allocation changes to the ETF portfolio.

VEA and VIGI will both be decreased from 11% to 8%, VWO will be increased from 11% to 16%, and AAAU will be increased from 8% to 9%.

**No Limits IB Portfolio**

I’m selling the existing 100 shares of EMXC, and putting that money into 100 more shares of VWO. I have plenty of ex-China emerging market diversification in that portfolio with individual stock selection, particularly in Russia and India, so the EMXC allocation can go into more VWO for consolidation.

**Technical Warning**

Each investor has their own unique situation. I’ve been slightly de-risking since June, although for me that still includes a rather broadly diversified portfolio with sizable equity exposure and an emphasis on a long-term time horizon. Older investors or more conservative investors may want higher cash or Treasury allocations.

On the other hand, this market has been known for years for its relentless dip-buying.

For me, U.S. fiscal policy remains the biggest variable to watch for global risk assets in broad terms, and U.S. stocks in particular. With fundamentals at rather high valuations, the technical charts not looking great, and uncertain fiscal policy for the next few months at a time when the economy remains unusually vulnerable, I lean a bit defensive until a more bullish catalyst emerges.

However, within that somewhat defensive posture, I’m still remaining long on individual areas and companies that I think are compelling in terms of valuation and growth. I like high-quality value companies, some of the reasonably-valued growth companies (or “GARP”: growth at a reasonable price), robust commodity producers, and a sizable exposure to international stocks with an emphasis on Asian emerging markets, all with a multi-year time horizon.

**Tesla Short Opportunity (for folks who like pain)**

TSLA is setting up another potential short opportunity as a downside hedge. Shorting TSLA has been very rough this year, but not for investors that have clearly-defined exit points so they can step aside of the momentum train if market sentiment (rather than fundamentals) pushes sharply against them, and try again when another opportunity presents itself.

I like a TSLA short here, with a stop at $500/share, which is roughly the previous all-time high. The trade, essentially, rests on the proposition that the recent rebound in price will simply be a lower high as part of a current bear trend that began with its August 31 all-time high and subsequent sell-off, and this view becomes invalidated if the stock does reach new all-time highs over $500/share.

The potential loss on that short with the stop level is about 14% from current prices as of this writing, while the upside is significant, and the overall trade can be quite uncorrelated to the rest of the portfolio, or partially inversely correlated as a hedge.
