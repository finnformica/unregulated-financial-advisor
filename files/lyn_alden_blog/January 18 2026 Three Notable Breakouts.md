## Macro View

**US Liquidity Conditions**

In my prior report, I pushed back on some of the social media sensationalism I was seeing around year-end window dressing:

> There are two major trends I’m seeing in social media commentary on this subject recently.
>
> The first and simpler trend is that people are saying things like, “the Fed injected another $75 billion” or other statements of this nature. Some of them are claiming there is a systemic banking crisis brewing. These tend to not be very informed commentators, and for starters, the whole point of overnight liquidity is that it’s not cumulative. It’s not “another” $75 billion. It’s the same tens of billions of dollars being added and taken out and added again.
>
> On December 31st, as financial institutions scrambled for liquidity and collateral amongst themselves, there was an uptick in their usage of the Fed’s standing facilities. The Fed’s repo facility was tapped for about $75 billion, meaning that financial institutions gave collateral such as treasuries to the Fed in exchange for cash, for one day. Interestingly, on the *same day*, the Fed’s *reverse* repo facility was also used for about $106 billion, meaning that financial instructions put excess cash into the Fed and took out some treasuries.
>
> ![Repo Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-repo-chart-annotated.png)
>
> ![Reverse Repo Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-reverse-repo-chart-annotated.png)
>
> On net, this means that about $31 billion in liquidity was actually sucked *out* of the market by the Fed on December 31st, at the behest of financial institutions. There were simultaneous pockets of liquidity shortage and liquidity abundance in the system, and those pockets didn’t all find each other efficiently and thus relied on the Fed’s two-way facilities to sort out the difference. Here in early January, the numbers have already died down from that window dressing spike.
>
> The second and more nuanced trend is that people are saying the Fed’s shift toward balance sheet expansion is not doing enough to put out these liquidity issues. The Fed’s repo facility is still being regularly used, and the secured overnight financing rate remains persistently elevated above the interest rate on bank reserves, which indicates a liquidity shortage. This is being said by more informed commentators and is a worthy observation.
>
> ![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-sofr-iorb.png)
>
> However, I think that second trend of commentary is premature. The Fed just started re-expanding their balance sheet in mid-December. They’re only up about $100 billion from their lows, and are at roughly similar levels to when liquidity shortages started to emerge back in September. Bank reserve levels are still near their post-2020 low end. It’ll likely take some time of persistent balance sheet increases to more permanently put out these ongoing pockets of liquidity shortage.
>
> ![Fed Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-fed-balance-sheet.png)
>
> ![Bank Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-bank-reserves.png)
>
> A few billion, or even a few tens of billions, being sloshed around in the Fed’s standing repo facility on an overnight basis is not much in the context of approximately $3 trillion in system-wide bank reserves.
>
> As we look into 2026, the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet increases combined with mild to moderate interest rate reductions, is likely to be decent for liquidity conditions. I expect there to be plenty of bumps along the way, but I continue to have a constructive view on emerging market equities, as well as on regional and super-regional US banks, and likely on bitcoin as well.
>
> -January 4, 2026 premium report

Since then, there has been basically no usage of the Fed’s repo facility:

![Repo Operations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-18-repo-operations.png)

With bank reserves back over $3 trillion and gradually rising, liquidity shortages among financial institutions have largely been alleviated. There will potentially be more usages of it, but this Q4 2025 period was likely the worst of it for quite a long while unless something large and external happens.

![Bank Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-18-bank-reserves.png)

The spread between the secured overnight financing rate and the interest rate on bank reserves (SOFR-IORB) has cooled off over the past week and a half as well, though that will likely take a bit more time to more fully settle down:

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-18-sofr-iorb.png)

The Fed intends to gradually add liquidity at least through tax season, and probably beyond that point practically indefinitely, albeit at a moderate pace.

The biggest aspect to this shift from the Fed reducing their balance sheet to increasing their balance sheet will not be the magnitude, in my opinion. Instead, I think the duration of it will be what’s notable. I’ve been referring to this as the “gradual print” for that reason, and that continues to be my base case.

**Fed/Treasury Legal Drama**

The Department of Justice launched a criminal investigation into Fed Chairman Powell, over remarks he made to Congress about renovations of the Fed’s facilities.

On January 11th, Powell came back with basically a scorched-earth response, unusually fiery for a central banker, saying this isn’t about the renovations but rather is about Fed Independence. Here was the key line of [his statement](https://www.federalreserve.gov/newsevents/speech/powell20260111a.htm):

> This new threat is not about my testimony last June or about the renovation of the Federal Reserve buildings. It is not about Congress’s oversight role; the Fed through testimony and other public disclosures made every effort to keep Congress informed about the renovation project. Those are pretexts. The threat of criminal charges is a consequence of the Federal Reserve setting interest rates based on our best assessment of what will serve the public, rather than following the preferences of the President.
>
> This is about whether the Fed will be able to continue to set interest rates based on evidence and economic conditions—or whether instead monetary policy will be directed by political pressure or intimidation.
>
> -Jerome Powell, January 11, 2026

This is the biggest conflict between the Federal Reserve and the Executive Branch since at least the 1970s, and arguably all the way back to the 1951 Treasury-Fed accord.

However, in the week that has followed, it has cooled off. President Trump said he was unaware of this, thus distancing himself from it rather than doubling down as one might normally expect.

[Reporting by Politico](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/01/12/freaked-out-about-the-bond-market-white-house-reels-from-powell-probe-00722861) and others suggested that many in the administration were surprised and spooked by the legal action against Powell, and that Treasury Secretary Bessent has been laying the groundwork for a smooth Fed transition for months that this threatened. Based on President Trump’s distancing response on social media thus far, that wing of the administration (essentially led by Bessent) likely advised him not to press the issue.

This is a headline-driven political environment, and many things can change. My base case is that Powell will continue his term as chairman until it naturally ends in May of this year, and from there it’s an open question on whether he will remain a governor on the board until that term ends in 2028.

Until recently, Kevin Hassett was the betting market’s leading candidate to replace Powell as the Fed chair. However, remarks by the President last week greatly reduced those odds, and so Kevin Warsh is now the front-runner. Ironically, Warsh is historically more hawkish than Hassett, despite Trump’s open preference toward more dovish monetary policy during his term.

I don’t have a firm view on who the President will select to lead the Fed starting in May, but the baseline outcome is that whoever it is, they will continue to keep repo markets and Treasury markets liquid, and thus nothing stops this train.

In general, the two-speed economy aspect remains in effect. Areas affected by US fiscal deficit spending or AI capex tend to be doing quite well. Areas negatively affected by medium-high interest rates, and lower/middle income consumers more broadly, tend to be struggling.

**Canada Cozying Up to China**

This past week, Canada’s prime minister went to China, and the two countries reduced some trade barriers. Reuters [reports](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/canada-china-set-make-historic-gains-new-partnership-says-carney-2026-01-16/):

> BEIJING, Jan 16 (Reuters) – Canada and China struck an initial trade deal on Friday that will slash tariffs on electric vehicles and canola, as both nations promised to tear down trade barriers while forging new strategic ties during Prime Minister Mark Carney’s visit.

In particular, Canada will allow up to 49,000 Chinese EV imports per year with low tariffs, gradually increasing to 70,000 over the next few years. This is at a time when China’s BYD [already displaced Tesla](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/teslas-quarterly-deliveries-fall-more-than-expected-lower-ev-demand-2026-01-02/) as the largest seller of EVs globally in 2025.

During the past year, the trade strategy by the United States has shifted a handful of times. At first, they sent out a flurry of tariffs, with an emphasis on China. Then, they shifted course a bit to focus-fire China, trying to forge a global coalition against China. I wrote at the time how difficult that plan would be to work.

> The second is that the rest of the world is unlikely to take that well, and they do have a lot of leverage. For example, China has about $3.6 trillion in annual exports, and only about $500 billion go to the United States. That’s why the latest move by the administration is to try to de-escalate with the rest of the world and try to get everyone to target China together. That’s not an easy gambit to win, so it’ll be the key thing to watch in the weeks ahead.
>
> -April 13, 2025 premium report

In the months that followed, that effort had limited success, and instead the US began escalating against places like India and Brazil, with somewhat of a de-escalation in rhetoric toward China despite a handful of flare-ups along the way.

Basically, China has the largest installed electricity generation in the world, more steel production capability than the rest of the world combined, a massive network effect around mechanical and electrical supply chains, and enormous port and logistics infrastructure, which collectively is very costly and time-consuming to reproduce elsewhere. It’s kind of like trying to displace New York City as a center for finance or Silicon Valley as a hub for venture capital and startups; not impossible but extremely difficult over a long period of time.

My base case for a while has been that we’ll see a more multipolar world emerge, with the United States and China being the two biggest opposing poles. That continues to be the case, although the United States’ willingness to open so many disputes with its neighbors all at once has resulted in less geographically-defined poles than I would have expected by this stage.

People who keep calling for the collapse of China, or inversely the hyperinflation of the US dollar, will continue to be incorrect as they have for years, at least looking out in any sort of investable time horizon. On a long enough timeline, of course, everything can happen as entropy wins.

**Tariff News**

US tariff revenue is coming in at about $30 billion per month, which according to most analysis is predominantly paid by US corporations, and to a lesser extent by US consumers by proxy of price increases, with foreign exporters paying the smaller remainder via price decreases to retain volumes.

This weekend, the US announced 10% tariffs against multiple European countries until such a time as Denmark allows the forced purchase of Greenland. They’re set to go into effect in February, and then increase to 25% in June.

Analysts continue to await the Supreme Court’s decision on the scale of emergency powers for tariff authority, bypassing Congress’s ability to tax.

Each day where the Supreme Court is scheduled to release some of its decisions, there’s a possibility that the tariff outcome will be among them, but so far it has not. It could be decided any time in the first half of this year, but is expected sooner rather than later because it’s an expedited issue given its broad scale and timeliness.

Prior to the Supreme Court hearing on the topic, betting markets put about 40% odds that the Supreme Court would side with President Trump, and 60% they would rule against him. Two lower courts sided against Trump, but by appointment those courts were liberal-leaning, whereas the Supreme Court is conservative-leaning, which makes it less clear of an outcome.

In November 2025 when the hearing occurred, multiple conservative Justices (let alone the liberal ones) showcased skepticism about the tariff authority, and betting market odds sharply fell to around 20% odds that they’d side with Trump. The longer the outcome goes on without an expedited decision, however, the odds gradually rise in Trump’s favor, albeit that’s still the minority outcome. Polymarket currently puts them at 33% odds for Trump and the remaining odds against him.

My base case is that the Supreme Court will limit the powers in some ways (not sure how much), but that the administration will find some legal workarounds where possible (they’ve already said they would), and so it’ll be a speed bump or soft ceiling on tariffs more-so than an outright elimination of them.

Tariffs continue to be an uncertain and significant factor for investors to consider, albeit at $300-$400 billion per year, it’s only one of many macro-scale swing factors.

## Three Notable Breakouts

**Uranium**

All the way back in my [October 25, 2020 premium report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-10-25/), I dedicated the full piece to making the bullish case for uranium.

At the time, it was around $29/pound. More interestingly, one could buy the Uranium Participation Corp (URPTF) at around a 20% discount to NAV, so in actuality it was less than $24/pound from an investment standpoint.

Then ten months later in my [September 5, 2021 report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2021-9-5/), I provided a lengthy update on uranium. At that point, uranium had risen to over $42/pound, and Sprott had acquired the Uranium Participation Corp, turning it into the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust (SRUUF). The discount to NAV closed and turned into a premium, and so we had a nearly 80% investment gain from the original 2020 position in less than a year. I reiterated that I was still bullish for the long run.

By January 2024, uranium had risen to around $100/pound, which was a local bubble. It then cooled off, bottomed in March 2025, and since then has been steadily rising again. Currently it’s back up to around $85/pound.

![Uranium Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-18-uranium-price.png)

The price is higher than the original target I had when I became bullish in 2020, but the world has changed since then as well. AI usage has taken off, which is very power intensive, and thus many governments and corporations have become more open to nuclear power development.

My bullish thesis had conservative expectations for demand (slow but positive growth) and was more-so a supply-side story, and since then the demand side has generally improved over time along with that supply-side story playing out as expected.

Even Germany, which infamously decommissioned (and in some cases demolished) its functioning nuclear plants, has softened its stance; Germany’s Chancellor Merz this week called that decision “a strategic mistake”. Germany in recent years has seen energy-intensive companies shift to other countries, as part of a gradual de-industrialization of the country toward places with cheaper and more abundant energy.

Positive price action has helped the supply side, but the overall supply/demand balance still remains rather tight. Uranium is a small percentage of nuclear power plant operation costs, so utilities will pay virtually anything for it during a shortage, and generally they try to contract their supply in advance. Additionally, given the uncertain state of geopolitics, cross-border supply contracts won’t necessarily be reliable, and so building strategic reserves is warranted.

![Uranium Supply/Demand](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-18-uranium-supply-demand.png)

*Chart Source: Yellow Cake Plc*

I have no firm view on the next six months of price action. But my base case is that I expect this breakout to be durable, and I doubt we’ve seen the highs in uranium for this cycle.

**Emerging Markets**

For the past year and a half, I have been watching for a potential rotation toward emerging market outperformance.

Any Fed cutting cycle and dollar weakening cycle is a potential catalyst for emerging market equities to start to do well, because it can contribute to a virtuous or vicious cycle.

When the United States economy is doing well, global capital flows into it, and the Fed has room to tighten monetary policy. This can strengthen the dollar, which is a problem for dollar-indebted emerging market economies. As their liabilities harden, they often have to tighten monetary policy despite economic weakness, and they become very undesirable places for global capital. Global capital then flees, further hurting their currency, and often further strengthening the dollar, in a vicious cycle.

When the United States economy shows cracks, and the Fed starts to loosen monetary policy, and global capital is already heavily stuffed into US equities at high valuations, the vicious cycle can eventually reverse. If a small bit of capital flows out toward emerging markets, their currencies can start to stabilize, and with such low valuations, they become attractive to contrarian, value-oriented investors. As they start to do well, momentum investors hop on board, and so valuations rise, their currencies rise, their liabilities become more serviceable, their economy improves, and so forth.

I’ve been a bit gun-shy on this trade, because it had a false start in 2019. Back then, the US economy was slowing, the Fed was starting to cut interest rates, and quantitative easing ended. Emerging markets started to catch a mild bid. But then COVID-19 emerged, and economies shut down, and the US and many other economies printed a ton of money, and the Fed hiked rates to try to contain inflation. This short-circuited what could have been the start to a bull market in emerging markets, and set it back for years.

And so, in 2024 when the Fed was starting to cut rates again, my view was that this is the next potential catalyst. In other words, this was the next window where emerging markets had a strong chance at outperforming, and shifting toward a virtuous cycle that could last 3-5 years or more. However, I stressed that it was not guaranteed, and that there were various ways it might be disrupted, even as it was my base case that it would go this time.

My [August 4, 2024 premium report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2024-8-4/) focused on asset rotations, including for emerging markets. That was the cycle when the BRICS were named in the first place.
>
> There was also a Fed cutting cycle in 2019. Global capital was stuffed into the U.S. at the time, but not quite as much as now, and then the whole 2020-2022 period was disrupted by a global pandemic and lockdowns and extreme fiscal and monetary actions.
>
> So, 2024 might or might not be the start of a reversal, but there are conditions present for it. Perhaps another disruption will occur and prevent any sort of reversal, such as a war that sends oil to $200/barrel. Maybe major emerging market leaders will shoot themselves in the foot with war, anti-market political actions, or other types of own-goals.
>
> And so I’m in the phase of watching for follow-through or the lack thereof, and am treating countries separately even though they are partially all linked to the dollar and global capital flows.
>
> **Examples of Emerging Market Stocks and ETFs**
>
> Chinese stocks had an almost 50% rally from their low in mid-September to their high, and then they gave up about half of those gains:
>
> ![MCHI 1 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-mchi-1-year.png)
>
> This was due to a combination of re-positioning and short-covering. I plan to watch to see if that new base starts to hold, to see if we get a higher-low, in other words.
>
> When we zoom out, even that huge 50% spike was a mere blip:
>
> ![MCHI Full](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-mchi-full.png)
>
> An example of a Chinese stock that I like is JD.com (JD). It’s a major e-commerce stock, it’s trading at under 11x earnings, and it has more cash than debt. But it’s broadly impaired by the fact that China is viewed as uninvestable. Investors are worried about economic conditions, but then also worried about the geopolitical tail risk of war-related impairments. In other words, even if JD does okay, a foreign investor’s claim of ownership on JD could be taken from them, either because China takes it, or because the investor’s home country sanctions them from it.
>
> ![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-jd-fastgraph.png)
>
> This is where an investor can use something akin to [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value) to build a probability-weighted outcome.
>
> For a simple example, JD’s ADR is currently around $40. An investor might say that over the next three years, due to a combination of Chinese stimulus, a global rate-cutting cycle, and rock-bottom valuations, that the stock has an 80% chance to double in price to $80. However, she also acknowledges that there’s a 20% chance that, perhaps due to a conflict in the South China Sea, the value of her investment will go to zero.
>
> The probability-weighted price in three years would therefore be .8\*80 + .2\*0 = $64. That’s a 60% total gain, or 17% annualized over three years. And of course that’s a very simple model. An investor would generally have a bigger range of outcomes than that. There’s a chance it triples, a chance it doubles, a chance it goes up 50%, a chance it stays flat, a chance it gets cut in half, a chance it goes to zero, and so forth. The big variable in that calculation are the odds that the ownership of the asset is zeroed-out for any reason. One investor might assign 5% odds over three years, and another investor might assign 50% odds.
>
> Similar analysis can be done on Alibaba (BABA), Tencent (TCEHY), and so forth.
>
> If we move over to Brazil, I continue to find their banking system interesting as a very small value slice. Their biggest bank had an explosive run of more than 8x price appreciation during the BRICS boom of 2002-2007, and then has chopped along for over a decade and a half since then:
>
> ![ITUB YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-itub-ychart.png)
>
> If any point in the second half of the 2020s we get a proper dollar downcycle and an emerging market upcycle, the move that something like ITUB could make is quite large.
>
> ![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-itub-fastgraph.png)
>
> -October 27, 2024 premium report

President Trump’s global trade war was the biggest wildcard to sort through in 2025, and one of the potential disruptions to the emerging market thesis to consider, but I provided multiple reports outlining the resilience of economies like Brazil to those tariffs:

> Brazil’s largest bank stock dipped a bit. That’s a dip I’m happy to keep accumulating:
>
> ![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-itub-fastgraph.png)
>
> Brazil’s government received a letter from President Trump telling them that they’ll be subject to 50% tariffs, which is on the punitive high end of the recent tariff letters. The reason stated in the letter is that Trump does not like how Brazil is handling its internal politics and legality around their former President Bolsonaro.
>
> ![Trump Brazil Tariff Letter](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-trump-brazil-tariff-letter.jpg)
>
> A challenge here is that the US already has a trade surplus with Brazil rather than a deficit with them, and referencing internal politics of a country argues counter to the president’s justification of emergency tariff powers which could end up circling back in court. Nonetheless, Brazil’s market has shown some concern for the tariffs, and Brazil’s President Lula has vowed to combat the US tariffs with reciprocal tariffs of their own if they go into effect in August, which could then result in higher US tariffs according to the letter.
>
> However, it’s worth pointing out that Brazil has a $2.2 trillion GDP economy, and they export about $41 billion annually to the US:
>
> ![Brazil Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-brazil-exports.png)
>
> Brazil’s exports are concentrated primarily in commodities, including agricultural commodities, and there is no shortage of global demand for them. Thus, the small share of GDP that those exports represent, and the ability to redirect those exports elsewhere, should keep the overall effects of tariffs minimal in terms of the national scale, even if they do disrupt some individual exporters. Thus, nothing about the latest round of tariff threats derails my bullish view on Brazilian financials.
>
> -July 20, 2025 premium report

## Final Thoughts: Precious Metals

Precious metals, despite having had a hell of a run, and despite showing increasing levels of volatility and euphoria, continue to hold up decently.

After having been a bull for many years on this space, I’ve recently shifted to neutral. I’ve not been in the camp that thinks it’s all just a bubble (rather, I think it’s a sustained re-pricing out of a long state of undervaluation), but the contrarian in me does get quite nervous around extremely overbought levels.

> Precious metals reached a crescendo in late December and have since sold off a bit.
>
> Here is silver, for example:
>
> ![Silver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-silver.png)
>
> This outcome is not surprising, and is in line with my warning from the prior report.
>
> Precious metals had a similar correction back in mid-October, so it is not as though a breaking of the momentum is necessarily a kill shot for the whole rally.
>
> However, I continue to view precious metals as a place where risk/reward is no longer asymmetrical. On one hand, precious metals benefit to the upside in my opinion from a combination of fiscal dominance and a world that is becoming more multipolar and distrusting over time, and so I think a lot of this higher pricing is likely to stick even after sentiment washes out. On the other hand, sentiment has become so positive, that there are indeed risks for a multi-month or even multi-year correction from these price levels.
>
> So, while I continue to hold precious metals, my new capital is likely to be deployed elsewhere.
>
> -January 4, 2026 premium report

Over the past two weeks, gold and silver both made new all-time highs. Platinum hasn’t yet recovered its December highs, but its chart is showing considerable strength:

![Platinum Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-18-platinum.png)

I continue to view precious metals as a place I want to hold capital, but not a place I want to add new capital. Out of the three, platinum currently has my interest the most, since on a relative basis it’s still rather cheap (about half the price of gold).
