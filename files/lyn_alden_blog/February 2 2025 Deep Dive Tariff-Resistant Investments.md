# Deep Dive: Tariff-Resistant Investments

*February 2, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report covers liquidity conditions and the initial wave of tariff announcements.

The digital asset section covers bitcoin price action and the announcement of USDt coming to the Lightning Network.

The investment analysis section looks at some tariff-resistant stock ideas.

## Macro View

**Domestic Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity has been rangebound based on most metrics:

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-bank-cash.png)

The Federal Reserve continues to perform quantitative tightening, gradually draining liquidity from the system, while the Treasury Department has been putting liquidity back into the system by issuing extra T-bills and thus drawing liquidity from the reverse repo facility back into the financial system. However, there is sub-$200 billion left in the reverse repo facility:

![RRP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-rrp.png)

The new Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has been critical of prior Secretary Janet Yellen’s shift from Treasury debt issuance toward T-bills. If he follows through with his perspective, then presumably he will issue more coupon securities, which puts more liquidity strain on the system.

However, this could potentially be offset by ongoing debt ceiling disputes. Until the debt ceiling is lifted, the Treasury’s general cash account is destined to head lower, which is good for liquidity while it happens. Thus, I don’t see major liquidity concerns in the very near term, but rather see them as likely to begin to form around the middle of this year. Much of it will depend on politics.

**Global Liquidity Indicators**

The dollar index was off its highs. However, tariff announcements are adding a lot of currency volatility, and the overall exchange rate of the dollar continues to be high and push upward against some currency pairs, putting downward pressure on global liquidity.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-dxy.png)

Somewhat offsetting that, China has been easing its monetary policy, and most major currency blocs (United States, Eurozone, and China for example) are currently growing their broad money supplies again and getting back toward all-time highs. Overall global liquidity continues to inch up, but faces a lot of uncertainties at the current time.

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-global-m2.png)

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Initial estimates are coming out for Q1 2025 real GDP, and once again the Atlanta Fed’s calculator has higher-than-consensus estimates that run a bit hot relative to the United States’ typical trend.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-gdpnow.png)

With fiscal dominance still in play, I think those results are achievable, but tariff uncertainty is the wildcard that could make this come in below expectations. A lot can change on that front in any given week.

**First Wave of Tariffs**

In response to Colombia not allowing U.S. military planes to enter with deported Colombians, President Trump imposed up to 50% tariffs on Colombia. He also threatened other activity including outright sanctions, which could potentially include confiscation of reserve assets. Colombia backed down and so those tariffs ultimately weren’t implemented.

More recently, as of yesterday President Trump announced 25% tariffs on Canada and Mexico, and 10% tariffs on China. This is a significant uptick from what Treasury Secretary Bessent had proposed regarding tariffs that start at 2.5% and ratchet up each month. Trump acknowledged this could cause market disruption.

For a magnitude check, the United States imports over $400 billion per year each from Canada and Mexico, totaling over $800 billion. So, putting 25% tariffs on that figure potentially represents over $200 billion in new taxes. The United States imports $500 billion per year from China, so 10% of that is another $50 billion. If tariffs are lowered or excluded on oil and oil-related products (the latest announcement seems to say those will be 10%), then that can trim those numbers modestly. It’s also possible that the Canadian and Mexican tariffs will be reduced to something like a 10% level on other goods if there are border security concessions, but that remains to be seen.

He also threatened tariffs against the European Union.

The question then becomes: who pays? That depends on a lot of factors.

If one country is sanctioned, then other international producers can compete on price, or that country can sell to another country who then re-sells to the United States. If multiple countries are sanctioned at once, then there’s not much of a competitive alternative besides the long-term process of bringing more manufacturing back to the United States at higher costs.

We can expect as a base case for wide-scale tariff-related costs to be eaten in multiple locations. The producer may have to accept lower margins. The middle-men might get squeezed a bit too, but their margins are razor-thin to begin with. Some portion of the costs will likely be passed onto American consumers.

If we see a 10% blanket tariff on China, for example, where would Apple (AAPL) relocate its massive supply chains to, quickly enough? How much negotiation power will they have with their Chinese suppliers? Diversifying Apple’s supply chains into India has already been a slow process. And what if India is hit by tariffs too? There’s a good chance that some of those tariffs are going to impact the consumer price of iPhones and other products. And this is at a time when Apple stock is trading at 36x earnings and hasn’t had revenue growth for two years.

Walmart (WMT) meanwhile is trading at almost 40x earnings, and is bid up as a massive in-favor blue chip stock. A problem is that it sells a lot of products made internationally. It runs on 4% operating margins, so it can’t really absorb tariffs itself. It either needs to pressure suppliers’ margins, go through the expense of finding new suppliers, or it needs to pass on tariff costs on some of its products to consumers.

![WMT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-wmt-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

And this is before we consider potential retribution, which is already starting around the margins. Countries that the United States puts tariffs on can band together and impose counter-tariffs to varying degrees. Consumer preferences can change and contract due to higher prices from tariffs, resulting in slowing trade and slowing economic growth.

Supply chains can move, but without lowering the cost of U.S. healthcare (which manufacturers often have to pay for their American workers) or taking other steps, manufacturing will be more expensive domestically. That’s why the manufacturing is not here currently; it’s not the lowest-cost place for it to be. These tariffs might change, but businesses and governments now have to operate in a state of higher uncertainty and higher costs.

Later this year, the Trump administration may extend tax cuts that were signed into law during his term, and may initiate new tax cuts. But before those hit, these tax increases from tariffs seem to be hitting first. Thus, there’s a potential dead zone of tax hikes without associated tax cuts at the current time, resulting in a bit of a fiscal speed brake in the first half of this year.

Back in January and February 2020, when China was starting to lock down cities over COVID-19, U.S. stocks just kept marching up with low volatility, unconcerned with some of the potential tail risks around lockdowns spreading globally and causing an economic disruption. In other words, markets didn’t discount for the risks at all. And then when those tail risks materialized, stocks fell down in a comically straight line until hyper-stimulus began. I see similar complacency in markets today.

**Some Takeaways:**

-Capital from all around the world is stuffed into U.S. large cap stocks, which have high valuations and high exposure to tariffs. U.S. stocks now account for 66% of MSCI’s all-world equity index. This sets up a potential stumbling block in the year ahead.

-Many countries will likely take a look at their U.S. Treasury holdings or other U.S. securities holdings. If a nation like Colombia can be threatened with sanctions, and Canada can be challenged with 25% tariffs, it lowers the bar for reserve confiscation and similar activities. Stephen Miran, President Trump’s nominee for chairman of the Council of Economic Advisors, has [proposed](https://www.hudsonbaycapital.com/documents/FG/hudsonbay/research/638199_A_Users_Guide_to_Restructuring_the_Global_Trading_System.pdf) placing taxes on reserve assets to discourage reserve accumulation and to weaken the dollar to make U.S. manufacturing more competitive.

-For many years, Canada has forgone many of its construction options to export its oil and gas products overseas, and instead let itself be reliant on the United States. The country might end up rethinking that approach, and indeed the leader of their Conservative party has already tweeted about that, given the unilateral impact the United States can have on them if it becomes unfriendly. We could see similar resilience-planning by other countries as well.

-I would think defensively for the time being. Gold, cash-equivalents, short-term Treasuries, and inexpensive equities can potentially provide some safe havens. Assume higher volatility ahead in terms of cash flow planning and balance sheet strength. I’m skeptical that the classic safe havens of this past cycle (mega cap U.S. stocks, as major beneficiaries of globalization) will be the best place to be.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Bitcoin continues to be in a consolidate pattern that it’s had since around November 21st.

However, while the market cap has barely budged since that time, the realized cap (i.e. on-chain aggregate cost basis) has increased from $713 billion to $847 billion. Plenty of entities are buying here, and plenty of entities are selling and taking profits, setting a new effective liquid price pseudo-floor.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

The new Treasury Secretary, Scott Bessent, disclosed that he owns bitcoin. It’s a small position for a billionaire, but nonetheless it’s a historically significant fact, since neither the prior two secretaries, Janet Yellen or Steven Mnuchin, were positive on the asset like he is.

Despite all of this, I continue to view the market as having lower levels of quantitative euphoria as it had during prior multi-year peaks, and so while there might be a range of risks related to liquidity or other issues, I don’t view the price as being too ahead of itself here, and thus I continue to be bullish over the next 18 months as a base case.

And despite various meme coin action, this overall cycle continues to be very dominant toward bitcoin. [Sam Wouters](https://x.com/SDWouters) of River published a chart showing the unprecedented decoupling between bitcoin and the rest of the ex-stablecoin cryptocurrency industry:

![Bitcoin Dominance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-bitcoin-dominance.jpg)

**El Salvador Complicates Legal Tender Status**

El Salvador has been pressured by the IMF to make it so that businesses don’t have to accept bitcoin as legal tender, which is a status that they gave the asset back in 2021.

In exchange for a $1.4 billion loan facility by the IMF, which generally holds an anti-bitcoin view as an institution, El Salvador [has agreed](https://dailyhodl.com/2025/01/31/el-salvador-passes-new-bitcoin-btc-legislation-tied-to-new-imf-1400000000-funding-deal-report/) to make it so that businesses can choose whether to accept bitcoin for payment or not, rather than being required to.

El Salvador continues to purchase bitcoin as a sovereign reserve asset, and continues to attract bitcoin-related businesses and tourists. Within the country, bitcoin as a medium of exchange began in El Zonte, El Salvador, in a bottom-up grassroots fashion. It then caught the attention of the president and was imposed nationwide, but the nation didn’t have that same level of education or enthusiasm about the asset, and the IMF has not been thrilled with El Salvador’s embrace of bitcoin as money, since it challenges their authority to some extent.

Bitcoin has been an important niche medium of exchange ever since Wikileaks resorted to accepting it in 2011. It’s volatile, but works when other methods are de-platformed. I continue to expect bitcoin to primarily be used as a portable store of value until its total value is much larger and it’s more widely held, contributing to lower volatility.

**Tether on Lightning**

During the recent Plan B conference in El Salvador, Paolo Ardoino, the CEO of Tether, and Elizabeth Stark, the CEO of Lightning Labs, announced that Tether’s USDt (the world’s biggest stablecoin) would be integrated into the Bitcoin ecosystem via Lightning Labs’ Taproot Assets protocol.

Tether’s [press release](https://tether.io/news/tether-brings-usdt-to-bitcoins-lightning-network-ushering-in-a-new-era-of-unstoppable-technology/) begins as follows:

> **San Salvador, El Salvador** – 30th January 2025 – Tether, the largest company in the digital assets industry, announced the integration of USDt into Bitcoin’s ecosystem, including both its base layer and the Lightning Network. Supported by a new Taproot-powered protocol, Taproot Assets, and developed by Lightning Labs, this integration combines Bitcoin’s unmatched decentralization and security with the speed and scalability of the Lightning Network, redefining how stablecoins can function within the Bitcoin ecosystem. 
>
> Once fully integrated, USDt will operate seamlessly on Bitcoin’s base layer and its layer 2 Lightning Network. This will enable high-speed, low-cost transactions while combining Bitcoin’s robust security and scalability. With USDt— the world’s largest stablecoin, serving over 350 million users—developers and users will enjoy Bitcoin’s unparalleled reliability alongside the efficiency and versatility of Lightning-enabled payments.

I’ve long held the view that bitcoin and stablecoins are the two killer use-cases of “blockchain technology”. This should make it easier for bitcoin-only wallets to incorporate stablecoins.

Three years ago, in an article that was generally critical of Ethereum, I stated that I nonetheless expect the market capitalization of stablecoins to increase:

> Stablecoins are particularly important, in my view. I’m bullish on the amount of money locked up in stablecoins.
>
> -“An Economic Analysis of Ethereum” January 2021

Since then, the total value of stablecoins has increased from around $30 billion to well over $200 billion, with much of that being on Ethereum and Tron. And I expect the total value of stablecoins to keep pushing up for a while. Most people who have sent a lot of international wire transfers and then try stablecoins can see the appeal. And many people trying to get access to dollars in high-inflation areas generally see the appeal too.

There are risks with stablecoins. They’re centralized, custodial issuers of digital eurodollars. The major ones are backed by dollar-denominated assets, and the U.S. government can sanction entities from using dollar-denominated assets. So, entities that hold a lot of Treasuries and issue dollar stablecoins have to keep U.S. policymakers happy enough. Stablecoins represent a new buyer of U.S. government debt, and generally also have to adhere to U.S. sanction laws where possible.

Stablecoins were originally born on Bitcoin’s Omni layer over a decade ago, but eventually mostly moved over to Ethereum, Tron, and other smart contract blockchains. Stablecoins are centralized, so their users don’t’ tend to mind using them over centralized blockchains if they’re fast and cheap. However, Taproot Assets can potentially pull some of the usage back to the Bitcoin network.

Some bitcoin enthusiasts are not thrilled with the idea of stablecoins returning to run at scale over the Bitcoin network. I generally observe that there’s a divide between developed-world and emerging-world bitcoin enthusiasts on this issue; people who already have access to dollar or euro bank accounts often fade the importance of stablecoins, whereas people in countless other countries often advocate for both bitcoin and stablecoins as being relevant for them in the wallet products they use on the ground where they are.

I think bitcoin veteran [Jameson Lopp](https://x.com/lopp)‘s recent generalized tweet summed it well. Taproot Assets is an open protocol, so users and companies that want to make use of it will do so, and those that don’t want to use it will not. In my view, I see a lot of potential demand for stablecoins within bitcoin-native wallets.

## Investment Analysis: Emphasizing Local and Cheap

As mentioned in the macro section of this report, I’m increasingly looking toward defensiveness in 2025 as tariff ripple effects work their way through the system, and as many U.S. equities are trading at high valuations while potentially facing a one-two punch of problems.

In response to heightened tariff tail risks, a useful start is to look for stocks that have less physical border exposure but are recently out of favor. And stocks with ideal tax-loss harvesting aftermaths, which is a topic I discussed in my prior report:

> Toward the end of the year, investors generally have some positions that have risen in value and others that have lost value. When those winning investments get sold, it generates a capital gains tax liability unless those assets are held in retirement accounts. And so, investors often try to pair the selling of winning investments with losing investments, so that the realized losses of the losers offsets some of the realized gains of the winners.
>
> The result of this is that if security A goes up for mostly fundamental reasons, and security B falls for mostly fundamental reason, then there is often additional selling pressure on security B at the end of the year in order to use those losses to offset gains elsewhere in a portfolio. There is “irrational” selling on top of the “rational” selling that already occurred, in other words. I use “irrational” in quotes because the year-end selling is actually pretty rational for the investor, but it results in price action that diverges from the fundamentals for a period of time.
>
> -January 19, 2025 premium report

**Adobe**

To me, Adobe (ADBE) comes to mind first:

![ADBE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-adbe-fastgraph.png)

The bearish view on Adobe is that open-source AI tools will erode the company’s market share.

The bullish view on Adobe is that the company’s category-leading product suite will continue to absorb various AI tools and thus expand the customer base that can use their products. Here was my view from over a year and a half ago when Adobe stock was down big:

> **10x Accessibility**
>
> The knee-jerk reaction to AI by investors is to invest in things that are needed for AI, such as the AI software and the chips that they use. That trade is already rather crowded at this point.
>
> Another theme to think about is to ask, “what products will become 10x more accessible thanks to AI?”
>
> Let’s use Adobe (ADBE) as an example. Photoshop is a semi-advanced program, in that it’s not exactly rocket science to use it, but it does take a considerable amount of time to learn to use it well.
>
> [This video](https://twitter.com/scottbelsky/status/1660992735040663553) shows how Adobe Photoshop is incorporating generative AI so that the user can easily make dramatic changes in seconds with basic text-based commands. The production of art, movies, software code, engineered designs, and all sorts of complex things are made more efficient by programs that can do many of the tedious and intricate underlying details of the work for the creator.
>
> In addition to making each Photoshop user more productive and powerful (they can get a lot more done per unit of time), it lowers the barrier of entry for using Photoshop. Just like how computers used to be difficult to use but became easier over time, all sorts of creator applications that have historically been moderately difficult are becoming way easier.
>
> The pessimistic case is that this type of creator AI assistance puts a percentage of creators out of work because each creator can do the work of what several creators used to do. However, that assumes that the same baseline amount of work needs to be done. The optimistic case is that by broadening access to this type of work, it grows the overall usage and demand for software-based creation. In other words, a similar or larger number of creators can do an order of magnitude more creation work, rather than the same amount of creation work being done by an order of magnitude fewer people.
>
> Imagine being able to pick up any creator application (videos, art, music, programming, 3D printing, engineering software, and so forth) and through verbal or written commands, intuitively use it to make what you want. This dramatically lowers the barrier of entry and allows the companies that make those creator applications able to sell far more subscriptions to far more users. Basically anyone can be a creator now or in a couple years for work or fun, and do what would have required an expert ten years ago. There will still be more experienced users than others, but the overall pie of who can effectively use advanced creator software can go up dramatically.
>
> ![ADBE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-adbe-fastgraph.png)
>
> -May 28, 2023 premium report

Adobe has an A+ credit rating and has more cash than debt. They continually buy back shares and are trading at price/earnings multiples that are cheaper than average. Paying a 23x earnings multiple for a software company that consists of nearly all equity and has decent growth seems to be worth the risk for potential disruption.

**Itau Unibanco**

On the other side of the risk spectrum, I’d reiterate Itau Unibanco (ITUB) as an interesting choice. It’s up 9% since I re-recommended it in my report from two weeks ago, and I think it still has room to grow.

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-itub-fastgraph.png)

Picking an emerging market stock might not seem the obvious choice during a potential trade war. However, position sizes matter, and I’m more concerned with high-flying equities that are structurally over-owned with no risks priced into them (e.g. Walmart at 40x earnings), than equities that already have a bunch of risks priced into them and are structurally under-owned.

Brazil and its assets continue to face [risks related to their fiscal budget](https://www.reuters.com/world/americas/brazil-meets-2024-primary-budget-target-critics-warn-challenges-2025-01-30/). However, as a base case I view that as likely to be more controlled than the pessimists expect, and its assets are priced cheaply.

**Chevron**

Major oil companies are in an interesting position here. Several of them have low valuations, strong balance sheets, and can potentially benefit from a pro-oil administration.

![CVX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-cvx-fastgraph.png)

Chevron (CVX) has 37 years of consecutive annual dividend growth and currently yields over 4%.

I continue to view oil producers as being past the big one-two punch of 2014 through 2020 when 1) U.S. shale oil disrupted global supply/demand equilibrium and then 2) COVID-19 temporarily destroyed demand to an unprecedented degree. That doesn’t mean that there aren’t risks ahead, but the oil market is a lot more mature than it was back then, and shale oil is more firmly part of the global supply/demand balance.

## Final Thoughts: Gold

Gold has reclaimed its late-October highs, chopping up to just under $2,800/oz.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-2-gold-chart.png)

This week, delays have materialized for gold deliveries in London. Traders have [reportedly](https://fortune.com/europe/2025/01/31/waiting-time-get-gold-out-bank-england-reportedly-octupled-trump-tariff-threats-spark-exodus-us/) moved tens of billions of dollars worth of gold from London to New York in anticipation of tariffs.

I continue to view gold as being more attractive than long-duration Treasuries, as more and more central banks reconsider the usefulness of gold as a debasement-resistant neutral reserve asset that they can hold in their own vaults.
