## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators: Focusing on the Treasury**

Domestic liquidity in the United States continues to be almost perfectly flat, and is likely what policymakers are managing for.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Treasury Department surprised market participants with their quarterly refunding announcement by substantially raising the percentage of upcoming debt issuance that will be done with T-bills. In a sign of fiscal dominance, this is probably the first time where a Treasury refunding announcement was more important for markets than a Fed FOMC policy update.

This is all kind of wonky but can be simplified as follows. A healthy sovereign bond market has plenty of liquidity (i.e. good trading volumes where even a large entity can buy or sell a ton of bonds without moving the price noticeably), and no problem issuing a sizable percentage of long duration bonds. However, the U.S. sovereign bond market is *not* very healthy due to such large ongoing deficits combined with the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet reduction. So, yields keep rising and are very volatile, bond liquidity is low, and small banks are being squeezed in the process. So, the Treasury decided to increase its issuance of short-duration T-bills for its upcoming deficit issuance beyond its normal T-bill ratio. This improves liquidity conditions, but around the margins is the kind of thing that emerging markets do when facing debt and currency problems.

The big pool of remaining liquidity available to buy Treasuries is the money that is still stuck in the reverse repo facility. All of this reverse repo money basically represents excess T-bill demand, and the Fed maintains the reserve repo facility so that the market doesn’t drive T-bill yields below the lower floor of their interest rate target. In contrast, there isn’t much demand for longer-duration bonds. And so as the Treasury issues more and more T-bills, it helps meet that excess T-bill demand that exists, and money starts to come out of the reverse repo facility and into holding T-bills. The Treasury then spends this money back into the economy and financial system.

The reverse repo facility is now under $1 trillion for the first time since 2021:

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-reverse-repo.png)

The ongoing drawdown of the reverse repo facility is putting liquidity back into the system, and how fast it is drained partially depends on how many T-bills the Treasury issues as a ratio of their total debt issuance. By increasing T-bill issuance, they continue to draw money out of the reverse repo facility. And at the current pace, it almost exactly offsets the Fed’s ongoing quantitative tightening (balance sheet reduction), to create a neutral domestic liquidity environment. Once it runs out, liquidity problems could become pretty severe and require a change of course by the Fed.

Why is this important? The reason is because the U.S. is facing a slow-moving fiscal crisis for the first time in decades. We’re running 7%-of-GDP deficits despite unemployment rates being very low, and since we are no longer in an environment of structurally falling interest rates, interest expense is starting to become a problem for the first time in decades.

![U.S. Interest Expense](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/october-2023-newsletter-interest-expense.png)

And that chart somewhat understates the issue. At $34 trillion in debt, and if interest rates just stay where they are, then as more and more public debt matures and gets refinanced at these higher rates, it will approach $1.7 trillion in annual interest expense, or 6% of GDP (a record high). A recession that forces the Fed to cut interest rates would alleviate some of the interest expense, but usually in recessions fiscal deficits rise sharply because tax revenue drops.

For most metrics, I prefer looking at figures relative to GDP or other proportional items, but I think looking at the absolute dollar amount of fiscal deficits is important for one key reason: large fiscal deficits can be inflationary and *fuel* nominal GDP growth. Analysts who only look at the deficit as a percentage of GDP will likely continue to be surprised by the sheer scale of the deficits and the sheer growth of nominal GDP.

![Fiscal Deficit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-fiscal-deficit.png)

**Liquidity Indicators: Focusing on the Fed**

Although the Treasury’s refunding announcement was more important during these past couple weeks than the Fed’s decision and some of Powell’s speeches, the Fed news was still important.

Fed Chairman Powell announced the Fed’s decision to pause interest rates for the moment. They implied to have no plans to cut them any time soon, and didn’t take future rate hikes off the table, but the overall remarks leaned somewhat dovish. He indicated that the sharp rise in long duration bond yields was doing some of his organization’s tightening for it, which gives the Fed more time to wait and assess incoming data.

And then, during the week after that statement, bond yields fell by 20+ basis points, and undid some of the tightening that Powell had referred to. This ironically might make them lean toward staying hawkish, or at least trying to talk a bit more hawkishly in speeches to re-ignite some of the tightening.

Senior loan officer surveys came out for Q4 2023, and across the board, they are easing up on their tightening of credit stands for the first time in a while:

![SLOOS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-sloos.png)

This survey checks with senior loan officers at banks to see if they are tightening lending standards or loosening them. The higher the number, the more that banks are actively tightening standards. If the number is negative, it means that on average they are loosening standards.

This recent reading showed that 1) banks are still tightening standards but that 2) a smaller percentage of them are tightening than previously. In other words the rate-of-change is toward loosening, even as the absolute situation is still tightening.

Does this tell us anything about the prospect for asset prices? Somewhat, yes. We have four prior recession points of data for this. The following chart shows the senior loan officer survey (inverted this time for visual clarity), vs the Wilshire 5000 total stock market index.

![SLOOS vs Wilshire](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-sloos-vs-wilshire.png)

-In 1990, the peak in credit tightening coincided with the market bottom.

-In 2001, the peak in credit tightening occurred way before the market bottom; there was still plenty of price declines to go. This was a mild recession by most metrics, but the equity bubble had been bigger than normal and so it took a longer time to deflate and fell by a bigger percentage compared to a normal recession.

-In 2008, the peak in credit tightening occurred a few months before the market bottom, when most of the drawdown was already finished. However, the real asset bubble this time was in real estate, and that didn’t bottom until early 2012.

-In 2020, the peak in credit tightening occurred after the market had already bottomed and was heading back up. This was a very fast recession due to the unusual impact of the lockdowns and stimulus.

Is there informational value to be derived from this relatively small three-decade dataset? Kind of. My takeaway is that when credit tightening starts to reverse a bit, that often marks the bottom or near-bottom of asset prices, except for when an asset class is in a particularly big bubble (like 2001 for equities or 2008 for housing).

I continue to be less bearish than many of the bears, and less bullish than many of the bulls. Unusually large fiscal deficits create an atypical environment, and are stimulatory and inflationary for asset prices and the economy even as monetary policy is very restrictive toward real estate, junk-rated companies, and small businesses. There are two very powerful forces offsetting each other.

I expect sideways choppy prices in many asset classes, within a very wide volatility band. If we look at CPI-adjusted equity prices, we can see that sometimes they take a long time to recover prior highs. It took nearly 20 years for CPI-adjusted equity prices to permanently surpass their early 1970s highs. It took about 15 years for CPI-adjusted equity prices to surpass their 2000 highs. How long will it take to surpass the 2021 highs? I would guess at least 5-10 years, but we’ll see.

![Wilshire CPI-Adjusted](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-wilshire-adjusted.png)

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The October 2023 payroll report came in weaker than expected, indicating a slowdown in employment. Notably, the unemployment rate ticked up to 3.9%. The low point for this business cycle was 3.4%, which means there has been a 0.5% increase. Historically, every time there is a 0.5% increase, that eventually leads into a recession.

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-unemployment.png)

Due to the several unusual attributes of this business cycle (lockdowns, stimulus, inflation, major deficits, military activity, and so forth), I wouldn’t necessarily stick too hard to “rules” like that, but they are notable nonetheless.

Some of the more cyclical aspects of the labor market such as overtime hours, temporary help workers, and transportation workers, also suggest labor market weakness.

The ISM manufacturing purchasing manager’s index started to look like it was finding a bottom, but the prior month’s print came in weaker than expected and drove it back down:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-pmi.png)

**Global Liquidity**

Global liquidity, defined here as the dollar-denominated global broad money supply (M2) continues to be mixed overall. This metric is based on both global broad money supply and the strength of the dollar relative to other top currencies. The majority of risk assets do better when liquidity is on an uptrend compared to when it is flat or down.

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-global-m2.png)

I think the absolute lows for global liquidity are likely in, but there’s no sign of a breakout in liquidity yet. We would likely need to see a significant reduction in the dollar index for that to occur, likely as a result of a Fed pivot, like ceasing its balance sheet reduction or trimming its interest rates. And I don’t think we’re at that stage yet, at least while there is still nearly $1 trillion in reverse repo money to feed domestic liquidity with.

In the meantime, I expect the absolute figure (blue line) to probably keep inching up, whereas the year-over-year percent change (orange line) is likely at a local peak right now due to base effects, since it is almost exactly one year from the deep low that the liquidity metric reached.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin broke out to new cycle highs again, briefly touching almost $38k and currently resting close to $37k.

So far, it still seems to be early in a potential bull market. The supply flow halving is coming up in April 2024, meaning that the number of new coins generated each day will be cut in half. And bitcoin spot ETFs are expected (but not certain) to be approved late this year or within Q1 2024 due to the SEC having lost a major appeals case regarding its reasons for continually denying spot ETFs.

And importantly, there is no sign of distribution yet. During major bull markets, especially during new all-time highs, some longer-term holders (people who have held on-chain for a year or more) tend to sell into that strength, which distributes coins more broadly. Many of them at that point are sitting on 5x or 10x or gains on their cost basis, and so they either rebalance some of their holdings or do something big with the money like buy a house. So far there has been none of that in this early part of the cycle, and a record high percentage of coins continue to be held by longer-term holders:

![HODL Distribution](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-hodl-distribution.png)

To be clear, I expect some major pullbacks in this next bull cycle. But what level they will drop from is unknown to me. It could drop from $37k and retest the breakout point of around $31k. Or it could soar to $45k and then retest back to current levels. And there are many other potential outcomes. I have no conviction on any given trading range.

However, I have a relatively high probability assessment that the next two years or so will be good for price overall. Although one can never be sure, most of the indicators point that way.

For my cold storage bitcoin, I don’t really plan to touch that for many years, unless my thesis on the asset structurally changes. And I [dollar-cost average](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into it.

**Other Coin News**

-Tether’s market cap has recovered to new all-time highs, surpassing its prior May 2022 peak. When Luna/Terra blew up in May 2022, Tether faced a massive $18 billion “bank run” where it met all customer redemptions. However, this bottomed shortly after the FTX implosion toward the end of the year, and since then, money has been flowing back into stablecoins including Tether. I continue to be bullish on stablecoin adoption unless the U.S. federal government decides to restrict them.

-BlackRock filed for a spot Ethereum ETF. There is already a futures-based Ethereum ETF, and based on court findings, it doesn’t make much sense for the SEC to allow a futures-based crypto product but not a spot-based crypto product. And thus an Ethereum ETF has a decent chance of being approved, but likely not as quickly as Bitcoin ETFs will be since they have been in the works for a lot longer. The news of this helped Ethereum rally, and find a local bottom in the ETHBTC ratio.

-Solana has done particularly well in terms of price this year, compared to competing chains like Ethereum and Avalanche. It is up about 6x from its major November 2022 bottom, and the SOLETH ratio is nearly back to where it was in May 2022 before Luna/Terra blew up. Solana was heavily endorsed by Sam Bankman-Fried, and some Solana-based assets (wrapped bitcoin and wrapped ether on Solana) were caught up in the FTX bankruptcy, and so the blockchain’s perception was heavily impaired by the fall of the SBF empire.

For that last point, I don’t have any particular view about Solana’s price but it’s getting a lot of attention on Twitter/X’s crypto community. The bigger debate on this topic is about scaling methods. Ethereum currently scales with several different roll-up layers, which have points of centralization, and which fractures liquidity/composability across different layers, but that keeps the base layer at least somewhat decentralized. Solana is more centralized and scalable on the base layer itself; the nodes require enterprise-grade infrastructure to run. For Bitcoin I certainly think the layered scaling approach is critical, because the decentralization of its base layer is *everything*. But for more programmable blockchains that optimize less strictly for decentralization, the question of how to best scale them is still being sorted out.

## Global Equity Valuations Deep Dive Analysis

This section continues my prior report’s theme of compiling international data to look for opportunities in global equity markets.

Here, price/earnings ratios and price/book ratios, along with the top three sectors of the stock market, are shown for 30+ countries. Specifically, the data comes from the most liquid ETF of each country, which for most of them is the iShares range of single-country ETFs:

![Global Valuations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-global-valuations.png)

This table can help investors find value, but comes with a lot of caveats.

-A country’s stock market doesn’t always closely reflect the underlying country. Some of them have major companies that operate globally.

-India is perpetually expensive in the modern era due to how much consistent growth it has, along with rather low leverage.

-Some countries like Argentina have their valuations increased by big weights to certain companies, like Mercado Libre (MELI) in Argentina’s case. Argentina is very cheap by many metrics if one goes and picks out the individually-traded ADRs. MELI is instead more of a growth stock with a presence in multiple countries.

-Some countries have their valuations decreased by big weights to certain companies, like Petrobras (PBR) in Brazil’s case. Brazil is indeed very cheap overall, but the typical equity is not quite as cheap as the 5.2x price/earnings ratio would imply.

-Many emerging markets including China, India, Brazil, Indonesia, and others became overvalued during the 2000s decade amid the so-called rise of the “BRICS”, and they’ve spent the past 15+ years deflating in valuation. In other words, these valuation differentials are not historically permanent; they go through major cycles.

**A Valuation Catalyst**

Right now, a lot of global exposure is concentrated in the United States. The United States has about 4% of global population, 15% of global GDP in terms of purchasing power parity, 25% of global GDP nominally, and yet 62% of the world’s stock market capitalization.

This is MSCI’s all-country weighting at the moment:

![All-Country Weights](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-country-weights.png)

These next charts show the S&P 500 performance ratio to MSCI EAFE (foreign developed) and MSCI Emerging Market indices. When the lines are going up, it means the S&P 500 is outperforming. When the lines are going down, it means those foreign equity markets are outperforming.

![Equity Ratios](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-equity-ratios.png)

In general, a lot of other countries have more highly-valued real estate markets than the United States. They tend to “monetize” their real estate more, and foreign buyers tend to monetize their real estate as well. This includes Canada, China, and Australia in particular, but also many European countries. In the United States, due to the breadth and depth of our capital markets, we tend to “monetize” our equities instead. We put more of our money into equities, and foreigners tend to be more interested in buying our equities than our real estate.

But even compared to that higher baseline focus on equities in the United States, a lot of pools of capital around the world are currently heavily exposed to the United States to a higher degree than average. This historically has tended to go through certain cycles based on major shifts in fiscal and monetary policy, which results in equity valuations and the dollar index pushed up together.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-dxy.png)

In the late 1990s, the U.S. had peak labor participation, a tech boom, and a budget surplus. Capital poured into the country, driving up equity valuations and the strength of the dollar. This reached a climax with the Dotcom bubble, and then when the U.S. encountered a recession and the Fed eased monetary policy, these capital flows began to sharply reverse. The 2000s decade ended up being a major boom for emerging markets. By the 2010s, it shifted back to the U.S. again, with the rise of all the big tech companies to become multi-trillion dollar powerhouses, as well as the rise of shale oil which reduced the overall trade deficit of the United States even as the goods/merchandise trade deficit continued to grow.

Capital flows tend to create vicious cycles or virtuous cycles for emerging market countries in particular. As more global capital crowds into the United States, it pushes up U.S. equity valuations and strengthens the U.S. dollar. The strong dollar then directly harms emerging market countries that have a lot of dollar-denominated debt, squeezing them and making them even less attractive to invest in. Everything gets more and more one-sided, with capital flows feeding off of capital flows, until there is some major shift. And then if capital starts coming out of the U.S. and goes elsewhere, it weakens the dollar, which then eases the debt burdens of emerging market countries with dollar-denominated debt and makes them even more attractive to invest into. Capital flows feed off of capital flows in the other direction.

![Debt and Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-em-debt-and-reserves.png)

What would make this cycle shift the next time? The short answer is that I don’t know for sure; I admittedly expected it to shift by now, and so the cycle has already gone on longer than I expected.

My base case is that when the U.S. is no longer able to maintain its blend of loose fiscal policy (large deficits) and tight monetary policy (shrinking Fed balance sheet and positive real rates), that will be the trigger for a reversal of capital flows into some of these cheaper markets. There’s only so much Treasury supply that can be absorbed by the market. Once the reverse repo facility is drained, for example, I would expect to see an event similar to 2019 where the Fed had to cease its quantitative tightening and return to a baseline rate of balance sheet expansion. I think if the pandemic and related lockdown/stimulus had not happened in early 2020, then that late 2019 period might have been the top of the cycle for U.S. equity outperformance.

The [IMF’s growth outlook](https://www.imf.org/external/datamapper/NGDP_RPCH@WEO/OEMDC/ADVEC/WEOWORLD) shown in the map below is not something I would consider to be super reliable, but it’s worth keeping an eye on. In terms of growth, India leads the pack among major investable countries. Indonesia is growing quickly. Brazil is a bit of a stand-out as well.

![IMF Growth Outlook](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-12-imf-growth-map.png)

**Overall Takeaways**

I don’t necessarily expect the catalyst to occur in the next six months, but I continue to view it as worthwhile to have international equity exposure. Most pools of capital are already very heavily crowded into U.S. equity markets.

A stand-out by many metrics is Brazil, in my view. One must of course manage their position size carefully.

The country has a manageable amount of dollar-denominated debt, is running a trade surplus, and is very inexpensive. It sits at the intersection of many potential shifts. If capital flows indeed go more toward commodity and commodity-related investments, then Brazil should get a boost, and any sort of shift away from the dollar should give Brazil a financial tailwind by weakening their dollar-denominated debt. In addition, in terms of tail risk management, Latin America broadly is out of the line of fire for most current or potential military conflicts (Eastern Europe, Middle East, and South China Sea).

Brazil’s largest bank stock is trading at 7.4x estimated 2024 earnings, and hasn’t run into the same duration problems as U.S. banks have. The past decade and a half has been a lost decade for it, as it was for most emerging market banks, but I expect the next 5+ years to be better, especially if there is a big reversal of global capital flows to end the vicious cycle against them.

## Final Thoughts:

With conflicting signals between liquidity, the business cycle, lending activities, and fiscal deficits, my level of uncertainty over the next six months is quite high. What I am more confident in is in identifying assets that are attractively priced with a 3-5 year view.

-On the defensive side, T-bills, 2-year Treasuries, intermediate-term TIPS, and gold all seem relatively attractive.

-For moderately volatile assets, a diverse mix of U.S. and international equities continues to be relatively attractive. My emphasis is on profitable companies across multiple sectors.

-On the volatile side of the spectrum, bitcoin, energy stocks, and select emerging markets (e.g. Brazilian equities) continue to be attractive in my view.
