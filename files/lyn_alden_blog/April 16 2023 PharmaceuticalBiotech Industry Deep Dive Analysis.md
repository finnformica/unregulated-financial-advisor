## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

The overall U.S. liquidity situation continues to trend sideways:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Fed balance sheet shown below, which is the biggest component of that domestic liquidity metric, remains in a channel above its lows but is coming down from its recent highs. I expect it to trend sideways for a period of time in a choppy range until it eventually has to increase again.

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-fed-balance-sheet.png)

When it draws down, it pushes liquidity back into the banking system. We’ll see what kind of tax surge they might get next week with tax deadlines in play, but overall, it doesn’t look good. The “final thoughts” section at the end of this report dives into recent fiscal deficit numbers and the debt ceiling impasse.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy has been decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, and is beginning to reach stall speed or becoming mildly recessionary in many metrics. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as several of them are now), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms.

![Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Gold and Oil**

Gold has been testing its prior all-time highs in dollar terms that it touched in mid-2020. It hasn’t decisively broken out yet, which I would define as settling firmly above $2,100 and continuing to trend up from there.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-gold.png)

I view gold’s price action as a likely cup-and-handle pattern. The price trend combined with macroeconomic factors leads me to expect gold to break out this year well into new highs, as a base case.

The Fed’s tightening cycle being somewhat constrained by bank liquidity limitations, foreign central bank gold buying (rather than U.S. Treasury buying), and gaping fiscal deficits, are all supportive of the gold price going forward.

Oil prices have broken back above $80. The United States never refilled its Strategic Petroleum Reserve when prices went sub-$80, and OPEC+ subsequently announced supply cuts, which may result in the United States missing its opportunity to refill.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-wtic.png)

Due to tightness on oil’s supply side combined with inflationary fiscal policy, I remain structurally bullish on oil and oil producers for the rest of the decade. Ever since the price became euphoric a year ago, combined with the ongoing economic deceleration that has been happening for a while, I have been tactically uncertain about oil in the intermediate-term, but I believe it’s starting to firm up and put in a price bottom. This remains an area of high uncertainty at the moment.

Meanwhile, high-quality oil producers are extremely profitable at current oil prices, let alone higher oil prices, and they are cheaply valued with generally strong balance sheets. There has been no big capex cycle, and no big wave of investor over-allocation to the sector. So, I view the oil bull market as still being in its early stages in the structural sense.

Gasoline prices have been rising since the start of the year, and crude oil bottomed in March and is no longer making lower-lows. If energy prices break out, it will put pressure on the current trend of disinflation. I don’t know if it will happen this year or next year (it partially depends on how strongly policymakers push the economy towards recession to try to keep a lid on inflation), but I think it’s likely to be a key catalyst in future waves of inflation after this current disinflationary trend is finished.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has broken above $30k and briefly touched $31k before consolidating back into the $30k+ range.

After such an explosive start to 2023, bitcoin is becoming mildly overbought (RSI > 70) on the daily and weekly charts. On a monthly chart, it’s nowhere near overbought, and it’s getting close to a bullish MACD cross-over.

![BTC Monthly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-btc-monthly.png)

I have no strong view on the next several months, but the combination of on-chain indicators, ongoing ecosystem development, the macroeconomic environment, and the halving cycle makes me bullish over the next two years and beyond.

For most people who want some bitcoin as part of their asset mix, I think [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) or rebalancing a slice of a portfolio into it is the optimal way to do it rather than trying to trade it too much.

**Ethereum Withdraw Unlock**

The Ethereum network performed another hard fork to allow for stakers to unlock their ETH. Prior to this, people could stake their ETH but not unlock it.  Now it can be unlocked over time in phases. Approximately 15% of Ethereum supply is staked.

Now that staking has been unlocked, more people are withdrawing than depositing, which makes sense after a multi-year lockup period that many of them had:

![Ethereum Stakes](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-etherum-stakes.png)

*Chart Source: [Ethereum Shapella Dashboard](https://query.nansen.ai/public/dashboards/Hk93n66vsO0uvycfui8ypF2xcpNhpraxfwX5AWZJ)*

The biggest percentage of stake withdraws are coming from the Kraken exchange, because the SEC applied securities regulation on them against staking. Some withdrawn stake gets re-deposited elsewhere, while other stakes get withdrawn for good.

Whether intentional or not, the fact that developers made it so that stakers couldn’t unlock during a bear market and can now unlock in what appears to be a renewed bull market or at least a consolidation, has likely been constructive for price action. People were basically forced to hold through the bear market. Ethereum’s price action performed well in recent weeks, and has an upward trajectory.

During March, bitcoin fiercely outperformed other cryptocurrencies as a flight-to-safety trade. Here in April, Ethereum and other coins are playing some catch-up. I saw a lot of negative sentiment on altcoins back in March, so I wrote that I don’t necessarily share that view for this year:

> While many people think bitcoin’s recent outperformance compared to other coins is instructive for future trends over the next year, I’m hesitant to read too much into small moves between coin prices like this. I don’t have a firm view on other cryptocurrencies over the next year, since their movements often depend heavily on liquidity and hype. From a fundamental perspective, I remain constructive on bitcoin and stablecoins, and remain less interested in other digital assets.
>
> -March 19th report

Bitcoin has what seems to be a firm price bottom in place, which is supported by attractive on-chain indicators. Tether is nearly back to its all-time high market capitalization, as dollars flow back into the ecosystem. I expect some other digital assets like ETH to do pretty well if there is a broad liquidity-driven bull market, although there are a lot of risks around them including ongoing regulatory crackdowns related to unregistered security offerings.

The challenge with Ethereum is that it has to re-invent new narratives in each cycle. Previously it was about ICOs, then it became about DeFi, and then NFTs. And now that interest rates are higher, it is harder for venture firms to raise capital. I expect to see more ICO, DeFi and NFT action in the next cycle as speculators leverage up, but a new theme like “digital identity” may be needed for them to have another huge bullish cycle.

On the other hand, ETH’s price is somewhat supported by deflationary monetary policy, so any new demand would have a pretty big upward force on price. In order to achieve the deflationary policy, they had to give up proof-of-work and maintain higher average transaction fees, with the former being a security/decentralization risk and the latter opening the door for cheaper competitors to compete for market share.

**Lightning News: Lightspark**

Many bitcoin-related companies are small and scrappy, with relatively low levels of funding and lean business operations. The focus tends to be on utility: wallet devices, payment infrastructure, apps, brokerages, collaborative custody, and so forth.

The altcoin ecosystem is where the multi-billion venture capital has been focused for the past several years, because it was heavily focused on trading, leveraging, play-to-earn gaming, and all sorts of similar speculative-driven investments (which tend not to hold up well in a bear market or with higher interest rates). A company like FTX had no problem raising billions of dollars of capital, for example. And Crypto.com could hire Matt Damon for commercials and sponsor the UFC for a 10-year contract.

In the Bitcoin-only ecosystem, there have only been a handful of truly large capital raises. And one of those exceptions was Lightspark; they raised $175 million last year in a pre-product series A round.

A decade ago, the current co-founder and CEO of Lightspark, David Marcus, was the president of PayPal (PYPL). From there, he went onto Facebook (now Meta Platforms, META) where he served for years as the Head of Messenger and then Head of Novi, which was Facebook’s Libra/Diem attempt to build a global stablecoin payment business that ran into regulatory headwinds.

In early 2022, Marcus pivoted and co-founded Lightspark to build on the Bitcoin Lightning network instead. This was an interesting choice considering how small the Lightning network was (and still is). He was convinced by the technical aspects of the network, and the opportunity set that the network provides. Due to his professional background, he was able to raise a huge capital round and begin finding product-market fit.

The reason I bring this all up is that this past Tuesday, Lightspark revealed their new product offering that they built: [enterprise-grade Lightning node infrastructure](https://www.lightspark.com/), which adds to the existing Lightning node infrastructure product mix that is currently available from companies like Voltage, Breez, Blockstream, and others.

Lightspark’s product suite currently comes in three parts. Here are the excerpts from their [product page](https://www.lightspark.com/products):

## Pharmaceutical/Biotech Industry Deep Dive Analysis

The healthcare sector, broadly speaking, is a useful portfolio segment due to its recession-resistant characteristics. Individuals and governments don’t tend to cut healthcare spending during recessions, even as other more cyclical sectors contract.

During raging bull markets, healthcare stocks will likely underperform the broad equity market. During bear markets, they tend to hold up better than the broad equity market.

Due to various macro factors including valuation, inflation, interesting rates, and capital flows, I don’t expect very strong S&P 500 performance during the 2020s decade, especially in inflation-adjusted terms. This gives an opportunity for more value-oriented and dividend-yielding companies, including a number of pharmaceutical and biotech companies, to grind out decent returns relative to the major indices.

**Bristol-Myers Squibb**

One of my preferred healthcare stocks continues to be Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMY). Their acquisition of Celgene in 2019 strengthened their pipeline against various exclusivity expirations, which has allowed them to maintain steady growth.

![BMY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-bmy-fastgraph.png)

They pay a dividend yield of well over 3%. Over the past four years since their Celgene acquisition, they have increased their dividend by an average of 8.5% per year. They also have been mildly reducing their share count.

![BMY YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-bmy-ychart.png)

**Amgen**

Amgen (AMGN) has been one of the largest and most successful biotech companies, and is now over four decades old. They have a diverse drug pipeline and more than a decade of consecutive annual dividend growth.

In the first quarter of this year, Amgen announced plans to lay off about 3% of its workforce, citing higher interest rates and some pressures on drug prices. Analysts are expecting a flat year for profits, before the company continues its general growth trend. The valuation is roughly in line with its historical average.

![AMGN FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-amgn-fastgraph-2.png)

While Amgen has continued its streak of dividend increases and currently yields over 3%, it has paused net share buybacks for the past year, which has been a significant contributor to the slower per-share growth.

![AMGN YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-amgn-ychart.png)

**Horizon Medical ETF**

Other than covering some major diversified blue-chips from a value investor’s perspective like BMY, AMGN, JNJ, and NVS, the healthcare sector is one where I have no edge in terms of individual company differentiation.

Therefore, I don’t pick emerging biotech companies or opine about one company over another.

Horizon Kinetics recently converted a long-running mutual fund into an ETF format, called the **Horizon Medical ETF (MEDX)**. It was originally launched back in 1999, and from that point until the end of 2022, it has had 8.96% annualized returns compared to 6.83% for the S&P 500, and with only two-thirds of the volatility of the S&P 500. It has had the same two portfolio managers since inception.

I spoke last week with one of the portfolio managers, Paul Abel, to better understand his process, due to his experience doing it for over two decades successfully. Here were the main points:

-The fund invests in three types of companies. The first type is the big, blue-chip diversified pharmaceutical companies. These are high-ROI compounders with massive R&D budgets, and Abel looks for ones that can divest non-core businesses and accumulate existing products into their company. The four healthcare stocks that I primarily invest in, referring to BMY, AMGN, JNJ, and NVS are all in the top ten holdings of the fund.

-The second type is profitable middle-stage profitable biotech companies. These have decent growth profiles for long-term compounding, and sometimes make decent acquisition targets.

-The third type is early-stage biotech, which can be considered “publicly-traded venture”. Abel filters for ones that have enough cash on the balance sheet to get their products to the finish line, with drugs taking $500M-$1.2B to get through FDA approval depending on the type. They don’t need all of that cash on hand at once, but they need to have enough cash to comfortably get to the next stage to raise more capital. As with the venture approach as a whole, most bets won’t work out but the few that do have upwards of 10x or 100x potential. Filtering out ones that don’t have the baseline requirements improves the overall risk/reward of the portfolio.

-AI is in the early stages of providing tangible benefits to pharmaceutical/biotech R&D. An example is protein-folding simulations; what would have taken months of research can now be done in a day. A lot of the R&D for new products is guessing and iteration, and AI can rapidly accelerate that part of the R&D process to more quickly get on the right path.

The combination of the track record and the process makes me like this fund. The 0.85% expense fee is fair in my view, especially considering it’s a sector that I don’t have the right experience to participate in other than through the major blue-chips. In terms of macroeconomic analysis, combined with keeping an eye on medical technology breakthroughs (gene therapy, the impact of AI on research and diagnosis, etc.) I view healthcare as an attractive sector to hold for the next decade as part of a diversified portfolio.

**Key Risk: U.S. Regulatory Changes**

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 gave the U.S. federal government more authority to negotiate drug prices, and includes other measures to try to rein in ever-increasing healthcare spending. In terms of estimated magnitude, the Congressional Budget Office expects that the drug provisions within this legislation will reduce the fiscal deficit by $237 billion over ten years. [Here is a good overview](https://www.kff.org/medicare/issue-brief/explaining-the-prescription-drug-provisions-in-the-inflation-reduction-act/) of the drug pricing portion of this act.

Most healthcare companies do not face the same type of cyclical/recession risk that most companies do, and so regulatory risks are instead their main concern. Healthcare companies rely on patents to charge high prices to recoup their R&D costs with a profit, and many customers of healthcare companies rely heavily on government payments to buy the products, which makes it a regulated and political industry.

Overall, I view the sector as already being reasonably-priced with the Inflation Reduction Act in mind. The split nature of Congress makes it unlikely for much larger changes to come through within an investable time horizon, and so for now I consider healthcare companies to likely remain highly profitable companies.

## Final Thoughts: Deficits and the Debt Ceiling

The U.S. Treasury’s fiscal year starts in October of each year, so it is shifted one quarter ahead of the calendar year. They posted fiscal results for the end of March, which for them marks the half-way point in their fiscal year.

The results were not pretty: it was a $1.1 trillion deficit in six months. In fact when showing the diagram, the U.S. Treasury Department reduces the size of the deficit bar by about half, which makes it look less bad, even though the rest of the chart is mostly proportional:

![Receipts and Outlays](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-receipts-outlays.png)

*Chart Source: [U.S. Treasury Department](https://home.treasury.gov/data/receipts-outlays)*

[The Happy Hawaiian](https://twitter.com/ThHappyHawaiian) on Twitter posted a good set of custom charts showing how this compares to prior years (and by extension, also shows the impact that April’s tax season generally has on deficits). He uses calendar years rather than U.S. federal government fiscal years.

![Calendar Year Deficit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-16-calendar-year-deficit.png)

The numbers for 2023 were worse than 2022 both in terms of receipts and outlays. The Treasury received less revenue from taxes, while also spending more.

And that’s a big thing that many monetary hawks don’t seem to take into account: the U.S. economy is so financialized, that when asset prices are not going up, tax revenue tends to suffer. Therefore, when the Fed tries to rein in speculative behavior with higher interest rates and quantitative tightening, it blows out the deficit both in terms of higher interest expense and in terms of less tax revenue. That means more Treasury issuance, which crowds out private sector balance sheets, and eventually requires monetization by the Fed.

Historically, when sovereign debt as a percentage of GDP reaches over 100%, especially when combined with a structural trade deficit, it’s highly unlikely that government bonds will be paid back in terms of full purchasing power. When debts are denominated in a country’s own currency, the most common path is through persistent/recurring inflation and negative real rates.

In other words, the Fed’s attempt to tighten monetary policy, while admirable, is running into a freight train of such a levered system:

![Nope Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-nope.png)

Someone is going to have to absorb all of that debt issuance. Currently the foreign sector is not buying much. Banks in the United States, which were big buyers in recent years, aren’t in a position to buy much anymore, and have been net sellers recently. Lately, U.S. retail investors have been the primary buyers, and less Treasuries in general have been issued due to the debt ceiling; funding for the deficit has come mainly from drawing down the existing balance of the Treasury’s general account.

The next year is going to be very interesting. The U.S. federal government has to deal with a debt ceiling impasse, and then if/when that is eventually resolved, the Treasury has to refill its Treasury general account. But with banking sector liquidity so low, it’s hard to suck that much liquidity out of the system without Fed support. The Fed, however, is highly focused on fighting inflation. There are likely to be some awkward decisions over the next year, where hawkish intentions run into a wall of reality.

Much like how the Bank of England had to buy some bonds in autumn 2022 in the middle of 10% year-over-year headline inflation, there are likely to be periods ahead where the Fed has to buy some bonds despite high inflation. The lending facilities they are doing now are an indirect way of helping with that, but after the debt ceiling is resolved and the Treasury resumes massive issuance of Treasuries, they may need to take other measures to support that issuance. A way to avoid that is for the Treasury to specifically issue a lot of T-bills rather than longer duration securities and therefore suck liquidity out of reverse repos, but they rarely change their average debt duration that significantly. Additionally, that’s currently the highest-yielding part of the curve, and so it would be the least profitable way to finance the deficit.

To put it simply, most likely either the Treasury Department or the Fed likely will have to perform some unusual actions within the next year, due to the mismatch between expected government debt issuance and the current state of financial liquidity.

I continue to view a three pillar portfolio as an ideal way to navigate this environment. The three pillars are 1) cash and cash-equivalents, 2) commodities, commodity producers, and hard monies, and 3) profitable equities at reasonable valuations.
