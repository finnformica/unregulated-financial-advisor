# Market Update: Stocks vs Bonds Checkup

*October 13, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report looks at liquidity indicators, the U.S. economy, and analyzes the importance of the fact that banks are buying Treasuries again in aggregate.

The digital asset section looks at bitcoin price action and MicroStrategy’s ongoing bitcoin accumulation and the premium over its net asset value that fuels it.

The investment analysis section looks at how scattered some large cap equity valuations are in comparison to each other, and re-examines the relative attractiveness of stocks vs bonds in general. It then finishes with a note on gold and the dollar index.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to move in a rangebound way, which I expect to continue through the rest of 2024.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-domestic-liquidity.png)

This is partially because the reverse repo component is now draining very slowly:

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-reverse-repo.png)

And partially because the Treasury General Account is pinned right around its target level of $700-$850 billion:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-tga.png)

Meanwhile, Michael Howell of CrossBorder Capital recently [shared on social media](https://twitter.com/crossbordercap/status/1845073191804301798) that his global liquidity indicators for central banks around the world are in easing mode in aggregate:

![Crossborder Capital](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-crossborder-capital-tweet.png)

We’re in a rate cutting cycle globally, and overall that’s generally good for scarce assets that have to compete with cash-equivalents and the yields that they pay.

**U.S. Economy**

We don’t have GDP data for Q3 yet, but Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow estimator continues to suggest that the quarter ran at a 3%+ annualized real rate of growth:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-gdpnow.png)

The consumer price index for September came out this week. It was a bit above consensus expectations across the board, but still down year-over-year. Core CPI (red line below) is proving to be stickier:

![CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-cpi.png)

This gap between headline inflation and core inflation is mainly because energy prices have been the inflation release valve over the past year. This brings down headline CPI but doesn’t affect Core CPI:

![CPI Categories](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-cpi-categories.png)

In addition, U.S. manufacturing data for September came out, and continues to show significant weakness. This data series showed signs of stabilizing in 2023 but then throughout 2024 has continued to languish:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-ism-pmi.png)

These charts continue to support a theme of fiscal dominance. Several areas of the economy are weak, but the U.S. is running [$1.8 trillion annual deficits](https://www.reuters.com/world/us/cbo-estimates-18-trillion-us-deficit-fiscal-2024-largest-after-covid-2024-10-08/). This money primarily pours out into Social Security payments, Medicare payments, Defense payments, and interest payments, and then in various ways that contributes to consumptive power, either by those directly receiving those benefits (e.g. Social Security recipients and interest expense recipients), or by executives and employees of companies in fiscally-supported sectors (e.g. healthcare and defense) that can then go out and buy various goods and services from other industries.

Since mid-September, there has been a pretty sharp jump in long-duration Treasury yields, which I think reflects higher inflation expectations and a fiscally-supported economy. Basically, the bond market got a bit too bearish on the economy there for a moment, and needed to bounce back a bit:

![10 Year Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-10yr-yield.png)

My base case is to see a choppy curve re-steepening, from a combination of the short end coming down a bit, and the long end chopping around or potentially moving higher:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-yield-curve.png)

And importantly, this is despite the fact that banks are buying Treasuries again. For a while, they went through a stretch where they were underwater on Treasuries and deleveraging their books of them. But now they’re back to accumulation, and I expect this to continue:

![Bank Treasuries](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-bank-treasuries.png)

When banks aren’t buying Treasuries, it means various non-bank entities are buying them instead, and that can cause a crowding-out effect where they are buying government debt rather than buying other assets or making other loans. But when banks are buying Treasuries via fractional-reserve balance sheets, this is generally net positive for liquidity. It can be pretty similar to quantitative easing.

To put it another way, most types of non-bank entities operate on much lower levels of total leverage than banks. So when they buy something, it generally means they’re *not* buying something else. However, because banks have extra protections and can lever a lot more, when they buy assets or make loans, it’s not anywhere near a 1:1 crowding out effect, and can be more of a total pro-liquidity effect.

Many economic and financial bears point to how weak the bank lending environment is to consumers and businesses, and they’re correct:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-bank-loans.png)

However, the banks buying Treasuries *are* lending, but to the government rather than the private sector. This trickles out into the private sector via Social Security payments, Medicare, Defense spending, interest expense, and other items. And thus a lot of consumers are indirect recipients of this bank-financing of government spending. Individuals and entities that are on the receiving side of fiscal deficits, directly or indirectly, are pretty well supported. Other areas are experiencing ongoing economic malaise.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Bitcoin continues to chop around in a range since March. As I’ve written about before, I consider it more likely that it will eventually break up out of this range than break down from this range, but I don’t have any firm view on timing. The good news is that the price action has stopped putting in lower-highs, which I think is beginning to turn into a good sign:

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-btcusd.png)

Most on-chain valuation indicators look reasonably safe as well:

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-btc-mvrv.png)

**MicroStrategy’s Surge**

MicroStrategy (MSTR) has been in the newsletter portfolio since they adopted their bitcoin strategy in August 2020, and they’ve been one of the few stocks in the world to outperform Nvidia (NVDA) during that timeframe.

Based on evaluation of net asset value or “NAV”, it is now overvalued by most metrics. As a back-of-the-envelope calculation, they have about $16 billion worth of bitcoin at current prices, a $1 billion software company business, and around $4 billion in debt that can be subtracted from that. So that’s $13 billion in rather tangible net asset value. Investors can choose to add a few billion to that figure to represent future optionality for new pursuits that the operating business might get into.

In comparison to that, the market capitalization is $43 billion now. This means that investors are paying in the ballpark of about 3x net asset value for the company. When they had their massive price surge in March 2024, I warned about the high NAV premium, and trimmed some of the position out. However, I haven’t trimmed all of it, and keep letting the rest of the position run. It touched new highs on Friday.

![MSTR Stock](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-mstr-stock.png)

Some traditional finance long/short shops have decided to arbitrage this, which I think is dangerous. Back in March 2024, [for example](https://twitter.com/KerrisdaleCap/status/1773337285062172793), Kerrisdale Capital went long BTC and short MicroStrategy, citing overvaluation. Well, here in October 2024, MicroStrategy has substantially outperformed BTC since March despite the high starting premium to NAV. Entities that short MSTR just keep getting blown out.

My way of arbitraging it is simply to say that from a risk/reward perspective, I prefer buying direct bitcoin versus MicroStrategy when the premium is this high like it has been since March. It feels safer owning the asset 1:1 when that disconnect grows to this degree. But it doesn’t mean the disconnect has to narrow anytime soon. It might or it might not.

And MicroStrategy directly benefits from this high premium to NAV. They can keep issuing more convertible notes or other types of capital, and use it to buy bitcoin, which increases their amount of bitcoin per share. (In contrast, if MicroStrategy were trading below NAV, then issuing equity or convertible notes would reduce the amount of bitcoin per share). In other words, if equity is overvalued, it means that their cost of equity capital is very low. Many companies don’t have a direct way to harness that cheap cost of capital, but MicroStrategy does, by issuing more of it and using it to buy bitcoin.

Back in 2020, there was a major Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) arbitrage trade. GBTC was trading at a premium to NAV, and so various entities would arbitrage that spread with leverage, and it accelerated how many bitcoin the GBTC fund would purchase, which helped drive the price of BTC up into early 2021. But eventually GBTC fell to a big discount to NAV as there became more and more ways to easily buy bitcoin with lower fees than GBTC, and therefore many entities went underwater due to arbitraging that premium with leverage. Three Arrows Capital was a major player in this strategy, eventually blew up, and that was a big piece for why so many CeFi lenders blew up in 2022.

But unlike GBTC, MicroStrategy has access to permanent equity capital. And unlike GBTC, there is not a big position among financial firms in terms of going long MicroStrategy and short BTC, whereas back when GBTC had a premium, there were a lot of entities like Three Arrows Capital going long GBTC and short BTC.

What MicroStrategy is doing here makes absolute sense. The market is giving them a big premium, and so by all accounts they’re just going to keep arbitraging it for their shareholders while meeting market demand for its securities. They’ll likely keep issuing more and more capital and suck in more and more bitcoin until the trade is exhausted and premiums over NAV diminish. The company now has over 250,000 bitcoins, compared to over 600,000 that GBTC had at its peak. And I think they could add tens or even hundreds of thousands more in the years ahead.

Part of the reason this is possible is because so few other entities are doing it. Metaplanet of Japan is now doing it at a small starting scale in public markets. Some private companies are doing it, but they have less access to capital markets compared to MicroStrategy as a publicly-traded stock on the Nasdaq. Some publicly-traded stocks are dabbling in holding some bitcoin, but not much. And because so many other publicly-traded companies and their leaders are hesitant, the vast majority of the arbitrage goes toward MicroStrategy.

And now there’s a liquidity network effect in play, which helps prop up the premium to NAV. MicroStrategy has more average daily volume than any spot bitcoin ETF, including the one issued by BlackRock (IBIT). And more volume than the big bitcoin miners. That’s pretty remarkable. And they have a liquid options market as well. For large institutions, MicroStrategy is a very liquid entity to arbitrage compared to other bitcoin entities. And MicroStrategy can reach into the bond market as well, which is what they’ve been doing with their convertible notes.

It’s important to consider investor mandates. There are portfolio managers that have a mandate to only own stocks, for example, and if a portfolio manager happens to be bullish on bitcoin, then a few entities like MicroStrategy and some miners are the only avenues for them to express that view. They can’t buy a spot bitcoin ETF or other similar instrument. Similarly, there are bond managers that happen be bullish on bitcoin, but have a mandate to only own bonds. So they can buy MicroStrategy convertible bonds to capture potential upside when the bitcoin price surges, within the context of a bond fund. There is no financial entity that has been identifying and meeting this need for certain types of security mandates better than MicroStrategy, and now they’ve reached a rather critical mass of liquidity, which helps the premium to NAV stick around for a while. It can remain until they’ve kind of fully tapped the total demand out there for stock-only or bond-only types of funds, as well as certain other strategies like vol-selling funds.

This is a recursive flywheel that I will continue to monitor. A lot of people monitor ETF flows, which I think are important, but this specific combination of high premium to NAV and thus the incentive to keep issuing capital to buy more bitcoin, creates a big vacuum that will keep sucking up bitcoin until it doesn’t anymore.

At the current issuance rate, about 170,000 new bitcoins are created per year. That number will get cut in half in 2028 with the automatic block subsidy halving that occurs every 210,000 blocks, but let’s focus on the next few years. MicroStrategy alone could suck in 50,000 to 100,000+ per year if the premium remains so high and there remains such significant demand for its capital.

Once the premium evaporates, either temporarily or permanently, I’ll highlight this fact in a report. I highlighted when GBTC’s premium disappeared in early 2021 as a potentially big catalyst, and I plan to do the same for MicroStrategy.

In my view, one of the best ways to play this is simply to buy bitcoin and hold for the next few years. When MicroStrategy’s premium to NAV gets high, I become more hesitant about owning MicroStrategy in size relative to bitcoin, but there’s just not much of a catalyst to try to short it and expect a gap closure within any specific period of time. Instead, I think it’s better to ask what that means for bitcoin itself, since it’s at a scale that can really affect it.

## Investment Updates: The Middle of the Road

Rather than delve into a new stock for this report, I’m going to use the investment section to review the U.S. stock market as a whole and provide updates for some select portfolio companies.

**Observation 1: Scattered Valuations**

The S&P 500 is such a powerful index that it’s often considered as one big unit, and U.S. stocks are often described as overvalued or undervalued as though they were one big group.

But when one looks under the surface, there are plenty of stocks that seem expensive, and others that seem quite reasonable. And some of the patterns have kind of broken down lately; it can’t just be described as one industry doing well and another industry doing poorly. Expensive stocks and cheap stocks in the market now seem quite scattered around.

I’ve used Costco (COST) as a repeated example of overvaluation since it trades at more than 50x earnings, so I’ll skip that one for now. Instead I’ll use its big cousin: Walmart (WMT).

![WMT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-wmt-fastgraph.png)

In mid-2022, Walmart was trading for under 19x earnings. Now it’s trading at over 33x earnings. That shows how erratic the market can be; the swings in valuation for a six decade old mega-cap retailer probably shouldn’t be that wide over the course of a little more than two years, and yet they are.

In contrast, let’s look at Alphabet (GOOGL). The “Magnificent Seven” stocks are today’s version of FAANG or similar acronyms to keep describing the power of the mega-cap tech stocks. And they’re often described as expensive. Well, this member of the Mag7 trades at under 23x earnings:

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-googl-fastgraph.png)

Should Costco be trading at more than twice as high of a price/earnings ratio as Alphabet? Should Walmart be trading at about one and a half times the price/earnings ratio as Alphabet? Each investor has to determine that for themselves but for my money, I’d pick Alphabet at these levels over those two.

For profitable companies, my rule of thumb is to try to find stocks with a dividend-adjusted price/earnings/growth “PEG” ratios below 2x, or below 2.5x for exceptional companies. Using that dividing line, Alphabet is trading at a dividend-adjusted PEG ratio firmly below 2x, whereas Walmart’s firmly above 2x.

Both of these companies have fortress balance sheets for their respective industries. But between the two, Alphabet’s is stronger. They have an AA+ credit rating, and have far more cash than they have debt, meaning they have a negative net debt position. Walmart, like many companies, has more debt than cash and is using that debt well. They’re rated only one step lower, at AA.

To build a case for Walmart at 33x earnings over Alphabet at 22x earnings, I would need to have a high conviction difference of opinion from consensus analysts. I would need to have a thesis that Alphabet’s growth is going to quickly stagnate or reverse, and/or that Walmart’s growth is going to quickly accelerate. And because I lack that conviction, I prefer Alphabet.

Of course, the risk is that I could be wrong about any given stock pair, and so I run this type of analysis over many different stocks, and build a portfolio of reasonably-priced equities to avoid too much damage from individual instances of getting a stock totally wrong.

**Observation 2: Blue Chips vs Bonds**

If you buy a 10-year Treasury note today, you’ll get a yield of around 4.1% per year. Those interest payments are fixed and thus won’t grow for a note issued at that time, and the Congressional Budget Office estimates that the U.S. public debt is going to increase by over $20 trillion over the next decade. So there are *a lot* of Treasuries coming to market each year for the foreseeable future. Generally speaking, you usually want to own scarce things, and government debt has been and will continue to be anything but scarce.

I’ve been a bond bear since 2019 [when I described them as a bubble](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/), and indeed bonds have underperformed almost every other asset class since then. It would have been better to own stocks, real estate, gold, bitcoin, art, or practically anything else than bonds.

![Asset Class Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-asset-class-performance.png)

But I have to constantly re-evaluate that thesis and ask, “at what point will it be worth owning bonds again?” And yet, even though five years have gone by, I still find bonds uncompelling.

Let’s look at PepsiCo (PEP) for example. They sell a broad mix of foods and drinks as a popular consumer staple stock.

![PEP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-pep-fastgraph.png)

Their dividend yield is a bit over 3%, and so compared to a 4% yielding 10-year Treasury note an investor will get a bit less current income from PepsiCo. However, PepisCo has grown their dividend each year for 52 consecutive years. The annual growth rate over the past decade has been over 7%, which means that by the end of that period, the annual dividend payment more than doubled.

And like many blue chip stocks, they have benefited by shorting bonds by issuing so many of them. They’ve issued low-yielding long-duration debt securities, and used the proceeds for growth opportunities, share buybacks, and so forth. Since the company’s ROIC is well north of 10%, issuing those types of low-yielding securities has been very lucrative for shareholders. Here is PepsiCo’s debt breakdown from their 2023 annual report, which was published in early 2024:

![PEP Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-pep-debt.png)

Long-term profitable companies like PepsiCo could choose to run completely debt-free, but they opportunistically issue reasonable amounts of debt to boost their return metrics. Their global brands and tangible assets represent a great set of collateral to issue super-cheap debt against, and so they’ve done it to the benefit of shareholders, without getting over their skis.

My favorite income-focused asset, Enterprise Products Partners (EPD), turns that PepsiCo example on its head. Instead of paying a 3% yield that might grow around 7% per year, they pay a 7% yield that might grow around 3% per year. And they have 25 consecutive years of raising that distribution.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-epd-fastgraph.png)

EPD’s unit price has found $30 to be a resistance level, but its fundamentals keep grinding higher in the meantime.

Any one of these types of stocks might have more risk than a 10-year Treasury note. But in terms of long-term compounding, I’d take a diverse portfolio of them over bonds any day of the week. And so overall, I still see little compelling reason to hold many bonds.

Short-duration cash equivalents or TIPS may be of use for portfolio shock absorbers, but the long end of the bond curve provides a decent amount of volatility without a ton of upside potential, unless one is playing it specifically as a recession/disinflation hedge with derivatives.

## Final Thoughts: Gold and the Dollar

In my prior report and a couple other reports, I’ve been a expressing a view that gold over $2,600 is looking a bit overbought, and the dollar index under 101 is looking a bit oversold, in the near term. This is despite the fact that I’m long-term bullish on gold, and think we could have more of a weaker dollar cycle ahead.

I’m not trading around those near-term views, but I express them to manage expectations.

Within the past two weeks, gold went down slightly, and the dollar index jumped from a bit under 101 to a bit under 103, which released some of that overbought/oversold condition. I’m not surprised to see some consolidation moves like this, and I think it’s healthy for the market to digest some of its strong prior moves. My base case is to expect a bit more consolidation before the moves continue onward.
