# Hormuz Crisis and Building a Watch List

*March 29, 2026*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report focuses on the Strait of Hormuz, along with a liquidity check-up to look for cracks in the financial system.

The investment analysis section highlights a few industries I’m watching for potential entry positions, and then finishes with updates on precious metals and bitcoin.

## Macro View

**The Strait of Hormuz**

Basically everything in macroeconomics is currently dwarfed by one question: when will the Strait of Hormuz substantially re-open?

Around 20% of the world’s oil and gas has been taken off the market for more than a month now. In the initial days of the war I didn’t raise the alarm, but after the two-week mark in my prior report, I’ve been categorizing this as a massive risk. This can potentially become a Covid-19 level shock if it persists for months.

Although Iran has claimed that the Strait is open to all ships besides “the US and its allies”, the effective throughput has been extremely low. As [Al Jazeera](https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2026/3/26/tehranstollbooth-how-iran-picks-who-to-let-through-strait-of-hormuz), [the Guardian](https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/mar/26/strait-of-hormuz-visual-guide-trickle-of-ships-iran), and others have reported, ships intending to pass through have to send extensive details to the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps (IRGC), who can then clear a ship to go through if they pay a toll to them in Chinese yuan.

One clear problem is that “allies” of the United States is a large and vague set of countries, and there are multiple ways to define a ship’s alignment (such as where it came from, where it’s headed, where its company is headquartered, who its crew are, etc). Another issue is that ships still need to be insured, and with an active war going on, companies, crews, insurers, and other stakeholders are all quite nervous about committing a ship to pass through the Strait.

It remains unclear when this war will end, and even at that point, it will likely take time to fully ramp up ship crossings and oil/gas output from shut-in or damaged production facilities. Each additional week with the strait closed further ramps up the severity of the issue.

The S&P 500 is only 8% down from its highs, which is minor relative to historical drawdowns:

![SPX Level](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-spx-level.png)

![SPX Drawdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-spx-drawdowns.png)

Long-duration treasury yields have risen since the war, meaning bond prices have fallen and they have *not* served as a safe haven. Their yields are still in their multi-year range, but they can potentially become unbounded if nations and funds around the world have to sell them to fund crisis-level interventions in their economies as they struggle for energy security.

![10 Year Treasury](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-10year-treasury-recent.png)

![10 Year Treasury](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-10year-treasury-longer.png)

A number of countries have already turned to various types of rationing.

For example, as Egypt’s monthly natural gas import bill has tripled, they’ve mandated that shops, restaurants, cafes, and malls will need to close by 9pm on weekdays and 10pm on weekends. For context, people in Egyptian cities tend to stay up late, and there is a big cafe/shopping culture at late hours, so shutting all that down makes for a pretty big economic impact and can severely damage the solvency of many retail businesses. Egypt will also be dimming street advertisements and street lights.

Similarly, South Korea has introduced fuel rationing wherein drivers are unable to drive on certain days based on their license plate number, and a week ago Slovenia became the first EU country to introduce fuel rationing. Other countries have introduced similar measures, and they’re likely to spread the longer this crisis lasts.

Energy prices continue to grind higher, and there are additional shortage risks for fertilizers, chemicals, plastics, sulfur, and other raw components and feedstocks.

![Brent Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-brent-crude.png)

![Gasoline](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-gasoline.png)

While I’m not chasing energy producers at these elevated prices, I’m continuing to hold the substantial exposure I have to them, for situations just like this. In addition, I’m letting cash accumulate to maintain high liquidity and optionality, rather than rush to deploy it into various sold-off assets.

In the coming weeks or months there will likely be buying opportunities, but at the moment it still seems premature to get too bulled up. There’s little reason to be a hero and start new positions without either 1) clear evidence of a price bottom and geopolitical de-escalation or 2) massive price drawdowns that enter truly deep value zones.

**Liquidity Check-Up**

If the prices of energy and raw materials rise sharply, and cross-border cashflows diminish, then many entities may have to sell assets to satisfy their liabilities and necessary expenses.

That can lead to a scramble for base liquidity (cash), and a simultaneous selling of stocks and bonds (as we’ve seen so far, albeit in a rather orderly manner).

The dollar index has only increased from about 98 to 100, which is in line with expectations but not a very strong move. It doesn’t signify acute liquidity stress yet:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-dxy.png)

Similarly, there is little-to-no usage of the Fed’s repo facility, which among other signs suggests that there is no acute liquidity issue in the domestic financial system:

![Repo Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-repo-usage.png)

Similarly, there is no material usage of swap lines for foreign central banks:

![Swap Lines](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-swap-lines.png)

There is a lot of data on foreign-held US treasury securities, but they tend to be slow to update. A portion of those treasuries, however, are custodied at the Fed and are often quick to move during liquidity problems. Their data updates weekly, and the latest data points do show that there is some selling pressure to raise liquidity:

![Treasuries in Custody](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-treasuries-custody.png)

Overall, high energy prices and shortages are already starting to negatively affect consumers and businesses in the United States and globally. However, the biggest impacts have not been felt yet. Should this persist, prices and shortages are likely to get worse, and liquidity problems are increasingly likely to show up.

**US Business Cycle**

Most normal economic indicators are nearly useless at the moment.

In a typical period of time, trends occur gradually, with occasional periods of acceleration or deceleration. But when a major shock occurs, such as global Covid-19 lockdowns or the prolonged closure of the Strait of Hormuz, prior trends become irrelevant and there’s a sudden step-wise change that dwarfs everything else.

We’re in one of those latter environments as long as this current situation continues.

The latest economic readings in the US suggest ongoing economic deceleration. But much of this is backward-looking, and not accounting for the effects that the Strait closure will have in the coming weeks/months.

## Building a Watch List

Due to the brewing global energy crisis, many stocks that were previously expensive have fallen into a more attractively-valued zone. In addition, many stocks that were already at reasonable valuations have become outright cheap.

However, there’s little reason to try to catch falling knives. Things could get a *lot* worse if the Strait of Hormuz doesn’t re-open in the coming weeks. Thus, I view this as a time for building cash liquidity and identifying places to deploy it once there is some evidence of a price bottom and crisis resolution in sight.

**SaaS Stocks**

I’ve written about software stocks a number of times lately, and how the market already seems to be pricing in the worst-case AI disruption scenarios for their per-seat pricing models.

Thus far, I’ve dabbled a bit in Adobe, but otherwise am mostly just watching the space. Once there is more evidence of a price bottom in place I’d potentially be willing to place some big long-term bets, but so far no potential price bottom has presented itself. Thus, most of them remain on the watch list for now. Salesforce, for example, is one that I’m watching pretty closely:

![CRM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-crm-fastgraph.png)

**Spirits Stocks**

As many long-time premium members will know, years ago I became quite bullish on tobacco stocks. They are slow-growing, but had fallen to valuations that were cheap even for a no-growth scenario. They paid hefty dividends along the way as well. It took a while, but eventually they recovered. Here was British American Tobacco, for example:

![BTI FASTGraphs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-bti-fastgraph.png)

During that multi-year low period, investors could collect a 7-9% annual dividend yield, reinvest into more and more shares, and then eventually the valuation normalized with a rapid doubling of its price.

Currently, I’m seeing a similar scenario coming together for spirits stocks and beer stocks.

Generation Z has been drinking way less than prior generations. On the surface, that’s a great thing. However, the issue is also somewhat correlated with both 1) reductions in frequency of real-world social meetups and 2) the two-speed economy that is putting a lot of financial pressure on younger people, forcing them to cut out discretionary spending. Eventually this will likely find a new equilibrium level.

Many spirits and beer stocks used to be quite highly-valued, and were seen as defensive plays. But as their growth rates have slowed down, in many cases their valuations have collapsed. Now some of them are entering deep-value territory.

Diageo, for example, is the world’s largest premium spirits company, with a huge collection of well-known brands. At their peak they traded at over 33x earnings, and now they’re trading at under 12x earnings. Growth has slowed to a crawl, and their new CEO has cut their dividend as part of a strategic reset. However, they do have a decent balance sheet with an A- credit rating.

![DEO FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-deo-fastgraph.png)

I believe the worst is already over for Diageo. However, it’s too early to jump in yet; we want to see a reasonable price bottom start to form first, along with some signs that the new strategy is stabilizing the company. Much like the tobacco companies, there are valuation levels that can turn even a no-growth company into an attractive investment.

**Payment Stocks**

I’ve been a long-time bull on Visa (V), and have exposure to it. Lately, its stock price has been sagging relative to its fundamentals, which I think is somewhat healthy. Trees can’t grow to the sky, and so Visa’s valuation will likely decline over time as the company’s growth decelerates.

However, their network effect remains incredibly strong, and they have a fortress balance sheet. If the stock price starts stabilizing, it’ll be a potential candidate for a big tranche of new capital:

![V FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-v-fastgraph.png)

On the other end of the spectrum, PayPal run into major growth headwinds. It has a fortress balance sheet as well, but has practically no growth, and it has traded down to a price that seems prepared for a negative growth scenario. It’s about as cheap as some of those tobacco stocks were a few years ago. As a $40 billion company, it’s a potential acquisition target in the years ahead, for a company that can unlock more value from its massive userbase (which also includes Venmo):

![PYPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-pypl-fastgraph.png)

Right now, adding to stocks is a low priority until we have more clarity into geopolitics and energy security. However, as things fall, contrarian investors can start preparing investment cases for various companies, and waiting until the time is right. These are among the candidates I’ve been watching, but I expect many more opportunities to form if this crisis continues.

## Final Thoughts: Precious Metals and Bitcoin

Many headlines have pointed out that bitcoin has outperformed gold since the start of the war on February 28th. Some bitcoin bulls argue that this shows that bitcoin has taken gold’s place as a safe haven asset.

![BTC vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-btc-gold.png)

I continue to view that narrative as overblown. Occam’s Razor suggests a simpler explanation: gold was overbought going into this crisis, and bitcoin was oversold going into this crisis. It doesn’t take much reason for an asset to fall after it’s gone up huge on extremely high sentiment, and so there are all sorts of liquidity-related reasons that could trigger a decline in gold and silver. Meanwhile, most of the fast money was already out of bitcoin prior to the war, leaving the majority of coins in rather strongly-held hands.

When gold and silver ran up a ton in price I warned in several reports about not chasing them, and how they were no longer the asymmetrical investments they were years ago. However, I also argued that while they seem very clearly overbought, they’re not necessarily fundamentally overvalued. The long-term macroeconomic and geopolitical trends (such as fiscal dominance and multi-polarization) are supportive of precious metals, but their prices can get ahead of themselves from time to time.

I continue to view precious metals through that lens. The recent sell-off has been healthy, in my view, and the price action since mid/late March has looked better. I’m watching gold and silver pretty closely at this point to see if they are putting in local bottoms, with gold in the $4,000+ range and silver in the $60+ range.

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-gold-weekly.png)![Silver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-29-silver.png)

Bitcoin continues to spend most of its time chopping sideways in the $60k-$70k range since early February. For those who are structurally bullish on the asset and the network (as I continue to be), I view this is an ideal time to be dollar-cost averaging into it, when sentiment remains very low. There’s still a decent risk that it could drop into the $50k-$60k range, but that level has quite strong support.
