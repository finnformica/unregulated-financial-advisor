## Macro View

The number of cases, deaths, and hospitalizations for COVID-19 are on the downtrend in the United States, which is good to see:

![US COVID Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-us-covid.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometers](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

The chart looks similar for the United Kingdom and many other countries.

However, there are signs of an upturn in virus cases in some countries in Europe, and a few others like India.

Italy has one of the most notable upturns at the moment, which is leading them to impose yet another round of lockdown restrictions this week:

![Italy COVID Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-italy-covid.png)

*Chart Source: Worldometers*

Countries within continental Europe have rolled out the vaccine more slowly than the United States and the United Kingdom, which may be contributing to this. Asian countries have also generally vaccinated their populations slowly, but most of them were hit less hard to begin with.

The US had 1,645 deaths per million people attributed to the virus, while Germany had 880, Italy had 1,687, Japan had 68, and South Korea had 33.

Most places continue to open up, so by late summer or autumn, many things could start to feel more normal. The death rate of the virus is overall very low, and hopefully the vaccines will improve the odds for the most vulnerable members of society as the initial data suggest that they will.

**The Jobs Situation**

Jobs, however, still have a long way to go. After an initial bounce-back, the US and many other countries have had a tough time getting people back to work. Here are the total job numbers for the US:

![Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Some people predict that once we get into the summer months with more re-opening, that jobs should quickly tick higher. I do think that’s a reasonable possibility, but it’s not as though further re-opening will bring them back immediately. Of the ten million jobs that are still lost, only about half are for restaurants and the hospitality industry.

I think we could start to see more of a gradual uptrend in jobs from here, which would be an improvement over the weak sideways action in recent months.

**Major Stimulus Passed**

Congress passed a $1.9 trillion stimulus package along party lines, which Biden signed into law. They were able to bypass a Senate filibuster (and thus required only 50 Senate votes rather than 60 votes as overruling a filibuster would have required) with a process of budget reconciliation.

This stimulus includes $1,400 stimulus checks for low and middle income groups, a significant child tax credit, several additional months of federal unemployment insurance, aid to schools and local governments, virus testing/vaccine distribution, and various other items like aid to the restaurant industry and aid to the underfunded pension system.

Over the past year, despite high job losses and lower private income, national personal income is up significantly due to the stimulus checks and high unemployment aid, rather than down like in a normal recession, which has kept many consumers in decent shape. The two rounds of stimulus checks in particular were noticeable:

![Personal Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-personal-income-annotated.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The first round of $1,200 checks went out in April 2020 (and many were delayed into May and beyond), and along with PPP loans that turned into grants and federal unemployment benefits, the nation had above-trend personal income for most of the year before falling back to trend when it all wore off.

The second round of $600 checks went out in January 2021, nine months later, and they were distributed more efficiently this time and also came with extended unemployment benefits.

The third round of $1,400 checks and another extension of unemployment benefits is going out here in March 2021, less than 3 months after the second round of checks. So, this third round of stimulus will build on the second round when it’s already above-trend and further kick it up a notch, rather than wait until it’s back down to trend.

**Potential Next Stimulus**

Interestingly, this March bill also made student debt forgiveness non-taxable (rather than treated as taxable income), even though it didn’t forgive student loans. This is being interpreted as strategically paving the way for student loan forgiveness later.

There is about $1.7 trillion in student debt in the United States. Of this amount, nearly $1.4 trillion is backed by the federal government, with the remainder being private loans. The fact that most of the industry is “federalized” is what makes it legislatively easier to forgive than other types of debt.

![Student Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-student-loans.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Although some politicians are in favor of complete student loan forgiveness, Biden is only supportive of $10,000 in forgiveness per person. In my view, this has a moderately high probability of being implemented within the next year or two, so it’s something to watch for as another potential stimulus.

There are 45 million people with student debt in the United States, so that would be up to $450 billion in aid if implemented. Realistically, not all of them have a full $10,000 to forgive (some have way more, others have less), so maybe $350-$400 billion would actually be forgiven by an across-the-board $10,000 per person student debt jubilee.

From there, the Biden administration is supportive of a multi-trillion dollar infrastructure bill, although that has more obstacles of getting through the Senate and even if it did, would take many rounds of negotiations to get through. I don’t have much of an opinion yet on the probability of that one being passed.

Nominal GDP growth is forecast to be very strong in 2021, due to easy year-over-year base effects from 2020, and due to the large stimulus. It remains to be seen how strong, and what percentage of it is inflation vs growth. The Atlanta Fed GDPNow model currently projects an 8% annualized GDP growth rate for Q1 2021 based on available data.

**CPI Forecast**

The recent consumer price index report from this past week showed 1.68% year-over-year inflation for February, at least in terms of the official basket that they use. The blue line in this chart is reported year-over-year CPI, and the red line is the 5-year forward breakeven inflation rate as expected by the TIPS market:

![CPI Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Things start to get interesting in the March-to-July 2021 period, because the comparable period in March-to-July 2020 was very disinflationary due to the virus and shutdowns. In other words, CPI took a dip back then, and so the year-over-year base effects for those months make for an easy comparison. Therefore, even mild ongoing month-over-month increases in CPI would result in a rather high headline number by April.

Here is a back-of-the-envelope calculation. CPI was 263.161 in February 2021, which was a 0.35% month-over-month increase vs January 2021. If March 2021 CPI increases by just 0.25% from February 2021, and April 2021 CPI increases just 0.25% from March 2021, then the April 2021 CPI will be 264.48. This isn’t very fast inflation, and yet it would be more than 3.2% above April 2020, since April 2020 was a low month (easy year-over-year base effects).

![Inflation Baseline](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-inflation-baseline.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

If we get a 3.2% year-over-year headline CPI print for April 2021 (reported in May), that would be the highest since 2011. May CPI (reported in June) could be even higher than April, but after that, the base effects start to get harder again.

If things continue to re-open, and/or stimulus checks get spent quickly, and/or gasoline prices continue to increase, then headline year-over-year CPI prints of above 4% aren’t out of the question. But for now I’m just looking for above 3%.

The Fed will call this transient inflation, and they might be right since these stimulus check and easy base effects are unique events. The question is how the bond market will interpret it. It may look past it into normalized summer/autumn period, or it may continue to act somewhat disorderly in terms of driving up yields. Will the 10-year yield want to be at 1.6% when headline CPI is printing twice that number?

So, this late spring period certainly has the ingredients for some bond market volatility, which then potentially affects other asset classes like equities and gold.

The other question is what happens with ongoing stimulus; if this stimulus round was the end of it, then inflation may indeed level off. If additional stimulus is passed, or if there is tightness in the energy market, it could keep the action going.

## Bonds and Gold Note

After spending the last year below the official inflation rate (which is historically atypical, mainly due to Fed intervention as the biggest buyer last year), the 10-year Treasury note yield continues to push higher.

![10 Year Treasury and Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-10yr.png)

Even if the bond market looks past the potential for 3%+ headline CPI prints in late spring, do they want their yields to remain below 2% when stimulus efforts continue to be on the table and commodity prices are strong?

Rising yields are presenting a headwind for overvalued growth stocks, since very low discount rates were a key variable into their high valuation models. As previously described in the CPI Forecast section, there are some landmines in the next few months and I’m not sure how the bond market is going to behave. If it doesn’t behave smoothly, the Fed might end up intervening to put more downward pressure on the long end of the curve.

Gold continues its struggle due to its strong inverse correlation with real yields (as measured by the 10-year Treasury yield minus the 10-year inflation breakeven rate as projected by the TIPS market), although it is showing very initial signs of maybe catching a bottom.

Here’s the 5-year chart:

![Gold vs Rates 5 Year Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-gold-rates-5yr.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

And here’s the 1-year chart, showing the recent consolidation in real rates (blue- topping?) and gold (red- bottoming?):

![Gold vs Rates 1 Year Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-gold-rates-1yr.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

I remain near-term neutral on gold; I don’t have a strong conviction yet about whether that was the bottom in gold and the top in real rates.

The two variables for real rates are nominal Treasury yields and inflation. Over the past several months since September 2020, nominal Treasury yields have risen faster than inflation, which has been negative for gold. In the months ahead, inflation may play catch-up, and it remains to be seen how yields will respond, and then how the Fed will respond if yields continue to go up sharply.

My [February public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/february-2021-newsletter/) described a game plan for this rising rate environment. Banks and other cyclicals typically do well in a rising yield environment with a steeper yield curve. Growth stocks, however, are somewhat pressured in that environment, at least if they were overvalued to begin with. Gold would likely continue to struggle if nominal yields continue to rise faster than inflation, but would likely catch a strong bid if the Fed acts to control the long end of the Treasury curve while inflation pushes higher.

The next FOMC meeting, where Fed officials decide on monetary policy, is March 17th. The market will be watching language regarding interest rates, and will be looking at whether the Fed extends its 1-year supplementary leverage ratio rule suspension for large banks.

Back in March 2020, due to emergency conditions in the Treasury market, the Fed temporary allowed banks to exclude their Treasury security and cash reserve assets from their leverage ratios, which effectively allowed them to buy more Treasuries.

This is set to expire at the end of March 2021, unless policymakers decide to extend it. If it expires, large banks would likely need to slow down their Treasury purchases, at a time when long-duration Treasury yields are sharply rising, and when headline CPI might print above 3% in the late springtime.

## Dollar Note

The dollar continues to be range-bound compared to a basket of foreign currencies.

After a sharp descent in 2020, the dollar began a climb in 2021, which so far has been similar to the counter-rally that it had back in September 2020:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-dxy.png)

The dollar index doesn’t become very interesting unless it firmly breaks over 92 or if it firmly breaks down below 88. Recently it has been on the upper end of that range, but no clear break-out yet.

Rising yields have been beneficial for the dollar lately. However, over the next 4 months, the Treasury General Account or “TGA” is projected to fall considerably, which all else being equal is likely a dollar-bearish variable. So far, it has only just started:

![TGA Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-tga.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The government funds its expenditures with taxes, and the remaining deficit is funded by issuing Treasury securities. When they issue Treasuries at a faster rate than they spend, the money collects in the TGA, which is the federal government’s cash account at the New York Fed. Then when they slow down Treasury security issuance and keep spending, the TGA goes back down.

In 2020, the US Treasury under Secretary Mnuchin built up a record cash pile in its TGA. At $1.8 trillion, it was more than 4x any previous level. This means the US Treasury issued a lot more Treasury securities than they spent, and this sucked dollars out of the system and held them there in limbo. An increase in the TGA tends to be somewhat dollar bullish while it’s going up.

In early 2021, under Secretary Yellen, the US Treasury announced that it’ll reduce its expected Treasury security issuance and rapidly reduce its TGA, by spending more than it borrows. The recent stimulus bill will affect both sides of that equation; the Treasury will be spending faster and will likely need to issue Treasuries faster as well, but still it’ll be on track to draw down the account by over $1 trillion this summer, which puts those limbo dollars back into the commercial banking system.

In a vacuum, I would interpret this as likely dollar bearish over the next 6 months. However, if Treasury rates continue to rise and we start to get plumbing issues from a lack of SLR relief extension or other factors, those effects could cancel out the TGA drawdown’s dollar bearish effect.

So, I remain longer-term dollar bearish, but I don’t have a lot of conviction either way for the next six months. There are too many decision points up ahead, although between the available options, I lean dollar bearish and the March 17th FOMC meeting might provide some clarity.

## Alibaba Analysis

For this week’s analysis, I’m going to touch on two growth stocks, Alibaba (BABA) and Adobe (ADBE), rather than focusing heavily on one stock.

Alibaba is one of the two largest companies in China, and has a massive network of businesses. Various types of e-commerce platforms represent their core operations, where they link buyers to sellers for retail and business customers. They’ve also moved into cloud computing, which is a source of major growth, and they own a variety of other businesses.

![BABA Overview](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-baba-overview.png)

*Chart Source: Alibaba Q3 2020 Earnings Presentation*

Importantly, Alibaba also owns a large stake in Ant Group, which operates Alipay, one of the two largest payment apps in China.

In addition to being one of the largest companies in China, Alibaba is one of the largest companies in the world, period. At any given time, it’s the #2 or #3 company in most international ex-USA equity indices, depending on valuation fluctuations.

For years, Alibaba was under the good graces of China’s government, but in recent years that relationship soured.

Jack Ma, Alibaba’s founder, is one of the richest people in China. He stepped down as CEO of the company in 2013, and then stepped down as chairman in 2019 ostensibly to pursue philanthropy from then on, and sold a big chunk of his shares, bringing his stake down to less than 5% of the company.

Daniel Zhang is a long-time executive of Alibaba and is the current CEO and chairman. His leadership has been successful so far; Alibaba’s fundamentals have continued to perform well under his leadership, and he worked elsewhere in the company prior to his promotion to CEO.

In 2020, Jack Ma publicly criticized the Chinese financial system. Ant’s IPO was subsequently blocked by regulators, and Ma went missing from the public eye, including cancelling a number of planned events. This marked the top in Alibaba’s stock, where it began a notable correction.

After much speculation about Jack Ma’s whereabouts (including some fearing for the worst), he showed up in an unusual video a couple months later, where he talked about rural education and visited a school while seeming rather awkward, and has since been seen golfing. There is much speculation about exactly what level of freedom he has lost here, and for how long. To what extent was he detained, under house arrest, or pressured into laying low on his own accord?

In recent days, the CEO of Ant Group also stepped down. That company is going through a reorganization to meet certain capital requirements, and may one day IPO as originally planned but with those capital guardrails in place and with new leadership that will toe the party line more closely.

A [recent WSJ article](https://www.wsj.com/articles/china-regulators-plan-to-tame-tech-giant-alibaba-jack-ma-11615475344) provided some commentary on recent events for Ma and Alibaba’s relationship with China’s government. The overall view of the WSJ piece is that China wants Alibaba to be successful, but wants Jack Ma out and further distanced from the company, and that does seem to be the direction that things are headed in.

On one hand, one of President Xi’s goals in China with his crackdown on these tech titan companies is to prevent monopoly-like practices, which is a familiar theme that many countries are grappling with. For example, Chinese regulators want to stop the practice of some e-commerce giants of making vendors choose only to work with one platform rather than multiple platforms.

On the other hand, the authoritarian nature of how China’s government operates is a massive issue for investors and human rights advocates alike. The oppression of Uighur people in China has been the worst issue lately, but also there is the reduction in Hong Kong political freedom, and other issues related to the personal freedoms such as speech and expression and access to information across mainland China.

For Alibaba, the pressure from the government, along with being on the top end of its historical valuation range, provided a catalyst for a correction. Now, it is at a record low in terms of its historical valuation:

![BABA FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-baba-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Similarly, we can see that the company’s price/sales ratio is at a record low despite solid growth:

![BABA Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-baba-valuation.png)

We should expect to see a company’s valuation trend down over time as the company becomes very large, but this has been a pretty sharp move down. In terms of absolute valuation, the price/earnings/growth or “PEG” ratio is extremely cheap. The company has a great balance sheet and an A+ S&P credit rating.

There are a couple ways to interpret this.

One way to interpret it is that the analyst consensus for continued growth is simply wrong, and that increased regulation will lead the company to underperform consensus estimates, and the market is correct to discount it so heavily. That’s the key risk. In addition, there have always been some questions about Alibaba’s accounting, and so there’s always somewhat of a “China discount” on many of these types of companies, which is fair in my view.

The other way to interpret it is that the market is overly pessimistic, as it is prone to do. With rising interest rates and excitement over re-opening among investors around the world, tech/growth stocks are out of favor for the moment. This goes doubly-so for the company at the heart of political drama in China. Investors hate uncertainty.

Overall, my base case is the second option; I think it’s natural for all of this uncertainty to weigh on the company’s valuation, but that Alibaba is one of China’s crown jewel companies and their government wants to reign it in rather than cripple it.

With Jack Ma increasingly distanced from the company and with Ant restructured and going towards new leadership, I think there’s a high probability that Alibaba will continue to perform well in the years ahead and the stock price will reflect that over time. Their cloud business in particular has a massive runway of growth potential. Alibaba’s domain is not just in China, but throughout parts of Southeast Asia and elsewhere.

However, with tensions between the US and China remaining, there’s always a possibility that Americans could be blocked from owning Chinese stocks, or similar issues. And there’s always a possibility that accounting issues or further regulatory crackdown could drive Alibaba’s reported fundamentals off course and below analyst expectations.

Would I invest heavily in Alibaba and make it a huge portfolio position? No.

Do I want it in my portfolio at this price level as part of my global diversification strategy? Absolutely.

So, it’s listed as one of the “aggressive” stocks in the Top 12 list. It has the potential for outsized returns over a 3+ year period, potentially becoming a trillion dollar market capitalization stock, but it also faces some major tail risks to be aware of.

## Adobe Analysis

Adobe is an American software company that makes a variety of software as a service “SaaS” applications, mainly for creative designers and marketers.

Their products include Photoshop, Illustrator, Premiere, Acrobat, and a host of other applications, and they are organized into various cloud groups. Whether you want to do video editing, digital art, marketing design, document creation, or e-signatures, Adobe has leading SaaS products in the industry.

![ADBE Overview](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-adbe-overview.png)

*Chart Source: Adobe 2020 Financial Analyst Meeting Slides*

In addition, Adobe Sensei is an AI machine-learning system that helps creators simplify their design process across multiple applications. When a creator is editing a picture or video, for example, the AI can figure out what the creator is intending to do, or can suggest things to do, to make their job easier.

We have been very fortunate with Adobe in the newsletter portfolio in terms of timing, via contrarian moves.

Adobe was added to the newsletter portfolio on January 8, 2019 at $233/share after it and several other growth stocks had a big price decline in Q4 2018. In fact, that’s when the “growth stock” segment of the newsletter portfolio was created, specifically to address that sell-off and load up on some good compounders.

We then dollar-cost averaged into Adobe for a while, and sold it on September 21, 2020 at $475/share when it was trading at a record valuation. It greatly outperformed the S&P during its holding period.

![ADBE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-adbe-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Adobe had an 18% drawdown from its high in early September through today, and more importantly, underperformed the S&P 500 by over 26%, since the S&P 500 went up while Adobe went down. We were able to put that capital to better use during that time.

For example, we added the cyclical luxury designer Capri (CPRI) in the growth stock section of the newsletter portfolio as one of our uses of capital during that period instead, on November 9, 2020 at $27/share. Capri is now $54/share, so that’s a quick double in a little over three months while Adobe stock temporarily languished.

I’m starting to view parts of the “re-opening” trade as a bit overdone. I still think many hyper-growth unprofitable stocks are majorly overvalued and are due to ongoing correction, but there are some “growth at a reasonable price” stocks like Adobe that are getting back to the attractive valuation threshold.

As a fast-growing company, Adobe corrected not only in price, but also in time. While its price went down 18%, it had two quarters of continued growth, which drove valuations down further.

Its drawdown wasn’t massive by historical standards, but it’s still the third-largest drawdown for Adobe within the past five years of it being a powerhouse SaaS company:

![ADBE Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-adbe-drawdown.png)

The challenging part of valuing Adobe is that as it has gotten larger, its growth has *accelerated* rather than decelerated, which is atypical for a maturing company.

Specifically, the switch from one-time software sales to software subscriptions has been a gamechanger for the company. Plus, management has been quite good under the long-time leadership (since 2007) of Shantanu Narayen.

With a subscription, more people are able to afford the software, and remain “stickier” to the company with monthly or annual payments rather than one-time big purchases. The fact that Adobe combined their existing products and acquired new products under a common cloud platform, also makes cross-selling easier. If I’m a subscriber to Adobe Photoshop, for example, I can easily add any of their other software applications to my existing account, and keep my work in a shared cloud environment.

If we look at Adobe’s long-term revenue and price/sales ratio, we see a complicated picture:

![ADBE Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-adbe-valuation.png)

One one hand, Adobe is near its peak valuation. The late-1980s valuation doesn’t really count; it was a tiny company back then and did amazing. However, the fact that Adobe is currently trading at the same price/sales ratio as it did during the peak of the dotcom bubble should give investors pause.

It’s important to note the purple line on the chart however; Adobe’s revenue. Their growth was lackluster during the late 1990s and early 2000s; so investors were paying a rather high price/sales multiple for a company with mediocre growth during the bubble. With the SaaS model, and much more product diversification due to ongoing development and acquisitions, Adobe is a much stronger company today and warrants a valuation premium. The question is how high of a valuation premium.

The analyst consensus is that the year 2020, where people were stuck indoors and relied more heavily on software, pulled forward some growth and that 2021 will be slightly weaker. My base case is that Adobe will mildly beat expectations in 2021.

My general rule for profitable “growth at a reasonable price” stocks in this interest rate environment is that I don’t particularly want to pay a price/earnings/growth “PEG” ratio over 2x. In other words, the price/earnings ratio shouldn’t be more than 2x as high as the annual growth rate of earnings or sales.

Adobe’s PEG ratio is below 2x based on recent 3-5 year historical numbers, but above 2x based on analyst consensus forward expectations.

I consider below $400/share to be quite attractive. The current price of $444 is better than it was in late summer 2020, but doesn’t present a very attractive margin of safety. Thus, Adobe is back in my borderline attractive category. Not a strong buy, but on the border of buy territory in terms of quantifiable metrics.

However, if I take a step back and look back five years from now, will I care about a 10% entry price difference?

When I recommended and bought bitcoin in April 2020 at $6,900/coin, would it have mattered if it was 10% higher at $7,600 instead at that time, now that it’s over $60,000? Not really. Adobe won’t be that extreme, but it’s a point worth considering when you’re analyzing a real long-term compounder.

I consider Adobe to be one of the strongest companies on the planet. It’s revenue is still only $13 billion; it has plenty of runway for growth in the 2020s digital economy even though I expect its current growth trajectory to eventually mature and slow down again. There aren’t many limitations for how many applications they can build or acquire, and combine under their cloud offering with AI-integration.

And yet, it’s in the weird middle area, where investors don’t focus on it. As a 39-year-old company, Generation Z Robinhood app users aren’t buying it as the next hot stock. Value investors certainly aren’t interested at these prices either. It’s neither a hyper-growth unprofitable tech stock, or a deep value cyclical stock.

Instead, it’s a long-term profitable compounder with a wide economic moat, that tends to be a bit rich in terms of valuation but also tends to deliver on that valuation over time, and yet never seems to be in the spotlight.

The risk of Adobe is that its growth could slow down over the next few years, and the current valuation would indeed end up being too high for good returns. Unlike Alibaba, Adobe has fewer tail risks, but in exchange for that, it does trade at a higher valuation, and so it has less upside potential if things go well for the respective companies. The main risk for Adobe is the valuation.

At these prices, I can begin scaling in with a small position, but not yet commit to a full position.

**The Adobe Cash-Secured Put Option**

Adobe stock currently trades for $444/share. However, investors can sell [cash-secured put options](https://www.lynalden.com/selling-put-options/) for June 2021, 3 months from now, with a strike price of $415, and get $15-$20/share in premiums for doing so.

In other words, the investor sets aside cash, and gets paid $15-$20/share up front in order to commit to buying Adobe stock if it falls below $415 prior to expiration. Regardless of what happens, they keep the premium. If Adobe stock falls and gets assigned to the option seller, then the cost basis for the purchase is under $400, when you take into account the entry price and the premium.

On the other hand, if Adobe stays above that $415 level, the put seller never ends up buying the stock or participating in the upside, but keeps the $15-$20 premium over a 3-month period, which is a 4% rate of return. At an annualized rate, that’s over 16% per year.

I consider that to be an attractive compromise; viewing Adobe as perhaps still a bit rich and likely to come down in price this spring if Treasury yields head higher, but also profiting if the stock bounces from here and keeps moving up. It’s like getting paid to place a 3-month limit order, preparing to buy the stock should it fall further amid ongoing Treasury yield increases or other sentiment factors.

## Summary Thoughts

There are a lot of conflicting and powerful forces occurring in markets over the next few months.

The Treasury General Account is planned to be drawn down, which will add a ton of liquidity to a system that already has high liquidity. Meanwhile, Treasury yields are rising and the fate of the 1-year SLR rule exemption that let major banks buy more Treasuries is uncertain in regards to an extension. Plus, the third round of stimulus is going out along with vaccines, and low base effects might lead to some 3%+ year-over-year CPI prints by late spring.

During the past several months, overbought growth stocks have rightly sold off, and oversold value stocks have rightly rebounded, in this rising yield environment with excitement about re-opening and normalization. The question is, what’s next?

My base case is that high-quality value stocks will likely continue to do well vs growth stocks in general. However, there are still a number of growth-at-a-reasonable-price stocks out there, that are neither value stocks nor hyper-growth unprofitable narrative stocks, and instead sit somewhere in the middle. After correcting over the past several months, some of them are in the range of being attractive again.

Alibaba and Facebook (FB) are some examples of reasonably-priced tech giants with still-rapid growth that are not in good graces with regulators, and thus have some tail risks. And for a different risk profile, we have high-flying Adobe coming back to earth, but still with a very strong business model and no major fundamental threats, with a big question of valuation since it’s still not cheap.

Overall, I like a mix of value stocks and “growth at a reasonable price” stocks in this environment, with diversified commodity exposure as well. I also like some cash ready, in the event that we get a volatility event that presents some buying opportunities later this spring.
