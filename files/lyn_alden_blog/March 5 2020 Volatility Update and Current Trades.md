# Volatility Update and Current Trades

*Published March 5, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This is another unscheduled research note to provide additional updates on the market, which has been bouncing around in a volatile sideways pattern.

![SPX Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-5-spx-chart.png)

The S&P 500 is down over 10% from all-time highs, which is remarkable given how fast it happened, but still unremarkable in terms of how significant bear markets can become during recessions.

Many stocks are still expensive according to most traditional valuation metrics. A 20% further sell-off from here would be normal, if we are indeed entering a recession. More than that is possible if there is a financial crisis. So this recent sell-off was nothing spectacular in terms of magnitude.

However, certain industries that have direct vulnerability to the coronavirus and that were already down a bit due to the slowing economy, have been absolutely slammed. Many airlines, cruise operators, energy producers, casinos, hotels, travel websites, and most recently restaurant chains, are 25-50% or more down from their all-time highs.

There is good reason for that in some cases, but for opportunistic value investors, some of these stocks are starting to get interesting. Their industries are in a recession, or expected to be soon.

So, while I’m not at the point where I want to shift a lot of cash and treasuries into the broad stock market, I am at the point where I want to shift a little bit of cash and treasuries into severely beaten-down industries.

However, it’s important to find companies that have little chance of permanent destruction; companies that have the absolute best balance sheets and financial flexibility to withstand massive negative shocks to their industries. We’ll still have restaurants and casinos and energy producers when this is all over, but some of the weaker ones are likely to go bust and have their assets bought by creditors. Flybe, a small U.K. airline, already declared bankruptcy over this, for example.

## Interest Rate Cuts

In my [March 1 report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-3-1/) that covered a macro update and the Tractor Supply deep-dive, I also highlighted gold and gold miners as a smart move after their sell-off at the end of last week.

The reasoning was that central banks, including the Federal Reserve, were about to intervene with stimulus, including rate cuts and quantitative easing.

This week, that is exactly what markets received. The Federal Reserve performed an emergency 0.50% interest rate cut, and the bond market is still pricing in a near-certainty that the Fed will cut interest rates again when they meet later this month. In addition, the Federal Reserve added $83 billion to their balance sheet during this past week, which is a large liquidity injection and is essentially a form of quantitative easing, which weakened the dollar a bit.

Gold and gold stocks did great this week, up considerably over broad stocks and bonds.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-5-gold-chart.png)

I continue to think that gold and gold stocks are a smart hedge in a portfolio, but after this run, it pays to be cautious and continue to manage position sizes and diversification appropriately. Asset prices don’t go up and down in straight lines. However, I expect good results for gold and gold stocks over the next several years, with ups and downs along the way.

## Record Volatility

Market volatility has been near record highs during the past two weeks, and remains near record highs as of today:

![Volatility Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-5-volatility.png)

This volatility is extremely unnerving for investors (especially ones that didn’t already own treasuries, cash, and gold going into this like we had in our model portfolios), but it is very good for investors that are starting to sell options, because option premiums are very high when volatility is this high.

Specifically, I am now very interested in selling cash-secured put options on high-quality stocks that have been slammed lately. The option premiums are now large enough that investors can sell deep out-of-the-money options and get paid to wait to potentially buy the shares at an even lower price. In other words, rather than buy at current beaten-down prices, investors can get paid a high fee for agreeing to buy at levels that are 10-30% lower than already beaten-down current prices in many cases.

This makes for attractive risk-adjusted return potential, and on Friday I will be taking some cash off the sidelines to sell out-of-the-money cash-secured put options on some stocks in my Fidelity and Schwab accounts. (M1 Finance does not have options trading.) It’s a slightly lower risk strategy compared to simply buying the stocks at current prices, but also comes with reduced near-term upside potential.

My tutorial on selling put options is [here](https://www.lynalden.com/selling-put-options/).

## Put Option Ideas

My exact trades tomorrow will depend in part on market conditions and will be listed in the “Other Holdings” tab of my Model Portfolios spreadsheet in my Google Drive mid-day, but these are two I’m particularly looking at.

**Cheesecake Factory (CAKE)**

Restaurant stocks took a harsh beating yesterday, falling 5-20% per stock due to coronavirus concerns, as investors sold off the entire industry, with an emphasis on stocks that focus on dine-in eating. My favorite restaurant stock, CAKE, took a 12% hit and is now 53% off of its all-time high that it reached back in 2017. It now has a dividend yield of over 4.5%.

As of today’s put option premium price, investors can sell July 2020 puts with a $25 strike price for $1.50/share. In other words, the option seller receives about $1.50 up front and sets $25/share aside, and gets to keep the $1.50 either way. If the stock price (which is currently over $30) never goes down to $25 within the next 4.5 months, the option seller makes over 6% returns on their cash-secured capital during those 4.5 months, or over 17% annualized. On the other hand, if the stock price falls under $25 during that time frame, the option seller will buy the stock at $25 (even if the price is lower then), and gets to keep the $1.50 premium, resulting in a cost basis of $23.50.

I’ll take that trade.

**Canadian Natural Resources (CNQ)**

If oil continues falling in price, many higher-cost and highly-leveraged producers will go bust, which will reduce supply. The strongest companies, with the lowest production costs and strongest balance sheets, will survive.

In addition to my favorite energy stock, Lukoil, Canadian Natural Resources has among the lowest production costs and highest levels of free cash flow of energy stocks. CNQ is able to withstand deep bear markets in the energy industry if need be. They are 35% down from its 2018 high and it just announced a dividend *increase* today. Their dividend yield is already well over 4.5%. They plan to spend less than originally expected on expansion this year, which will be a common theme for many oil companies, curtailing supply over time.

CNQ’s share price is currently $24.25. Investors can sell the June 2020 put options at a strike price of $22 for $1.30/share. In other words, if CNQ stays above $22/share for the next 3.5 months, investors make $1.30/share on their reserved capital, which is over 6% returns for the period or over 20% annualized. If CNQ drops below $22/share, the option seller would buy it at a cost basis of $20.70 after the option premium is factored in. Sounds good to me.

**Tranches to Manage Risk**

Rather than trying to time market tops or bottoms, I scale in gradually. I build up cash, treasuries, and gold in expensive markets, and begin deploying those reserves in cheaper markets, and especially in high-quality companies in beaten-down sectors.

Even these lower strike prices on CAKE and CNQ might not be the bottom in an extremely adverse scenario. For example, CNQ fell into the mid-to-high teens during the global financial crisis and the 2016 oil price crash. You never know just how low some things will go. Stock prices usually undershoot and overshoot the fundamentals of their companies in bear and bull markets.

However, my goal with each transaction is to put money into a stock at a price that is likely to do well over the next 5 years or so, and at my target prices in these examples, I think there’s a high probability that they will. Maintaining appropriate position sizes and diversification helps to reduce risk further, because sometimes stock picks simply won’t work out as expected for one reason or another (like adding Carnival cruise lines to a portfolio shortly before a worldwide virus outbreak occurred).

I do continue to hold a sizable amount of cash, treasuries, and gold as well, because risks remain elevated and we are likely to be presented with multiple buying opportunities in multiple industries over the next year or two.
