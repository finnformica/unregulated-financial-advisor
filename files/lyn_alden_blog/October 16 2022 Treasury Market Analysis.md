## Macro View

This macro section will focus on four parts: 1) the US business cycle, 2) the recent CPI report, 3) Treasury dynamics, and 4) the global energy supply/demand situation.

**US Business Cycle Update**

The purchasing manager’s index continues to head south:

![ISM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-20220-10-16-ism.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Sometimes PMI heads south but then gets intercepted by fiscal or monetary or currency intervention to re-accelerate the cycle (e.g. 1998 and 2013 and 2016). Other times it heads south into a recession (e.g. 2009 and 2020). The partially-inverted Treasury yield curve and current Fed policy point towards a probable recession.

The Atlanta Fed’s [GDPNow estimator](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow) has a pretty decent 2.8% annualized growth expectation for Q3 (the quarter that ended a couple weeks ago but hasn’t been reported yet).

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-gdpnow.png)

The problem with this good GDP estimate, however, is that the the biggest contributor to it is a marginally improving trade deficit. Both exports and imports fell (not good), but imports fell faster, largely because US oil and gas exports held up well in the current energy situation. So, outside of the US energy sector, the US economy broadly is weaker than this number suggests.

Retail sales came in soft on Friday. Nominal numbers (blue line below) are flattening out, but those numbers include high inflation. Inflation-adjusted numbers (red line below) show the downtrend more clearly:

![Retail Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-retail-sales-nominal-vs-real.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Monetary policy has a long lag time for its full effects to hit the economy. Interest rates are higher now, which only affects a small percentage of people at first. Most homeowners have locked-in mortgage rates, and corporations have debt maturity profiles that are as long as possible. As time goes on, as rates stay where they are or go higher, more people have to move or buy a home, and more corporations have bonds come due that they need to be refinanced, and all of this debt rollover occurs at these higher rates. This should lead to less consumer discretionary purchases, and less corporate profitability.

Additionally, the strong dollar means that US corporations will have bad currency conversions. The 40% or so of the S&P 500 revenue that comes from overseas will be translated into fewer dollars due to how strong the dollar is, likely resulting in lackluster S&P 500 earnings numbers going forward.

**CPI Report**

The inflation report came in hot last week; specifically the core numbers were above consensus expectations.

The year-over-year figures have a local top in place for headline inflation, but core inflation continues to grind higher in large part due to the shelter component that is operating on a lag relative to the actual housing market (because it’s a pretty bad way to calculate shelter inflation).

![CPI YOY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-cpi-yoy.png)

Some of the toppy-ness in year-over-year CPI was due to base effects. The month-over-month figures show the issue more clearly; core CPI is 0.58% month over month which means even just recently, that would translate into 7% year-over-year CPI if it were to continue for a year.

![CPI MOM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-cpi-mom.png)

However, as previously mentioned, a decent chunk of that elevated core number is due to the lagging shelter component.

**Treasury Market Dynamics**

The US Treasury yield curve is rather inverted. The 10yr-2yr curve is inverted, and while the 10yr-3mo curve is hanging on at a mildly positive level, it could easily go negative with another rate hike or two. This points towards a likely 2023 recession.

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-yield-curve.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Just as importantly, the US Treasury market continues to run into liquidity problems, which has been a big focus of my research this year (and to some extent going all the way back to the expiration of the SLR exemption in spring 2021). This was a niche subject last year and early this year, but now that it is becoming acute, it keeps hitting mainstream media headlines. Bloomberg ran a [good piece](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2022-10-14/fed-s-next-crisis-is-brewing-in-us-treasuries?leadSource=uverify%20wall) on it on Friday with a number of charts including a measure of liquidity and a measure of volatility:

![Treasury Illiquidity Bloomberg](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-treasury-illiquidity-bloomberg.png)

In 2022, the Fed is letting Treasuries mature off its balance sheet. Foreign pools of capital are mostly not buying Treasuries due to the strong dollar (that puts them in defense mode; they stop buying and even start selling Treasuries in order to defend their currency). US banks, due to supplementary leverage ratio limitations, are also mostly not buying Treasuries.

Those are the three biggest balance sheets for Treasuries, which means as they sell or just stop buying, more Treasuries have to be absorbed by the smaller balance sheets (hedge funds, bond funds, retail investors, and so forth), and this process is disorderly, illiquid, and puts government borrowing costs on an unsustainable path for the long run, due to the high debt-to-GDP levels that they have.

We can back up and note for a moment that bond yields are determined by the market in terms of two components: expected inflation and expected default risk. Corporate bonds in particular can go up or down either because inflation expectations are changing, or because recession risk and the perception around their default risk is changing.

If we look at the Treasury market, using the 10-year duration in this example, we see that market expectations of forward inflation went down in 2022 (blue line below), while Treasury yields (red line) went up at a record pace:

![Treasuries Breakevens](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-treasuries-breakevens-annotated.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

In other words, this sharp rise in US Treasury yields, along with near-record volatility and illiquidity, is not because market expectations of forward inflation spiked this year (quite the contrary). Does that leave us with an increasing market assessment of default risk on… Treasuries?

I would say no, which instead leaves us with a third option: along with hikes on the short end of the curve, there’s literally not enough balance sheet capacity to hold these long duration Treasuries. It’s therefore a more mechanical problem. Foreign central banks are trimming Treasuries (and mainly just not buying more) not because they think they are badly priced, but because they are doing it to defend their currencies. Banks don’t necessarily think Treasuries are overvalued; their SLR limits prevent them from buying more. And the Fed is selling Treasuries to try to tackle inflation by tightening monetary conditions.

A similar example is when oil went negative in April 2020. Did the market truly assess oil to have a negative value? Not really. Me and many others theoretically would like to buy unlimited oil at negative dollars per barrel. The problem instead was that with such an unprecedented demand collapse due to lockdowns, there wasn’t anywhere to put the supply that was just pumped out of the ground.

That’s what is likely happening to Treasuries now: there is price-insensitive selling from the biggest balance sheets, and not a comfortable amount of balance sheet space elsewhere to absorb those Treasuries. The point is, this explosive move in yields, liquidity, and volatility is not about a recent rise in inflation expectations. It’s about about a lack of buyers due to specific supply/demand issues that are largely separate from the question of price.

The Treasury Department [has been asking primary dealers](https://www.reuters.com/markets/us/us-treasury-asks-major-banks-if-it-should-buy-back-us-government-bonds-2022-10-14/) if it should start buying back some of its own off-the-run securities to improve liquidity. For people that aren’t familiar with the term, “off-the-run” securities are bonds that are not the latest issuance of that specific maturity, whereas “on-the-run” securities are the latest issue. For example, if the Treasury Department sells 10-year government notes, and then 3 months later sells another round of 10-year government notes, that first wave of notes becomes off-the-run, and now has a maturity profile of 9 years and 9 months. On-the-run Treasuries are more liquid than off-the-run securities. In March 2020 when the Treasury market literally broke into almost complete illiquidity, it was mainly the off-the-run securities that broke.

Back in March 2020, the Fed fixed the Treasury market illiquidity by buying $1 trillion worth of Treasuries in the next three weeks, and then continued buying at a slower pace until the end of 2021. The optics of doing that now with official inflation over 8% would be worse, and so policymakers are looking at other more conservative options.

One option is that the Treasury Department can keep issuing more on-the-run securities and keep buying back off-the-run securities that reach problematic levels of liquidity. Ultimately this is a band-aid solution, but it does kick the can down the road a bit if that’s what they decide to do. Treasuries are supposed to be among the deepest and most liquid markets in the world and this scenario goes way against that historical norm.

If the Treasury Department uses this tactic to change the duration profile of their debt, they can extend Treasury market functioning longer. There is over $2 trillion sitting in the Fed’s reverse repo facility, mostly from money market funds. This money would be happy to be in T-bills, if there were more T-bills issued. The Treasury could buy back off-the-run longer-duration securities, and increase its issuance of T-bills, basically doing an “operation twist” to absorb this capital and relieve pressure from longer-dated off-the-run securities. Money market funds and their usage of reverse repo remains one of the few big balance sheets available that has a lot of space, but the catch is that it would only work for T-bills:

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-reverse-repo.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

That type of Treasury buyback twist operation to suck capital out of reverse repos and into T-bills could potentially allow for quite a bit more Fed quantitative tightening before truly breaking the Treasury market.

The long-term issue with that is it is like refinancing a fixed-rate mortgage with an adjustable rate mortgage or other short-term financing where rates can change. The government debt’s average duration would be shortened, which if we enter a structurally higher interest rate environment, means higher interest expenses.

My base case continues to be that the Treasury market or an adjacent financial plumbing issue will be the limiting factor for how tight the Fed can be in terms of rates, quantitative tightening, and strong dollar policy. Most analysts are looking at the unemployment rate and economic indicators for signals when the Fed will pause or pivot, but the closer issue seems to be these deep liquidity issues due to a lack of Treasury buyers.

However, if policymakers use really tactical and intermediate-term Treasury operations (e.g. this buyback twist concept), it probably would give the Fed the most possible runway to really push their monetary tightness to its extreme.

For an added thought on this, make sure you read the final section of this report.

**Energy Supply Problems**

As I’ve covered in a number of newsletters and reports this year, the Fed is trying to rein in demand to fight inflation. They are explicitly targeting a higher unemployment rate.

The problem however is that the supply side, particularly for energy, doesn’t have any clear path to resolution in the next few years. In fact, energy producers now have higher costs of capital to deal with on top of their other constraints, when it comes to bringing new oil and gas supply online.

Right now, oil is like a beachball held under water, meaning it structurally wants to head higher but is being held down pretty hard for the time being. We can review some of the supply/demand dynamics.

The US continues to draw down its Strategic Petroleum Reserve, which is a temporary supply boost to help stabilize the oil market prior to the midterm elections:

![Strategic Petroleum Reserve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-spr.png)

The Biden Administration has issued [an order of magnitude fewer drilling permits](https://www.wsj.com/articles/federal-oil-leases-slow-to-a-trickle-under-biden-11662230816) on federal lands than other presidents, measured by the first 19 months of an administration. This was part of his campaign promise to stop drilling on federal lands, but that promise is a lot more complicated now with higher energy prices domestically and for US allies.

The Fed is tightening monetary policy which hardens the dollar, slows the US economy, slows US corporate profits, and slows the economy of a lot of developing countries. This puts some downward pressure on oil demand.

Many European manufacturers have shut down due to extremely high energy prices. This will likely contribute to future inflation and/or shortages of things those manufacturers were making (including fertilizer and metals). Germany was an incredibly efficient machine at turning billions of dollars worth of cheap Russian gas into trillions of dollars of finished products for the world. That machine is now short-circuiting.

China continues to do a policy of “zero-Covid”, [despite mounting issues with that](https://www.cnn.com/2022/10/13/china/china-zero-covid-lockdown-party-congress-intl-hnk/index.html). As the virus became more transmissible and less fatal over time, it became increasingly hard to contain. Much of the rest of the world has moved on with reasonable precautions for the most vulnerable populations, whereas China is still performing recurring major lockdowns on large percentages of its population. This removes considerable oil demand for things like jet fuel, gasoline, and certain industrial uses while it continues to persist.

OPEC+ is cutting its production quotas, and already has been failing to even meet their quotas. There is considerable evidence that OPEC+ is nearly at its current production limits. Furthermore, Russia was partly reliant on Western oil major capital and equipment, and with that gone, Russia may have trouble maintaining current production in future years.

There are places like Venezuela, Iran, Nigeria, and Libya that have a lot more oil production potential, but have been unable to get anywhere near their potential due to political or geopolitical problems. So those are unreliable or unpredictable swing variables.

Offshore oil still has a lot of potential but the project lifetimes are very long, with huge upfront capital costs and long payback periods. That is the type of project that companies have been hesitant to take risks on, but that in the coming years I think they’ll be increasingly turning towards out of necessity.

Europe will likely continue to increase LNG imports in the following years, which all else equal will keep prices elevated. North American companies can build more export capacity in the coming years which will likely exert upward pressure on North American gas prices, as the price differential between LNG and onshore gas narrows. This export capacity could be federally blocked, but that would be a big hit to US relationships with its allies and would be opposed by the US energy industry.

Europe will likely continue to substitute to other energy sources, which can include burning oil for electrical power (which is not as ideal as gas in terms of economics or the environment, but is a fallback option). Parts of New England in the US do this because they block the construction of natural gas pipelines from the nearby major Northeastern gas basins. This is often for environmental reasons but the result is higher electricity and heating prices for the region and ironically likely worse environmental outcomes.

Emerging markets including India continue to consume more hydrocarbons. For example, India still only has around 10% air conditioning penetration, and the chart of the number of operating vehicles in India is up and to the right and still has pretty low penetration:

![India Vehicles](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-india-vehicles.png)

*Chart Source: [Statista](https://www.statista.com/statistics/664729/total-number-of-vehicles-india/)*

The combination of all of this is that with global energy usage continuing to grind up slowly over time, when the US ends its SPR drawdowns, and if China ever decides to come out of its lockdowns, that can be a pretty big total swing in terms of oil supply and demand.

I am neutral with no firm view on energy prices over the next 3-6 months due to this aggressive attempt by central banks at demand destruction, but I am bullish for the years going forward.

**Lightning Bridge**

There was a [good interview](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=226xf487_vI&ab_channel=KevinRooke) with James Andrew on Kevin Rooke’s show to talk about [Lightning Bridge](https://www.lightningbridge.com/). I recommend the interview, especially for people that aren’t that into bitcoin and wonder why I write about the Lightning network so much.

James Andrew’s background is that he was a pioneer in high frequency trading two decades ago, with an expertise in the technical details and speed/liquidity aspects of that industry. Several years ago he started working in altcoin/web3/blockchain type of projects, and subsequently found his way to Lightning, which is a pretty unusual path for Lightning developers but makes sense for someone with his background. If you’ve [read my research on Lightning](https://www.lynalden.com/lightning-network/), you’ll see why liquidity and speed are key aspects of the network.

Lightning Bridge is James Andrew’s company’s new web interface which allows Cash App, Strike, Wallet of Satoshi, or other custodial platforms to send value over to various smart contract blockchains like Solana and some of the Ethereum layer 2 protocols, very quickly and very cheaply.

It uses the Lightning network to do this. Much like how Strike and other platforms can use the Lightning network for fiat currency transfer (e.g. USD-to-BTC-to-USD to get dollars from one entity to another, or USD-to-BTC-to-EUR to transfer and exchange fiat internationally, or similar transactions like that within a second), the Lightning network can also be used for crypto transfer (e.g. USDC-to-BTC-to-SOL to transfer crypto between entities within a second).

This initial application to bridge Cash App and Strike to crypto protocols doesn’t interest me since it’s all about getting capital into trading and NFT environments, which aren’t really my thing, but I recommend the interview since it’s on the broader topic of why Lightning is likely a big deal for the long run even aside from bitcoin as an investable asset. James Andrew is using this as a concept and plans to expand into more Lightning/Taro opportunities over time.

Topics of the [interview](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=226xf487_vI&ab_channel=KevinRooke) include things like:

* Why he pivoted from Solana to more Lightning development.
* Potential long-term applications for Taro assets on Lightning.
* Lightning as backend payments infrastructure for all sorts of things.
* How Lightning looks and feels a lot like the early internet.
* Lightning as an ecosystem is full of energy and top-notch developers.
* How Lightning enables more machine-to-machine payments.

## Defensive Stock Update

Subject to a caveat about a likely risk asset pop in the coming weeks, overall defense still makes sense. “Defense” means different things for different portfolios (trading portfolios, investment portfolios, diversified portfolios, concentrated portfolios, etc.) but in general I’ve been using it to mean overweighting cash equivalents (bank deposits, money markets, and short-term Treasuries), gold, healthcare stocks, tobacco stocks, and energy pipelines. However, I don’t use it to mean to sell all other assets.

As the Fed continues to tighten monetary policy into a decelerating economy, there is little reason to stick one’s neck out and take undue risk. However, this could switch when one least suspects it. For example, in late 2019 the rally was fueled by a repo spike that few people were expecting. We could wake up one day and see a problem in the US Treasury market that the Treasury or Fed responds to, which partially disrupts the Fed’s tightening plan and triggers a risk-on bear market rally.

Furthermore, geopolitics are getting more tense. Within the past couple weeks, the US government [imposed restrictions](https://foreignpolicy.com/2022/10/12/biden-china-semiconductor-chips-exports-decouple/) on trading advanced semiconductors to China. Initial reports suggest that this is will have a major impact. The Biden Administration may have just taken its gloves off in terms of trade relations with China and gone for the jugular in a way that goes above the Trump Administration’s scattershot sanctions approach. This represents the US specifically trying to hobble Chinese technological development as part of this great power competition, rather than merely to improve the US/China trade balance, and this will probably elicit a response.

I think many analysts underestimate how intertwined the US and Chinese economies are. Similar to how intertwined Europe and Russia were, the level of intertwinement between China and the United States is huge. Tit-for-tat games are fine but if there is more actual decoupling, then things get economically dangerous pretty quickly.

For starters, many of the big Nasdaq stocks would become more suspect in terms of risk/reward potential if the US and China escalate their trade tensions and start engaging in more of an economic cold war. Apple (AAPL), Tesla (TSLA), Amazon (AMZN), and others all have considerable exposure to Chinese supply chains, as do most of the S&P 500.

But beyond that, we’d have to worry about persistent inflationary pressures and shortages. All of this is still early; we need to see more information on US and Chinese trade. Overall, the trend is very protectionist, across both the Trump and Biden administrations now.

For this reason, and also due to the global energy situation, I continue to view energy pipelines as attractive. The Alerian MLP ETF (AMLP) is the easy way to get exposure while minimizing tax complexity. For individual MLPs, I like Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) and Energy Transfer (ET). These issue K-1 forms during tax season. I’m looking at these for 3-5 year holding periods or longer, and with an emphasis on income generation.

**The Key Risk**

Energy pipelines, despite being well-positioned fundamentally, are not without risk. And that risk is protectionist wartime policy.

Over time (years), I expect more North American LNG capacity to come online, which will help alleviate European and Japanese high natural gas prices. However, that will partially come at the cost of higher North American onshore natural gas prices.

In other words, this extreme spread should eventually narrow:

![Natural Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-10-16-natural-gas-ycharts.png)

Since natural gas supply has limits, the long-term narrowing will likely go both ways, with upward pressure on North American natural gas prices, and downward pressure on European natural gas prices. The gap between these lines is a huge long-term arbitrage opportunity for producers and transporters.

In other words, every natural gas producer in the US now has a strong incentive to get their product to an export facility, into LNG, and out to Europe or Japan where they get better prices.

This could become a political issue in the US. The idea of an “export ban” keeps being floated. If that were to be enacted, it would reduce the future growth potential of domestic pipeline companies and producers. It would also be a major geopolitical event, basically leaving Europe out to dry.

I think it’s important to monitor this risk when it comes to overall position sizing in the US energy producer and transport sector, even though overall I’m quite bullish on the sector with a 3-5 year view.

I also view jurisdictional diversification as an important way to mitigate this risk. In other words, I like owning energy production and transportation assets in the US, Canada, and other countries.

On the other hand, we need to ask “what could people be missing?” With all of these conditions in play, will we continue a nearly unprecedentedly-fast period of dollar up, yields up, and equities down? Will retail investors actually get paid a ton of money for a market crash scenario, for once?

It doesn’t smell right, and I suspect an upward risk asset pricing event; a sharp bear market rally perhaps. Near-term market structure is not my expertise, but I definitely wanted to highlight this collection here so that readers are aware of it.

The trillion-dollar question is, what could be the catalyst for an unwind of some of this positioning and sentiment? Would could cause a drop in the dollar, a drop in yields, a spike in stocks, and a spike in bitcoin?

Early in this report I wrote:

> As the Fed continues to tighten monetary policy into a decelerating economy, there is little reason to stick one’s neck out and take undue risk. However, this could switch when one least suspects it. For example, in late 2019 the rally was fueled by a repo spike that few people were expecting. We could wake up one day and see a problem in the US Treasury market that the Treasury or Fed responds to, which partially disrupts the Fed’s tightening plan and triggers a risk-on bear market rally.

That would be my guess. We could see some sort of “sudden pop” in the Treasury market or adjacent market, requiring a liquidity intervention by the US Treasury or the Fed. If it were to happen, it wouldn’t be the type of all-out liquidity bazooka that we saw in March 2020, but rather would look more like the repo spike of 2019, where a mechanical issue seemingly came out of nowhere and caused a sharp re-liquification to occur. Such an event in Treasuries or adjacent markets could make a local top in yields and the dollar, and an upward spike in risk assets, at least for a period.

Or it could be political. A catalyst could be the US Treasury launching their bond-buyback program that I discussed earlier in this report. Mid-term elections are in early November, just a few weeks away. The current administration wouldn’t mind a bounce in risk assets, right? If Secretary Yellen is going to do anything regarding Treasury market liquidity, it would make tactical sense to do it within the next two weeks.

Or maybe nothing happens, and all of this continues in the same direction despite this unusual level of one-sided positioning. In this scenario, the rubber bands get even more extreme, and the current administration does nothing to nudge a risk asset rally prior to the midterms. That’s not my base case but it’s certainly possible.

I recommend, whatever your fundamental views are on the market, to at least keep your head on a swivel for a right-tail (upward) risk asset event within the next couple of weeks. That means different things for different portfolios, but could for example mean rebalancing into some of your preferred risk assets, or mean buying a small upward hedge (call options), or mean reducing leverage if you are short, even if you remain focused on defense/bearishness overall.
