# AmerisourceBergen Corporation Deep-Dive Analysis

*Published February 2, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report provides an update on the U.S. business cycle, discusses the economic impact of the Wuhan coronavirus outbreak, makes a case for silver, and then explores a potentially undervalued health care stock that has very little cyclical exposure, offset by some additional legal exposure.

## U.S. Business Cycle Status

The economic growth rate of the United States continues to decelerate based on most metrics. It is still growing, but its rate of growth is slowing.

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy for the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms:

![Heatmap February 2 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years (20 quarters), and each column represents one of the economic indicators. Areas of red and orange indicate significant slowdowns. Areas of yellow are okay, and various shades of green indicate solid growth.

As you can see, we had a significant slowdown in the 2015/2016 timeframe, re-accelerated during 2017/2018, and since late 2018 and early 2019 we have been in another slowing phase.

**Heat Map Indicators Referenced:**

* Capital Goods Orders, Nondefense, Excluding Aircraft
* Exports of Goods and Services
* Imports of Goods and Services
* All Employees, Total Nonfarm
* 10 Year Minus 3 Month Yield Curve
* Consumer Prices, All Urban Consumers, All Items in City Average

Currently, most indicators are yellow, orange, or red. The overall GDP, industrial production, construction spending, capital expenditures, exports, imports, payrolls, and the yield curve are all slowing.

Retail sales had a great December based on current data, but everyday consumers are often the last to get the memo that the economy is slowing. As long as they have a job and receive regular paychecks, their ability to spend remains decent. Prior to almost every recession, CEO confidence surveys turn down before consumer confidence surveys do, because the CEOs know what they are about to do (reduce their workforce to cut expenses in the face of slowing growth) while their employees are blissfully unaware until it happens. That’s exactly what we have seen during this cycle as well; CEO confidence has lowered over the past year while consumer confidence has been in a high topping pattern.

The consumer price index (inflation) has been increasing and is one of the only two green areas on the heatmap for Q4 2019. Rising inflation is often associated with economic growth, but can occasionally occur during times of stagnation as well. In that environment, it is referred to as “stagflation”. In addition, like consumers, it can turn down very sharply once the more impactful aspects of an economic slowdown manifest themselves, because businesses suddenly have trouble raising prices, industrial commodity prices fall due to diminished demand, and so forth.

**Poor Q4 GDP Report Internals**

The initial GDP report for the fourth quarter of 2019 came out on Thursday. The headline annualized real GDP growth rate for the quarter was 2.1%, which means the full year 2019 came out at just 2.3% real GDP growth.

However, there are unfortunately some bearish internals to this report that are worse than the headline figure suggests. GDP consists of consumer expenditures, private investment, government spending, and net exports. The two most important components, consumer expenditures and private investment, both had low sub-2% numbers, indicating further economic deceleration. On the other hand, government spending was up, which offset a portion of those deteriorating private numbers. Lastly, net exports were up as well. This is because both imports and export fell (which is generally bearish), but imports fell more than exports, and this contributes positively to net exports and therefore GDP based on the way it is calculated.

**Yield Curve Re-Inversion**

Before recessions occur, the interest rate on long-term treasuries tends to fall below the interest rate on short-term treasuries, and this is referred to as a yield curve inversion. In a healthy bond environment, longer-term bonds with the same risk profile should pay higher yields. When the Federal Reserve responds by cutting interest rates on the short end of the curve, it generally un-inverts the yield curve.

In spring 2019, the yield curve inverted, and the Federal Reserve cut short-term rates. The long-end of the curve also rose a bit after that, and for several months, the yield curve has been un-inverted. However, in recent weeks, the bond market pushed the long end of the curve down again, which re-inverted the yield curve. The bond market is basically telling the Federal Reserve that it needs to cut rates again, and the market-driven probability of two more rate cuts in 2020 has now significantly increased from about 30% a month ago to over 80% today.

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

**Chicago PMI**

On Friday, the purchasing manager’s index of the Chicago region, which measures the health of the manufacturing sector, came in with a surprisingly poor number. The previous number for December was 48.2, which indicates mild contraction, and it has been in mild contraction almost every month since summer 2019.

The consensus expectation for this January report was that it would increase slightly to 48.8, but instead it fell sharply to 42.9. Maybe this will rebound next month, but as of this writing, it’s an awful number.

![Chicago PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-chicago-pmi.png)

**Jobless Claims**

One of the most important aspects of the economy, initial and continuing jobless claims, remained relatively stable for the past two weeks, which is positive.

After a ten-year continuous decline, these indicators have been in an apparent bottoming process for the past 18 months and started to increase late last year, but are currently back to a stable sideways pattern for the moment.

## Wuhan Coronavirus Outbreak

I sent out an [extra note](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-1-26/) last week indicating that, due to bad news associated with the Wuhan coronavirus outbreak at a time when the S&P 500 was significantly overbought based on RSI/MACD weekly technical momentum indicators, as well as with high fundamental valuations, that the overall risk level in the market has swiftly increased.

As of this writing, the S&P 500 indeed peaked a week ago and has been in a choppy corrective pattern with heightened volatility since then. We will see how this develops, but for now the market has elevated risk levels.

The impact of the virus continues to worsen, potentially beyond the scale of other recent epidemics. Small caps and foreign stocks have fared worse than the S&P 500, and January 2020 was the worst month for value stocks relative to growth stocks in 20 years.

**“Viral Epidemiology 101” and Economic Impacts**

I have relatively little to write about this topic because I’m not a viral epidemiologist, but after having read the reports from a number of viral epidemiologists, there are a few facts to point out for investment risk management purposes.

For several weeks, it has been reported that a coronavirus jumped from animals (likely bats) to humans in the city of Wuhan in China, and has since spread internationally. When novel viruses make this jump, it means it is new and that humans don’t really have collective immune defenses against them, and they can therefore become quite serious.

To set a baseline, the seasonal flu is estimated to kill about 500,000+ people worldwide in a given year. Some years are worse than others. It affects countless millions of people and usually has a low fatality rate (a small fraction of 1%), but the sheer scale of the numbers means that hundreds of thousands die, including tens of thousands in the United States. For most healthy people, it’s not considered especially dangerous as long as you rest and take it seriously. It’s extremely contagious but not very fatal, which makes it nearly impossible to contain but also not very necessary to.

On the other end of the spectrum is a disease like the SARS coronavirus, which made international headlines in 2003. Nearly 10% of infected people died, with extremely high fatality rates (~50%) for elderly people. However, fewer than ten thousand people were infected, so fewer than one thousand died. It showed symptoms prior to being contagious, which made it easy to quarantine once it was identified as a problem, which stopped it from spreading exponentially.

One of the worst pandemics in history was the 1918-1920 Spanish Flu, which infected hundreds of millions of people worldwide and killed tens of millions of them. The global population was a lot lower back then, so somewhere between 2-5% of all people on Earth died from it, which is remarkably bad considering that there was no commercial air travel or any other form of fast transportation at the time.

As of this writing, the Wuhan coronavirus has infected over 17,000 people, but the number is continuing to grow exponentially, and it is broadly considered likely that the Chinese government’s statistics are under-reporting the figures, either for political purposes or because they simply don’t have the number of testing kits available to test everyone that might have it. They are, however, taking nationwide crisis-level intervention against it, which shows that from their perspective, it’s an extremely serious problem to get control of.

The Wuhan coronavirus is contagious before showing any symptoms, which makes it very difficult to contain. The fatality rate is unclear, but based on available numbers appears to be a lot higher than the seasonal flu. So, the combination of being extremely contagious with a substantial fatality rate is alarming, and the news headlines and economic responses are contributing to a global equity market sell-off.

In response, China has several cities including Wuhan on lockdown, quarantining tens of millions of people. Even in other cities that are not locked down at the epicenter of the problem, schools are broadly closed, people are generally remaining indoors or wearing masks if outside, and the massive annual Chinese lunar new year celebration is basically cancelled. Overall, provinces in China representing about two-thirds of the country’s GDP have told people to stay home and not resume working until mid-February or so. Daily train passengers are reportedly down about 75% countrywide, and rail is a primary transportation system in China.

China is by far the world’s biggest commodity consumer. For example, they account for about half of all copper demand worldwide in a given year, with the rest of the world combined representing the other half. The same is roughly true for steel, zinc, aluminum, and so forth.

The price of oil dropped almost 20% over the past month. A large portion of junk-rated debt in the United States is with small troubled shale producers, and a big decline in oil prices is the last thing they need. We’ll likely see an increased rate of bankruptcies this year in that industry, which was due to occur anyway.

The price of copper has had 12 consecutive days of declines, which is the longest consecutive streak on record going back 50 years. It’s extremely oversold on the daily chart, but there hasn’t been even one day of dip-buying yet:

![Copper Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-copper-technicals.png)

I suggest keeping an eye on this chart. Copper and copper producers could become interesting when the dust settles.

People in multiple other countries have now become sick with the virus as well. The World Health Organization and the U.S. Center for Disease Control have both declared the virus to be an emergency. Dozens of major countries have closed their borders and flight routes to China, including the United States as of Friday.

Each year, China sends out about 150 million tourists to places around the world (more than the entire population of Germany and the United Kingdom combined), but that’s basically on hold for now. Tourism-related stocks are getting hurt, including cruise operators that I’ve otherwise been bullish on. Apple, Starbucks, and McDonalds have closed many of their Chinese locations, Disney has closed their resorts there, and many offices for U.S. companies in China ranging from Tesla to Alphabet have closed.

China has vowed to do a large liquidity injection this week as it re-opens its stock exchanges. We’ll see if things start to bounce and stabilize, or if selling pressure continues.

When all is said and done, we’ll see how damaging the virus itself was. With these lockdown measures, it might be contained mainly to China and a handful of quarantined cases elsewhere. Even if it ends up not being as bad as some fear it may be, the response to contain it by basically shutting down the Chinese economy should have a negative impact on the global economy in Q1 2020. We may find some very interesting opportunities in these hard-hit sectors related to travel, commodities, emerging markets, and other areas.

At its worst, the virus itself could be quite impactful and start taking root in other countries (rather than just isolated and quarantined cases), but it’s too early to say. Apart from the clear humanitarian implications, this virus occurred at a weakening point for the global economy with its epicenter in one of the largest and most financially leveraged nations.

## ETF Spotlight: SIVR

Silver is one of my favorite commodities at current price levels, as it is historically cheap compared to gold and represents exposure to both precious metals and industrial metals. It is increasingly used in circuits, solar panels, electric vehicles, and so forth, while also being a store of value or jewelry material for its precious qualities.

Historically, whenever the [cyclically-adjusted price-to-earnings ratio (CAPE)](https://www.lynalden.com/shiller-pe-cape-ratio/) of the S&P 500 has been as high as it is currently, silver and gold have done rather well in relative terms compared to stocks over the next ten years period. In the following chart, the blue line represents the inverse CAPE ratio (the cyclically-adjusted earnings yield) while the gray bars represent the ten-year forward outperformance of stocks over silver (or underperformance in the case of negative bars):

![Silver vs CAPE](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-silver-cape.jpg)

In other words, stocks usually outperform precious metals, but when the S&P 500 reached historically high valuation levels in the late 1920’s, late 1960’s, and late 1990’s, that performance gap disappeared and usually reversed in favor of precious metals.

Like gold and bonds, silver became technically overbought in August 2019, but has since consolidated sideways. I am bullish on it for the multi-year long-term from current levels due to valuation characteristics. I have less conviction in the shorter term, but its technicals have improved as of late, so I consider it to be a reasonable technical set-up for the intermediate term as well.

![Silver Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-silver-technicals.png)

The Aberdeen Standard Physical Silver Shares ETF (SIVR) is one of the easiest ways to maintain exposure to the metal. Physical coins or bars are a good method for very long-term holding as well.

Very aggressive investors might consider silver miners via the Global X Silver Miners ETF (SIL), but the volatility is extreme and it is best kept to a rather small position if invested in at all.

I have some individual silver miners as very small positions within my newsletter portfolio, including Pan American Silver (PAAS), which is up 82% during my holding period. I also have First Majestic Silver (AG) which is up about 60%, Fortuna Silver Mines (FSM) which is up 37%, and Wheaton Precious Metals (WPM) which is up 32%. These were added within the past year, so the gains have been remarkable. The volatility goes both ways though; these stocks can easily get cut in half if silver falls sharply, and thus are riskier than the metal itself.

Silver itself is the safer way to get exposure than the miners, even though it too can be quite volatile. Unlike miners, silver itself can’t really go to zero and is less likely to cause unrecoverable capital damage when bought at reasonable prices, but it can have multi-year periods of underperformance. I like it currently as part of a diversified portfolio.

## AmerisourceBergen Corporation (ABC) Deep Dive Analysis

AmerisourceBergen Corporation (ticker: ABC) is one the three primary drug distributors in the United States, along with McKessen (MCK) and Cardinal Health (CAH).

They serve as the highly-regulated healthcare logistics middlemen to transport tens of billions of dollars of medicines from their sources to their destinations, including pharmacies, hospitals, and care centers. Here’s how ABC describes their company in their 10-k:

> AmerisourceBergen is one of the largest global pharmaceutical sourcing and distribution services companies, helping both healthcare providers and pharmaceutical and biotech manufacturers improve patient access to products and enhance patient care. We deliver innovative programs and services designed to increase the effectiveness and efficiency of the pharmaceutical supply chain in both human and animal health. More specifically, we distribute a comprehensive offering of brand-name, specialty brand-name, and generic pharmaceuticals, over-the-counter healthcare products, home healthcare supplies and equipment, outsourced compounded sterile preparations, and related services to a wide variety of healthcare providers located in the United States and select global markets, including acute care hospitals and health systems, independent and chain retail pharmacies, mail order pharmacies, medical clinics, long-term care and alternate site pharmacies, physician practices, medical and dialysis clinics, veterinarians, and other customers. Additionally, we furnish healthcare providers and pharmaceutical manufacturers with an assortment of related services, including data analytics, outcomes research, reimbursement and pharmaceutical consulting services, niche premium logistics services, inventory management, pharmacy automation, pharmacy management, and packaging solutions.

The company has raised dividends for 14 consecutive years, and recently announced an increase in their dividend from $0.40/quarter to $0.42/quarter.

![ABC Price and Dividend Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-abc-price-dividends.png)

**Management and Performance**

ABC and MCK both have had particularly strong fundamentals, while CAH has been the underperformer. The long-term performance differential of ABC and MCK over the S&P 500 and CAH has been remarkable:

![ABC Outperformance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-abc-vs-spx.png)

ABC has the highest credit rating of the three, and has a very strong balance sheet with nearly as much cash as debt.

About 1/3rd of ABC’s business is with Walgreens with a contract that currently extends to 2026, and Walgreens owns a minority of ABC’s stock. There was a time not that long ago when it was considered probable that Walgreens would acquire ABC, but that never materialized.

**Valuation Analysis**

ABC stock currently trades at an adjusted P/E ratio of about 12, which is below its long-term historical average of over 16.

Its chart pattern is what I refer to as a “fundamental cross”. A fundamental cross is when the price of a stock gets ahead of fundamental earnings growth (overvalued), and then chops along sideways or down for a few years as earnings eventually catch up and surpass the stock price. In this sense, the fundamentals eventually catch up and “cross” the stock price to the upside. If this continues, the fundamentals eventually drag the stock price up to break its consolidation pattern.

![ABC Stock FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-abc-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

You’ll notice in the F.A.S.T. Graph above that ABC’s earnings are not very cyclical. Healthcare fundamentals in general are quite removed from the credit cycle in general, even if their stock prices will fluctuate somewhat with the broader market. During recessions, people’s healthcare needs don’t change.

However, ABC stock as well as its two primary competitors became overvalued in 2014. It corrected sharply downward for a year, and then chopped along sideways for four years as per-share earnings and dividends continued to increase, forming a fundamental cross. McKessen has an even sharper fundamental cross, and I like both stocks currently.

Investors often blame the company for their stock going sideways like this, even though the fundamental performance of the company is fine and growing. It’s just the stock that got ahead of itself. Many investors likely got excited about the free cash flow jump in 2014/2015, but free cash flow is inherently choppy and vulnerable to various one-time impacts.

**Risks**

ABC is not very exposed to the business cycle in a traditional sense, but it has other risks. In addition, even if its fundamentals are not historically sharply affected by recessions, its stock price can still be sold off as part of broad-based index fund selling during bear markets, although this tends to happen less strongly for them.

*Potential Health Care Reform*

Americans pay by far the highest costs for healthcare in the world as a percentage of GDP and on a per-capita basis, and yet our average life expectancy is lower and our infant mortality rate is higher than most of our developed country peers. (In fact, our infant mortality rate is roughly tied with Serbia and worse than Cuba and Belarus, [according to the CIA World Factbook](https://www.cia.gov/library/publications/the-world-factbook/rankorder/2091rank.html).)

In addition, health care costs continue to expand at a rate much faster than the official inflation rate:

![Healthcare Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-healthcare-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Because it is both essential and expensive, healthcare affordability is an increasingly big topic for both voters and politicians.

The biggest investment risk area from politics is for healthcare insurers, because some political candidates have single payer healthcare as part of their platforms, which if enacted would take business from those companies and essentially expand Medicare to more people. A less significant proposal is a public option, which would not replace private insurers but would likely take market share from them.

Pharmaceutical companies and medical device companies would likely be less affected than insurers under such a scenario, but could face higher pricing pressures, potentially reducing their margins. Many healthcare companies charge higher prices in the United States for the same product compared to their prices overseas, including in other fully developed countries, and that tactic might come under fire from major or minor healthcare reform proposals.

The drug distributors like ABC could also face pricing pressure, but as I’ll show in the next section, their margins are already razor thin. There’s not a lot of money to save there.

*Margin Pressure*

Drug distribution has low margins, moderate returns on invested capital, and solid evidence of an economic moat. This makes it a bit odd, because usually companies with strong economic moats and decent returns on invested capital have thicker profit margins.

The three big players control about 90% of the market share for drug distribution in the United States, and their large logistics infrastructure creates high barriers to entry, which is what gives them a moat. However, between those three big players, their services are very commodity-like, meaning that they compete significantly on price and efficiency, which drives margins down. The industry is basically working as it should, in that sense.

All three of the major drug distributors have operating profit margins ranging from 1-2%, and net profit margins well under 1% in most years. For example, ABC does about $182 billion in revenue per year but pulls in about $2 billion in operating profit, and then after taxes and various impacts, they keep only a portion of that.

![ABC Stock Margin](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-2-abc-margin.png)

In fact, the net profits of all three major drug distributors combined is about 0.1% of U.S. annual healthcare spending. So, this is not likely a source of major cost savings when it comes to any potential healthcare reforms that the United States might attempt over the next decade.

However, investors must be aware of how thin the profit margins are for this industry, and this plays a role in why free cash flow tends to be choppy for them. The top two companies have shown for decades that they can reliably outperform, but it’s not something to take for granted.

*Opioid Lawsuits*

The most challenging risk to quantify for ABC stock is related to the opioid epidemic in the United States. Over the past five years or so, the number of people dying each year from prescribed painkiller addiction and overdose has gone up dramatically, and now more people die from this than from car accidents.

Lawsuits have already targeted doctors and drugmakers for improper prescriptions, bribery from drugmakers to doctors to prescribe them more, and so forth. Some truly awful practices have been discovered and addressed.

The three major drug distributors including ABC have also been targeted by lawsuits. They don’t prescribe or make the painkillers, but they transport them from the source to the destination and can track volumes and other detailed information, and may have been able to do more to address this problem, especially for specific towns where a large increase in opioid use has been identified. Higher prescription volumes means more money for the drug distributors; did they not notice the problem areas within their analytics, or did they notice them but choose to do little to halt what makes them money?

Drug distributors ABC, MCK, CAH, and drugmaker TEVA reached a combined $260 million settlement in October 2019 related to the opioid problem in two Ohio counties. They face similar lawsuits from many other regions around the United States. ABC’s share of this settlement is tens of millions, which is not very significant for them, but if this happens in tens, hundreds, or more than a thousand jurisdictions around the United States, the combined cost could reach into the tens of billions of dollars, paid over a long period of time. The distributors proposed a combined $18 billion settlement paid over 18 years (which comes out to hundreds of millions per distributor per year), but it could potentially be worse or better than that.

So, ABC stock may continue to face valuation pressure until a nationwide settlement occurs. However, with a P/E ratio (adjusted for one-time items) of around 12, along with moderate growth, a strong balance sheet, and a decent dividend, the stock has so far been quite resilient to lawsuit-related news.

I would not make any drug distributor a large investment position, but as a small part of a portfolio, it is a potentially useful diversifier. Many stocks are highly-valued and/or exposed to a recession in the United States, while drug distributors instead face a very different set of risks.

**Conclusions**

The credit cycle shows continued signs of weakness according to the most recent data, and the coronavirus outbreak represents an external shock to the global system at this already fragile time. It’s hard to say how significant this shock will be.

Risks are elevated, and investors should ensure that their risk profile matches their risk tolerance. I consider precious metals and U.S. treasury bills to be useful defensive allocations at this time.

Stocks like AmerisourceBergen and McKessen represent some stock ideas that may be relatively uncorrelated with the rest of the market at this time, but they face their own challenges in the form of unclear legal issues, so like most stocks they should likely not earn an outsized position. As a small part of a well-diversified portfolio, I consider that dollar-cost averaging into them is likely to improve the risk-adjusted returns of a combined portfolio by spreading out the types of risks that one is exposed to.
