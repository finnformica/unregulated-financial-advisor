## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be rangebound. I expect this to continue into 2025 as the Fed continues to draw down its balance sheet and while the Treasury offsets it somewhat with a greater proportion of T-bill issuance to drain the reverse repo facilities back into the banking system.

![Domestic Liquidity Indicator](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-domestic-liquidity-annotated.png)

Global liquidity is looking a bit better, grinding moderately upward from a combination of gradual credit growth and the dollar index being on the lower end of its recent multi-year range.

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-global-m2.png)

Regarding the dollar index, nothing gets particularly exciting about it unless it breaks down below this current level of support, like firmly below 100.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-dxy.png)

And the dollar index is dominated by the dollar/euro pair in the weighting, which I don’t view as the most interesting pair. The dollar relative to major emerging market currencies is more useful in my view.

For example, as its exchange rate weakened to 7.4 yuan for each dollar, the Chinese currency was recently flirting with its weakest relative level since 2007. However, since July 2024 the exchange rate quickly strengthened in the yuan’s favor down to below 7, which put the currency pair back into its normal range.

For a second example, the exchange rate between the Brazilian real and the U.S. dollar reached about 5.9 back during the pandemic highs, and then spent a few years settling down and consolidating. But this calendar year was particularly weak for the Brazilian real despite having a strong current account surplus. The exchange rate weakened from 4.9 in January 2024 to over 5.7 by the end of July 2024 which is bad, and has since fallen back to around 5.4 which is better.

A lot of emerging markets have dollar-denominated debts, which is why exchange rate fluctuations can result in significant liquidity and economic impacts. In many cases, it’s not the absolute level that matters, but the rate of change of the currency pair. And so for example, Brazil’s money supply grows at a faster pace than the U.S. money supply, and so when the Brazilian real is trading sideways against the dollar on a unit-to-unit basis for a while, it actually means that the Brazilian money supply as a whole is strengthening relative to the U.S. money supply.

I have a positive view on global liquidity over the next 12-18 months, largely due to a rangebound dollar index and an uptick in foreign credit growth.

**U.S. Economy**

The U.S. economy continues to run at two speeds. Part of it is pressured by high interest rates and other variables (i.e. commercial real estate and low-income consumers), while the other part of it is unconcerned with or even benefits from high interest rates (i.e. cash-rich companies and high-income households with locked in low-rate debt).

The Atlanta Fed’s GDP Now indicator suggests some minor re-acceleration for Q3, which is the quarter that is now ending:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-gdpnow.png)

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index continues to be weak, but in rate-of-change terms it’s not as bad as it was a year or two ago:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-lei.png)

This is a topic I’ve been emphasizing for a while. That on one hand, we do have some classic recession indicators like an inverted yield curve and a nationwide unemployment rate that rose from 3.4% to 4.2%. But on the other hand, certain key parts of the economy already showed a lot of recession indicators in 2022 and if anything are showing signs of gradually emerging from that now. And the way that I tie these views together is to say that recessions look different during periods of fiscal dominance. When a country is running 6% of GDP deficits during maximum employment, they’re basically pre-stimulating before any recession fully emerges.

I described it in more detail in my report from four weeks ago:

> There’s a classic Phil Knight (co-founder and former CEO of Nike) quote that goes something like, “everyone was looking for the next Michael Jordan on the basketball court, but he was walking down the fairway.” In other words, everyone was wondering who the next generationally dominant basketball player would be, assuming that would be the next big and marketable athlete, when instead the next generationally dominant and highly marketable athlete was Tiger Woods, in golf. Everyone was looking for a repeat of the previous thing, rather than the next thing.
>
> Analysts have been trying to forecast or spot a recession in the United States for years, looking for the classic disinflationary recession. And maybe we’ll still get one in the year ahead. But fiscal dominance is a different environment than the past four decades in the United States, and in many ways the United States already went through an emerging market style recession in 2022. It was one where the pain was less concentrated but more widespread. Where it didn’t show up in the unemployment rate, but rather showed up in a significant subset of the population having paychecks that don’t go as far, which doesn’t necessarily show up in GDP, especially since government spending is a component of GDP. But it does show up when you look at those who don’t own a house and don’t have a sizable asset portfolio specifically.
>
> This also showed up in lending conditions. Banks already went through a credit tightening cycle in 2022 similar to that of a recession, but without having an official recession (which are marked in gray on the chart):
>
> ![Senior Loan Officer Survey](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-sloos-annotated.png)
>
> As we perform analysis in this unusual economic environment, it pays to make sure we’re treating it with fresh eyes each time. By many metrics, ironically other than the traditional developed market metrics, we’ve a*lready* had a recession, and are arguably still in the later stages of it. But instead of showing up as a credit event and unemployment increase, it showed up as inflation and major affordability problems.
>
> -The actual unemployment rate is rising, but from a low level, and that still looks like an early recession indicator. And the Fed is preparing to cut interest rates in response to that, which is also an early recession indicator.
>
> -Credit conditions are easing a bit but still tight, and look how they typically look shortly *after* a recession or in the later stages of one.
>
> -The most expensive stocks currently are generally the defensive non-cyclical ones. The strong performance of the S&P 500 as of late is not because investors are crowding into bullish cyclical stocks on a strong economy; they’ve instead crowded into wide-moat “sure thing” risk-off stocks with low earnings volatility, such as big tech and companies like Costco (COST). The only cyclical area that’s performed particularly well is semiconductors, due to the momentum around AI. The overall S&P 500, inclusive of reinvested dividends, has mildly underperformed gold over the past three years. This looks kind of late-stage recession too.
>
> I continue to view this economy as likely to surprise people in multiple directions. Being familiar with fiscal dominance and emerging market recessions can help us see some of the unusual characteristics of this cycle, and navigate through it.
>
> -September 1st report

I re-iterated this view in the report two weeks ago, and emphasized that the rate cuts that central banks are now doing should positively impact these weaker areas more than the areas that have held up well:

> But for both the economy and the stock market, I think it’s better to look at specifics. Which sectors of the economy have *already* gone through a recession or malaise? Right now, the answer is real estate, manufacturing, banks, and several emerging markets. They’re the areas that have more to gain from Fed rate cuts than the mega-cap cash-rich defensive stocks. And they’re the areas that few investors are excited to own.
>
> -September 15th report

Now that the Fed is firmly on a rate-cutting cycle, and China is putting more focus on growth and stimulus, I think it continues to strengthen the case for a two-speed economy and potential rotations for which parts of the economy hold up better than others.

**Chinese Stimulus**

In a recent few reports, I’ve been emphasizing the importance of China’s sluggish economy and noting that they have lot of stimulus levers to pull if they get to a point where they want to.

> I view China as the #1 variable to watch regarding global macro cycles, even more-so than the U.S. Federal Reserve. Whether the Fed cuts 25 basis points or 50 basis points is somewhat meaningful, but how long China’s leaders consider the cost/benefit analysis of remaining in a malaise of deleveraging versus performing a pivot and stimulating out of it, is likely more important.
>
> -September 15th report

Well, in this past week, Chinese stocks had the biggest weekly gain since 2008 due to a series of monetary and fiscal stimulus announcements. As reported by [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/markets/asia/chinas-central-bank-cuts-banks-reserve-requirement-ratio-by-50-bps-2024-09-27/) and [FT](https://www.ft.com/content/3fab1fa7-3630-4c8f-91f5-dde0b321cd3e) and others, China cut interest rates to record low levels and announced fiscal stimulus for the stock market and for consumer housing support.

So far, the amount of announced stimulus is equivalent to a few hundred billion dollars. So it’s not trivial, but it’s not a trillion-dollar stimulus or anything like that. However, the signal is important. I’ve been closely watching for Chinese authorities’ pain point, and it seems we’ve found it. There are certain levels they don’t want stocks to go below, and there are certain depths of consumer confidence that they don’t want the economy to fall into, even as they do try to deleverage their real estate sector and internationalize their bond market.

My base case is that the stimulus will take longer to play out than many people think, but will be more persistent and longer-lasting than people think. If the Chinese economy remains in a malaise and asset prices drift back down or sideways, I think it’s likely that we’ll see more stimulus announcements. And upon the announcement of the stimulus efforts, their exchange rate strengthened, rather than weakened.

This happens with the United States sometimes too, even though it can seem counterintuitive. One would think that an announcement that currency debasement is going to uptick would be bearish for a currency. However, if it entices investors to be more optimistic on that jurisdiction’s economic prospects or financial asset performance, it can entice investors to buy back into that financial system, thereby strengthening the currency.

And this has significant implications for markets outside of China. China is the largest trading partner with the majority of countries, and is a major economic engine in the world. So this continues to be worth watching as much as, if not more than, the Fed.

I continue to be bullish on emerging market equities, including Chinese equities, with the caveat that positions sizes have to be managed due to geopolitical tail risks.

A lot of Chinese stocks have adopted shareholder-friendly policies amid the long bear market there, like JD’s $5 billion buyback [announcement](https://www.reuters.com/markets/deals/chinas-jdcom-announces-5-bln-share-buyback-plan-2024-08-27/). Valuations in general are low, and many pools of capital consider Chinese assets to be uninvestable. And over a long enough timeline, maybe they’ll be proven right due to some geopolitical event, similar to what happened to investors in Russian assets after Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine. But if that’s the case, I’d be concerned about a lot of American companies with significant operations in China as well, which the market seems unconcerned by.

Therefore, I view it as a pricing mismatch. I am bullish with a 3+ year view on Chinese equities, with a caveat about tail risk and position sizing.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Since March of this year, bitcoin has been consolidating with lower highs and lower lows. The daily high in March reached over $73k, while the daily low reached down to approximately the $53k range a few times.

Recently, bitcoin touched back over $66k, which broke the pattern of lower-highs, since that high exceeded its August high. With bitcoin’s volatility anything is possible, but as a base case I do think we’ve seen the lows, and the next major move is more likely to be a break out of this big trading range to new highs rather than a break down of this trading range. However, I don’t have a firm timeline on that view. It could happen next week or it could be several more months of choppiness.

![BTC Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-btc-price.png)

**Liquidity Correlation Analysis**

I collaborated with Sam Callahan to [publish a research report](https://www.lynalden.com/bitcoin-a-global-liquidity-barometer/) on bitcoin’s high level of correlation with global liquidity, which I and a few other analysts have emphasized for a while.

The conclusion was that in any given 12-month period, bitcoin follows the direction of global liquidity 83% of the time, which was more often than other asset classes.

![BTC vs M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bitcoin-liquidity-btc-vs-m2.png)

![BTC vs M2 YoY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bitcoin-liquidity-btc-vs-m2-yoy.png)

![Directional Correlation with Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bitcoin-liquidity-directional-correlation-chart.png)

And then the correlation has historically been improved further by paying attention to valuation. We used market value vs realized value or “MVRV” as our preferred valuation metric, which compares the market capitalization of bitcoin to the aggregate on-chain cost basis of bitcoin. When the market capitalization of bitcoin goes up a lot faster than the cost basis, it can be rather unsustainable, and lead to a big correction and for the cost basis to catch up. We found that among the 17% of the time where bitcoin moved in a different direction than global liquidity, it was often after a major valuation excess had occurred.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-mvrv.png)

![Bitcoin Correlation with MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bitcoin-liquidity-correlation-mvrv.png)

I have a positive view on global liquidity indicators for the next 12-18 months, which contributes to me having a positive view on bitcoin price over the next 12-18 months. And because valuations are reasonable in terms of MVRV and other measures, that further supports the price-positive view.

**Shielded CSV**

Researchers from Blockstream, Alpen Labs, and ZeroSync [published](https://x.com/n1ckler/status/1837194004552655077) a new whitepaper called Shielded CSV: Private and Efficient Client Side Validation.

It’s yet another in a line of BitVM-adjacent research breakthroughs. Teams keep finding new ways to click layers or functions onto Bitcoin without the need for a soft fork, even though some soft forks would further improve those features in terms of greater efficiency or minimization of trust.

This particular approach can potentially perform 100 transactions per second with good privacy. It’ll need more development, just like the rest of BitVM does, but I continue to be optimistic that we’ll see useful BitVM-based technologies deployed in the coming years.

**Spot Bitcoin ETF Options**

The SEC [approved](https://www.reuters.com/technology/sec-approves-spot-bitcoin-etf-options-2024-09-20/) BlackRock’s spot bitcoin ETF options listing. This means that the largest and most liquid bitcoin ETF will have an options market on it, as many large ETFs do. This gives investors more ways to express a position, and should improve overall liquidity and interest in the ETF from the traditional finance sector. We’ll likely see more of the large spot bitcoin ETFs introduce options over time as well.

Options are also a key way for an ETF to build a liquidity moat. First, an ETF can gather a lot of assets under management and have higher trading volumes than its peers, which makes it more attractive to large pools of capital that need that liquidity to enter and exit positions smoothly. And then options markets are highly fragmented, with multiple different strike prices and durations. As a leading ETF builds out a more robust and liquid options listing, it goes a long way to lock in that ETF as the de-facto main way to express a trade, due to a combination of high trading volumes and decent option liquidity across a range of strike prices and durations, which is hard for competitors to replicate.

## Deep Dive: Air Products and Chemicals

Air Products and Chemicals (APD) is a large corporation that produces industrial gases and equipment for companies around the world.

Here’s how they summarize themselves in their latest annual filing:

> Air Products and Chemicals, Inc., a Delaware corporation originally founded in 1940, is a world-leading industrial gases company that has built a reputation for its innovative culture, operational excellence, and commitment to safety and the environment. Focused on serving energy, environmental, and emerging markets, we offer a portfolio of products and services that enables customers to improve their environmental performance, product quality, and productivity.
>
> \_\_\_
>
> Air Products has a sustainability-driven two-pillar growth strategy consisting of the expansion and efficient operation of our core industrial gases business and the execution of projects that provide world-scale clean hydrogen. Our industrial gases business provides essential gases, related equipment, and applications expertise to customers in dozens of industries, including refining, chemicals, metals, electronics, manufacturing, medical, and food. We also develop, engineer, build, own, and operate some of the world’s largest clean hydrogen projects that will support the transition to low- and zero-carbon energy in the heavy-duty transportation and industrial sectors. Additionally, we are the world leader in the supply of liquefied natural gas (“LNG”) process technology and equipment and provide turbomachinery, membrane systems, and cryogenic containers globally.

The method of producing and supplying the gas differs depending on the type and amount of gas. For large customers, they build gas production facilities on or near customer sites with 10-20 year contracts that provide recurring revenue. That constitutes more than half of the company’s total sales. For smaller customers, the company also sells liquid gases in cylinders and similar containers.

In terms of end-use, these gases are used by all sorts of industries. Hydrogen fuel can be used as an energy source. Other gases are used as inputs into the production of chemicals, metals, manufactured products, and semiconductors. And still others are used for cooling, preservation, oil and gas extraction, and more.

Air Products and Chemicals is a “dividend champion” which means it has raised its dividend every year consecutively for at least 25 years. In their case, they have 42 consecutive years of dividend growth through four different recessions.

**Balance Sheet Analysis**

Air Products and Chemicals has an A credit rating by S&P, which is quite strong. Its measures of debt relative to assets and income are quite reasonable. And the debt they do have is mostly locked in at low fixed rates, so they’re successfully shorting fiat currency and plowing the money into higher-returning things.

![APD Debt to Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-apd-debt-assets.png)

**Valuation Analysis**

The challenge with Air Products and Chemicals is that since it’s such a high-quality business, it’s rarely cheap.

Back in 2020, the stock became overvalued with a price/earnings ratio that exceeded 35x. The stock has now spent four years chopping sideways in a big range as its fundamentals catch up. I think that this price range has now become reasonable:

![APD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-apd-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

The cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio is currently a little over 31x, compared to the long-term average of a little under 27x.

![APD PE 5](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-apd-pe-5.png)

My preferred valuation metric for stocks like this is the dividend-adjusted price/earnings/growth “PEG” ratio.

-The price/earnings ratio is a little over 24x.

-Consensus analyst estimates are for 8% annual forward earnings growth. The dividend yield is in the 2% to 2.5% range, and so can likely get a total of over 10% dividend-adjusted earnings growth.

-This means that the dividend-adjusted PEG ratio is 24 divided by 10, or about 2.4x. I normally look for PEG ratios under 2x, and will adjust my criteria depending on the quality/risk of the stock. Since Air Products and Chemicals is a very high-quality company, I’m willing to extend my valuation threshold up to a maximum of a 2.5x PEG ratio.

-Their earnings growth over the past ten years was actually 11% per year on average. So if that rate were to continue in addition to the dividend yield of over 2%, it would bring the PEG ratio to under 2x.

My view on this valuation depends on the timeframe. If I were looking to swing trade it, I’d want to get in under 20x earnings if possible. But if I treat it as a dividend aristocrat that I want to hold and let compound for 5-10 years or more without paying capital gains taxes and so forth, up to about 25x earnings is my valuation limit.

**Risk Analysis**

Although it’s not a cyclical business for the most part, severe economic downturns can result in a year or two of fundamental stagnation or contraction for the company’s earnings. Fewer projects are brought online, some existing customers can run into financial troubles, and that ultimately shows up in the company’s bottom line if it’s a broad enough issue.

A big mitigation for that is the lack of share repurchases. Many mature blue-chip stocks have limited investment opportunities and so they plow capital back into share buybacks. APD isn’t doing that, and has a gradually rising share count over the past decade:

![ADP Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-apd-shares-outstanding.png)

If they reach a period of slower growth, they do have the option to invest in less capex and return more capital to shareholders by increasing shareholders’ ownership stake in the company via buybacks.

Commodity costs are a potential risk, but APD pushes most of that through to its customers with various contract arrangements. It’s the rare type of company where its revenue fluctuates more than its earnings, because both their revenue and costs are fluctuating with market pricing while their margins are pretty tightly controlled.

The company performs global sales, and no customer accounts for more than 10% of revenue. So, there are relatively few surprise headlines that an investor could wake up to with this company.

In my view, the biggest risk is simply that of valuation. When I pay a dividend-adjusted PEG ratio of over 2x, I acknowledge that the company’s fundamentals could do well and the valuation might soften over a period of time, resulting in lackluster stock price performance. Especially if they also run into a soft patch in earnings due to an economic slowdown.

I’d like to see the price/earnings ratio below 20x rather than around 24x, but compared to a lot of other blue-chip U.S. stocks, I think it compares favorably. For example I’d rather pay 24x earnings for Air Products and Chemicals than 34x earnings for Apple or 54x earnings for Costco, which all have similar forward earnings growth rate estimations by consensus analysts.

## Final Thoughts: Gold

Gold has been touching new all-time highs at over $2,600 per ounce, which means it has approximately doubled in price since it was added to the newsletter portfolio in late 2018.

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-29-gold.png)

I pivoted some of that bullishness toward bitcoin at the time, which was trading for under $12k, and expected bitcoin to outperform gold into late 2021, which it did (by a lot, since it went up to $69k by late 2021):

As a bull who is long gold, I hope that I’m incorrect and that it just keeps grinding higher.

I don’t really plan to trade around the position unless it reaches true extremes; I structurally prefer gold over long-duration Treasuries with a 5+ year view.

Meanwhile, bitcoin has been consolidating since March of this year. So I like that mix of bitcoin and gold, because the two assets often undergo price pumps and price consolidations at different times.
