## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

As I’ve been covering for months, domestic liquidity has been trending sideways because the Treasury has been emptying its Treasury General Account into the financial system (which is positive for liquidity), and this has offset the Fed’s quantitative tightening (which is negative for liquidity).

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-domestic-liquidity.png)

However, the Treasury General Account is now nearly empty, and so we’re getting close to “crunch time”. If the debt ceiling is lifted, and the Treasury issues more bonds and refills its Treasury General Account, then along with the Fed’s ongoing QT, that could be a very bad liquidity situation. Both the Treasury and the Fed would be performing actions that are negative for liquidity.

In other words, the last thing that investors want is for the blue line to start going up while the red line is still going down:

![TGA + Fed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-tga-vs-fed.png)

I don’t think such a liquidity problem would last very long due to the damage it would cause, and there are ways to temporarily avoid it (e.g. the Treasury could issue debt primarily in the form of T-bills to suck liquidity out of the reverse repo facility), but this has to be watched by tactical investors. Liquidity may get messy and force some changes by the Fed or Treasury to navigate it.

**Debt Ceiling Updates**

Over this weekend, President Biden and Speaker McCarthy reached a deal to raise the debt ceiling. It entails relatively minor spending cuts, and restarts student loan repayments which have been deferred for some years now. They still have to convince enough House representatives to agree to it, which will likely come with some degree of friction. Some of the most right-leaning members of Congress don’t think the spending cuts went far enough, and some of the most left-leaning members of Congress think Biden cut some important things too much.

Overall, it looks likely that they’ll get the agreement through the House, but it’s not assured.

If they don’t get enough votes to pass the deal, then the Treasury could run out of cash in early June. However, by mid-June, some revenue will come in from quarterly tax payments. So, if they can avoid running out of cash for those first two weeks of June, then they can extend their runway a bit further. That’s why there are some pretty wide estimates for how long the Treasury can last without running out of cash; it depends on whether they can make it long enough for those mid-June quarterly tax receipts to come in.

If the Treasury runs out of cash without the debt ceiling having been raised, the government has a choice on which payments to miss. They could start skipping some Social Security payments, Medicare payments, military payments, or Treasury security payments (with the latter being a credit default).

Alternatively, the Treasury has some unlikely and wonky measures that they could turn to at the 11th hour if they really want to. For example, they can fill up their Treasury General Account by changing the official price of gold on its existing gold certificates (currently locked at $42.22/ounce for the past 50 years, despite major changes to the actual market price of gold since then). This is basically a more sophisticated version of the “[trillion dollar platinum coin](https://www.lynalden.com/sovereign-default/)” trick. In other words, Secretary Yellen could basically say “Our official price of gold is $2,000 now, up from $42.222, and so we’ve added $509 billion to our general account to reflect this adjustment to our 260 million ounces worth of gold certificates. Thanks for playing.”

> #### 2.10 Gold Certificate Account (110-025)
>
> The Secretary of the Treasury is authorized to issue gold certificates to the Reserve Banks to monetize gold held by the U.S. Department of the Treasury (Treasury). At any time, Treasury may reacquire the gold certificates by demonetizing the gold.
>
> Treasury maintains an account with the Board of Governors entitled “Gold certificate fund—Board of Governors of the FR System.” When the Treasury monetizes gold, it credits this account in return for deposit credit at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York (FRBNY). When demonetizing gold, Treasury decreases the account and authorizes the FRBNY to charge its deposit account. The offsetting entry in each case on the FRBNY’s books is made to the Gold Certificate account and the U.S. Treasury—general account. The FRBNY accounting staff sends an advice of these entries to the Board. Also, whenever the official price of gold is changed, Treasury adjusts the account and, simultaneously, the deposit account.
>
> –[Financial Accounting Manual for Federal Reserve Banks](https://www.federalreserve.gov/aboutthefed/chapter-1-balance-sheet.htm)

An increase in the official price of gold would create spendable dollars without creating debt, and would be significantly pro-liquidity. In other contexts, filling up the Treasury General Account is negative for liquidity because it sucks money out of the financial system, but in this unique case it would create dollars ex-nihilo within the Treasury General Account, which they could then continue spending into the financial system. It would be a glitch in the Matrix, as far as financial plumbing is concerned.

Of course, such an action would be historically noteworthy, and thus can’t be expected as the base case. It depends on how hard the Biden administrative wants to negotiate if debt ceiling negotiations somehow fall through, compared to how little they dare to open up some *very* old monetary tools that come with reputational/geopolitical ramifications.

**Bitcoin Conference**

In mid-May I attended and spoke at the Bitcoin Conference in Miami, which is the largest bitcoin conference in the world (and more broadly, one of the largest finance or tech conferences in the world).

In addition to seeing the latest news from the Open Source Stage (the subset of the conference that is focused on the latest tech developments), it’s a good event for networking with VCs, developers, and business leaders to see what the latest trends are within the ecosystem. Here are some of the major takeaways I had from the conference:

*Bipartisan Interest*

A number of political candidates and ex-politicians were among the speakers on the main stage, and they came from both major parties. Two presidential candidates (one Democrat, one Republican) used the opportunity to raise funds over the Lightning network. By displaying a QR code to the audience on a presentation, audience members could use their mobile Lightning wallets to immediately send them funds during their speech and/or when watching the video later.

Right ahead of the conference, Jason Maeir published his new book, [A Progressive’s Case for Bitcoin](https://www.amazon.com/Progressives-Case-Bitcoin-Equitable-Peaceful/dp/B0C1J3DC2X?&_encoding=UTF8&tag=lynalden-20&linkCode=ur2&linkId=fed0037bffe98642017a7876036051c3&camp=1789&creative=9325), from his perspective as someone on the political left who views Bitcoin as an important and ethical technology. Bitcoin has historically not been particularly embraced by the American left, but there are a number of proponents like him that have been interested in changing that. Over the past 18 months, there has been a growing foothold among that political community.

So far, data actually suggest that among politicians with a nationwide reputation, age is more correlated with cryptocurrency opposition/acceptance than political party. Older politicians in their late 70s and above have a higher rate of opposing it, whereas politicians that are in their 30s-60s range tend to be more accepting of it. Exceptions apply, of course.

*Ossification or Development*

Compared to other cryptocurrencies, the Bitcoin network is the most decentralized and the hardest to change. This is a key feature that makes it interesting as a form of global money (rather than a sort of equity security with a centralized issuer, which many other cryptocurrencies resemble), but it also leads to debates within the ecosystem about how to update the protocol.

The Bitcoin network has undergone a number of soft forks, including Segwit in 2017 and Taproot in 2021, that updated the code in a backwards-compatible way. These updates kept the core ruleset in place (otherwise the updates would not be possible), but they added more flexibility to the code. The bigger and more decentralized that the Bitcoin network gets over time, the harder it will probably become to make even these backwards-compatible changes to the base layer. By design, it’s really hard to change, because changes require acceptance by most miners and developers and users, after gaining consensus by most developers.

-Development proponents assert that the current status quo is not good enough to scale to billions of people, and that another soft fork or two needs to be done in order to really finish the development of the base layer of the Bitcoin network. Ossifying the network in its current state would be premature in their view, and thus would risk slowing down adoption and driving development elsewhere. Some of these proposed soft forks can scale the network and make it easier for smaller bitcoin holders to self-custody their bitcoin in various ways.

-Ossification proponents assert that the base layer shouldn’t be tinkered with anymore other than to fix critical bugs if needed. Updates that reduce miner fees due to extensive scaling, or updates that risk causing unexpected bugs, should be avoided in their view because it threatens the underlying stability of the entire network which is already in a strong place. Therefore, further development should be limited to higher layers on top of the network rather than on the base layer, so that risk can be compartmentalized by those putting the capital to work.

To the extent that another major soft fork is possible, there is growing interest in a soft fork that would add covenants to Bitcoin. This would add a bit more programmability to transactions, and enable new scaling features. A new second-layer network called [Ark](https://www.arkpill.me/) was proposed at the conference, which requires a soft fork for covenants to reach its full potential. Ark has some pros and cons relative to the Lightning network, and is interoperable with the Lightning network. However, it remains to be seen if developers will be able to form a consensus on which specific covenants-related soft fork to push forward, and then if the majority of miners and nodes will agree to it or not. Beyond that, there are other future soft fork potentials that would enable zk rollups and other scaling methods to the base layer.

This is a trend I’ll be watching closely.

*Fedi Alpha*

I’m an advisor to [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/portfolio), and one of our portfolio companies, [Fedi](https://www.fedi.xyz/), announced the launch of the alpha version of their product at the conference, which I’ve had the opportunity to test. The scale of what Fedi is building is incredible.

-Rather than a mere wallet, Fedi is an application that allows communities to set up their own federated wallet. So, Fedi doesn’t hold user funds, but rather they provide the software for various communities around the world to easily set up their own community bank and hold user funds. They’ve dramatically increased the efficiency and lowered the cost of communities setting up their own local bank.

-It takes minutes for the creators of a new federation to set one up, and it takes seconds for a user to join an existing federation. From within the Fedi app, a user can be a part of many different federations.

-Communities for which a federation can be created can be local and physical (e.g. a town in South Africa), can be online and remote (e.g. a distributed group online associated with some shared interest or organization), can be associated with a corporation (e.g. like how a company may offer a credit union for its employees), can be set up for a temporary event (e.g. a conference or festival), or whatever else people may decide to use it for.

-In addition to Bitcoin/Lightning wallet functionality, a federation comes with in-built chat features for a true community experience. This is something that has been lacking in many other wallet designs. People can discuss things in group chats or direct messages, and they can easily send or receive payments from others embedded in those chats.

-Federations come with inbuilt privacy for the user, unlike most custodial wallets. However, some federations can elect to operate in a non-private way for various reasons as well.

-Fedi is built on the open-source protocol called Fedimint. Various developers can add “modules” to Fedimint. These can include all sorts of different features like stablecoin contracts, programmability options, data storage, etc. Communities can customize their federation when they set it up, and therefore can make it very simple or quite complex, depending on the needs of their community. The combination of Bitcoin/Lightning wallet, chat, data storage, and other functionality is very powerful, especially in environments where access to such technology is prohibitively expensive.

Along those lines, in my opinion the most interesting use-case is within developing countries. Many people in developing jurisdictions face rapid currency devaluation and limited banking access. It’s often not profitable to administer small bank accounts, and authoritarian countries often freeze bank accounts or impose capital controls on people. With Fedi, a user can join what is basically a community bank with a simple QR code rather than an extensive administration process. It’s an example of software making financial services radically easier to interact with, but without the centralized and inflationary methods that are most often used in developing countries.

Overall, Fedi can be thought of as a federated operating system. Those who run a federation with their computers offer services to users, who in many places might only have a smart phone.

## AI Impacts on Equities

I’ve not written much about AI yet because I’ve been researching and testing it amid the hype. The topic of AI gets a lot of clickbait, but I try to be “thoughtful” rather than “first” when it comes to emerging and hype-filled trends, to see what has legs vs what is likely a fad.

I’ll write more about it in upcoming newsletters and articles, but for now I’ll give my initial impressions to subscribers.

**Technology Through Step-Change**

When we think of technological development, we often imagine a smooth exponential line upwards.

In reality, technological development is mostly a series of overlapping step-changes. Certain technological breakthroughs occur, cause a multi-decade technological boom, and then run into a hard ceiling and stagnate, until the next breakthrough emerges.

Often, the discovery and application of a more concentrated energy source serves as a big step-change. The invention of the steam engine and the associated mining of coal ushered in the industrial revolution, which then was compounded by the invention of the internal combustion engine and the associated production of oil. This gave humanity enough energy density for high-speed travel, broadly available running water, and the mechanization of agriculture. All of these things freed up most people to work on other things and to have more sanitary living conditions.

In the medical industry, the invention of antibacterial medicine, anesthesia with surgical techniques, and vaccines against diseases like polio massively increased survivability into old age during the early 20th century. Eventually our capabilities kind of stagnated, as we haven’t really increased the maximum lifespan but have increased the percentage of people that make it into their 70s and older. One day, perhaps gene editing and age-reversing technologies will create another big step-change towards much longer maximum lifespans.

Humans spent thousands of years wanting to fly without much progress at all. In the 1700s, people made the hot air balloon, followed by hydrogen/helium airships, and then stagnated for a while. The combination of hydrocarbon and aluminum production finally allowed humans to create a true flying machine in the form of an airplane, and due to the technological boom that ensued, just decades later we had safe commercial trans-continental flights and human space exploration. But then the technology ran into a practical ceiling and development slowed down, with an inability to maintain economic supersonic commercial flight, and an inability to maintain the prior pace of human space exploration.

The discovery of electricity and application of it, along with telecommunication systems, was another massive source of innovation which further automated our surroundings and connected the whole world together. The invention of semiconductors then compounded this, giving us science-fiction level abilities to communicate with each other peer-to-peer through air, and have supercomputers in our pockets.

Artificial intelligence has been with us for a while. For over two decades, chess programs have been able to beat the best human chess champions. At first it required a large supercomputer to beat the best human champion, but as technology improved, it could be done by a normal desktop computer, and now can be done by a mobile phone. The field of machine learning has continued to advance, with pockets of commercial relevance but nothing groundbreaking up until recently. Now, it seems to have finally hit broad commercial relevance, like when engineers put hydrocarbons and aluminum together and opened up all sorts of new possibilities for flight. Specifically, computers and software have reached a tipping point where we can apply AI to all sorts of different software tasks, and it can learn and adjust quickly to substantially impact how we work.

**10x Accessibility**

The knee-jerk reaction to AI by investors is to invest in things that are needed for AI, such as the AI software and the chips that they use. That trade is already rather crowded at this point.

Another theme to think about is to ask, “what products will become 10x more accessible thanks to AI?”

Let’s use Adobe (ADBE) as an example. Photoshop is a semi-advanced program, in that it’s not exactly rocket science to use it, but it does take a considerable amount of time to learn to use it well.

[This video](https://twitter.com/scottbelsky/status/1660992735040663553) shows how Adobe Photoshop is incorporating generative AI so that the user can easily make dramatic changes in seconds with basic text-based commands. The production of art, movies, software code, engineered designs, and all sorts of complex things are made more efficient by programs that can do many of the tedious and intricate underlying details of the work for the creator.

In addition to making each Photoshop user more productive and powerful (they can get a lot more done per unit of time), it lowers the barrier of entry for using Photoshop. Just like how computers used to be difficult to use but became easier over time, all sorts of creator applications that have historically been moderately difficult are becoming way easier.

The pessimistic case is that this type of creator AI assistance puts a percentage of creators out of work because each creator can do the work of what several creators used to do. However, that assumes that the same baseline amount of work needs to be done. The optimistic case is that by broadening access to this type of work, it grows the overall usage and demand for software-based creation. In other words, a similar or larger number of creators can do an order of magnitude more creation work, rather than the same amount of creation work being done by an order of magnitude fewer people.

Imagine being able to pick up any creator application (videos, art, music, programming, 3D printing, engineering software, and so forth) and through verbal or written commands, intuitively use it to make what you want. This dramatically lowers the barrier of entry and allows the companies that make those creator applications able to sell far more subscriptions to far more users. Basically anyone can be a creator now or in a couple years for work or fun, and do what would have required an expert ten years ago. There will still be more experienced users than others, but the overall pie of who can effectively use advanced creator software can go up dramatically.

**Flip the Question Upside Down**

In addition to asking the question, “what will benefit from AI?” we need to understand what will be disrupted by AI. This can lead us to a set of investments that, while they might not benefit greatly from AI, also *won’t* be heavily disrupted by it. They will continue to provide good risk-adjusted returns at low valuations.

AI has the capacity to disrupt all sorts of legacy-white collar businesses. Administrative overhead within organizations can be greatly streamlined, for example. Their margin is AI’s opportunity to eat.

Unless or until AI helps us solve some foundational physics problems, however, it’ll likely to be slower to affect the physical world than the digital world. What I mostly mean by this is that the production and distribution of energy and materials remains a key limitation.

Within say a ten-year time horizon, AI has all sorts of capacities to change how we interact with digital systems, but is unlikely to change energy and materials markets much, other than giving us small or moderate efficiencies around the margins (e.g. better software applications for finding resource deposits, better and faster designs for equipment, and cutting down on administrative overhead costs for energy/materials corporations).

To put it another way, the field of robotics isn’t easy. Robots are complex, energy-intensive, materials-intensive, and maintenance-intensive. As a simple example, robot vacuums despite being invented decades ago, still aren’t everywhere, because they still have all sorts of [limitations](https://medium.com/@jaimecloudman/irobot-employee-cleans-store-with-mop-and-contradiction-goes-viral-14e107aae0c1). Other types of robots are 5x or 10x more complex than that and thus have greater constraints when it comes to totally replacing humans.

People have historically overestimated the speed of hardware development (supersonic planes, flying cars, robot assistants) and underestimated the speed of software development. I suspect the same will be continued over the next decade; software will absolutely astound us in the years ahead, and yet our physical world will remain remarkably similar to how it is now with only minor changes. I would adjust this base expectation only if I saw some major physics breakthrough that starts to suggest otherwise.

**Inflation is a Vector**

For decades, the combination of offshoring and automation of manufacturing has pushed the cost of goods down. Shoes, textiles, electronics, plastic goods, and similar items have decreased dramatically in price compared to most other things. Price inflation has primarily been limited to services, which could not be as readily automated.

AI over the coming years and decades is likely to give us a lot of price deflation in services. AI can eventually allow one accountant to do the work of several accountants, one executive assistant to do the work of several executive assistants, one graphic designer to do the work of several graphic designers, one programmer to do the work of seeral programmers, and so forth. It’s hard to predict how fast and widespread the impact will be but that’s clearly the direction.

We should have greater abundance ahead, in terms of creating things efficiently and running a business efficiently.

The problem, however, is that the growth of money supply is likely to remain significant. Debt-to-GDP and Debt-to-Money-Supply ratios remain very high. It’s hard to sustain the current levels of government, corporate, and household debt without greater amounts of broad and base money.

From the start of 2000 to the end of 2022, we can see a significant divergence in asset and consumer prices. The gold price increased by 8.3% per year. The broad money supply per capita grew at 6.8% per year. The price of hospital services grew by 5.3% per year. The oil price grew at 4.7% per year. The median house price grew by 4.7% per year. Childcare prices grew by 4.2% per year. Average hourly wages earnings for non-supervisory workers went up by 3.2% per year. Official consumer price inflation averaged 2.6% per year. The average bank account had a yield of less than 2% per year. Apparel was flat in price. Electronics, plastic toys, and software of all sorts dropped in price.

Truly scarce things like bitcoin, fine art, gold, and waterfront properties kept up with and surpassed official consumer price inflation. Semi-scarce things we don’t know how to easily make more of such as hospital services and childcare services and oil and the median house went up a bit slower but still above the rate of inflation. The official inflation number was weighed down by all sorts of deflationary items that were assisted by exponential technology that makes them nearly costless to produce.

AI may exacerbate this trend. AI can make several types of services cheaper and more abundant to us, while still not really changing the scarcity of certain goods and services. We continue to need to visualize inflation as a vector (to quote Michael Saylor), spread across multiple different categories.

-If someone wishes to buy prime real estate, fine art, gold, or bitcoin, their income and net worth better keep up with the growth of broad money supply.

-If someone wishes to own the median house with a good healthcare plan and retirement savings and nutritionally-dense food, their income and net worth better surpass official CPI even if it falls short of broad money supply growth.

-If someone wishes to just get by, living in multifamily housing or with their relatives, with their time spent mainly in the virtual world and with cheap consumer goods, then they can get by with less growth of income and net worth, in line with official CPI or lower.

Overall, if AI becomes economically relevant faster than many of us think, it could lower official CPI, but is unlikely to lower the effective price of the top tier of goods and services that are truly scarce and/or energy-intensive. It’s likely to make the easily-replicable things cheaper and more abundant, and to make those expensive things slightly more affordable around the margins due to greater economic efficiency at making them.

**AI and Bitcoin**

An AI agent can spin up a bitcoin/cryptocurrency wallet, earn funds, and spend funds.

AI agents are already being given bitcoin balances over Lightning, from which they can use to access services that they need to complete their assigned tasks:

![Lightning AI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-lightning-ai.png)

In that case, the AI agent is purposely set up by the creator to have some funds to spend as needed. However, it doesn’t take much creativity to imagine a sufficiently advanced AI agent being able to recreate this process from scratch. In other words, an AI could start with no money, and be given a task or just “decide” to do a task. As it assesses the best way to do that task, it determines that certain paid services would be helpful or critical to it. So, it generates a bitcoin/lightning wallet (unlike a bank account it doesn’t need permission to do this, since it’s open source), and either convinces its creator to give it funds or otherwise finds a way to earn funds online (maybe it posts a bunch of clever content on Nostr and earns tips). Now, with access to money, it pays for things it needs.

Imagine the eeriness of finding an AI agent with money as part of some middle step along the way towards completing a task that was assigned to it, and even the creators don’t really know how it got the money.

Efficient open-source digital-native money, which can be used faster, permissionlessly, and with smaller amounts (microtransactions) than existing fiat currencies, opens up more possibilities for machine-to-machine payments, machine-to-human payments, and human-to-machine payments. It is a natural type of money for AI agents to use.

**TSM vs NVDA**

One of the quintessential AI stocks is Nvidia (NVDA). It’s absolutely soaring. They design the GPUs (the computational picks and shovels) that most AI projects rely on, and they have limited competition.

![NVDA FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-nvda-fastgraph.png)

They’re roughly a trillion-dollar company now. They’re very expensive, and prone to volatility, but they can grow into that valuation (and larger) over time. Would I chase it here? Personally I would not.

A key thing to keep in mind about Nvidia is that they don’t make their own chips. They design them and then have a foundry make them, which generally is Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM). TSM’s market cap is about half as much as Nvidia, and yet Nvidia is quite reliant on TSM. TSM is currently the undisputed global champion in chip manufacturing.

![TSM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-tsm-fastgraph.png)

A key risk with TSM is that as the name implies, they’re centered in Taiwan and thus prone to tail risk associated with a Chinese takeover. We could wake up one morning and see TSM down 80% due to this sort of geopolitical headline. They’re building foundries in the United States to geographically diversify from their home base, but that’s a long and expensive undertaking.

One question investors need to ask is, what happens to Nvidia if something really bad happens to TSM? Eventually Nvidia could migrate to other foundries, but a sudden shock to TSM would be a massive blow to Nvidia. Similarly, TSM is diversifying its geographic footprint so that maybe one day they won’t be so concentrated in Taiwan, but a sudden shock would be a massive blow to them in the meantime. So Nvidia and TSM both have adjustment opportunities over time, but right now they’re joined at the hip to each other and to Taiwan.

I’ve been long both TSM and Nvidia at various points in the past, and sold them during big bubble spikes. I currently prefer TSM over NVDA.

Going long TSM with a smaller short for Nvidia could make an interesting risk-adjusted play here. This combination gives exposure to Nvidia’s GPU production (which almost inevitably is going to rise a lot), without Nvidia’s high valuation, and with a hedge against something really bad happening to TSM (since by extension that would be bad for Nvidia too).

Over the next week or two since that report, [data came out](https://asia.nikkei.com/Business/Automobiles/China-surpasses-Japan-as-world-s-top-auto-exporter) (that’s a link to a Japanese media source) that pointed to China surpassing Japan as the world’s largest auto exporter in the first quarter of this year. They’re now exporting over a million cars per quarter:

![China Auto Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-28-china-auto-exports.png)

*Source: [Caixin](https://www.caixinglobal.com/2023-04-14/chinas-car-exports-jump-nearly-60-in-first-quarter-on-nev-shipments-102018927.html)*

The exact numbers may be contested between domestic and foreign sources, but multiple foreign sources (e.g. Forbes, Nikkei) are reporting on it, and the upwards trend is clear. China is now an auto export powerhouse.

We don’t see this in the United States since we have no shortages of premium domestic and foreign (Japan, German, Korean) auto brands, but globally Chinese cars are beginning to dominate the market. This gives them yet another point of leverage when making financial deals with their economic partners. China surpassed the United States as the largest trading partner with the majority of countries years ago, and continues to solidify that trend.

China faces a considerable set of problems associated with weak demographics (they’re extremely top heavy), authoritarian governance (which is not great for innovation), and restricted geography (they’re boxed in with multiple country borders and a lack of deep-water harbors), and their global navy is lacking in terms of global projection (they have only a fraction of the aircraft carriers and similar deep water capabilities as the United States). However, China has become an industrial powerhouse that is extraordinarily expensive to replicate, they’ve cornered the market on rare earth minerals and solar panels, and they’ve secured a number of long-term global deals for commodity imports and global infrastructure development for the years and decades ahead.
