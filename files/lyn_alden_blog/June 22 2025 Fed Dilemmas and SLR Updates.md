## Macro View

**Attacks on Iran**

The attacks on Iran by Israel and the United States have done significant damage, and it remains to be seen to what extent Iran can or will retaliate.

From an investment standpoint, the Strait of Hormuz (through which a significant percentage of global oil exports are shipped) is of course the area to watch. Iran’s parliament has voted to close the strait, but the decision ultimately rests with Khamenei. Speculators on Polymarket currently place odds that Iran will close the Strait of Hormuz before July at 36% and before the end of the year at 53%.

Oil stocks have significant lack of correlation with broader equities, which is part of the reason I invest in them. In general at the current time (after a considerable wash-out from 2014 through 2020), they are profitable and inexpensive companies with solid balance sheets. I think of them as positive carry indefinite options on the prospect for higher energy prices. In a normal environment, they should perform decently, whereas in an oil shock environment where broader equities may suffer, oil stocks should do well and help buffer a portfolio.

**US Economic Update**

Economic data remain noisy here in Q2 as trade ricochets back after the front-running of Q1, which I outlined in my prior report.

The labor market is cooling, but still at a very gradual level. The tight monetary policy and the loose fiscal policy of the country continue to be at odds with each other, and so I will keep emphasizing that each sector needs to be examined separately in terms of economic forecasting. It’s like holding the gas pedal and the brake down together, spinning the wheels.

-Large fiscal deficits mainly flow toward Social Security, Interest Expense, Medicare, and Defense. Older Americans in the upper third of the income spectrum who spend substantially on travel, healthcare, and so forth are in pretty good shape financially, as are those who work in deficit-supported industries. People keep being surprised by the resilience of the consumer economy, but with 7%-of-GDP deficits we’re stimulating at a scale normally only seen in recessions, before there even is a recession.

-High interest rates and quantitative tightening are putting pressure on commercial real estate solvency, and rendering residential homes unaffordable for most. Real estate turnover is low, because nobody wants to get out of a low fixed-rate mortgage and not many people can afford homes at current higher mortgage rates, so buyers and sellers are both slow to show up.

Overall, wealthier and older individuals (and large corporations) are more likely to have had access to cheap credit a few years ago and locked in low-rate long-term debt, thus protecting them against the rise in interest rates. They’re also more likely to be on the receiving side of the ongoing deficits. Meanwhile, less wealthy and younger individuals (and small businesses) are more likely to have shorter-term debt and/or floating-rate debt that has re-adjusted to these new higher interest rates. And then if they’re not in the low income group that’s receiving Medicaid or food stamps (i.e. they’re in the broad middle), then they’re not likely on the receiving side of the fiscal deficits either.

**Domestic Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity continues to be rangebound, leaning toward positive:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-domestic-liquidity.png)

This is because with the debt ceiling still unresolved, the Treasury’s cash account continues to drain back into the financial system (ever since tax week in mid-April), which is pro-liquidity while it happens:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-tga.png)

Once the debt ceiling is resolved, the Treasury will likely move toward refilling its cash account. That’s when I would be more concerned about liquidity problems.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has been critical of prior Secretary Treasury Janet Yellen for issuing excess T-bills to fund the deficit and has vowed to extend duration. However, he hasn’t extended the duration yet, and it’ll be particularly hard to do it while refilling the cash account. It’s quite possible that they’ll refill it with excess T-bills just like Yellen did, and say the Fed’s high rates are the reason they’re not terming out the debt yet.

**Global Liquidity**

After a sharp drop, the dollar index is now meandering sideways-to-down, which mildly benefits global liquidity. All else being equal, that’s decent for international equities, gold, and bitcoin.

We’re in the fun zone, but only barely. It’ll be interesting in the coming weeks to see if the fun zone becomes overhead resistance or not:

**The Fed’s Impossible Position**

This past week, the Fed held their regular FOMC meeting. They projected lower real GDP growth than their prior estimates, and they projected higher inflation than their prior estimates, for the next couple years. In their projections, they don’t see inflation getting back to their 2% target even in 2027.

![FOMC Forecast](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-fomc-forecast.png)

As the market had already priced in, the Fed did not cut interest rates. President Trump publicly stated that he thinks the Fed is late, and that the Fed chairman Jerome Powell is not a smart person.

When I back up and examine the situation, I think the larger problem is that we’re in fiscal dominance now. That was the key point in my [Las Vegas keynote](https://youtu.be/Giuzcd4oxIk) and both my [July 2023](https://www.lynalden.com/july-2023-newsletter/) and [September 2024](https://www.lynalden.com/september-2024-newsletter/) newsletters.

The Fed’s interest rate tool is mainly about accelerating or slowing down private sector credit growth. Lower rates encourage more borrowing, while higher rates tend to suppress borrowing.

The problem is, this cycle is *not led* by private sector credit growth. Private sector credit growth is pretty mediocre and sluggish currently. Instead, it’s defined by large public sector credit growth (i.e. fiscal deficits).

![Fiscal vs Monetary Forces](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-fiscal-vs-monetary.png)

In addition, changing short-term rates doesn’t always have the same effect on long-term rates, especially during periods of fiscal dominance. They have extreme tools to control long-term rates (such as opening an infinite bid at a specific yield level for long-duration treasuries or mortgage-backed securities, which would be yield curve control), but those are only trotted out in emergencies. When the Fed most recently cut their short-term rate, it didn’t benefit mortgage rates:

![Rates vs Mortgages](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-rates-vs-mortgages.png)

That leaves the Fed stuck with two directions, and neither of which is great:

Option 1) Keep rates elevated, and thus keep private sector credit growth sluggish and the real estate market semi-frozen. But keep blowing out the fiscal deficit, thus still contributing to total credit growth. Those large fiscal deficits are a stimulative and inflationary force, and they’re *fueling* it.

Option 2) Cut interest rates. Encourage more private sector borrowing and a weaker currency. Potentially contribute to higher energy/commodity prices, in an environment where inflation is still above-target. This would also allow emerging market central banks to ease more, thus potentially fueling more economic growth and commodity demand from their countries, which could contribute to inflation. And with no guarantee that cutting short-term rates will help with long-term rates.

![Larry David Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-larry-david-unsure.gif)

During fiscal dominance, I have far less interest in what the Fed is doing. They’re not irrelevant, because whether rates are high or low could affect what sectors I’m interested in or other details, but they don’t affect the overall macro landscape as much as many people expect. The importance of fiscal policy now dwarfs their impact.

## Digital Assets Note

I continue to view the current bitcoin bull cycle as alive and well.

For this cycle, most of the fresh demand is coming from bitcoin treasury companies, so I’ll dedicate a section for them.

**Bitcoin Treasury Companies**

I get a lot of questions about bitcoin treasury companies. MicroStrategy (MSTR) began this type of playbook back in 2020 (and the newsletter portfolio has been long MSTR ever since), and starting in 2024 more companies began adopting a similar playbook. I wrote [an article](https://www.lynalden.com/a-new-look-at-corporate-treasury-strategy/) about it back in August 2024 when this trend was starting to take off.

The most basic strategy is for a corporation to just put some of its cash balance into bitcoin. The more active strategy is to take out debt (usually convertible bonds) with rather long durations, and use it to buy additional bitcoin. When done appropriately (i.e. reasonable debt-to-equity ratios and sufficiently long bond terms) this is a powerful strategy because operating companies have access to these better types of longer-duration non-callable debt that other types of entities (hedge funds, individuals, etc) don’t have access to. Being long bitcoin and short 5-year debt is a powerful mix. It’s way better than the short-term debt that gets wrecked by volatility, which is the type of debt associated with levered ETFs and other funds.

During the first several years of Bitcoin’s life, it was mainly for techies. You had to basically mine it yourself or figure out how to get your money to a Japanese trading card exchange (Mt. Gox) to buy it. In the next several years of Bitcoin’s life, it reached more of an early-retail audience. In the US and many other places, you could more easily buy it on a domestic exchange without going through as many weird hoops. Meanwhile, hardware wallets came into existence and made it easier and safer to store it. In this current stage of Bitcoin’s life, it’s large and liquid enough, and securitized enough, and with regulated institutional-grade custodians, to reach the trillions of dollars worth of managed capital in walled gardens via ETFs and corporations that hold it.

For me, watching this unfold is not just from a distance. I’ve personally met, and in some cases am friends with, executives and/or board members of most of the large bitcoin treasury companies these days. In some cases I’ve known them for years.

There are two main questions regarding bitcoin treasury companies that I get asked by investors, and that I frequently think about myself, and that I’ve discussed with some of those doing the strategy, so I’ll address them separately.

*1) What Type of Market-to-NAV Premium is Fair?*

Many of these companies trade at a premium to net asset value or “NAV”.

If a company has $10 billion worth of bitcoin and $2 billion worth of USD debt, and very little net income to speak of, then one would expect them to be valued at around $8 billion. However, most of these companies are valued at much higher multiples, sometimes over 2x or 3x or more. In other words, investors might pay $16 billion for that hypothetical $8 billion company, or $24 billion, or more. Metaplanet has gone above 6x NAV.

The short answer to the question is that I don’t know what the right multiple is, but that at least a mildly positive premium is fair due to the fact that they can access better debt terms than other types of entities. I’m comfortable with 1.5x for example.

The longer answer is basically this: the industry’s total premium over NAV is a measure of how much unmet demand exists for this strategy. It’s dynamic.

There are many stock fund managers and bond fund managers that are 1) bullish on bitcoin but that 2) can’t buy bitcoin or bitcoin ETFs because it falls outside of their fund mandate. We’re talking trillions of dollars worth of managed capital with these constraints: they can only buy equity securities or bond securities respectively. So, a market opportunity is to give them a stock or a bond that has bitcoin price action embedded in it. That falls within their mandate.

There’s not a defined amount for how much demand there is for this strategy, because it’s time-dependent. There was a few billion dollars worth of demand in the 2020/2021 bull cycle. During the bear cycle of 2022 and 2023, MSTR briefly went below 1x NAV, after all the demand dried up. Now in this 2024/2025 bull cycle there has been tens of billions of dollars worth of demand.

The strategy is tapping into an ocean of trillions of dollars of assets, like a rising tide of waves that come in, go out, come in bigger, go out, come in bigger again, etc.

And I agree with David Bailey’s point that practically every large financial market (i.e. each currency that has a sizable capital market) is ripe for one. He and others are now going around, finding any market that doesn’t have one (which is still most of them), and determining ways to make one. It gives individual investors and fund managers in that jurisdiction access to the strategy.

![Bailey Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-bailey-tweet.png)

But of course, there are risks of the strategy being exhausted at some point this cycle. Especially when we start seeing premiums over NAV built upon premiums over NAV.

![Lubka Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-lubka-tweet.png)

So, the answer is that the multi-cycle demand for this strategy is likely enormous, but that the amount of demand at any given time has constraints and will likely get washed out occasionally.

In fact, when premiums over NAV do start going away, that could signal that we’re in the later stages of this bitcoin cyclical bull run, similar to how when GBTC’s premium over NAV went away in early 2021, the primary fuel for that bull run was exhausted.

I’ve personally trimmed MSTR whenever it reached about 3x NAV. I also monitor Twitter/X and so during those times I see absolutely one-sided positive sentiment for it, I fade it. I’ve been constantly long the stock, but for volatility management I take some chips off the table at those extremes. A couple of months ago I got more bullish exposure to MSTR when it went under 2x NAV by selling cash-secured puts against it, pocketing plenty of income.

*2) Will They Blow Up?*

Some of them, probably. Almost certainly.

But that doesn’t mean a strategy of buying bitcoin with 5-year convertible debt is an unsound idea. It needs to be managed well.

The best teams will make sure not to get over their skis. They’ll keep debt low relative to assets, they’ll make sure to term out their debt properly, and they’ll make sure they have enough residual income to cover interest payments even during times where the capital markets shut down on them.

The weaker teams probably will get over their skis. They’ll ratchet up debt too high, they’ll fail to term out their debt properly such that it risks coming due in a bear market, and/or they’ll struggle with interest payments if capital markets close on them. There’s also reputational risk; if the executives of a company hype up their stock too much during periods of very high premiums to NAV, it can look like a rug-pull of their investors when premiums eventually come back down due to dilution.

Investors should be careful about paying too-high of a premium. Just like other cycles, this will most likely be taken to excess and eventually washed out. It pays to be conservative and countercyclical so that you can weather any potential storm and keep getting access to the bullish waves when they come.

**Bitcoin Cycle Checkup**

In a couple of recent interviews I was asked my thoughts on if we’ve reached the top of this cycle, and if we already have a “double top” in place, similar to 2021. My answer was that I don’t think so, because various quantitative signs of euphoria aren’t present like last time.

Prior multi-year bear markets in bitcoin occurred after market prices got way ahead of on-chain cost basis. This cycle has been defined by modest periods of excess based on that metric (after the ETF launch, then again after the US election) followed by 6-7 month consolidations to reset:

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

The percentage of coins that haven’t moved on-chain in over a year only dropped about 8% (from 70.5% to 62.5%) so far, which is smaller than any prior bull market distribution cycle. Long-term holders with enormous gains tend to trim into bull markets either to diversify or to upgrade their consumption, and these price gains have enticed a rather small share of them to sell so far:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-hodl-wave.png)

Plus, I still think global liquidity indicators will be okay for the next year or so. We could have some bumps along the way (such as when Treasury Secretary Bessent seeks to refill the Treasury’s cash account after the debt ceiling is raised), but we can assess those periods when they come.

So by most accounts, I still think we have further to go. In this bitcoin cycle, retail investors are mostly absent. Bitcoin treasury companies are still trading at large premiums to NAV, meaning they can keep issuing shares or debt to buy bitcoin (which could suppress their premiums but keep up the buying pressure on bitcoin itself), and the HODL wave hasn’t shown signs of exhaustion.

## SLR and Bank Updates

Last week, it was announced that regulators are looking at reducing the Supplementary Leverage Ratio or “SLR” for large banks.

Bloomberg reports:

> The top US bank regulators plan to reduce a key capital buffer by up to 1.5 percentage points for the biggest lenders after concerns that it constrained their trading in the $29 trillion Treasuries market.
>
> The Federal Reserve, Federal Deposit Insurance Corp. and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency are focusing on what’s known as the enhanced supplementary leverage ratio, according to people briefed on the discussions. This rule applies to the largest US banks, including JPMorgan Chase & Co., Goldman Sachs Group Inc. and Morgan Stanley.
>
> –[Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-18/us-plans-to-ease-capital-rule-limiting-banks-treasury-trades), June 17, 2025

Banks have various requirements to hold a certain amount of risk-weighted capital relative to assets (as opposed to reserve requirements). “Risk-weighted” in this context means that for example a Treasury security is worth more credit than a loan to a business, when it comes to determining if the bank has enough capital.

However, in addition to that, banks subject to the Fed’s prudential standards also have an additional requirement, the SLR, which is not risk-weighted. The SLR applies to Treasuries as well, in other words. For the largest bank holding companies that extra buffer is 5% of assets, and for their banking subsidiaries it is 6% of assets. The proposed rule change would drop both to 3.5% to 4.5% of assets.

Policymakers have discussed reforming the SLR for a while. I bring it up from time to time in my liquidity sections, such as back in February:

> I view the Fed as likely to end quantitative tightening by the end of the year, and potentially go back to balance sheet expansion or offer some type of SLR adjustment to make it easier for large banks to buy more Treasury securities and operate with lower cash levels. However, there are no imminent signs of liquidity problems at this time.
>
> -February 16, 2025 premium report

This news represents the first *tangible and specific* update in a while, indicating that SLR relief is nearer to actually happening, and now we have more color on what it might look like when it does happen.

Why does it matter? The primary answer is that it matters because it can increase the total amount of Treasuries that banks can hold, or can let them reduce their capital a bit by giving more dividends or share buybacks to shareholders while keeping their asset levels flat.

From March 2020 through March 2021, in response to dysfunction in the Treasury market during the COVID-19 pandemic lockdown asset price crash, the Fed temporarily exempted banks from SLR, so that they could buy and sell huge swaths of Treasuries and re-liquify the market alongside the Fed’s QE program. To permanently reduce SLR requirements would let banks structurally hold more Treasuries.

Some critics of the idea point out that most banks aren’t currently SLR-constrained. Many of them could buy more treasuries currently, but don’t want to. And when SLR relief is given, many of them might just use it to return more capital to shareholders.

However, the bigger story is that it would be relevant for the 2026-2027 period and beyond. As the Fed continues to perform QT, they drain base-layer liquidity from the financial system. They’re already slowing down their rate of it, and eventually they will stop. At some point, they’ll need to return to balance sheet expansion again to maintain what they call an ample reserves regime. But the timing and magnitude of that change depends on various factors, including whether banks are SLR constrained or not. As QT gets near its end, several banks could indeed be SLR constrained, and so SLR relief would indeed allow them to buy more treasuries.

The only other time in American history where we had public debt as a percentage of GDP as high as we have now, was back in the 1940s. The Fed bought a lot of the treasuries (i.e. it was “QE” before that term was invented), but also commercial banks were huge holders of that debt. They temporarily became big public utilities for holding all the federal debt, basically.

St. Louis Fed data on bank holdings of treasuries goes back to 1947, so we can easily see some numbers here.

Here’s the aggregate amount of treasuries held by banks as a % of total bank credit. The ratio was over 65% back in 1947:

![Bank Treasuries vs Credit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-bank-treasuries-vs-credit.png)

And here’s the aggregate amount of treasuries held by banks as a % of federal debt outstanding. Banks held over 27% of federal debt in 1947:

![Bank Treasuries Share of Total](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-bank-treasuries-share-of-total.png)

Here is a chart of total treasuries held by banks, in both normal chart form and log chart form:

![Bank Treasury Holdings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-bank-treasury-holdings.png)

Although this particular dataset only goes back to 1947, older data sets exist and can be pieced together. Back during 2020-2022, when writing my various articles and amid research for my book *Broken Money*, I looked through a lot of documents with numbers from the 1910s through 1940s and compiled all sorts of spreadsheets and references on the topic.

The conclusion: banks weren’t like this in the decades prior to the 1940s; it was during the 1940s when the Fed and commercial banks all took in massive ratios of treasuries. And then over time that ratio gradually came down, not because they materially reduced their treasury holdings, but rather because *everything else* they held grew. Same thing for the Fed’s holdings of treasuries.

A lot of the public debt was inflated away. A significant percentage of it was held by banks, but the liabilities of those banks (i.e. customer deposits) were *also* inflated away. Therefore, it wasn’t really banks that took the hit; it was bondholders and currency holders, with banks mainly serving as the levered intermediaries for that pain.

My base case is that this’ll happen again over the next decade or so, albeit to a less extreme degree. The Fed’s balance sheet will resume gradually expanding, bank balance sheets will gradually expand with treasuries securities as a decent chunk of their assets, and banks will continue to profit from the net interest spread between customer deposit rates and their various assets (treasuries, mortgages, loans, reserves, etc).

A lot of sizable, defensive banks are trading close to 10x earnings:

![USB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-22-usb-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Banks also indirectly benefit from the growth in AI. The banking industry as a whole is regulated from competition, pretty necessary to use (i.e. it’s very hard to function in society without a bank account, and many individuals and businesses need to access their lending services), and yet banks will likely be able to use AI to reduce their backend expenses. Each bank employee on average, by harnessing AI, will be more productive in their role at the bank, which should reduce overall bank costs.

Stablecoins will likely continue to grow in size, but they’ll be increasingly regulated and likely used by more banks as well. Bitcoin, and companies building on top of it, are long-range competitors to banks, but that’s still a $2 trillion network growing into a massive global fiat currency system, with a long way until it challenges American banks at scale. By then, the smartest banks will have incorporated it, and bitcoin financial services companies themselves are customers of banks.

**A Key Risk: Financial Repression**

When sovereign governments get very indebted, especially in a currency they can print, they generally turn to some form of subtle or overt financial repression. In other words, they find places to stuff more and more treasuries at below-market rates, often via various regulatory enforcements.

Most of these methods don’t hurt banks too much; they hurt individuals and other entities. However, there are some versions of it that can harm banks.

Specifically, proposals to increase bank reserve requirements and then stop paying interest on reserves, would be a profitability hit for banks. Those proposals are out of favor currently, but it’s important to keep an eye on them. The bank lobby is powerful, so they have a lot of methods to protect against major policy changes that could impair them.

So overall, my base case is that this ongoing environment of fiscal dominance will be fine for banks, especially from the starting point of such inexpensive valuations. However, I’ll be on the lookout for disruptions to that thesis, such as various regulatory proposals that would leave banks as bag-holders for treasuries in ways that don’t pass on the pain to depositors. Should they show signs of materializing, which I view as improbable but not impossible within a 3-5 year investment time horizon, it might be time to make an early exit.

At the end of the day, I’m excited about the long term prospect of bitcoin and for startups building on bitcoin. After all, I’m a general partner at [Ego Death Capital](https://egodeath.capital/), where we’re financing companies that disrupt the status quo, and I’m on the board of [Swan](https://www.swanbitcoin.com/), a bitcoin financial services company.

But in the meantime, for value investors during this decade of fiscal dominance, I also think many banks domestically and internationally will do reasonably well. Their assets and liabilities will keep increasing, they’ll have manageable loan losses here and there, they’ll save on expenses via AI, and they’ll pass on dividends and buybacks to shareholders. We mainly have to watch out for the potential of financial repression directed their way.

I continue to be long-term bullish on gold, but still view it as being in a healthy period of consolidation. It has been one of the best-performing assets over the past 12 months, and various measures of sentiment reached significant levels, so it’s normal for it to cool off for a bit.
