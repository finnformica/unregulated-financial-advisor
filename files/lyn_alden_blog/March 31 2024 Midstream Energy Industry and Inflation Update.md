## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

The U.S. dollar index has gone back above 104, which has weighed a bit on global liquidity. Overall global liquidity continues to be rangebound since late 2021:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-global-m2.png)

Various measures of global liquidity are significantly up off of their late 2022 lows, but haven’t broken out to new highs yet. This is a headwind to many risk assets worldwide, outside of the top most desirable ones. Overall, the dynamic from late 2021 through early 2024 has been similar to the dynamic that occurred from early 2018 to late 2019.

My expectation is to see another positive liquidity cycle over the next two years, but I continue to look for a breakout of the actual data. In the meantime, liquidity is still rangebound, and overall liquidity data tends to get messy around tax season (April).

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Most cyclical aspects of the business cycle in the United States continue to re-accelerate, but from low levels. The Conference Board’s Leading Economic Index has been moving in the right direction for nearly a year and a half, but from recessionary levels:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-lei.png)

The Atlanta Fed continues to expect positive real GDP growth for Q1, but only with mild numbers:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-gdpnow.png)

In my view, the extremely loose fiscal policy has been the primary driver of this cycle, and this overall topic of fiscal dominance has been the defining area of my research.

And it’s what made me pivot from recessionary views starting in early 2022 to being more bullish since early 2023; that’s when the impact from the higher interest rates on the public sector (which is stimulatory, via larger deficits) started to override the impact from the higher interest rates on the private sector (which is recessionary, via suppression of credit growth). Some aspects of the economy have been recessionary (e.g. commercial real estate), but the fiscal side has been overwhelming those aspects.

The longer it goes on, the more that various economists notice and comment on it. For example, Robin Brooks was previously chief FX strategist at Goldman Sachs and chief economist at the Institute of International Finance, and is currently a senior fellow at the Brookings Institute:

![Brooks Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-brooks-tweet.png)

The United States running such aggressive fiscal deficits and financing them with short-term debt has been very supportive of market liquidity, nominal GDP, and asset prices. And ironically, a Fed that keeps interest rates high *fuels* that trend rather than quells it, because it dramatically increases the government’s interest expense to the public, and for the public that represents interest income that is spendable. This trend isn’t on track to reverse any time soon.

If the Fed keeps interest rates high, it’ll keep blowing out federal interest expense as it has been. If the Fed cuts interest rates, then it could meaningfully weaken the dollar and noticeably boost the price of oil and other commodities. It’s a rock and a hard place.

Perhaps the most optimal move at this time is for the Fed to trim short-term rates but try to elevate long-term rates, and that would likely give us a mixed result between those two outcomes. That would keep downward pressure on private sector credit growth, but without adding too much to public sector interest expense.

All of these decisions are somewhat cyclically important for traders with a 6-18 month trading horizon, but structurally somewhat irrelevant for investors with a 3-5 year time horizon or more. In terms of the overall fiscal situation, nothing stops this train.

**Potential Re-Acceleration of Inflation**

To continue with a trend I have been closely monitoring lately, a number of recent data points support the narrative that inflation is stabilizing and potentially re-accelerating:

![Core PCE](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-core-pce.png)

*Source: Michael Kramer, Twitter/X*

During the post-pandemic/stimulus environment, many analysts have used different lengths of data when analyzing inflation. This chart above uses a 3-month annualized figure. The main reason to assess different timeframes like this is to strip out arbitrary 1-year base effects to get a more realistic read of current conditions, but sometimes this can be used in a biased way to cherry-pick timeframes and support a narrative that inflation is higher than it seems or lower than it seems.

For the latter, many economists that projected that inflation would be nonexistent or transitory kept cherry picking data to downplay the magnitude of inflation as it developed (“if you factor out x, y, and z, and look at it during this specific timeframe, it’s not so bad!”). And on the other side of the spectrum, many economists with a pro-inflationary view were late to acknowledge the peak in inflation and kept cherry picking marginal data that supported their narrative (“reported numbers are completely fake, inflation is as high as ever!”).

I’ve tried to be as objective and data-dependent as possible when providing outlooks on inflation. My structural analysis on this topic comes from historical research on inflationary periods and the key drivers of inflation during them, and on top of that I have a cyclical overlay that adjusts to what is actually happening in the present. My consistent outlook has been that inflation will average higher in the 2020s decade than the 2010s decade, but that it will have a cyclical aspect to it. And then when possible, I try to get more specific about timing and magnitude.

Along these lines, I wrote about the prospects for higher inflation in response to the next recession starting in [mid-2019](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/), and began pounding the table on the concept in [2020](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/) and [2021](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/) as major fiscal stimulus occurred that was very different than the 2008/2009-style stimulus that did not produce much inflation. Basically, the 2019 piece set the stage for what would *likely* happen (a major monetized fiscal stimulus in response to the next economic crisis), and then the 2020 and 2021 pieces confirmed that it was now *indeed* happening. Widespread price inflation then followed in 2021.

But then by late 2021 and early 2022 as inflation was raging, I started to forecast that inflation would peak in Q1 2022. I then updated the projected peak to Q2 2022 as more data and events came in, especially the Russian invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

> From base effects in the CPI calculation, there’s a decent chance that we’ll see a local top in official CPI sometime in Q1 2022 within the 7-9% year-over-year range. As we look later into 2022 beyond that point, the future is less clear and depends on certain variables.

Official CPI in the United States indeed ended up peaking in late Q2 2022 (June) at 9%, and the data for that came in by Q3 2022 (August).

![US CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-cpi.png)

Since inflation peaked in mid-2022, I have been in the camp that expects cyclical disinflation, but in a structurally higher inflation regime and with likely move waves of inflation coming this decade.

In my view, a preponderance of evidence is now showing that various measures of price inflation are stabilizing and potentially re-accelerating from their current levels. The latter observation (re-acceleration) needs a bit more evidence but the former observation (stabilization) is pretty solid at this point. In other words, I’m not in the disinflation camp anymore, and am back in my more structural stance of expecting inflation.

This is not to say that inflation will quickly retest the highs it reached in 2022 (unless there’s an energy shock), but it is to reiterate that baseline inflation in the 2020s is likely to be higher than baseline inflation in the 2010s, largely due to the shift toward fiscal-driven policy that has been occurring and that I’ve been writing about since 2020, as well as due to energy supply tightness that I’ve been emphasizing since 2021.

Over the past year and a half, I highlighted the likelihood of a second wave of inflation in a number of different reports and newsletters.

> This tight energy situation in my view is likely to keep pressure on the sovereign bond market for years, by making inflation always ready to return should the economy begin to re-accelerate. Until new energy supply comes online and the problem is fixed more structurally, energy-led inflation is a problem that can be temporarily suppressed but that can’t really be avoided. Much like how the 1940s and 1970s had recurring waves of inflation (rather than just one big inflationary spike), my base case is that the 2020s decade will likewise have waves of inflation, led by energy.

>
> In the long-term, as we look out a few years, the interest increasingly looks like a problem. Rising debt levels relative to GDP, large structural deficits year after year, and with flat-or-rising interest rates on those rising debts, will contribute to an inflationary fiscal spiral that will eventually require Federal Reserve intervention in order to monetize fiscal deficits despite above-target inflation.
>
> -November 2022 Newsletter
>
> How persistent inflation will be in the 2020s, and how it will be fixed, remain open questions. I think we’ll see waves of inflation, and take quite a bit of time to fix it fundamentally. And then it tends to shrink a lot during and shortly after recessions, as some borrowers default and as lenders are more reticent to make new loans or buy new bonds. Acting somewhat countercyclically to that, fiscal deficits (red lines) tend to be lower during economic expansions, but tend to shoot up in the aftermath of a recession. During the second half of 2022 and throughout 2023, weak demand conditions in Europe, China, and to some extent the United States helped keep energy prices in check. Manufacturing in particular was in contraction (i.e. PMI below 50) for about a year and a half. The question I’ve been asking is “what happens to inflation when there’s another upward wave in PMIs and similar economic measures?” And my tentative answer has been that inflation will indeed return with the next rising PMI cycle.

We’re not quite at the point of a rising PMI cycle, but we’re at early signs of it with PMI stabilization:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-pmi.png)

So far, PMI has stabilized and inflation has stabilized. Once we do firmly get another upcycle in PMI, will it come with an upcycle in inflation as well? I think it probably will.

And a key part of that outlook involves the energy demand and capex cycle. WTI crude has been back over $80, and more importantly for the consumer, gasoline prices have been bouncing back up along with it:

![Gasoline](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-gasoline.png)

Rent was a major inflationary force in 2021 and 2022, but since then has been stabilizing. There is permanently a lot more money in the system, and wages are permanently higher, so I expect rents to start stabilizing in the coming year or two and stop making lower-lows and lower-highs that they have been since the peak in 2022. In other words, rents will likely stabilize at these permanently higher levels, and then eventually start moving back up. There is a lot of new supply coming online which I think will keep a lid on it to a certain extent in rate-of-change terms (i.e. no immediate repeat of the 2021-2022 period), but overall I think it can resume a sideways-to-up trend.

![Median Rent](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-median-rent.png)

*Chart Source: [Apartment List](https://www.apartmentlist.com/research/national-rent-data)*

Wages remain in a disinflationary trend, but at above-normal levels of 5% still, which can keep consumer price inflation at or above the Fed’s 2% target.

![Wage Tracker](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-wage-tracker.png)

My base expectation is that older demographics will be supportive of wages, offset somewhat by AI adoption and immigration. And due to that combination, I expect separate wage outcomes for skilled blue collar work (which is resistant to replacement by AI until such a time as robotics are way better than they are now, especially out in the field) vs mid-level white collar work (which is the ripest target for AI to disrupt).

Meanwhile, China is showing a [similar stabilization](https://asia.nikkei.com/Economy/China-s-factory-activity-expands-for-first-time-in-six-months) of its cycle:

> SHENZHEN, China (Reuters) — China’s manufacturing activity expanded for the first time in six months in March, an official factory survey showed on Sunday, offering relief to policymakers even as a crisis in the property sector remains a drag on the economy and confidence.
>
> The official purchasing managers’ index (PMI) rose to 50.8 in March from 49.1 in February, above the 50-mark separating growth from contraction and topping a median forecast of 49.9 in a Reuters poll.
>

Policymakers have wrestled with persistent economic sluggishness since the abandonment of China’s strict COVID curbs in late 2022.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has been consolidating around $70k during March, which I think is pretty healthy behavior at this time.

Since it has been firmly in a bull market, the question is how early or late in its bull market bitcoin likely is. I think the majority of evidence indicates it is still on the early side and beginning to enter the middle period, but a few outlier indicators suggest it is later than that. The asset always goes through violent corrections, but the question is what is the chance that it will encounter a multi-year bear market from this point.

In terms of price so far, bitcoin has barely surpassed its prior highs, whereas all prior cycles it rose to many multiples of its prior all-time high. In terms of on-chain cost basis (realized price), it’s just starting to seriously move up after a long consolidation:

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

Its relative strength (a momentum indicator) is becoming elevated, but remains rather early-stage compared to prior cycles:

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-btcusd.png)

The percentage of coins that hasn’t moved in at least one year is down from 70.76% to 66.51%, which is little more than a 4% reduction. During major bull markets, some of these older coin holders are up 5x or 10x or more in profit, and start to sell into the market to meet new demand until all of the new demand for the cycle is exhausted. In the prior cycle, the percentage of coins that haven’t moved in over a year dropped by over 9%, the one before that dropped by about 20%, and the one before that dropped by about 12%.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-hodl-wave.png)

However, if we look at supply-adjusted coin days destroyed (which weights the much-older coins more heavily), then the amount of on-chain movement from much-older coins is at major highs, which it typically reaches in the middle/late stages of a cycle:

![Coin Days Destroyed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-coin-days-destroyed.png)

Similarly, the RHODL ratio which also takes into account the age of moved coins, is also rather high:

![RHODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-rhodl-wave.png)

My base case is that the bull market is entering its middle stage, but it’s useful to pay attention to some of these other metrics that suggest it’s already further along than that.

Ultimately it’s a question of how much external demand will come into the network this cycle, which is hard to predict. The 2017 cycle was unusually strong by many metrics and was arguably the first “mainstream” cycle, while the 2021 cycle was rather stunted since it was crowded by virtually all types of assets rallying together from the influx of pandemic-related stimulus and liquidity.

My expectation is that this current cycle will be approximately as strong as the 2021 cycle if not more-so, although I don’t have a firm level of conviction behind that view, and instead it’s just my assumption from a variety of factors including these charts above. In particular, if we get a significant positive global liquidity cycle over the next two years and break out of this current multi-year liquidity consolidation, I expect it’ll be a significant tailwind for bitcoin:

![Bitcoin vs Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-bitcoin-vs-global-m2.png)

For investors that want exposure to bitcoin as part of their asset mix, I generally [recommend](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) establishing a non-trivial starting position and dollar-cost averaging into it from there, rather than trying to trade it too aggressively. I trim bitcoin ETF exposure and other bitcoin proxies during major bull runs to keep position sizes in check within the various model portfolios, but I don’t touch my cold storage coins.

## Energy Midstream Sector Update

The U.S. midstream sector develops and operates the infrastructure that moves oil, gas, refined products, and petrochemicals around the country and for import/export purposes.

I conceptually separate the industry into three main periods of time:

![AMLP Price Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-amlp-price.png)

The 1990s up through 2014 was the **boom phase**. This was when the industry was rapidly growing, and they were constantly issuing equity and debt to finance that growth, which the master limited partnership structure was built to encourage.

The period from 2015 through 2020 was the **bust phase**. The energy sector experienced a major bear market as unprofitable shale oil flooded the market, and even the midstream sector was hit hard because they were so reliant on constantly issuing equity and debt at high valuations, and thus ran into crises when demand for their equity and debt issuance dried up. Some of the best operators saw this coming ahead of time so they began shifting toward self-financing models ahead of time, while others ran into the problem head-first and had to cut distributions and quickly fix their capital structures.

The period from 2020 to the present is what I view as the **recovery and capital return phase**. This is when the industry has moved past its boom/bust phase, and is now in maintenance mode. Midstream businesses have slower growth, but trade at lower valuations and are more self-financing now, meaning that many of them are not constantly issuing equity to fund growth, and some of them are even buying back their units. Most of them have eliminated their incentive distributions rights, and some of them have converted from MLPs to corporations.

The blue chip standard in the industry is Enterprise Products Partners (EPD). They’re one of the largest, they have the best credit rating, and they were one of the businesses that gradually shifted their capital structure ahead of time and thus were able to grow their distributions per unit for 25 consecutive years through all of these phases including the bust phase. The F.A.S.T. Graph shows their changing valuation over time:

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-epd-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Their units outstanding helps show the phases of the midstream era as well. During the boom phase, they were constantly issuing units. As that phase entered the bust phase, EPD was earlier than most to start reducing its distribution payout ratio, becoming more self-financing, and relying more on cash flows to fund growth projects and less on issuing equity to fund those growth projects. Ignore the brief COVID blip on these charts, which is structurally irrelevant:

![EPD Units](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-epd-units.png)

With EPD, we can reasonably earn a 7% distribution yield and 2-5% annual distribution growth, which can give us 9%-12% total returns with fairly high clarity. On top of that fundamental return engine, valuations will of course fluctuate.

Even with no real top-line growth, if the business just raises prices in line with inflation, replaces aging infrastructure with new infrastructure, and returns the majority of remaining cash flows to investors via distributions (primarily) and buybacks (secondarily), then they can likely achieve the low end of this return range. Any mild growth on top of that would be a nice-to-have.

Energy Transfer (ET) is another example, and one that was more heavily disrupted by the bust phase but still held up better than most. In exchange for a weaker credit rating and higher debt metrics, investors get a yield of over 8% that is rather well-secured by cash flows now:

![ET FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-et-fastgraph.png)

While slower growth in the industry isn’t exactly attractive, the self-financing model of operating infrastructure is a lot more stable. As cash flows and credit availability ebb and flow throughout a business cycle, they have a steadier foundation to operate from, since they are less acutely reliant on external credit and have more shock absorbers in place to deal with fluctuations in cash flows.

As the price charts above show, there has been a bit of a positive breakout lately. After consolidating from mid-2022 through mid-2023, the industry ETF AMLP is trading at post-pandemic highs. And returns are a lot better than the price chart alone implies, because investors collect large distributions along the way and can reinvest them into more ownership.

Midstream businesses that have converted from MLPs to corporations are easier for major indices and foreign investors to own, and so they tend to trade at higher valuations. Midstream businesses that remain as MLPs tend to have more tax complications for funds and foreign investors, and so they tend to trade at lower valuations.

But the thing about lower valuations is that you get a higher distribution yield and better returns from share buybacks, and thus total returns can be superior. For long-term domestic investors, the MLPs are rather attractive. Filing out a K-1 form each tax season is a minor hassle, but overall it’s a very tax-advantaged structure and the businesses typically trade at lower valuations currently.

The AMLP ETF allows an investor to own those MLPs without dealing with the tax forms, which can be more attractive for domestic funds and foreign investors (individuals and funds). I avoided the ETF during the boom and bust phase since I only wanted to own the best firms, but in the post-2020 recovery and capital return phase, I am more open to owning the whole sector with an extra emphasis on some of those best ones.

Overall, I think energy producers, energy service companies, and energy transporters are attractive assets at current prices. The key, in my opinion, is to buy into businesses that can operate well in a range of price environments, including that do well at current oil and gas prices and have a priority of returning capital to shareholders for them to either spend or to reinvest into more ownership stake of the business.

## Final Thoughts: Gold’s Quiet Rally

Hardly anyone cares, but gold has continued its breakout toward new all-time highs, and is now above $2,200:

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-gold-price.png)

Gold has been underperforming the S&P 500, but has been outperforming most other national stock markets over quite a long timeframe. In a tail risk scenario, such as a major supply chain dislocation between the United States and China that heavily impacts the biggest U.S. companies, it could considerably outperform the S&P 500 as well.

I expect bitcoin to be the faster horse than gold, but I think gold is likely to do well over the next few years also, even outside of major tail risk scenarios. It represents the self-custodial analog hard money of choice for sovereign entities, and has been gaining momentum over Treasuries as the world is gradually shifting from being financially unipolar to being financial multi-polar.

Gold, which is primarily used for long-term savings and displays of wealth, is arguably the largest unlevered monetary base in the world even though it has very little usage as a medium of exchange anymore. Bitcoin recently rose in the ranks to surpass silver and the UK pound in size, which places it at #6 on the list of monetary assets:

![Monetary Base Rankings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-31-money-rank.png)

*Chart Source: [Porkopolis Economics](https://www.porkopolis.io/topmoney/)*

Bitcoin has been eating into gold’s market share, which represents a long-term risk for gold investors. I still think there’s plenty of room for them to climb together for a while, though.

Bitcoin would have to climb to about $300,000 per coin to surpass the United States’ monetary base in size, and would have to climb to nearly $700,000 in order to exceed gold’s total estimated value. By the time bitcoin potentially reaches those numbers, I would expect various national monetary bases and gold to be larger markets as well, and so all of the monies on this list are moving targets that bitcoin has been gradually moving up the ranks in comparison to.
