## Tesla Update

I am considering a small Tesla (TSLA) short next week in my other holdings as a portfolio hedge. Unlike Bitcoin, I do think Tesla has a decent probability of being a bubble. Where the top of that bubble is, is anyone’s guess.

I did a number of brief Tesla shorts this year as temporary portfolio hedges. The February one paid off a bit, while the later ones were all quickly covered each time Tesla moved up against my pre-determined cover price levels, resulting in small losses. This approach of tight cover points kept the overall result relatively neutral (mildly profitable for the first one, and a few percent loss in the subsequent 2-3 attempts), but had plenty of potential gain if Tesla were to have fallen instead.

Well-known investors that tried to just hold a Tesla short got killed this year; it had to be done tactically as we tried, with minimal loss. Don’t stand in front of a freight train, in other words, even if you think the freight train makes no sense.

In addition to the February 2020 local blow-off top, Tesla stock had a second local blow-off top in late August 2020 along with the rest of the Nasdaq, and entered a multi-month consolidation. However, in mid-November, S&P announced that Tesla would be added to the S&P 500 index, which gave Tesla yet another massive leg higher as traders front-ran the stock’s inclusion in the index. Trillions of dollars of passive S&P 500 funds had to buy Tesla at any price to keep following the S&P 500.

As of Friday, Tesla is now in the S&P 500, and thus potentially out of catalysts for further bullishness. There was record-setting ~$150 billion in Tesla stock volume on Friday.

If Tesla remains below $700 next week, I think a small short position to hedge an otherwise long equity portfolio is worthwhile, with a cover point at $700 if Tesla goes higher than that.

On the other hand, if Tesla does shoot up past $700 as part of a Santa rally this year, then we just wait, watch, and see if it sets up a top somewhere and trends down, which would give a different cover point.

![Tesla Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-tsla-price.png)

Tesla is currently worth as much as the next nine largest automakers combined, despite selling less than 1% as many cars as they collectively do:

![TSLA Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-tsla-chart.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Research Affiliates](https://www.researchaffiliates.com/en_us/publications/articles/819-tesla-the-largest-cap-stock-ever.html)*

The pushback against that sort of fundamental comparison by Tesla bulls, is that Tesla is not just a car company. Tesla is pushing battery technology forward, building charging stations, are involved with solar energy, have some home-based products based on these technologies, and have increasingly sophisticated machine-learning self-driving software.

However we measure it though, Tesla is mainly a hardware company with some software aspects, trades at pure software valuations, and the stock is pricing in a ton of growth potential. The largest US solar stock, First Solar (FSLR), is worth $10 billion, or 1/60th of Tesla.

Tesla stock is up 730% this year even though their number of car deliveries for the first three quarters is up just 25% vs 2019 figures. The price-to-sales ratio started the year at 3x, and is now 25x. The stock price change is almost pure sentiment around forward expectations, resulting in a massive valuation increase on top of only moderately rising fundamentals.

The strongest fundamental catalyst for Tesla this year has not really been their product sales; it has been stock sales. Elon Musk smartly did stock offerings at these super high levels, which raised cash for the company and repaired the balance sheet. When there is insatiable demand for the stock at any price, it would be foolish not to create new shares and sell them to folks that want them. Tesla’s balance sheet is technically classified as junk status, but because they raised billions in cash this year from issuing high-valued shares, their balance sheet actually looks pretty good now.

Instead, valuation is the main issue here. If Tesla increases their car production by 10x their current production, along with other non-car products and licensing as well, they would still arguably be overvalued at current price levels, let alone what price levels are now vs current production. The fundamentals would need a true moonshot to justify its $600 billion in market capitalization; basically becoming the de-facto operating system of self-driving cars around the world for other brands, while also selling more cars than Toyota.

Meanwhile, Porsche and other high-end automakers represent serious competition for the high-end electric car market.

My concern with Tesla is that it could end up looking like Cisco (CSCO). Cisco stock soared in the late 1990’s, and crashed back down in epic fashion. Meanwhile, its revenue did keep growing, and its products did become the backbone of the internet, but none of that justified the price in the late 1990’s. The stock just got way too far ahead of itself, priced in three decades of potential growth all at once, and never recovered once the sentiment shifted:

![CSCO YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-cisco-chart.png)

So, I use a small Tesla short with tight cover points sometimes as a small hedge to my broader long equity portfolio. So far it has been neutral, neither paying off nor costing much money, but one of these times it might, if sentiment shifts. Valuation doesn’t matter until it matters, which is when sentiment shifts.

Tesla used to regularly have 20% short interest. Due to the parabolic run this year, it’s now down to less than 5% short, as short-sellers were forced to cover all year:

![Tesla Shorts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-tsla-shorts.png)

Tesla, being one of the largest companies in the S&P 500 now, might give it ever-stronger price appreciation as passive capital keeps flowing into the index, allowing Musk to keep issuing shares to bring in cash as needed.

On the other hand, now that it’s already in the index, it could very well be a “sell the news event”, where there are no further catalysts to keep propping up the stock at these super high levels. And as the sixth-largest holding, it’s now a big enough component of the S&P 500 that if it were to decline, it would be a headwind against the S&P 500.

As long as tight cover points are maintained, it’s an interesting candidate for a short as a portfolio hedge, if/when it initiates a downtrend with a clear high water market to use as a cover point.

## Bank Update

Before I get into this report’s stock analysis, I want to provide an update on my bank stock outlook.

Back in my May 24th report, where I focused on Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B), I also recommended the financial sector more broadly. The reasons were that a) banks were cheap and b) banks are far better capitalized this time compared to the previous recession. Too many investors are fighting the last battle.

The 2008 recession was specifically a banking crisis. Banks were systematically overleveraged and got crushed.

In contrast, banks have been very cautious over the past 12 years, and went into this 2020 recession with a higher-than-normal combined amount of cash and Treasuries as a percentage of their assets. So, they were well-capitalized or even over-capitalized. Banks faced losses from client insolvencies this year, but due to widespread bailouts and stimulus for consumers and businesses, the banks’ losses have so far been contained. Losses will continue into 2021, but banks already set aside a ton of capital this year to cover those losses as well.

Starting from that May report, the financial sector outperformed into June, then lagged a bit, and recently has outperformed again:

## DBS Group Holdings (DBSDY) Deep Dive Analysis

I have a preference for international banks at this time. I’ve highlighted Scotiabank of Canada (BNS), Sberbank of Russia (SBRCY), and HDFC Bank of India (HDB) as some of my favorites.

We can add DBS Group Holdings (DBSDY) to the list. They operate DBS Bank, the largest bank in Singapore and by extension the largest bank in Southeast Asia. It trades on the Singapore stock exchange but is also available over the counter via ticker DBSDY as an ADR. The ADR contains 4 native shares per ADR share.

Singapore, a wealthy sovereign city-state in Southeast Asia, is one of the largest creditor nations in the world as a percentage of GDP, similar to Switzerland, Norway, and Taiwan in that sense. In other words, Singapore has a massive sovereign wealth fund, owns a ton of foreign assets and foreign-exchange reserves, and has a fortress financial position. Singapore generally gets top scores in global rankings of high stability and low corruption, compared to just about any other country. It’s a very pro-business environment while also managing the needs of consumers reasonably well.

However, Singapore is highly-dependent on global trade, which makes them economically sensitive quarter to quarter. They’re basically Asia’s Switzerland at this point, serving as a financial hub and trade center within the region. With China extending their reach over Hong Kong, Singapore’s position in the region as an independent financial hub appears set to strengthen.

**Company Overview**

As Singapore’s largest bank, DBS offers a broad array of services including consumer and corporate banking, investment banking, wealth management, credit cards, payment processing, and various other services.

Singapore represents about 2/3rds of their business, China is their second largest area, and the rest of Southeast Asia makes up the rest.

![DBS Overview](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-dbs-overview.png)

*Chart Source: DBS 2019 Annual Report (February 2020)*

Like all large banks, their revenue comes from a blend of interest income and fee income. Interest income is highly sensitive to prevailing interest rates, since they make money on the difference between short-term borrowing rates and longer-term lending rates. Fee income is derived from wealth management, transaction fees, and so forth.

Like many other banks, DBS had bad numbers in Q2 2020 when the global economy cratered, but has done better in Q3.

Specifically, their fee income is doing great, considering the biggest economic shock in modern history happened this year:

![DBS Fee Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-dbs-fees.png)

*Chart Source: DBS Q3 2020 CFO Presentation (November 2020)*

Their weak area continues to be interest income, because the net interest margin continues to tighten:

![DBS NIM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-dbs-nim.png)

*Chart Source: DBS Q3 2020 CFO Presentation (November 2020)*

The good news is that Singapore’s 10-year bond yield has been relatively flat, so it appears that this NIM problem will level out. Meanwhile, their deposits are up 9% over the past 9 months, in large part due to stimulus, as is the case for many other countries.

The combination of rising deposits, solid fee income, hopefully stabilizing net interest margins, good management, and reasonable valuations, makes for a solid stock in my view.

**Digital Asset Exchange**

On December 10th, DBS [announced](https://www.dbs.com/newsroom/dbs_digital_exchange) that they will launch DBS Digital Exchange, which will be an exchange for accredited and institutional investors to trade digital assets. Singapore Exchange, which runs Singapore’s stock exchange, is also taking a 10% stake in this endeavor. So, this project has serious fire-power behind it.

DBS Digital Exchange will have three parts:

The first part is an exchange to trade major digital tokens (BTC, ETH, BCH, and XRP) against a variety of fiat currencies (SGD, USD, HKD, and JPY) for accredited and institutional investors.

The second part is an institutional-grade custody service. In the United States, places like Fidelity, Gemini, Coinbase, and others offer custody solutions for big institutions. These custody services will most likely increasingly pop up around the world; every major market will probably have leading companies involved in the safe storage of digital assets. Some of them will be start-ups, like Coinbase and Gemini, while others will be established companies, like Fidelity and DBS Holdings that get in relatively early.

The third part is, “a regulated platform for the issuance and trading of digital tokens backed by financial assets, such as shares in unlisted companies, bonds and private equity funds”. This has been one of the potential disrupting technologies of digital tokens- they can make it easier and cheaper to trade small parts of something, like a piece of art, a private business, or anything of value. It’s basically another industry that software is displacing, as software makes it easier to manage ownership stakes of financial assets. Neither DBS nor Singapore’s stock exchange want to miss that or be harmed by it, and instead want to keep that under their wing.

This project is a big deal for a couple main reasons:

Firstly, this re-iterates my earlier point that Bitcoin is increasingly being bought by institutional investors, rather than just retail traders. This new exchange is for professional money, not everyday consumers. The more that the ecosystem around Bitcoin and other digital assets develops, with buy-in from the major establishment institutions, the less likely it is for major countries to ban it, especially a place like Singapore. As a matter of game theory, if any major country tries to ban it, as some have in the past, other countries can welcome it and the capital that comes with it, and then the country that previously banned it ends up changing its ban into more benign regulations.

Secondly, it’s probably great for DBS. Digital assets are a potential bank disruptor. The best thing a bank can do is acquire or start up their own business line for digital assets, and incorporate them into their business model. DBS is a profitable dividend-paying bank that now has this new growth vector to prevent it from being totally displaced by new technology.

Singaporean companies have already been at the forefront of blockchain technologies; they’ve tested blockchain-based transactions with Sberbank (SBRCY) in the past, and they’ve tested major commodity producers like Rio Tinto (RIO) selling iron ore to Chinese companies through a blockchain-based payment system. So, DBS adding exchange, custody, and security token offerings is just another move forward in this realm.

**Valuation Analysis**

DBS trades for a historically reasonable earnings multiple of less than 14x. Here’s the F.A.S.T. Graph:

![DBS FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-dbs-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

DBS used to pay a dividend of $0.30 SGD/share, then raised it to $0.33 SGD/share, but banks across Singapore were forced by regulators to cut dividends in 2020, so the dividend is temporarily 0.18 SGD/share.

The shares on the Singapore stock exchange trade for about $25 SGD, so the dividend yield at the current rate is about 2.9%, and would be 5.3% if the full $0.33 SGD dividends are restored (which the company has enough profit to do, once regulators allow it, which they probably will by next year).

Each DBSDY ADR is worth 4 shares, and currently trades for about $75 USD (or about $100 SGD, with the current exchange rate of $1.oo SGD = $0.75 USD).

Prior to the 2008 global financial crisis, banks around the world including DBS traded at higher valuation multiples than they do now. In the aftermath of 2008, banks in the US and globally have been pretty cheap, including banks like DBS that got through the crisis relatively unscathed.

Due to the [way the global monetary system is structured](https://www.lynalden.com/fraying-petrodollar-system/), the strong dollar period from 2015-2020 (with a partial alleviation in 2017) has put a lot of pressure on US and global growth. Value stocks including banks suffered, while most capital poured into growth stocks.

However, with fiscal policy and monetary policy changing, the dollar has been weakening lately, and if that keeps up, global cyclical stocks could do quite well over the next 5 years, in stark contrast to the past 5 years.

**Company Risks**

DBS has a variety of risks, in line with the rest of the global banking sector.

*Credit Risk*

The global banking system faces considerable uncertainty. A solvency crisis takes a while to play out, even as the liquidity crisis and market bottom can happen pretty early. As the economy likely improves in 2021, many businesses will still be defaulting on obligations. However, we’ve likely reached the peak for banks setting aside funds to absorb those defaults; they’ve front-run the next year, and came into this crisis well-capitalized.

We’ll see over the next year how bad some of the solvency problems get. Plus, it partly depends on fiscal stimulus and bailouts, which makes it inherently impossible to predict for sure. If solvency problems are not as bad as feared, then it means banks over-allocated funds for the solvency issues, and they’ll get to take those back. If solvency problems are worse than feared, then the expected uptick in earnings for banks like DBS will likely need to be revised down as they set more money aside to absorb defaults.

*Interest Rate Risk*

One of the biggest risks for banks around the world is interest rate risk. Unlike credit risk which hits hard and fast, interest rate risk slowly bleeds the banks of profitability over time. Banks don’t do well in zero-rate or negative-rate environments, and tend to prefer steeper yield curves. European and Japanese banks have been absolutely crushed by low rate policy for a very long time.

Singapore and the United States offer better prospects for this than their European and Japanese peers. DBS further distributes their risk by having plenty of fee income, and exposure to several markets in Asia, including emerging markets with higher yields.

At this time, I like gold as a hedge for this. The gold price is strongly inversely correlated to real yields. Gold does better if real rates stay low. Banks do better if nominal yields go up and the yield curve has a positive slope.

*Currency Risk*

DBS operates in multiple countries and has broad currency exposure. In addition, non-Singaporean investors have considerable exchange rate risk between their local currency and the Singapore dollar. This goes the other way as well; if Singapore’s dollar outperforms their local currency, it can create a positive tailwind.

Due to the positive current account balance and strong financial position, Singapore’s dollar remains one of my top-ranked currencies in terms of being a long-term safe haven, similar to the Swiss Franc or the Japanese Yen. However, Singapore’s deep connection to global trade presents both risks and opportunities.

*Disruption Risk*

Investors are concerned with all sorts of disruptive risks for established bank companies. Fintech companies ranging from Paypal to smaller peers have made payments easier, and now cryptocurrencies and decentralized finance applications can further disrupt established banking channels. And then on top of that, some investors are worried about the prospect for central bank digital currencies to go around the commercial banking system and compete with them.

This is why I prefer to focus on some of the more digitally-savvy banks. I particularly like Sberbank of Russia (SBRCY), which has extremely high mobile adoption, invests in tech companies, and has already tested blockchain applications. Similarly, I like HDFC Bank of India (HDB), Discover Financial Services (DFS), American Express (AXP), J.P. Morgan Chase (JPM), Scotiabank (BNS), and DBS of Singapore. DBS is mitigating the risk a little bit by initiating its digital asset exchange, token platform, and custody services.

A simple way to hedge these disruptive risks is to have a nonzero Bitcoin position. I like the banks+Bitcoin combo at this time, within a diversified portfolio. If decentralized finance and tokenization massively disrupts the profitability of established banks quicker than almost anyone expects (rather than those banks gradually adopting to the tech as needed like DBS is), then Bitcoin is an asymmetric play on that outcome. If banks continue to do reasonably well from beaten-down valuation levels, then they can continue to be solid dividend payers and participate in a potential growth-to-value equity rotation in the context of a cyclical recovery.

## Summary: Zooming Out

This is a very interesting time in the markets. Many stocks appear overvalued and overbought, while a growth-to-value rotation seems to be making an attempt.

This chart shows the US value stock index divided by the US growth stock index. When the line is going up, value stocks are outperforming. When the line is going down, growth stocks are outperforming:

![Growth to Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-growth-vs-value.png)

There are always fake-outs along the way, and maybe this is another one, but big shifts in fiscal and monetary policy tend to mark turning points for the growth and value relationship. Plus, the ratio is heavily tied to the dollar; the strong dollar in the late 1990’s and late 2000’s played a role in boosting growth stock outperformance over value stocks, due to the dollar’s tendency to slow global growth (and thus put a lid on cyclical/value/commodity stocks more generally) when it gets too strong.

The dollar index (which compares the dollar to a basket of major foreign currencies) has been showing weakness over the past several months, and it just broke below 90. I’m watching the 88 level pretty closely, because if it breaks below that, it’ll be at a 5-year low and without much technical support below:

![Dollar Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-20-dollar-annotated.png)

*Chart Source: StockCharts.com*

As I annotated on that chart, this current multi-year strong dollar cycle was kicked off in the second half of 2014 by the end of quantitative easing “QE” by the Fed, meaning the Fed stopped expanding its balance sheet and thus the US had relatively tight monetary policy compared to global peers. The dollar immediately soared vs other currencies.

When the dollar fell in 2017, its fall was cut short at the start of 2018 by the introduction of quantitative tightening “QT”, meaning the Fed was actually reducing its balance sheet, which further made it rather tight by comparison, along with interest rate increases.

Since September 2019, the Fed ended QT and has been expanding its balance sheet continuously with QE again, and the dollar has trended down (albeit with a brief spike in early 2020 during the pandemic).

By some metrics at this point, “short dollar” has become a crowded trade, so I wouldn’t be surprised to see a bounce to let off some steam. However, unlike early 2018 where the dollar bear trend turned back around, there likely is no tight monetary catalyst like QT to pull the dollar back to strength. The Fed is still committing to $120B+ in monthly QE and zero rates, while the US runs larger twin deficits (fiscal and current account) as a percentage of GDP than the rest of the developed world. And the dollar isn’t even oversold on the weekly chart, which it was after the ends of the previous two major dollar cycles. When a multi-year strong dollar cycle truly ends, it historically has a tendency to fall pretty hard, more than most people think.

So, I remain bearish on the dollar by default with a 3-5 year view, with the caveat that a near-term bounce within that bear trend wouldn’t be surprising, and would even be welcome. That could also come with a correction in many reflationary assets, ranging from copper to energy to Bitcoin. I’m not basing my trades around that, but don’t be surprised if it happens.

Given that context, I continue to like high-quality cyclical stocks, including certain banks, industrials, and energy/commodity companies with a 3-5 year view, although I’d welcome a correction in the next couple months after such strong moves lately. I also continue to like some foreign tech/internet companies, and a handful of US ones, although they tend to be broadly expensive and so careful selection is required.
