# Unilever (UL) Deep Dive Analysis

*Published March 29, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This issue discusses the challenging macro environment and then dives into a globally-diversified consumer staples stock with a strong record of dividend growth.

## U.S. Business Cycle Update

Liquidity returned to the market last week as the U.S. Federal Reserve and other central banks bought enormous amounts of bonds, and various fiscal authorities around the world proposed and/or passed stimulus bills aimed to bail out companies and provide a lifeline to affected workers.

The S&P 500 finally had a month where it ended higher than it started. It was deeply oversold in terms of sentiment and technicals, and was due for a rebound. However, is this just a bear market really before we head to lower lows, or have we seen “the bottom”?

![S&P 500 Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-spx.png)

Timing the bottom will be harder than timing the top, which is why I don’t particularly plan on trying to do it precisely. Instead, I will move some assets from treasuries into equities in tranches. In recent premium reports, I already shifted some treasuries into equities, and am willing do another round or two if we stay low or go lower.

If this was indeed “the bottom” already, it would be unusual in the historic sense of how bear markets usually work, because it means that the bottom was struck before the unemployment rate even went up and before corporations even reported their first terrible quarters. In other words, the stock market would have front run the entire stretch of economic damage that’s going to happen this year (the extent of which we don’t know yet) before almost any of it actually happened.

Bear markets usually have various sharp rallies in them, and take months to grind out their eventual low points before turning back up. My base case is that we will likely see a lower low. That would be the cleaner and less unusual scenario. That doesn’t mean it has to happen, but it would be the historically less surprising outcome. It would be easy to see another round of selling in April during corporate earnings season.

As I’ve shown before, the scale of this impact (and specifically why markets crashed so hard and central banks responded so utterly aggressively and quickly) has as much to do with the record global debt level as a percentage of global GDP, as it does with the virus itself. The virus was the lightning strike on a very dry forest.

For the United States alone, total debt (public and private) is equal to 350% of GDP. Total U.S. corporate debt is equal 5.5x the total annual corporate income.

If you had an income of $100,000, and had $550,000 in debt (a mortgage, an auto loan, student debt, and maybe a bit on a credit card), then you couldn’t afford to lose your income source for very long either.

And then imagine that it’s not an income, but rather that it’s a business with operating leverage. So you earn $500,000 in revenue and have $400,000 in expenses and taxes, for a total income of $100,000 (and still $550,000 in debt). If your revenue drops 3o-50% or more and you can only cut variable expenses while fixed expenses remain in place, then you’re in for a rough time.

The reason that a really bad fiscal quarter of quarantining and shutdowns affects the world economy so much, is that the world economy is so highly levered and so fragile in terms of cash levels and just-in-time highly-optimized international supply chains. Everything is maximized for the short-term, and the system lacks resiliency.

This last week had a number of initial jobless claims in the United States that was about 5x higher than what was previously the worst week in jobless claims ever.

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-jobless-claims.png)

Most recessions start slowly and build up over months. In contrast, this has been a huge up-front shock that has “pulled forward” a lot of job losses into the first week, especially from the service sector. We’ll most likely have another seven-figure jobless report next week, and then from there we’ll see what happens.

Over 13 million Americans work in the food service industry, including chefs, waiters, bartenders, and so forth. Many restaurants are closed or on a lighter delivery-only schedule. Then add airline workers, hotel workers, amusement parks, cruise workers, and gaming workers for several million more. Then add other services like, say, janitors losing hours of pay because the building they work for is on telework-only and only needs a fraction of the janitorial staff.

Much of this can come back quickly once they’re allowed to open. But the aftershocks from this- companies streamlining their employment rolls to offset sluggish demand, corporate de-leveraging, delayed capital investments, less confident consumers, more skittish venture capitalists that are less likely to fund startups, and all of the classic “recession” effects, are likely to follow in some form or another.

So, I plan to continue to dollar-cost average into my portfolios, and to focus on equities. I’m willing to shift more treasuries into equities if this market environment persists or gets worse, with fair or cheap valuations providing more opportunities. I also continue to like precious metals as a component in a well-diversified portfolio.

## The Contrarian Question

One thing that investors should ask themselves is how much risk and volatility they are willing to take. Investors that are in or closer to retirement generally should take much less risk and focus more on capital preservation, while younger investors can take more calculated risks, with a mind towards diversification and risk management. The amount of capital and one’s level of conviction, regardless of age, can also determine risk tolerance to some degree.

The best returns in the investing world generally come from acting like firefighters, who run into burning buildings rather than run out of them. As the saying goes, the best time to buy is when there’s blood in the streets. On the other hand, many safe and low-drama companies just got a 15-30% valuation haircut as well. So there are opportunities in both the beaten down value space and the premium quality dividend growth stock space.

For example in the value space, the best time to buy Russian stocks or energy stocks, was in late 2015 or early 2016, when Saudi Arabia launched a price war on U.S. shale producers, and oil dropped to $25/barrel. And that’s exactly when nobody wanted to buy those things and everyone was selling what they had. Some weak oil producers and service companies in the United States and elsewhere went bankrupt or were permanently impaired, but the strong ones bounced back. Saudi Arabia eventually relented and cut production. We’re now in a much worse version of that, with oil bouncing down near $20/barrel, a global reduction of oil demand as many people quarantine, and an oil price war involving both Saudi Arabia and Russia.

Similarly, I see many opportunities today in U.S. industrials, emerging markets, beaten-down hospitality/service-sector stocks, and the absolute top tier quality oil producers and copper miners. I have them as sections of my portfolios, but would not align a whole portfolio towards them. Their volatility is useful for rebalancing; as I put money in each month, it naturally goes to areas that have been beaten down below their allocation targets.

I did a poll on financial Twitter this weekend with over 3,000 responses, to get an idea of sentiment for some of the more volatile or most affected areas of investment:

![Twitter Poll](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-tweet-poll.png)

The poll itself is a bit unfair, because emerging markets are already a familiar small chunk of a modern portfolio, and energy is a major sector. Few people buy copper, copper stocks, or restaurant stocks in general, which are all more niche. But it gives an idea of where people’s focuses are. Many people answered the question in the comment section as, “none of them!”.

It’s worth keeping in mind that many top U.S. stocks are still fairly-valued or even still somewhat expensive, especially some of the major software names and other high-end companies. After this big market crash, some industries are particularly cheap like commodities producers, restaurants, hotels, casinos, financials, and other hard-hit areas. But as investors have flocked into less-cyclical names, they take on valuation risk in companies whose earnings could do okay, but whose valuations could easily deflate 10%, 20%, 30%, or more in a prolonged bear market.

For example, the utility sector had drawdowns of over 45% during the past two recessions despite being the quintessential non-cyclical sector:

![Utility Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-xlu-drawdown.png)

**A Look at Restaurants**

Since U.S. restaurant stocks scored worst in the poll above, I figure that they are worth another look. People would rather buy oil stocks with oil at $20-$25/barrel than buy restaurant stocks, apparently.

Many restaurants, including Cheesecake Factory (CAKE), are deciding to preserve liquidity by not paying their leases. In some ways, this is a default, although it’s not the same as a default on bonds. In other ways, their unsaid argument for it is easy to see. They pay a lease in order to use a property and run a business with it, but are currently barred by the government from using that property for a time, either partially or completely. So they are not paying the lease for that time. It’s an aggressive and morally questionable way of negotiating with landlords, even though mathematically, they do indeed have the liquidity to pay.

The Federal government is providing a massive corporate bailout, but it takes time to trickle down. Big high-profile companies like Boeing and airliners are likely first in line. But what about small and medium-sized businesses?

Back in my March 15th report, I discussed Darden Restaurants (DRI), one of the largest restaurant chains (but still just a mid-cap stock) with a relatively strong balance sheet, that had sold off aggressively. My conclusion then was that I would wait and watch with no position as of yet, and indeed its stock price is flat-to-down since then. Analysts have updated their forecasts, showing a massive but temporary drop in expected earnings (which may or may not be optimistic):

![Darden Restaurants FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-dri-fg.png)

Normally, restaurants don’t behave like this during recessions, but this virus specifically results in the shutdown of restaurants and other close gathering places. So, the rebound for sit-down restaurants largely depends on when health authorities say it is safe for them to be open. Many of them are on diminished delivery and pick-up only schedules, and some locations are outright closed.

When I discussed Darden two weeks ago, I showed how their large revolving credit facility could cover expenses for a long time if need be. Darden did a announce, on March 19th, that they are drawing on their entire revolving credit facility so that they have $1 billion in cash on hand, as well as halting their dividend and pulling their previous guidance. Cheesecake Factory also announced they are drawing on a large portion of their facility.

However, in addition to this, many businesses are not paying leases, so that they don’t quickly draw down their liquidity. If restaurants, gyms, movie theaters, and other businesses refuse to pay leases on property that they are not allowed to use, then real estate companies are harmed. However, if real estate companies then refuse to pay mortgages, banks are harmed. Banks, however, are first in line for support and bailouts.

So, one way for small and medium-sized businesses to get their share of bailouts before they run into liquidity problems, is to push the problem up the chain to the big companies that have more direct lines of support. They’re basically saying, “If I’m not allowed to use my space this month or next, why pay?” Then, they can work out the details with all parties later.

It’s going to be interesting to see how this ultimately works out for countless small and medium-sized businesses across the country, legally and economically. The restaurant and hotel sectors are very cheap after the sell-off, but I’m not investing aggressively in those spaces until I have more clarity about when they could open. I continue to hold my Cheesecake Factory (CAKE) position as part of a diversified portfolio, but am not currently buying into other names in the space.

## The U.S. Federal Reserve’s Big Backstop

As liquidity dried up over the past few weeks and corporate risk was re-assessed, credit markets consequently crashed, the U.S. Federal Reserve announced a series of actions to backstop as much as they can.

They began buying U.S. treasury bills, notes, and bonds at a record pace. They bought mortgage-backed securities again, and now commercial mortgage-backed securities as well. And they began buying municipal bonds. They opened currency swap lines with more and more countries than ever before, to alleviate the [global dollar shortage](https://www.lynalden.com/global-dollar-short-squeeze/).

Although it’s not legal to do so, the U.S. Federal Reserve also began buying corporate bonds. Normally they would need an act of Congress for this, but instead, they worked with the U.S. Treasury Department to set up a special purpose vehicle, and they buy the corporate bonds there. So, technically the Federal Reserve isn’t the direct buyer. In a crisis, for better or worse, rules are bent or broken.

All of this helps to drive bond yields lower and avoid raw price discovery. The risk profile changes with a Fed backstop. Debt becomes monetized, representing an increase in the monetary base in exchange for credit risk.

I’ve been covering this for the past couple months. First, the Federal Reserve announced a liquidity bazooka of bigger repo lines. When that failed, they launched larger and larger liquidity nukes instead. They began with a $700 billion cap on outright asset purchases, but as of early last week, they have an unlimited cap. They increased their balance sheet by $1.1 trillion over the past month, including $586 billion in the last week alone, and plan to do another $400 or $500+ billion this week as well.

As a result, the U.S. Federal Reserve’s balance sheet is increasing at a faster rate than ever:

![Federal Reserve Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/depression-balance-sheet-1.png)

It’s a challenging environment for short-sellers, with this much money being pumped into the market. A $500 billion per week rate is a $2 trillion monthly rate if sustained. Short-sellers can be tactical, but it makes it hard for them to stick with a short position.

## Unilever (UL) Deep-Dive Analysis

This week’s stock is one of the high-quality “safer” names that has nonetheless had a sizable valuation cut during this market sell-off.

Unilever is one of the world’s largest consumer products companies, with a large assortment of brands in three divisions: beauty and personal care, foods and refreshment, and home care. Over the past several years, they have gradually shifted their brand portfolio more towards the higher-margin beauty and personal care segment.

Some of their top brands include Lipton, Dove, Lux, and several others. They also have a collection of “green” brands such as Seventh Generation.

![Unilever Presentation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-ul-overview.png)

*Image Source: Unilever CAGNY Presentation, February 2020*

Unilever is dual-listed in the United Kingdom and the Netherlands. It then trades as two ADRs on the NYSE for American investors. Between the ADR versions, the one with the ticker “UL” is for the United Kingdom version, while the one with the ticker “UN” is for the Netherlands version. Due to dividend withholding tax differences, the UL ticker is more tax-efficient for most American investors.

What makes Unilever different than many consumer staples companies is the fact that 60% of their sales are in emerging markets. So, even though the company is located in slow-growth Europe and has sizable market exposure to Europe and North America, they also have foundations in almost every country in the world with more favorable demographics. Whether I go to Thailand or Egypt or Argentina or anywhere else when I travel, I see Unilever brands in every grocery store that I’m in.

In that sense, the company is a global play, with emerging markets opportunity combined with the strong capital structure of a developed market blue chip stock.

As it relates to COVID-19 and the global shutdown, most of Unilever’s products are sold in grocery stores and pharmacies, which remain open. As people eat out less and eat at home more, with many restaurants closed, Unilever’s food segment could see mildly increased sales. On the other hand, their beauty and personal care category could see a reduction as people leave their homes less for the next few months. The company, overall, should be on the lower end of the scale in terms of how impacted it is relative to other companies, with neither a big windfall nor a big catastrophe.

**Management and Performance**

Unilever has mildly outperformed the S&P 500 over the past few decades, and with similar volatility:

![Unilever Outperformance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-ul-outperformance.png)

The company has excellent returns on capital, and specifically cites optimizing ROIC in their management presentations.

![Unilever ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-ul-roic-roe-roa.png)

Management’s decision to shift their focus over the past decade from food products to health and beauty products has been a good one, with improved margins and better income growth. Going forward, they are focusing on expanding their share of luxury beauty products and personal nutrition products, and these are on the far end of the company’s margin spectrum.

The company has plenty of liquidity, with over 5 billion euros in cash on the balance sheet. They also have 25 billion euros in debt, which sounds like a lot, but the net debt position of 20 billion is about 4x their annual income, which is fair for a company with this level of diversification and stability through recessions.

**Valuation Analysis**

This is a particularly challenging time for valuation, as 2020 analyst forecasts are basically useless as they model something never-before-seen, and the comparable discount rate is exceedingly low.

When valuing stocks, it is common practice to compare them to a “risk free” rate of government bonds. From there, you determine an appropriate equity risk premium, meaning the extra returns you expect to achieve for taking on extra risk, and this will vary by company and industry.

Historically, bond yields have often been 3%, 4%, 5%, and other solid numbers above the inflation rate.

Currently, the 10-year U.S. Treasury note yields 0.68%. The 10-year sovereign notes of the parent countries of Unilever, the UK and the Netherlands, yield 0.35% and -0.28% respectively. All developed country government bonds current yield very low or outright negative figures. Unilever, in contrast, has an earnings yield of about 5-6%, including a dividend yield of 3.6%, in exchange for more volatility and operational risk.

However, it also has less inflation risk; Unilever can gradually raise prices on its products over time and has a history of 7% annual dividend growth or more. On the other hand, 10-year government bonds lock in their low or negative yields for the next decade, even as most central banks target inflation rates of above 2% annually.

So would I take a diversified portfolio of Unilever-like stocks over a large allocation to government paper? For me the answer is yes, especially after this sell-off. However, I continue to hold some treasuries for tactical purposes, and those sorts of volatility absorbers are particularly important for investors that are in or closer to retirement.

Here is Unilever’s F.A.S.T. Graph:

![Ul F.A.S.T. Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-ul-fg.png)

Their adjusted P/E ratio has come down a bit below their long-term average, but in the high-teens in terms of its earnings multiple, the company remains a premium international stock rather than a deep value opportunity.

The dividend yield is about 3.6%, and the company has grown its dividend by an average of more than 7% per year, compounded over the long run.

Here is a baseline [StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/stockdelver-book/) model:

![UL StockDelver Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-ul-stockdelver.png)

The company over the past decade has had sluggish topline growth, but a big reason for this is that the company has sold some of their lower-margin food brands and bought some higher-margin beauty and personal care brands, which is good for net income but not necessarily additive to revenue.

With that transition potentially out of the way and a better product balance achieved, the company might more readily experience revenue growth. On the other hand, I also anticipate lower profit margin improvements than what the company had over the past decade.

Overall, the company appears reasonably-valued for high single digit or low double-digit returns going forward, with moderate volatility and uncertainty, and an above-average dividend yield.

**Company Risks**

*Valuation Changes*

Unilever is now trading slightly below its historically average valuation, and especially given how low bond yields are it likely represents a favorable long-term investment compared to many alternatives.

However, Unilever, like many stocks, has encountered drawdowns over the past 50 years larger than the one it just experienced.

![Unilever Drawdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-29-ul-drawdowns.png)

If the global equity sell-off hits another leg lower, it wouldn’t be too hard to see the company briefly at a 30-40% drawdown. There’s nothing stopping it from going to a P/E ratio of 15, or 12, for example, if investor sentiment turns bearish enough on equities. This looks very similar to the utility drawdown chart I posted earlier in this report.

A wave of inflation in the 2020’s could also push down valuations in the long run, as bond investors demand higher yields, which then typically pushes down equity valuations.

*Consumer Preferences*

Consumer preferences change over time, and a large company like Unilever needs to be able to acquire and sell brands as appropriate to keep its portfolio of brands relevant. This year, the company has even been considering selling its flagship Lipton brand of tea, as more consumers shift their preference to green tea and away from black tea.

So far, Unilever management has done an excellent job of navigating this risk, but it’s something that needs to be monitored as time goes on. Their wide economic moat and source of stability is their strong and diverse set of global and local brands, and that moat can evaporate over time if the company doesn’t keep their product lineup fresh.

*Margin Deterioration*

Unilever has been proactive about growing its profit margin, but the consumer product industry is very competitive, and companies compete partially on price. In addition, in an inflationary environment, consumer staples companies may have trouble passing on costs to customers as quickly as their internal costs rise. It’s important to monitor Unilever’s margins over time.

*Currency Risk*

Unilever sells products to over 100 countries, with 60% of its sales exposure to growing emerging markets that have a tendency towards currency volatility. The company hedges some of its currency exposure, but can still be impacted by weakening foreign currencies or strengthening foreign currencies.

Unilever reports earnings and pays growing dividends in euros. For American investors, the dollar-vs-euro exchange rate can affect their dividend income in dollar terms, and can affect the stock price as it translates into dollars. This goes both ways though; if the dollar weakens, Unilever’s multi-currency exposure can be a great benefit.

**Conclusions**

This is a challenging time for investment, with a larger than usual number of both opportunities and risks. The length of the COVID-19 global quarantine, combined with an unknown quantity of fiscal stimulus that will occur through this year (some is currently passed, some is likely yet still to come), makes it especially difficult to model companies for the next year or two.

I’ve been by rebalancing and selling some treasuries to increase equity allocations, but gradually rather than all at once.

My approach is to buy some of the top companies in beaten-down sectors as well as some of the more stable plays at richer valuations, because a portfolio with a wide diversification can generally offer superior risk-adjusted returns to sector-concentrated portfolios. Unilever is a high-quality company that recently became more attractively-priced, and as such offers a solid risk/reward opportunity in my view.
