## U.S. Business Cycle Update

We have official results in for total job losses in April, and the numbers are predictably ugly.

Total nonfarm employees in the United States decreased by over 20 million in one month. So, despite a rising population over this time, we only have as many people employed as we did at the bottom of the previous recessions in 2009 and 2003.

![Nonfarm Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-nonfarm-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/PAYEMS)*

As a result, the official unemployment rate at 14.7% is the highest it has been in modern U.S. history. We have to go back to the Great Depression to find higher unemployment levels within the United States.

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-unemployment.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/UNRATE)*

Broader measures of unemployment, such as the U-6 rate, are now over 22%, which is also a modern record.

Although these numbers are shocking headline figures, a key thing to watch is weekly initial claims, because it gives us a more frequent update about the rate of change for job losses. Millions of initial jobless claims continue to pour in each week, but the trend is slowing as the big burst of job losses already happened towards the beginning of the economic shutdown.

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-initial-claims.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/ICSA)*

There have been well over 30 million cumulative initial jobless claims so far, and they’re still coming in, so we can expect the 20+ million total job losses and 14.7% official unemployment, as reported for the end of April, to continue rising as well, unless a huge swatch of people return to work by the end of May for the next month’s reporting period.

One reason why monitoring the rate of change in initial claims is so important, is that the stock market tends to bottom closer to the peak of initial claims rather than the peak in total jobs lost.

If we look at the 2009 recession, for example, initial claims topped within weeks of the market bottom:

![Wilshire vs Claims 2009](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-wilshire-vs-claims-2009.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

So far in 2020, we again had a local market bottom in place within a week of the top in initial claims. We don’t know if that’s “the bottom” yet but it’s at least “a bottom”:

![Wilshire vs Claims 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-wilshire-vs-claims-2020.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As one more chart in this section, here is the Wilshire stock market index vs nonfarm payrolls in the 2001 and 2009 recessions:

![Wilshire vs Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-wilshire-vs-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

In both recessions, the stock market topped before the top in total jobs, and bottomed before the bottom in total jobs.

The 2001 bear market was quite drawn out, taking a few years to form a bottoming process. The recession itself was short and mild, but the massive overvaluation in Dotcom stocks took years to deflate. The market bottomed somewhat after the top in initial claims, but before the bottom in total jobs.

The 2009 recession was longer and deeper, but the bear market was also sharper and started from less extreme valuations. The market bottomed right during the top in initial claims, and before the bottom in total jobs.

The first half of the 2020 recession and bear market was very compressed due to the unusual nature of the pandemic shutdown. It took over a year to reach the top in weekly initial jobless claims during the 2008 recession, but we reached a massive jobless spike, almost ten times higher, within the first couple weeks of the 2020 recession.

What these charts collectively show is that the market historically tends to bottom at the top of initial claims or a bit thereafter, meaning the part of the recession where jobs are being lost the fastest. In the months after that, jobs are still being lost and total employment is still decreasing, but the market is seeing the slower rate of job losses, and is starting to factor in an eventual recovery before the final bottom is in for jobs.

This 2020 environment could pressure that trend, because the amount of lost jobs is so astronomically high, and yet the market didn’t fall as much from its peak as in 2002 or 2009. However, so far the timing does make sense; the market hit a local bottom when initial jobless claims were spiking, and began recovering as job losses slowed, before the bottom in total employment is in. Basically, the first half of the recession that normally takes a year, was compressed into weeks, but the timing trends still played out in a historically normal way within that compressed timeframe.

I don’t have a strong opinion on whether we will see lower equity lows for the broad indices in this cycle, but I do think there’s a high probability of a very “grindy” outcome for the next few years, meaning the stock market could meander up and down for quite some time as it bounces around between economic devastation offset by in part by unprecedented fiscal and monetary support.

**A Note on the Dollar**

The dollar index has been very flat for a couple months now, after it’s initial period of volatility in March. This index compares the dollar to a basket of major currencies such as the euro, yen, pound, and franc, but with a very heavy weighting towards the euro:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-dxy.png)

*Chart Source: MarketWatch*

The trade-weighted dollar index, which has a lot of emerging market currency comparisons in the basket, has also stabilized, but at a higher level. The dollar index is shown in blue, while the red line shows the dollar liquidity swaps that the Federal Reserve is doing to supply the rest of the world with dollar liquidity to try to prevent it from continuing to break out:

![Dollar Swaps](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-dollar-swaps.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

For anyone that hasn’t done so, I strongly recommend reading [my article on the dollar short squeeze](https://www.lynalden.com/global-dollar-short-squeeze/) that is occurring globally because it’s one of the biggest macro issues affecting almost all risk assets. That article was written shortly before the COVID-19 crisis hit. My [article on the $40 trillion problem](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4336136-40-trillion-problem) is a follow-up to that piece as well and explains the ramifications of that dollar short squeeze during the crisis.

The key premise is that there is $13 trillion or so in USD-denominated debt held by companies and countries outside of the United States, plus other USD derivative liabilities for a total that likely exceeds $20 trillion. These foreign entities rely on international trade to get dollars to service their USD-denominated debts, and during recessions, trade dries up and it becomes hard for them to get dollars to service their debts. This can create a vicious cycle where everyone is bidding for dollars to service their debts, and even going so far as to sell U.S. Treasury securities and other dollar-denominated assets to get dollars to service those debts.

Some analysts have been predicting a major break-out in the dollar that will crush much of the global financial system. However, working against this is the Federal Reserve’s policy response to provide dollar liquidity swaps with other central banks, and to perform QE where they create new dollars to buy Treasuries that foreigners and other entities sell, to try to relieve that global dollar liquidity shortage by providing dollars until global trade starts to pick up again.

I found Raoul Pal’s chart from last month on Twitter very interesting. For anyone who doesn’t know him, he’s a former Goldman Sachs employee that publishes the Global Macro Insider newsletter and is a co-founder of Real Vision.

His chart shows the inverse correlation between the global ISM (a monthly measure of global economic activity) and the year-over-year percent change in the trade-weighted dollar index (a measure of how strong or weak the dollar is vs other currencies in rate-of-change terms), and he argued that with the ISM going much lower in April and May due to the virus shutdown, the dollar index should spike higher:

![Raoul's ISM Dollar Argument](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-raoul.png)

*Chart Source: Raoul Pal*

It’s a compelling argument to consider, and if so, a strong dollar breakout would likely lead to a sharp sell-off in risk assets globally, including U.S. stocks, and in that scenario we could test the previous market bottom or possibly even hit a lower low.

For example, look at the inverse correlation between the trade-weighted dollar and the Wilshire U.S. stock market index from back in March and April:

![Dollar Index Wilshire Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-dollar-wilshire.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

That troublesome inverse correlation between the dollar going up and risk assets going down is due in significant part to the aforementioned [$40 trillion problem](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4336136-40-trillion-problem), whereby foreigners that need dollars to service USD-denominated debt end up having to sell some of their $40 trillion in U.S. assets (and other risk assets globally) to get dollars whenever the global dollar shortage becomes acute.

The same thing happened in 2009. The U.S. market double-bottomed when the U.S. dollar double-topped:

![Dollar Top 2008 and 2009](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-dollar-2008.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

It also happened in 2016. It’s a common pattern because a dollar shortage is an acute global problem and results in forced-selling of dollar-denominated assets to get dollars. If we get another dollar spike later this year, we’re likely to see another sell-off in equity indices.

If we zoom out and look at the really big picture rather than the smaller changes, we can see that the dollar has had three major cycles of strength and weakness against other major currencies since the beginning of the current monetary system in 1971. Each of those three dollar peaks crushed some emerging markets. In the 1980’s, Latin American countries were hurt. In the late 1990’s, southeast Asian countries and Russia were hurt. More recently, Argentina, Turkey, and several other countries were hurt.

![Dollar Cycles](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-dollar-cycle.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

For that reason, I made an annotated version of Raoul’s great chart, because I view the causality between the dollar and the ISM a bit differently than him.

His argument is that the dollar should follow the ISM, meaning that as we get terribly low ISM reports in April and May, the dollar should continue that inverse correlation and thus spike higher.

I would point out, however, that historically in strong dollar environments, major pivots in the dollar **lead** major pivots in the ISM, rather than the other way around:

![Dollar vs ISM Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-dollar-vs-ism.jpg)

*Chart Source: Raoul Pal, Annotated by Lyn Alden*

The bar at the bottom of the annotated chart show the periods where the dollar was in a major period of strength or weakness (you can compare to the strong/weak dollar chart above from 1999-present). The orange portions of the bar are shown during periods of major dollar strength (such as the late 1990’s until 2003, and again from 2015-present), while the maroon portion of the bar is shown during the major period of dollar weakness (from 2004-2014)

And then on the chart, I put green and blue markers on key tops and bottoms in ISM and the YoY trade-weighted dollar index during strong dollar environments (whenever the bar underneath is orange), which is what we’re in now.

As you can see, in strong dollar environments, major pivots in the dollar come first, and major pivots in the ISM come after with a lag.

In the weak dollar environment (whenever the bar underneath is maroon), the relationship between the ISM and dollar is less clear. Sometimes the dollar pivots first, sometimes they are coincident, and in 2009, the ISM pivot came slightly first.

Why does it work out like this? Because a strong dollar acts like quantitative tightening for much of the world economy due to the global dollar shortage, including for the United States. A strong dollar reduces global liquidity, while a weak dollar improves global liquidity. It’s hard for credit to improve and trade to pick up **until** the dollar eases, due to policy responses of liquidity swaps and other measures. In other words, particularly when the dollar is strong and fluctuates up or down from that strong base, the dollar is playing a **causal** role in making global growth rates go faster or slower by providing liquidity or taking it away, rather than following global growth rates as a trailing correlation.

We had a sharp local dollar top in mid-March, and various measures by the Federal Reserve have eased it since then by providing liquidity. I don’t know if that’s “the” dollar top for the cycle or not, but it could be. And I would argue that the dollar doesn’t just follow ISM lower or higher in this environment, but rather, ISM will have a very tough time recovering until the dollar top is in and the dollar eases.

The dollar and economic activity as measured by the ISM certainly affect each other in both directions; the virus shutdown led to less global trade, which made the global dollar shortage become acute, and resulted in a scramble for dollars and a brief spike in the dollar index in mid-March until central bank liquidity provisions started to kick in to address that. However, for ISM to start to recover, countries and businesses will need liquidity, which for the most part can only happen if the dollar eases. Liquidity comes first, and then a beginning of a recovery. If liquidity doesn’t come in sufficient amounts, there is likely more pain ahead.

## Ambev (ABEV) Deep-Dive Analysis

This week’s stock analysis is for Ambev (NYSE: ABEV).

Ambev is a large beer producer in Brazil that sells products to much of South America, Central America, the Caribbean, and Canada.

The company has a long and complex history of mergers. Its formal name is Companhia de Bebidas das Américas, which means Americas Beverage Company and is the root of the Ambev name. In 2004, it merged with Belgian beer company Interbrew to form the company called Inbev. And then in 2008, Inbev merged with American beer company Anheuser-Busch to form the Anheuser-Busch Inbev (BUD) multinational beer conglomerate.

Ambev exists as a separately-traded stock, but Anheuser-Busch Inbev owns over two-thirds of Ambev’s shares. The rest of the shares can be directly bought, including as an ADR on the New York Stock Exchange under the ticker ABEV.

I have no interest in the parent conglomerate, but Ambev itself does interest me at current price levels for the long run. The reason for my interest is that the company has effectively no debt and a reasonable valuation after such a massive stock decline, but we’ll dive into those numbers in the sections below.

Restaurants and bars are closed around the world, including in Ambev’s markets. So while people can still drink at home, they haven’t been drinking in social environments. So, it’s natural that Ambev’s stock is beaten down at the moment, but there appears to be value down here.

![ABEV Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-meme.png)

**Management and Performance**

Since the 1990’s, Ambev’s stock has roughly matched the performance of the S&P 500 with dividends invested, but since it’s an emerging market stock that is primarily priced in Brazilian currency, it has had big swings in performance.

For a while, the stock was absolutely trouncing the S&P 500 in total returns, but after its recent crash, the S&P 500 has caught back up:

![ABEV vs SPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-vs-spy.png)

The stock was trading at over $9/share back in 2013, and over $7/share as recently as mid-2018, but in recent weeks has been bouncing down around $2/share.

![ABEV Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-price.png)

This is because although the company has continued to grow in terms of Brazilian currency, ever since the dollar rapidly strengthened at the end of 2014 (and remains in its current period of strength), Ambev’s revenue as measured in dollars has been stagnating:

![ABEV Revenue Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-revenue-income.png)

As far as how the company is managed, they have made very good use of capital. Their annual returns on assets, equity, and capital have all been great, indicating a substantial economic moat. Their ROIC in particular has generally been in the teens or twenties, which is very high:

![ABEV ROA ROE ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-roa-roe-roic.png)

If there’s a key thing that I like about the company, it is that they have practically no debt.

In 2019, the company made over R$10 billion (ten billion Brazilian reais, local currency), which is over $1.7 billion in USD terms at today’s exchange rate.

Compared to this, the company has over R$13 billion in cash on their balance sheet, or over a year of net income in liquidity. And they only have R$3.5 billion in debt, which is very low in dollar terms.

![ABEV Cash and Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-cash-debt.png)

Directly from the latest quarterly report, the company has R$13.2 billion in cash and R$3.5 billion in total debt as of the end of March, which at today’s exchange rate translates into $2.2 billion USD in cash and less than $600 million USD in debt. The market capitalization of the company is $32 billion USD.

So, Ambev has a pristine balance sheet with virtually no debt relative to its size, high returns on assets, equity, and capital, and a beaten-down stock price. They face growth challenges and currency risks, but their durable assets can retain value through recessions.

In comparison, the parent conglomerate Anheuser-Busch Inbev has an annual net income of about $9 billion USD, has over $100 billion in debt, and $7 billion in cash. So, their net debt to income ratio is over 10x, which is very high. With that much leverage for the conglomerate, I’ll pass for now and stick with Ambev.

**Valuation Analysis**

Back in 2013, Ambev stock was dramatically overvalued, with a price/sales ratio of 38 and a price/book ratio of 23. This was at the tail end of the hype in “BRIC” investing (Brazil, Russia, India, China), after those regions dramatically outperformed during the weak dollar period from 2003 for the next ten years or so in an emerging market boom.

During the strong dollar period from late 2014 to the present, Ambev stock’s valuation has been stagnating. And now with the COVID-19 crisis, the stock has fallen to multi-decade valuation lows.

Here is the stock’s price-to-sales and price-to-book ratios, both at major lows:

![ABEV Price/Sales and Price/Book](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-pb-ps-ratio.png)

And here is the stock’s price/earnings ratio and dividend yield, which are at major lows and highs respectively (even more-so than Brazil’s nasty 2014-2016 recession):

![ABEV Dividend and Price/Earnings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-div-pe-ratio.png)

Parent company Anheuser-Busch Inbev (BUD) is even cheaper, but as previously-mentioned, it has a very high amount of debt, unlike Ambev that has tons of cash and very little debt, and thus has a pristine balance sheet with no net debt.

Another beer company that has a pristine balance sheet is Boston Beer, the smaller company that makes popular Sam Adams beer. This company has been doing better than many other beer producers, but still has had sluggish growth for the past few years, and yet its valuation metrics are extremely high as investors have piled into American consumer staples stocks:

![Boston Beer Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-sam-comparison.png)

Despite having slow growth in the past several years, Boston Beer stock has a P/E ratio of over 50 (and it’s not an anomaly due to a drop-off in earnings, that’s the actual price vs peak level earnings), a price/book ratio of over 7, and price/sales over 4. Analysts expect a strong period of growth for Boston Beer in 2021 and beyond, in part from the Truly brand of flavored hard seltzers, but we’ll see.

Boston beer stock does deserve a valuation premium over ABEV, but should the premium be a 4-5x higher earnings multiple, and a more than 3x higher book value multiple, and a nearly 2x higher sales multiple? Probably not, in my view.

The F.A.S.T. Graph for Ambev is challenging. I wouldn’t want to be an analyst trying to determine precise beer sales and profits for 2020 or 2021, especially when taking into account currency risks as well. They are looking for a big reduction in 2020 (which based on Q1 numbers is indeed happening), followed by a gradual rebound in 2021 and thereafter:

![ABEV F.A.S.T. Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-fg.png)

The company earned as much as $0.33 USD per share during the weak dollar environment near the end of Brazil’s economic boom. This figure fell to $0.17 USD per share in 2019. They’ve also had to deal with the hyperinflationary environment of Argentina, which is one of their beer markets, and that has messed up that segment of their earnings. (In my 2018 and 2019 global opportunities investment reports, I gave the Argentine peso the bottom-ranked currency score, and it has indeed collapsed over that time. The next report comes out soon, within May.)

At the current price of $2.15/share, Ambev is trading at 6.5x its peak earnings from 2012, or 12.6x its recent pre-COVID earnings from 2019. If analysts are correct about $0.11 earnings in 2020, it is trading at 19.5x trough earnings, and 15.4x the earnings that analysts are expecting by 2022.

So, there’s a pretty big valuation range that the company could trade at depending on how COVID-19 and the associated global economic problem works itself out in the years ahead.

Even modeling with [StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/stockdelver-book/) is quite hard for this company, but here’s an example using a two-stage model that assumes more dollar liquidity in the years ahead, meaning that some EM currencies such as the Brazilian real begin to stabilize.

Analysts expect $0.11 in earnings per share and $0.09 in dividends in 2020 and a recovery from there. Suppose that by 2025, the company is back to $0.22/share (which is less than what the company earned in 2016 or 2017), and then from there only grows at a 3% annual rate for the five years after that.

That would be a 15% five-year earnings growth rate from a low trough to get back to normalcy as restaurant/bar activity returns and margins stabilize, and then back to slow growth from there. If that overall scenario looks bullish, keep in mind that this includes inflation and margin normalization, so actual volume growth would be slower than this.

![ABEV StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-10-abev-stockdelver.png)

At $2.15 as of this writing, Ambev stock is trading at a bit of a discount to this estimated fair value of $2.36 for investors that seek 10% annualized returns. There is, however, a pretty wide range for a fair value estimate, given different scenarios over the next 5 years.

On the bearish side, if the company has trouble returning to normalcy and/or global dollar liquidity continues to be problematic for EM currencies and economies (which would also likely be a big problem for the U.S. economy and stock market), this estimate could prove to be too high and returns may be very lackluster.

On the bullish side, with such a strong balance sheet, the company could also make acquisitions to boost growth. With a wide base of assets, plenty of cash, and very little debt, the company has a lot of flexibility to adapt itself as needed. And if global dollar liquidity improves significantly, this estimate could prove to be low and returns could surprise to the upside.

The stock did recently have a double-bottom at $2/share, although there’s no guarantee that this will hold. However, I’m happy to start dollar-cost averaging in from here as part of a diversified portfolio.

**Company Risks**

*COVID-19*

With restaurants, bars, hotels, concerts, sporting events, and other venues and events around the world shut down, people can still drink beer at home but are not drinking it at these typical social settings.

At this point, both policymakers and investors are just making educated guesses about what the global economy could look like in 1-3 years, including how fast people can and will return to these venues. If the trend takes a lot longer than estimated, Ambev’s stock price could languish for a while.

*Declining Beer**Interest*

Beer consumption has been in a sluggish trend in recent years, as young adults aren’t consuming it as much. And among beer sales, craft beers have been taking market share from the cheap major beer brands. So, people don’t drink beer as much, and when they do, they prefer a more premium experience rather than a cheap generic corporate beer.

To counter that trend, beer corporations have been buying craft beer companies and adding them to their portfolio of brands. Consumers tend to notice this though, and some of the “spark” can be gone when a craft beer brand is bought out by a major corporation, so it’s not a sure-fire recipe to stay relevant.

South America has less craft beer penetration into the market so far than North America. This is in part due to lower income. Premium beers would take a much larger income share from the median worker in an economy with a per-capita GDP of $11,000 (for Brazil) than from the median worker in an economy with a per-capita GDP of $55,000 (for the United States). The large efficiencies of scale that large beer corporations have to produce beers cheaply (among a range of price levels) gives them an advantage in particularly price-sensitive markets.

The industry also adapts as consumer tastes change. Lately, flavored hard seltzer is popular, so beer companies have produced various brands of flavored hard seltzer to take part in that growth trend. Boston Beer has one, and so does this beer conglomerate.

*EM Currency Risk*

As described in my article about the global dollar short squeeze, we are in the third cycle of dollar strength within the current global financial system that has been in place from 1971, and these periods of dollar strength tend to hurt emerging markets (and as described in that article, also tend to hurt the United States as well). Slightly over half of Ambev’s sales come from Brazil, and Brazil has been hurt by low commodity prices and high dollar-denominated debt in this environment. Argentina has had a hyper-inflationary recession, so that hasn’t been great for margins.

If dollar liquidity around the world remains tight for the multi-year long-run (which is not my base case), then even high quality companies like Ambev in emerging markets could continue to languish until a more fundamental shift occurs towards liquidity and reflation.

**Conclusion**

There are cheaper stocks out there, such as emerging market banks, but Ambev’s blend of quality and value is appealing for risk-adjusted return potential down near two bucks per share.

They reported their rough Q1 2020 COVID-19 performance last week and the stock initially sold off, tested its $2/share bottom as investors digested the predictably poor news from the quarter, and then rebounded sharply. I don’t know what the stock will do in the coming weeks and months, but in this price range, I’m willing to start dollar-cost averaging into it.

A key thing to watch in the months and years ahead, both for this stock and for the broader global economy, is dollar liquidity. If the dollar remains overvalued and hard-to-get outside of the United States, in the current global financial system that relies so heavily on dollar availability (as the currency predominantly used globally for commodity pricing, international trade, and international lending), then things will have a very hard time recovering both in the United States and abroad. On the other hand, improvements in dollar liquidity could be a catalyst for a bottom in beaten-down emerging market stocks, particularly ones with a high degree of quality and durability that can weather the storm.
