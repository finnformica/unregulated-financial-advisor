*June 26, 2022*

## Macro View

Multiple economic indicators continue to point towards recession, or at least significant economic deceleration/stagnation. Meanwhile, demand destruction is starting to impact some of the more cyclical commodity prices.

As the market starts to perceive that inflation is plateauing, and hits peak “hawkishness” in terms of its forward expectations of Fed monetary tightening, it potentially gives oversold risk assets a chance to rally for a period of time from oversold levels. Sentiment is currently terrible for most risk assets, which is usually a contrarian indicator that allows for a bounce.

However, beyond that, a stagnation in corporate profits is likely, based on various leading indicators. I don’t expect most Q2 earnings reports to be very good, both in terms of their numbers for the quarter and their guidance for the rest of the year. If this is the case, it’s likely to continue to drag the S&P 500 sideways or down after it goes through a possible bear market rally it seems to be doing at the moment.

I would remain focused on risk management, and emphasize high-quality assets throughout this stagflationary environment.

**Economic Deceleration**

The US manufacturing purchasing managers’ index or “PMI” for May was reported at 52.4 this week, which was deep below consensus economist expectations of 56. This represents major economic deceleration:

![US PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-us-pmi.png)

However, it was worse than that overall. Services PMI for the US also came in below expectations, and then both manufacturing and services PMIs for the Euro Area came in below expectations as well.

Corporate profit margins have already been softening in this environment from their recent peak levels, and history is not in their favor either.

I recently looked through the past century of [Professor Robert Shiller’s data](http://www.econ.yale.edu/~shiller/data.htm), and found that five of the past six inflationary spikes of this magnitude were associated with weak corporate earnings periods.

* 1941-1944 (weak)
* 1946-1948 (strong)

Most of the “weak” corporate earnings periods in this sample were rather sideways/choppy in nominal terms, and down in real terms.

**Copper-to-Gold Ratio**

The copper-to-gold ratio also looks very weak. I highlighted back in my May 2022 newsletter that the ratio was rolling over, and now as an update it has fully broken down into a downtrend:

![Copper to Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-copper-gold.png)

The copper-to-gold ratio tracks global PMI cycles rather closely. During rising PMI cycles, there is more construction and industrial usage of copper, whereas in falling PMI cycles, there is less usage of it, and more investor interest in defensive assets like gold.

Here’s a quick recap for people following my copper producer trade, for those that haven’t followed the reports over the years. In 2019, I added a stake in Southern Copper (SCCO). And then in July 2020, I added Freeport-McMoRan (FCX) to my holdings. I subsequently sold them in March 2021 after SCCO doubled and FCX tripled in a surprisingly short period of time. Their stock prices continued to grind around for a while after my sale, including a bit higher, but have since broken back down.

It’s too early for me to jump back in yet, but if the copper market gets stressed and out-of-favor enough, and leading economic indicators start to bottom and show some green shoots for the next cycle of global growth, then I would become very interested in getting back into copper producers. Maybe that will happen in 2023 or 2024; it’s too early to say at the moment.

For 2022, I still prefer gold.

**Global Liquidity Near Rock Bottom**

Professional trader and analyst [@MrBlonde\_macro](https://twitter.com/mrblonde_macro) put out an excellent chart on global liquidity last week:

![Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-global-liquidity.png)

Let’s ignore the blue bitcoin line for a moment, and focus on the orange line for global M2.

M2 represents broad money supply- the physical currency in circulation plus the money that people and companies actually have in their various checking accounts, savings accounts, and other liquid cash-equivalent accounts. M2 mostly increases from either a) commercial bank lending or b) large fiscal deficits that are monetized by the central bank. I described this concept in detail in my [2020 money-printing article](https://www.lynalden.com/money-printing/).

Central banks by themselves have limited ability to directly increase or decrease M2. For example, the Bank of Japan has been a huge printer of money for the past decade, and its balance sheet is now well over 100% of Japan’s GDP. Unlike the Fed or the ECB, the BOJ almost never stops printing. And yet, Japan’s broad money M2 has been among the *slowest-growing* in the world over the past decade.

![Japan Money-Printing](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-japan-money-printing.png)

This is because, if bank reserve creation is offset by reduction in commercial bank loans, and/or doesn’t get it out into the broad money supply via fiscal transfers, then it doesn’t really translate into much M2 growth. And M2 growth is what is pretty inflationary for consumer prices, with a lag.

In Japan’s case over the past decade, corporations significantly deleveraged, which reduced money supply and partially offset Japan’s monetized fiscal deficits that added to money supply.

The orange line in Mr. Blonde’s chart is very good because it specifically shows global year-over-year global M2 growth translated into USD.

In other words, if the USD strengthens vs a basket of major currencies, then the global M2 in USD will flatten or even go down, even if the US and most countries’ local currency M2 figures are all still individually growing. The total USD-denominated value of global M2 stops growing if either 1) several major country M2’s actually stop growing in their local currency terms or 2) the USD strengthens so much that, when all local currency M2 is translated back into USD, it’s equal or less than it was months ago.

Currently, partly due to slowing M2 growth among countries, but mainly from the strong USD, global M2 growth as denominated in USD has come to a halt.

This is relevant because as the primary currency for international trade and international debt markets, the USD is basically the unit of account for the world. When the USD hardens, the whole world, including ironically the US as well, gets squeezed. Developing countries that have high USD-denominated debts, low FX reserves, and current account deficits get squeezed the hardest, but almost everyone gets squeezed to some degree.

We’re around what has historically been a cyclical bottom in global M2 USD growth rate, at around zero year-over-year. The global economy is decelerating, housing markets are seizing up, stock and bond indices are suffering, and consumer sentiment in many regions is at all-time low levels.

I don’t think the Fed is near pivoting on its tightening plan yet, but I think we’re at or near the point where the market reaches peak hawkishness and starts pricing in fewer rate hikes going forward, which can ease USD strength to some degree.

Rising liquidity environments will typically be associated with accelerating economic activity, cyclical assets doing better than defensive assets, copper doing better than gold, and cash performing poorly relative to risk assets broadly.

Declining liquidity environments will typically be associated with decelerating economic activity, defensive assets doing better than cyclical assets, gold doing better than copper, and cash holding up well vs risk assets broadly.

**Green Shoots in China**

China is not tightly-aligned with the US and Europe as far as recession timing is concerned. Its economic cycle is several months ahead.

While US economic activity was still soaring in the first half of 2021, for example, the Chinese economy was already rolling over from its highs, and its government was cracking down hard on its tech giants. And then by early 2022, China was engaging in an unusually strict lockdown in several major cities, especially Shanghai, as part of a zero-COVID policy.

As a result, China’s PMI entered into contraction territory, and may have bottomed already:

Over the past month, top-level figures in China have been promoting stimulus efforts to revive their forcibly shut-down economy.

My base case going into 2022 was that Chinese officials would want a relatively smooth-functioning economy as a setup for their sensitive 20th National Congress, which takes place in Q4 2022. Every five years, the National Congress is held and represents the closest thing that China’s political system has to elections, and with President Xi presumably going for an unprecedented third term, he needs some degree of support during this time. Xi would presumably want a lot of “wins” under his belt to ensure that he has a critical mass of power, and he has enough (but not absolute) authoritarian power to arrange things to benefit his intended outcome.

So, the draconian lockdowns of early 2022 were outside of my base case for what makes sense for President Xi to do, even though he made “zero COVID” a key goal. However, here in mid-2022, I do think they are now moving past this and towards this Q4 National Congress. So, the timing was a bit later than I expected but I think directionally this will play out roughly as envisioned.

With quibbles about what exactly defines a recession, China entered what is basically a recession for the later half of 2021 and into the early half of 2022. And as the US and Europe enter what is basically a recession (subject to the same quibbles about definitions) during 2022/2023, China will likely be emerging from theirs.

Everybody hates the trade. It’s out of consensus. It has major risks. I was a bit too early on my view for it. But I think Chinese equities will be a decent place to have some exposure to from now through maybe 2024.

This thesis is obviously off the table if we wake up to a Taiwan invasion headline or something along those lines, and therefore western investors must be cautious about position sizes.

**Russian Geopolitics**

Speaking of geopolitically important but rather authoritarian countries, we’re due for a macro update on Russia as well.

Russia invaded Ukraine in late February, and has been bogged down in war ever since. Ukrainians have been fighting back, and NATO countries have supplied them with arms to support their ability to do so. Russia is a formidable aerospace and nuclear power, but their ground/logistics game is not exactly their strong point. Russia currently controls large swaths of eastern Ukraine, but their earlier push towards Ukraine’s capital was unsuccessful.

Alongside their aerospace and nuclear capabilities, what Russia really has in terms of leverage at the geopolitical poker table is commodities, and the political organization to wield that leverage rather effectively. It’s not some small rogue state; in addition to being a major nuclear power, it’s the biggest natural gas exporter in the world, the biggest fertilizer exporter in the world, the biggest wheat exporter in the world, the biggest palladium exporter in the world, the second biggest oil exporter in the world, and a major exporter of nickel, coal, copper, and other commodities. In short, it’s probably the most important individual country for commodity exports overall, with makes sense considering that it has the biggest landmass.

I see American analysts say things like, “Russia is only 1.5% of global GDP, which is similar in scale to Italy,” but that misses the point. Commodities are a small percentage of global GDP, but they are the foundational input for everything else. Without key commodities, a large percentage of the rest of that GDP comes to a grinding halt. Add significant aerospace capabilities and nukes to that mix of commodities, and Russia is worth a lot more than 1.5% of global GDP. I also generally view total energy consumption as a better indicator of a country’s true economic size than nominal GDP, and by that metric, Russia is 5x as economically big as Italy.

As a result, Russia punches far above its weight geopolitically and in terms of macroeconomics relative to Italy, despite similar GDP figures compared to Italy. But of course between the two I’d rather live in Italy.

Due to high energy prices and reliance on Russia for delivering them, many of Europe’s national economies are floundering now. Their continental energy security is very much in question for a significant period of time going forward.

Back in 2020, I became [bullish on uranium](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-10-25/) and showed studies on how Germany kept its reliance on coal, ironically, as a result of its push towards de-nuclearization. I highlighted how Germany could have phased out more coal if they had stuck with nuclear, but did not. Now, they are falling back on [even more on coal as an emergency option](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/06/23/germany-triggers-alert-level-of-emergency-gas-plan-sees-high-risk-of-long-term-supply-shortages.html) if their natural gas is shut off.

In my May 2021 Global International Report, I ranked Russia a 5/5 in terms of fundamental currency strength. It had a fiscal budget surplus, low debt, high foreign reserves relative to GDP including a big gold component, and a structural current account surplus. It had weaknesses of course, such as slow growth and high corruption, but its currency metrics were among the best. I was not anticipating a war, however.

After a brief collapse in the beginning of the war, the value of the Russian ruble relative to the dollar and euro recovered sharply to hit a 7-year high. It’s the best performing currency of 2022.

![USDRUB](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-usdrub.jpg)

Some analysts say this increase in ruble strength is merely a result of Russia’s capital controls, and therefore that it’s an artificial figure. While there is some truth to that, the fact is that capital controls don’t work if a country does not have critical exports that the rest of the world needs.

Argentina and Turkey, for example, are not able to hold up the value of their currencies with capital controls. That’s because although they can partially prevent capital from leaving, they can’t necessarily entice capital to want to come in, either.

It’s the specific combination of capital not leaving, *but capital still going in*, that is driving up the Russian currency.

Several western nations sanctioned Russia, which reduced Russia’s import capabilities. This has a real negative effect on Russia’s economy; they have a lot more economic frictions and shortcomings due to this. These import restrictions, combined with Russia’s capital controls, reduced the ability for capital to leave Russia. At the same time, however, the West and the rest of the world still buys Russian commodities, and many of those commodities went up in price. And so, the rest of the world is still pouring capital into Russia, out of necessity. In fact, Russia was able to ease its capital controls last month, and yet its currency still headed higher.

Basically, as a result of multiple countries limiting Russia’s imports and still buying Russia’s high-priced exports, Russia’s trade surplus and current account surplus exploded higher, along with their currency value.

![Russia Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-russia-trade-balance.png)

Meanwhile, China and India are able to buy Russian oil and other commodities at a discount to what western nations are paying for the same things.

> TOKYO — China and India have increased Russian oil purchases as prices decline due to Western import bans, the latest data shows, creating a loophole that allows Moscow to secure export revenue.
>
> China imported 800,000 barrels of Russian petroleum daily by sea last month, according to data from Refinitiv, a figure that does not include oil delivered via pipelines. The volume has soared by more than 40% from January.
>
> The number indicates that China is deliberately going after cheap Russian crude. India’s marine imports of Russia’s oil also spiked from zero in January to nearly 700,000 barrels a day in May.
>
> –[Nikkei Asia, June 8th 2022](https://asia.nikkei.com/Business/Energy/China-and-India-buy-more-Russian-oil-blunting-Western-sanctions)

Russia can also blend its oil with various OPEC nations. They can sell oil to them, who then resell this fungible oil to the rest of the market. Many developed countries will optically reduce their reliance on Russian commodities by not buying directly from Russia, while still actually using them, as they get mixed in with the rest. Meanwhile, many developing countries will continue to buy directly from Russia, and get a discount for doing so.

Along these lines, I think over the next 5-10 years, global FX reserves will diversify a bit more, and that the “gold” and “others” categories in the chart below will increase their share to some extent. That doesn’t mean, however, that any asset in that chart will eclipse the USD in reserve share any time soon.

![Global Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-reserves.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Incrementum](https://www.incrementum.li/en/home/)*

The USD+EUR share peaked in the early 2000s with over 80% share of global FX reserves, with only 8% of the global population. That was likely the high water mark. The world seems to be going from being unipolar towards being more multipolar, which is likely to extend to more diversified FX reserve practices over time.

Adversarial countries have less incentive to hold each others’ liabilities as reserves, gold as a neutral and inflation-protected asset is more desirable, and to the extent that countries do want to hold other countries’ liabilities as reserves, an emphasis on recycling trade surpluses into their biggest trade partners’ financial assets is the path of least resistance.

The average on-chain bitcoin holder is now below their cost basis. This is only the fifth time in bitcoin’s history that this has occurred:

![BTC Realized Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-btc-realized-price.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/realized-price/)*

Historically, this type of price action represents rather extreme capitulation, forced-selling, and deep value within the ecosystem. That doesn’t mean it can’t go lower, but it means that there’s blood in the streets.

The last time it was at these types of levels was spring 2020 during the Covid crash, which not-coincidentally was also the first time I bought and recommended bitcoin, at $6,900/coin.

Let’s look at Mr. Blonde’s chart again from the earlier section in this report about global liquidity, this time also considering the blue line which represents the year-over-year price of bitcoin:

![Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-global-liquidity.png)

If compare the blue bitcoin line to the orange global M2 line, it does a good job of answering the question, “what exactly does bitcoin hedge, anyway?”

Bitcoin, a decentralized peer-to-peer self-custodial cryptocurrency with a low supply inflation rate that eventually approaches zero supply inflation, has received criticism lately for not performing well during this sharply rising CPI environment. However, bitcoin generally does well when global liquidity is increasing, that is, when global broad money supply is growing rapidly in USD-denominated terms.

Bitcoin, in other words, hedges global money supply growth, a.k.a. currency debasement. By the time actual increases to consumer prices show up with a lag after that money supply growth, central banks are already trying to fight back and drain liquidity as much possible.

Bitcoin went up a ton when global liquidity was exploding higher, and then became under pressure when fiscal and monetary authorities reduced their printing and began to talk up their efforts to deal with the price inflation that followed in part from all of that liquidity.

I would expect bitcoin to do well again the next time we get an upturn in global liquidity, either from a weaker USD or from global acceleration of money supply in response to economic weakness.

**The 2022 Crypto Bank Panic**

The digital assets ecosystem has been struck by what is basically a bank panic. A lot of opaque leverage between counterparties has been liquidated.

It’s actually quite similar to what happened to the US financial system in 1907.

> The **Panic of 1907**, also known as the **1907 Bankers’ Panic** or **Knickerbocker Crisis**, was a financial crisis that took place in the United States over a three-week period starting in mid-October, when the New York Stock Exchange fell almost 50% from its peak the previous year. Panic occurred, as it was during a time of economic recession, and there were numerous runs on banks and on trust companies. The 1907 panic eventually spread throughout the nation when many state and local banks and businesses entered bankruptcy. The primary causes of the run included a retraction of market liquidity by a number of New York City banks and a loss of confidence among depositors, exacerbated by unregulated side bets at bucket shops.
>
> –[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panic_of_1907)

Back in 1907, the US had no central bank. Any bailouts that would come, had to arrive from elsewhere in the private sector. Similarly, the crypto ecosystem in 2022 of course has no central bank either. Insolvent crypto companies either fail or get bailed out by someone bigger.

Here’s roughly how the bank run played out so far.

First, liquidity was withdrawn from risk assets in general over the past year, due to tightening global liquidity conditions. I put this illustration together back in May for my [Digital Alchemy](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/) piece:

![Crypto Ponzis](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-crypto-ponzi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics, annotated by Lyn Alden*

Second, that liquidity withdraw broke the biggest ponzi-like thing in the industry, which was Terra/Luna. This one was rather transparent and thus relatively easy to identify ahead of time. I wrote about it with increasing concern in my April 3rd and May 1st premium reports, and then it unraveled by mid-May (which was even faster than I expected, but not shocking). A number of other people saw this one coming as well, since the numbers were out there for public analysis, even though most people in general were blind to it.

Third, that collapse of Terra/Luna crippled a number of entities in the broad digital assets ecosystem, and it took time to fester, with various asset prices falling across the board. A number of people began calling out Celsius as a particularly vulnerable lender and crypto yield platform. One of the most prominent voices about it was Cory Klippsten, the CEO of Swan Bitcoin. Celsius is a company that takes depositor funds, with the promise of high yields, and then uses those funds in various DeFi and lending practices, many of which are extremely risky, including more risky than other lenders in the industry.

Fourth, after a while of festering, the next shoe dropped. A big piece of this was the stEth-to-Eth depeg that I described as a problem in my June 2th premium report.

> One of the problems with DeFi is that people use it for recursive leverage. For example, they deposit ETH into a liquidity pool and get stablecoins in return, collateralized by that ETH. Then they take those stablecoins, deposit them into a liquidity pool, and get some other token, collateralized by those stablecoins, and then deposit that other token, and so on. There are various arbitrage opportunities to get high yields from layers of rehypothecation, and so there’s a lot of rehypothecation in the ecosystem in general. The same sort of recursive thing is happening with stETH arbitrage; some big players could really blow up if the stETH/ETH spread gets too wide for a persistent period of time.
>
> I would personally avoid having any significant deposits in crypto yield platforms going forward in this bearish environment.
>
> -Lyn Alden, June 12th 2022 report

Just hours later that night, Celsius indeed froze customer withdraws. They had exposure to stETH. The problems with Celsius and over a million frozen depositors subsequently became the hot topic in the digital assets ecosystem for several days.

Fifth, broader contagion occurred. Celsius ended up not being the biggest entity to go down; there was an even bigger and more opaque problem. Three Arrows Capital or “3AC”, a proprietary crypto trading firm with over $18 billion in peak net assets, reportedly went insolvent by June 14th due to leveraged trades that went bad. They were an investor in Terra/Luna, and they apparently got caught up with the stETH-to-ETH problem as well.

3AC was founded in 2012, survived through a decade of volatile crypto trading, and its CEO Zhu Su was a very prominent voice in the digital assets ecosystem with over 500,000 twitter followers. The firm had borrowed billions of dollars from multiple counterparties to invest in various altcoin/DeFi projects, and invested in multiple things that ended up being bad bets. Their collapse spread contagion around the whole ecosystem, with multiple of their lenders facing various levels of solvency risk.

Specifically, some firms reported that they had collateralized loans to 3AC, and thus were able to liquify them and recoup or minimize their own exposure to 3AC. A number of others like Voyager Digital, however, were hit by several hundreds of millions of uncovered losses due to their willingness to give uncollateralized loans to 3AC.

To what extent firms survive and/or have to restrict customer withdraws depends in large part on their risk management practices. Higher-grade ones are holding up better than lower-grade ones for the moment, but there is broad contagion.

The collapse of 3AC was not on most peoples’ 2022 crypto bingo sheets, and it was not something specific I was looking for either. 3AC was a proprietary trading firm and so they didn’t take depositor/investor funds, and they spread out their loan liabilities with multiple opaque counterparties that don’t communicate with each other well, and so there was minimal public analysis possible on their activities. Several of their actions allegedly have felony implications. They were huge, highly-leveraged, and using a lot of shady practices.

This was a classic example of “when the tide goes out, you find out who was swimming naked”. 3AC was pretty widely regarded as smart money, but it turns out they were just swimming naked with excess leverage and less equity than people thought. And they were big enough to risk bringing down most of the centralized crypto lenders, since they borrowed huge amounts from everyone.

Thus far, the private lender of last resort to a number of crypto lenders has been billionaire Sam Bankman-Fried and his firms FTX and Alameda Research.

Back in the Panic of 1907 Mr. J.P. Morgan served as the final liquidity backstop for the floundering banking system, and so far this time for the altcoin lending ecosystem, the backstop has been Mr. Bankman-Fried.

It remains to be seen if we’ve already seen the worst of it or not. I expect many altcoins to stagnate in price for the long run, but the “shock and awe” part of the crash due to the sudden withdraw of liquidity and bad leverage may be behind us by this point. We’ll see.

**Bitcoin-Only Ecosystem**

Bitcoin’s price has been sharply pulled down by all of this leverage, which was mostly used for altcoin trading.

Various entities routinely use bitcoin as their collateral to get loans, and they use those loans to bet in various altcoin projects. If those altcoin projects blow up, they find themselves unable to repay their loans and get their bitcoin collateral liquidated.

However, bitcoin itself continues to function normally, as carnage occurs in the surrounding ecosystem due to leverage. This past weekend was an environment of forced-sellers.

One noteworthy observation is that bitcoin-only startups having been weathering this storm better than altcoin startups so far.

Coinbase and many other altcoin-focused companies have been announcing layoffs. Meanwhile, many bitcoin-only companies have been retaining staff, still expanding staff, and using this as a marketing opportunity to get people off of risky platforms and into some self-custodial unlevered bitcoin instead. Some of them will surely face pressures to their growth expectations if bitcoin demand weakens for a period of time, but overall it was a much healthier and less speculative ecosystem to start with.

Earlier this year, I wrote about the significant cultural and practical divides between the bitcoin-only ecosystem and the broader (and orders of magnitude larger) altcoin/multicoin ecosystem:

> There were a lot of high-profile crypto company ads at the Super Bowl this year, including Coinbase, FTX, and Crypto.com. Some of these companies have been sponsoring major sports organizations, teams, or stadiums over the past year. These exchanges have large marketing budgets, and many of the protocols themselves have marketing budgets as well (Solana, Ethereum, Terra, Avalanche, etc) either in the form of a DAO or a traditional company that serves as a development and marketing hub for the protocol.
>
> This is in contrast to the more commodity-like bitcoin, which has no central organization or official marketing budget. Any marketing for it is grassroots word-of-mouth, independent analysis, or done by some of the exchanges and other companies in the industry.
>
> -Lyn Alden, February 22nd 2022 report

As an update since then, Coinbase and Crypto.com are now among the biggest crypto companies that publicly announced layoffs. Terra completely imploded. Solana faces increasingly recurring uptime issues where the whole blockchain goes down and gets manually restarted by validators coordinating in a Discord channel. Ethereum delayed their shift to proof-of-stake again, which is now over six years late compared to their original estimated timeline.

I continued with more detail here:

> Along these lines, the current digital asset ecosystem startup environment is very different depending on what part of the ecosystem you look at.
>
> *Bitcoin-Focused Startups*
>
> VCs and developers that build things on top of bitcoin today are consciously forgoing digital seigniorage when they do so.
>
> Projects in the bitcoin ecosystem tend to have a pretty high utility-to-speculation ratio, meaning they are trying to solve real-world problems rather than focusing on speculation or financialization. Bitcoin-focused companies tend to work on things like collaborative custody solutions, better wallets, payment solutions, peer-to-peer information transfer, mining-related liquidity provision, and so forth.
>
> Startups in this bitcoin-only industry use the established startup route of issuing normal illiquid seed and venture equity with a long-term exit horizon, rather than initial coin offerings. The Impervious browser is a good example; this is a company that is basically offering all the popularized applications of Web3, but doing so without a speculative token attached to it and instead is just doing it on the bitcoin network. Have most people heard of it yet? No.
>
> The downside of this long duration approach is that the seed/venture deals in this ecosystem are much smaller; we likely won’t see bitcoin-only firms named on major sports stadiums any time soon like what we’re seeing with broad crypto firms. And without a speculative incentive mechanism, it generally means slower adoption based on needs, rather than immediate adoption based on arbitrage opportunities and VC-funded temporary incentives to get users in quick.
>

## Emerging Markets Analysis

Emerging markets have been a challenging trade for quite a while.

Every time they start to get going in recent years, they get derailed by something. And investors have to maintain cautious position sizes, especially in any specific country with weaker rule of law than what is common throughout advanced economies.

But I see another opening for a reversal forming currently. Emerging markets as an asset class is currently out-of-consensus, few people are talking about it, and China’s credit impulse is turning up.

Will emerging markets once again snatch defeat from the jaws of victory, or will this be a longer reversal scenario in terms of equity returns and economic growth?

US investors also had (and still have) near record low exposure to emerging markets during this time. US investors and global investors have stuffed themselves pretty hard into US assets, similar to the late 1990s and the late 1960s, which were both at the later stages of long disinflationary growth periods.

Inflationary decades (such as the 1940s, 1970s, and the 2000s) have historically not been very good for US stock index performance, especially because at the start of those decades, US equity indices tend to be dominated by highly-valued growth equities after disinflationary decades. Inflationary decades tend to be good for value equities, commodities, and international/emerging types of markets.

Right now, it’s a risk-off environment broadly, similarly to shortly after the dotcom bubble blew up. So, US and global equities are under pressure. Meanwhile, the US Federal Reserve is trying to tighten monetary policy into an economic slowdown, in an attempt to rein in demand for goods and services to get a grip on inflation. The dollar is rather strong relative to other currencies.

As we move closer to a US and European recession, with the Fed eventually pausing its rate hikes and potentially doing some sort of SLR adjustment to boost Treasury market liquidity (which would allow commercial banks to buy a lot more Treasuries), I think the dollar index could reach a local top. Investors would likely want to own global things like emerging markets equities, gold, bitcoin, energy, and other assets like that for quite a while. US value equities still have a role to play in that environment as well.

In other words, I think we’ve seen the top in terms of investors piling into US equities for quite a while, possibly decades. That doesn’t mean that the S&P 500 won’t hit new highs, but if I’m right then it means that we won’t see that high of a ratio of US equity values relative to GDP and household assets. The conditions that led to that extreme would be hard to repeat anytime soon.

However, after this liquidation period, some of the cheap global equities that already spent the past decade stagnating, drained of dollar liquidity and investor fund flows, are primed for a period of outperformance.

Until then, there is quite a bit of turbulence, and I’d emphasize defense overall. As I write this, US economic indicators continue to decelerate and the Fed is tightening policy into that deceleration, and that puts pressure on the US and the rest of the world.

**Dollar Cycles**

Emerging markets are more sensitive to dollar strength and capital flows than advanced economies.

This is because a significant amount of their debt is dollar-denominated and owed to foreign creditors (and for the most part, not US creditors, ironically). When the dollar strengthens significantly, it acts as a form of quantitative tightening on emerging markets, and doesn’t care about the state of the economic cycle they are in. They could already be in a recession, and rather than getting the benefit of more dovish monetary policy, they get a second punch from a strengthening dollar, which hardens their liabilities.

The dollar has been in a strong cycle from late 2014 to the present time, with some ebbs and flows along the way.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-6-26-dxy.png)

Ever since Russia invaded Ukraine, I partially dropped my bearish view about the traditional dollar index itself, because the euro is such a big part of it, and the Euro Area faces rather extreme energy supply problems. My bullish energy view had to trump my dollar bearish view, when the new situation made them conflict somewhat. However, when we look more broadly at the dollar vs various currency pairs, I think the dollar is overbought here.

The dollar normally does well in two types of environments.

Firstly, the dollar typically does well when the US economy is booming and has some degree of tight monetary policy. George Soros and Stan Druckenmiller popularized this observation decades ago. In particular, the combination of loose fiscal policy and tight monetary policy sucks global dollar liquidity into domestic US markets, but is not permanently sustainable and eventually exhausts itself, because eventually the US runs out of foreign lenders for its Treasury market and starts crowding itself out after crowding the rest of the world out.

Secondly, the dollar does well if there is a liquidity crisis. The ex-US market has $13.4 trillion in dollar-denominated debt, according to the BIS. Most of this is owed to entities in Europe, Japan, and China, despite the fact that it is denominated in dollars. When global trade or capital flows run into a wall, such as in 2008 or 2020, dollar cash flows dry up but various entities still owe dollars, which represents demand for dollars. As a result, there is a “global dollar shortage” that spikes the dollar and forces the Fed to respond. If the Fed doesn’t respond, then the foreign sector on net can start selling US Treasuries and other US assets to get dollars, which if done at scale renders the US Treasury market illiquid like it did in March 2020, which is when the Fed responded. In total, various parts of the foreign sector owns about $50 trillion in US assets, of which $7 trillion is in Treasuries.

In recent months, we’ve been closer to the second scenario. Global liquidity has hit very low levels, and the Treasury market is getting wobbly in terms of liquidity, in part due to a lack of foreign buyers in recent months. The foreign sector has been a net-seller of Treasuries according to recent data, while the Fed is not buying any more and plans to start letting them mature off its balance sheet.

However, going forward for the next two years, there likely isn’t much specific fiscal stimulus in sight for the US due to political gridlock. A Democrat president with a Republican Congress has historically been a pretty austere fiscal environment. When one party controls both the Presidency and Congress, or when there is a Republican president with a Democrat Congress, there tends to be more fiscal stimulus in the form of higher spending or unfunded tax cuts. The existing structural deficits are pretty much locked in, but it’s hard to see major cyclical stimulus on top of that.

The US economy is more reliant on consumer spending than most other countries. In addition, US tax receipts are more reliant on rising asset prices than most countries’ tax receipts. And right now, the consumer and asset prices are being hit pretty hard.

As a result, I expect weak economic growth for the US going forward. Many investors may want to look outside of the US and Europe for places to put capital to work, which could benefit select emerging markets. However, there are a lot of landmines out there that US and global investors will be wanting to avoid as well. There aren’t that many attractive jurisdictions for capital at the moment.

**Risk Analysis**

The elephant in the room for a typical broad emerging markets index is China. China represents about 31% of the MSCI emerging markets index. Taiwan, which China claims as theirs, is another 16%. Together, they represent 47% of broad emerging markets exposure.

There’s a non-zero chance that China performs some sort of invasion of Taiwan within an investable time horizon (e.g. the next 0-5 years).

A reasonable question from an investor perspective, then, is whether they can afford to have any exposure to assets in China’s sphere of influence, due to the possible sanctions that could arise from such an event. Investors could be basically zeroed out of their positions, similar to what happened to foreign investors in Russian equities. Meanwhile, Chinese investors should be asking the same thing about their American investments, and their western investments more broadly.

China holds over $1 trillion in US Treasuries, and Chinese nationals hold trillions in total US assets, plus a lot of real estate in Canada, Australia, and elsewhere. These countries could freeze these assets, similar to what they did to Russian reserves, but at a much bigger scale. Meanwhile, a large portion of the S&P 500 has manufacturing operations in China; this infrastructure took many years to build and would take many years to replace. China could confiscate those assets.

Americans are heavily reliant on Chinese imports, including for critical things like medical equipment and supplies. Europe is also heavily reliant on China for two-way trade. China is the biggest trading partner with the majority of countries worldwide.

If China invades Taiwan at some point, Alibaba and Tencent and Taiwan Semi would be among the least of my concerns. In addition to the humanitarian situation, from an investor/macro perspective I’d be concerned about Apple, Tesla, and a vast swath of US mega-cap corporations, and the tight interconnection between the American economy and Chinese economy in general. There are trillions upon trillions of dollars of interconnections between the two nations.

To mitigate Chinese jurisdictional risk, I prefer not just holding a single broad emerging markets ETF for my emerging markets exposure. I also like some regional or single-country ETFs like the iShares MSCI India ETF (INDA) or the iShares Latin American 40 ETF (ILF) to spread out my emerging markets exposure, so that I don’t have 47% of my emerging markets exposure in China and Taiwan.

Investors can also consider the relatively small Freedom 100 Emerging Markets ETF (FRDM) which uses various criteria to filter out countries that don’t have a strong rule of law. Their biggest exposure is Chile followed by Taiwan, South Korea, and Poland. China doesn’t make the cut, and so in practice this ETF performs similarly to an ex-China emerging markets ETF.

Besides China-related risks specifically, the other key risk for many emerging markets is dollar liquidity and its association with dollar-denominated offshore debts, which I covered earlier in this report.

## Final Thoughts

My theme for a while has been that this will be an inflationary decade but not in a straight line, and we are indeed likely shifting to one of those corrective periods now. There will be some disinflationary cycles within what otherwise has a setup for a persistent inflationary situation.

I view prices of opex commodities (such as energy markets primarily) as being relatively sticky, while prices of capex commodities (such as copper especially) are more vulnerable in this weakening economic environment.

Opex commodities refer to commodities that are used on a regular basis as part of recurring operational expenditures, while capex commodities are more discretionary for bursts of usage, such as construction, and then can go through periods with less usage. Examples of opex commodities would be oil and gas, while examples of capex commodities would be copper and nickel.

Beyond this cyclical downturn in capex commodities, there is a lot of building to do this decade to bring more commodity supplies online, to increase and improve various types of infrastructure, and to change global supply chains to reflect a more multipolar high-friction world rather than a unipolar low-friction world.

This is all pretty inflationary in general, especially when combined with financial repression (which refers to policymakers holding interest rates below the rate of inflation due to unsustainably high debt levels).

In the meantime, I continue to favor gold, cash, Treasuries, energy pipelines, and defensive value equities overall, along with some more volatile exposure to emerging markets, bitcoin, and a few growth stocks.
