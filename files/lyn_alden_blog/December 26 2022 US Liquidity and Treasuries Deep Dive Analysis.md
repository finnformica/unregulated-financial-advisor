## Macro View

Overall, the outlook for the global economy in the first half of 2023 suggests further weakness ahead.

The one potential saving grace for the global economy is a weaker dollar (which eases the debt burdens of many developing countries, since there is vast amounts of dollar-denominated debt in countries that cannot print dollars), but so far the data are suggesting that this won’t be enough.

The dollar, while off its highs, still remains restrictive for much of the world.

Some of the indicators like that have turned up in the second half of 2022 while others remained in a downtrend. This is likely tied to the decline in inflation, the drop in the dollar index, and easing of forward Fed policy expectations.

The market basically sees a light at the end of the tunnel for ongoing Fed tightness as inflation cools off, and has priced the dollar down after a huge run-up all year. This has allowed certain parts of the global economy to breathe a little bit while other parts continue to sag.

However, the Fed is still tightening policy, and even their current level of tightness naturally gets tighter over time if they simply hold rates where they are, because more and more debt (public and private) starts maturing and getting refinanced at a higher interest rate over the coming quarters. This includes federal debt, corporate debt, mortgage debt, and other forms of debt. They’re also performing quantitative tightening month after month.

Specifically, the Fed is trying to create higher unemployment levels to try to reduce wage inflation. The gray lines on the chart below represent recessions:

![Unemployment vs Fed Funds Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-unemployment-vs-fed-funds.png)

Economies and markets are extraordinarily complex systems, and everything is a matter of probabilities. Back-tests that worked well over the past two or three decades are likely unsuitable for this environment, which has many characteristics that were present back in the 1940s.

With that complexity in mind, my base case analysis points towards recession in 2023, but I continue to look for signs that could suggest otherwise, and will happily report them if they materialize.

**Impact on US Real Estate**

Commercial real estate never fully recovered from COVID-19 and the shift to (at least partial) remote work, beginning in 2020. The pandemic didn’t create the remote work trend, but it did accelerate it.

High-rise office buildings in general remain below pre-pandemic occupancy rates. Now that interest rates are higher, as real estate debt matures and needs to be refinanced quarter after quarter, it will put pressure on the marginal property owner. If cashflows are already struggling, and interest expense keeps rising, then that takes a toll on the bottom line and eventually results in liquidations, bankruptcies, and reduced employment.

The residential real estate market in the US is more robust, since it isn’t negatively impacted by (and even benefits from) the trend towards remote work. However, it is coming off of a stimulus high.

The Case-Shiller US National Home Price Index is slightly off its highs and rolling over, but is still positive on a year-over-year basis:

![Case Shiller Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-case-shiller.png)

The number of houses sold has fallen dramatically, but house prices in aggregate are holding up (especially in linear markets), because there’s both a buyer’s strike and a seller’s strike. [Liz Ann Sonders](https://twitter.com/LizAnnSonders), the chief investment strategist at Charles Schwab, charts the current trends:

![Sonders Housing Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-sonders-housing-tweets.png)

With so many people having locked in long-term fixed mortgage rates at much lower levels than they are currently, there is a very strong incentive for people who own homes to keep them, if possible. However, affordability for new buyers, with the combination of high prices and high mortgage rates, is extremely poor at the moment, so buyers have dropped.

Over time, some people simply have to sell their house. There are deaths in the family, changes in employment conditions, downsizing or upsizing of housing needs, and so forth. Similarly, some people are simply going to buy houses, either with cash or with a mortgage, and do their best to push through affordability challenges. And quarter after quarter as that happens (at sharply reduced volumes), the average mortgage on the street goes up, and it negatively impacts household cashflow.

If Jerome Powell came out tomorrow and basically said, “inflation is done, we’re going to cut rates by 200 basis points, let’s go!” then there would be a large drop in the dollar index, a sharp rise in global liquidity, and probably a big bounce in global asset prices across the board. Price inflation would likely re-assert itself as well, probably tied to higher energy prices in particular.

But of course, that’s not what Jerome Powell is likely to do; he is keeping his foot on the brake, trying to keep grinding inflation lower via demand suppression and higher unemployment rates. However, his rate of change of interest rates hikes is slowing down, and the market has been pricing that in for the dollar. And he’s going against the fact that the federal government is running structural deficits, which continually pours money into the private sector.

The year 2022 was a story about valuation compression. Tighter monetary conditions resulted in downward pressure on equity earnings multiples, as earnings themselves still held up rather well. Even things like gold can struggle in that environment because it’s mainly the dollar that is hardening.

The year 2023, on the other hand, is likely to be a story about weaker corporate earnings, as the slow economic growth rate and compressed margins take their toll. As a result, I think it is likely to be a better environment for gold relative to equities.

**The Bank of Japan’s Shock Announcement**

The Bank of Japan surprised global markets last week by announcing that it would raise its yield curve control cap to 0.50% from 0.25% on the 10-year Japanese government bond.

That doesn’t seem like much in terms of basis points, but it’s a huge percent gain in yields overnight, which sent the yen up and Japanese stocks down in a stepwise move. The sharp drop in bond prices to reflect the higher yields wiped out years of meager interest payments for bondholders.

Here’s the chart of Japan’s 10-year yield:

![JGB Yield Cap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-jgb-cap.png)

In order to maintain a yield cap that is below where the market would otherwise price yields, a central bank has to buy any bond that tries to trade over that yield cap. So, they give up control over the size of their balance sheet in order to maintain the yield at the level they want. When phrased in reverse, they are providing a price floor for bonds (since bond prices trade inversely to their yields), and are willing to buy any bonds that try to trade below that price.

The end-game for countries with very high public debt-to-GDP ratios who have debts denominated in their own currency is generally yield curve control. Otherwise, they encounter a fiscal spiral. For example, a country with 150% debt-to-GDP paying an average interest expense of 5% on its debt would pay out 7.5% of GDP in interest every year, and with gaping deficits as it can’t realistically raise enough revenue or cut enough expenses to offset that rising interest expense.

The last time that developed countries found themselves in this situation in aggregate was back in the 1940s. Yield curve control is one of the mechanisms for “inflating the debt away” since they artificially hold interest rates well below the prevailing inflation rate.

Here’s the US for example. They did a 2.5% yield curve control cap on the 10-year yield (and lower for shorter durations) through much of the 1940s to maintain cheap financing for World War II, when federal debt-to-GDP reached over 100%. This was despite the fact that inflation averaged about 6% per year from the early 1940s through the early 1950s, and despite the fact that inflation reached as high as 19% year-over-year at the worst point. It was a very different response to what they did in the 1970s when debt relative to GDP was low.

![Policy Response](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-policy-response.png)

Japan has a government debt-to-GDP ratio that is well over 200%, which necessitates low yields. However, earlier this year I remarked that I thought their yield cap was too low. In order to prevent a fiscal spiral they have no choice but to keep yields low, but they don’t have to be *that* low. By setting the yield cap too low, it becomes unnecessarily hard to maintain and results in fast currency devaluation.

> The BOJ could have chosen a higher yield cap, like 0.50% or 0.75% or 1.00%, which would have made it less costly on the currency to defend. With their debt as high as it is, they have no choice other than to keep yields low, but I would argue that they chose too low of a yield cap. So, this is basically an unforced error, unless significant currency devaluation was indeed their intention.
>
> -May 1, 2022 Premium Report

The problem, however, is that if a central bank lets go of their first yield cap, then the market may not trust future attempts at yield caps, which can make them difficult to maintain even at a higher level. Yield caps are easier to maintain when the central bank convinces the market to do most of the work for them- if a bond yield goes over the yield cap, a private sector entity could buy the bonds knowing that they could sell them for a higher price (lower yield) to the central bank, if yields remain above the cap. However, if private sector entities aren’t sure about the reliability of a yield cap, they may be less likely to jump in and buy bonds whose yields trade over the cap, since the central bank might leave them holding the bag.

So, at the same time as the Bank of Japan announced an increase to its rate cap, it also announced an increase in the amount of bonds that it is buying.

The Bank of Japan therefore finds itself in an awkward place. Their official inflation target is 2%, but Japan’s CPI is 3.7%, and they are *increasing* their rate of bond purchases. They have higher rates now, but these rates are well below the target inflation rate and the actual inflation rate. They’re not necessarily in a competition for positive real rates; they are in a competition about their rates not being too far below inflation compared to other countries.

Overall, we’ll see how this new policy sticks. This should be decent for the yen in the intermediate term. I expect that the Bank of Japan will be able to retrain the market to this new baseline. Long-term yield curve control by major countries hasn’t been seen in decades, and back then, information moved a lot more slowly. Watching central banks perform yield curve control is a rather novel thing to watch in real time.

*Portfolio Notes on Brookfield*

The company formerly known as Brookfield Asset Management “BAM” recently performed a transaction that should unlock decent value and make things more transparent, but requires some commentary due to the name change.

The existing entity, which is the parent company, changed it name and ticker from Brookfield Asset Management “BAM” to Brookfield Corporation “BN”. It then spun off a 25% interest in a new entity called Brookfield Asset Management “BAM” (hence the potential confusion). Brookfield Corporation owns the majority of Brookfield Asset Management.

Brookfield has always been a complex business entity. They directly hold and operate various assets (commercial real estate, infrastructure assets, renewable energy assets, private equity, etc) and also operate funds as an asset manager that invest in those types of assets as well. There are a number of publicly-traded partnerships that they serve as the asset manager for. Their full reach is huge; they’re one of the biggest asset managers of “real assets” (real estate, infrastructure, etc) in the world.

With this transaction, the asset management business is transparently separated from the operating business, although the operating business owns the majority of the asset management business. BAM, the asset manager, doesn’t have many expenses or reinvestment needs and will pay out the majority of its earnings as dividends, and thus will be a higher-yielding entity. BN, the operating corporation, will carry on basically as the conglomerate historically did, with a lower dividend payout ratio and more reinvestment and internal compounding. For an investor that wants exposure to the whole entity, the corporation BN is likely the better fit. For an investor that wants more current yield, the asset manager BAM is likely the better fit.

The chairman of the new publicly-traded asset manager entity, BAM, is Mark Carney. He is the former Governor of the Bank of England, and prior to that, the former Governor of the Bank of Canada.

I am keeping the operating corporation (BN) in the newsletter portfolio. That basically just continues the prior holding of what was previously known as BAM. Since BN owns the majority of BAM, BN is mostly the same entity as it was before, except with a 25% portion of the asset manager spun out to trade publicly.

In contrast, I am adding BAM to the fortress income portfolio in place of BlackRock (BLK), due to its higher yield. The fortress income portfolio does not currently own Brookfield in any capacity but will do so after this small move.

## US Domestic Liquidity Conditions

One of my preferred indicators for US domestic liquidity conditions is the Federal Reserve balance sheet, minus the Treasury general account, minus reverse repos.

Here’s the chart for it (blue line):

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-domestic-liquidity.png)

It’s expected to head lower in Q1 2023. I discussed the importance of this chart and its recent correlation with the S&P 500 (red line) back in my [November 27th report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2022-11-27/):

> These charts (5-year and 18-month respectively) show the S&P 500 compared to a particular liquidity calculation. The liquidity calculation, which I’ve seen used by a number of analysts and makes sense, is the Fed balance sheet minus the Treasury General Account and minus reverse repurchase agreements:
>
> ![S&P 500 vs Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-spx-vs-liquidity.png)
>
> It’s arguably a chart crime to some extent, because correlation does not necessarily imply causation, but in my view the mechanism is pretty straightforward.
>
> As more bonds are issued and the Fed isn’t buying (or is even a net seller like they are now), then that means the private sector has to absorb more of the bond issuance, which crowds out potential equity investment. It’s also generally strong for the dollar, which slows down global growth.
>
> The Fed controls the size of its balance sheet, and the Treasury Department controls the size of the Treasury General Account.
>
> When the Fed expands its balance sheet by creating reserves and buying bonds, it is injecting liquidity into financial markets. When it reduces its balance sheet, by being a net seller of bonds and destroyer of reserves, it is removing liquidity from financial markets.
>
> When the Treasury expands the TGA, by raising debt and bringing in taxes faster than it spends money, it is sucking liquidity out of the system into a void. When it reduces the TGA, it is spending more money into the economy than it is currently withdrawing, or in other words putting liquidity from that void back into the economy. So, the smaller the TGA, the smaller the void of dead capital. There are some months where the Treasury can be injecting liquidity while the Fed removes it, or vice versa.
>
> Going forward, the Fed plans to continue draining its balance sheet month after month, as part of their goal of quelling inflation. The Treasury General Account is intended to be flat-but-choppy. Right now, the TGA is $480 billion. They expect to have $700 billion by the end of this quarter (which I doubt they’ll reach, I don’t think they’ll get over $600 billion), and then they expect to get to $500 billion by the end of Q1 2023 (which would be virtually unchanged from $480 billion).
>
> So, overall, with the Fed continuing to drain liquidity and the Treasury probably being rather neutral, liquidity conditions continue to point downward unless or until something changes, which (at least as one important variable) suggests ongoing unremarkable performance for many risk assets. I don’t have a strong view towards whether the market will chop along in a big sideways band, or if it will reach lower lows. If we do get a recession as the yield curve expects, I would expect the latter, with stocks like Apple and Tesla near the top of the equity indices weighing things down.
>
> -November 27th, 2022 premium report

This ended up being a very useful indicator to share; both it and the S&P 500 indeed went down since November 27th. Plus, Apple and Tesla went down more than the S&P 500.

And it could have been worse. Had the TGA hit their year-end target of $700 billion, this domestic liquidity indicator (and probably equities) would have been down more over the past month. However, I was right about the TGA not reaching $700 billion by year-end. I said they probably wouldn’t even hit $600 billion, and they actually remained below $500 billion. The Treasury Department, both previously under Secretary Mnuchin and currently under Secretary Yellen, is persistently unreliable at meeting the various targets it sets for itself.

![TGA Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-tga-annotated.png)

Going forward, the Fed is the main driver of domestic liquidity, as it draws down tens of billions of dollars per month from its balance sheet. They continue to push liquidity down, in addition to raising interest rates.

Meanwhile, around that downward baseline, the TGA is likely to be flat and choppy, in some months adding to liquidity and in some months taking it away.

Overall, domestic liquidity is likely going to decrease in Q1 2023. Due to that, US equities might or might not go down in that environment, but they are unlikely to do great. They could be choppy-but-positive as they were through the period of 2018-2019, for example. Or they could outright go down, especially if the US encounters more recessionary conditions and weaker corporate earnings.

## US Treasury Market Update

Given lackluster liquidity conditions and lackluster economic conditions as described above, I think short-duration Treasuries remain one of the better places to store excess capital in the intermediate term.

I spent a few years being bearish on Treasuries, especially long duration ones, and especially in inflation-adjusted terms. The parts of my portfolios that are not in equities or precious metals have tended to be in short-duration Treasuries of various types, and I have underweighted Treasuries in general despite being rather bearish on the economy at times.

My bearishness on Treasuries mainly began in 2019, where I wrote a [public article](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/) analyzing the aspects of what I viewed at the time to be a global bond bubble. I also discussed the prospect for major currency debasement and inflation, even though at the time the prevailing environment was about disinflation and negative-yielding bonds.

> I read thousands of emails from my readers, and one of the key themes I see is that people are concerned about the next stock market crash, and perhaps rightly so. By many measures we have high stock valuations in the United States after a decade-long bull market, and we have high corporate debt levels both inside the United States and globally.
>
> But one question I rarely receive is: “Are bonds safe?”
>
> From a historical perspective, the bond market is acting a lot weirder than the stock market at the current time. The stock market looks like it often does at this point in the business cycle, which is not great for the probable range of forward returns, but bonds are doing things they haven’t done *ever before in history*, which should give investors pause.
>
> […]
>
> So far, central bank tools have not been inflationary because they have primarily benefited asset prices rather than middle class consumption. They printed money, but kept the money on the central bank balance sheets by buying bonds.
>
> If central bank actions get more aggressive, combine with fiscal policies, and start targeting the middle class, they have the power to override these various deflationary forces with sheer monetary expansion. They can issue helicopter money to pay off debts, boost inflation, build infrastructure, bail out unfunded pension systems, and prop up the middle class if that’s what policymakers decide to do.
>
> I wouldn’t want to be holding a 20-year or 30-year bond at super-low fixed yields in that kind of environment. Negative yields would be even more vulnerable.
>
> –[July 2019: Are We in a Bond Bubble, or is This the New Normal?](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/)

I followed this up with another [public piece](https://www.lynalden.com/treasury-bonds-risks/) in 2020, analyzing periods in history where US Treasuries severely underperformed inflation:

> U.S. Treasury bonds (and bills and notes) are often thought of as risk-free investments.
>
> This is true in a sense; the U.S. Treasury has always fully paid back its debts in dollar-denominated nominal terms. However, what many investors miss is the fact that the purchasing power of those bonds is not guaranteed. They can lose real purchasing power during their holding period, and sometimes rather dramatically.
>
> This chart, for example, shows the historical yield of the 10-year Treasury note (blue line) along with the forward 10-year annualized inflation-adjusted return from the start of that year (orange bars):
>
> ![Treasury Bond Real Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/treasury-bond-real-returns.png)
>
> *Data Sources: [Robert Shiller](http://www.econ.yale.edu/~shiller/data.htm), [Aswath Damodaran](http://pages.stern.nyu.edu/~adamodar/New_Home_Page/datafile/histretSP.html)*
>
> As you can see, there were long stretches of negative real returns.
>
> In addition, ETFs such as the iShares 20+ year Treasury Bond ETF (TLT) don’t hold their bonds to maturity, so they can experience a capital loss on their investment if the bond yield goes up (and thus bond price goes down) during the investor’s holding period.
>
> It’s important for investors to understand the risks associated with this defensive asset class, and to ensure that they have a risk-management process for their holdings, just like with any other asset class. I like Treasuries as a tactical holding, but it’s a position that needs to be managed just like any other.
>
> –[The Subtle Risks of Treasury Bonds, February 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/treasury-bonds-risks/)

Bonds briefly did well during the COVID shock, but overall have underperformed stocks since 2019, and the long-duration ones are down significantly in nominal terms. The year 2022 was one of the worst years for long-duration US Treasuries on record.

In the middle of 2022, as Treasury bond prices fell rapidly and yields shot over 3%, I became a bit more optimistic about bonds, and added a small amount of 7-10 year Treasuries (IEF) to some of my portfolios as a disinflation hedge (in what is generally an inflation-oriented set of portfolios). This ended up being premature, as bond yields went higher before eventually cooling off. The 10-year Treasury note is currently yielding around 3.75%.

In other words, as structurally bearish on bonds as I have been, even I wasn’t quite bearish enough. It has been absolutely brutal for the bond bulls.

**Current Long-Term View**

I remain structurally disinterested in bonds for what will probably be the remainder of this decade, at least in inflation-adjusted terms and also perhaps nominal terms.

The sheer indebtedness of most developed nations means that although their central banks can tighten monetary policy for a period of time, they can’t afford structurally positive inflation-adjusted rates on their debt. The debtholders pretty much mathematically have to be the bag holders with long-term negative inflation-adjusted purchasing power.

As a result, I expect inflation rates to spend more time above bond yields than below them for this decade. My [November public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/november-2022-newsletter/) covered the fiscal spiral that will likely become an increasing problem in the second half of this decade.

My base case continues to be that the 2020s decade will be an inflationary one overall, with periods of cyclical disinflation (including the cyclical disinflation we are in right now). Depending on various policy actions, I expect the next cyclical wave of inflation to occur in the 2024/2025 timeframe, although this is subject to change as economic data and policymaker decisions roll in.

**Current Intermediate-Term View**

I am tactically bullish on short-duration Treasuries on a risk/reward basis for the first half of 2023 until there is more clarity about the global economy.

With recession indicators piling up, I expect 2023 to be another volatile, choppy year for many risk assets unless or until there is a policy shift regarding domestic liquidity.

Historically, when the 2-year Treasury yield is below the Fed’s interest rate, the Fed is at or near the top of their rate hiking cycle (unless they go full-on Volcker and push way higher above all market objections). The Fed probably has a bit higher to go, but not necessarily much higher:

![2Yr vs Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-2yr-vs-fed-funds.png)

Meanwhile, the spread between the 10-year note and 3-month bill points towards recession:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-yield-curve.png)

In a bullish case, it should be reminded that sometimes yield curve inversions precede recessions by 18 months or more, which could delay a recession until 2024. Other indicators point towards 2023 as the probable recession year, but this analysis could change over time.

**Treasury Yields vs Dividend Yields**

There are dozens of blue-chip companies in the US that have raised their annual dividend for multiple decades in a row without fail. The most distinguished subset of them have surpassed 50 or 60 years of consecutive annual dividend increases.

One indicator I like to look at it is the spread between the dividend yield of these sorts of blue chip stocks and the 10-year Treasury yield. Historically, the 10-year Treasury yield spends most of its time higher than various blue chip dividend yields, because the dividends grow every year while a 10-year Treasury yield to maturity does not grow. Naturally, a 10-year should give you more current income while a dividend growth stock gives you the potential (with some risk) for more total income over the long run thanks to growth.

However, from the early 2010s to the early 2020s, most of these blue chip dividend stocks were yielding higher than the 10-year Treasury yield. The yield spread for almost all of them looked like this:

![Dividend Treasury Yield Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-26-dividend-treasury-spread.png)

In other words, with a large number of blue chip dividend stocks, you could get higher current income *and* expected growth of that income, relative to 10-year Treasury yields.

This was not because dividend stocks were unusually cheap, but rather it was because Treasuries were unusually expensive. Sovereign debt of the US and the rest of the developed world was in a bubble. At the peak, there was over $18 trillion worth of nominally negative-yielding debt. In the US yields weren’t negative, but they were very low. Another way of phrasing this was that bond prices were unusually high.

In 2022, this Treasury bubble has popped, and once again the 10-year Treasury note generally yields higher than these blue-chip dividend growers. However, the spread still remains smaller than normal, meaning that Treasuries are still rather expensive compared to blue-chip dividend growers. Treasuries just aren’t *as* expensive relative to them as they were for much of the past decade.

There may be 6-12 month periods where overweighting Treasuries makes tactical sense (e.g. if you expect a major recession), but over a 5+ year view going forward, income-oriented equities generally remain more attractive than Treasuries.

## Final Thoughts: Market Regime Change

The 2010s decade of investing, stretching into mid-2020 during COVID-19 lockdowns and stimulus, was defined by the rise of big tech and social media.

I continue to view that group as unattractive for the remainder of the 2020s decade compared to the opportunities available in real assets (commodities, infrastructure, industrials, pipelines, gold, and so forth).
