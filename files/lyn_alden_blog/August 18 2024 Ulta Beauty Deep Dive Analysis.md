## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to trend almost perfectly sideways, as the Treasury and Fed partially oppose each other in terms of policy actions.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-domestic-liquidity.png)

As a base case, I continue to expect this to trend approximately sideways until the reverse repo facility usage drops to zero (from the current level of $330 billion) or until some other acute liquidity issue emerges. Basically, policymakers seem intent on maintaining sideways liquidity conditions until they find the next liquidity floor. And that floor is gradually rising due to the gradual nominal expansion of the banking system’s assets and liabilities over time.

**The “Yen Carry Trade” Market Hiccup**

In my premium report from two weeks ago, which was about the increasing potential for asset rotations, I discussed how much foreign capital was stuffed into U.S. capital markets:

> There is a giant engine of almost automatic capital that keeps flowing into large-cap U.S. stocks in particular. Foreign capital keeps flowing in, and domestic employee 401(k) paychecks keep flowing in. And a lot of it is passively weighted by market capitalization, and so much of it automatically flows toward the top.
>
> Effectively the way this works is that we buy depreciating goods from abroad and we sell portions of our appreciating capital ownership to them in return. But it feels nice while we do it, at lease for those of us that own assets, because it’s really good for asset prices.
>
> However, China has been reducing its Treasury holdings, and Japan has been using some of its assets to intervene in its currency weakness. In addition, Japan raised its interest rate to 0.25%, which doesn’t seem like much but it’s their highest level in over 15 years.
>
> ![Japan Interest Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-japan-interest-rate.png)
>
> Combined with Japan’s structural current account surplus, large amounts of capital allocated to foreign markets, and high levels of productivity, relatively minor hawkish moves from them can be rather supportive of their weak currency. There’s been a significant drop in the USDJPY exchange rate, both as U.S. economic data softens and as Japan gradually takes more measures to support its currency from getting too weak.
>
> ![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-usdjpy.png)
>
> Right now, all of those attributes have pushed a lot of capital to one side of the boat. Foreign investors have a very high percentage of their allocations in U.S. assets, which props up our equity valuations and our currency (which in some ways is beneficial, and other ways is harmful), which then feeds on itself.
>
> -August 4, 2024 premium report

Humorously, within several hours of that report coming out (Sunday night eastern time, which was Monday morning of August 5th in Japan), there was a huge market shock somewhat related to this issue. I wrote about it because there were some tremors along these lines in the prior week, but soon after the report on Sunday/Monday some of it came out with a bang.

Japan’s stock market dropped over 12% in one day on that Monday the 5th, which was their biggest one-day drop since 1987. When U.S. markets opened on Monday, they also fell about 3%, which was the biggest one-day drop in a while. During the late hours on Sunday, bitcoin had a flash-crash of over 15%, because there’s very few things that can be sold on Sunday overnight, and bitcoin is the most liquid 24/7 market.

Starting on Tuesday the 6th, markets quickly began to recover. Bitcoin quickly regained its price, and both the S&P 500 and the Nikkei 225 began recovering and are higher now than where they were on the close of Friday, August 2nd.

The “yen carry trade” became the main news item of the week. A carry trade is when a low-yielding weak currency is borrowed, and then used to buy a higher-yielding stronger currency or other assets. For example, an investor or institution might borrow yen which has a 0.25% short-term interest rate, and use it to buy Mexican pesos which have a 10.75% short-term interest rate, so that they can profit from that spread with leverage. Or they might literally buy U.S. stocks. As long as the yen keeps weakening, that works well. Both foreign investors and Japanese investors can do that trade, since Japan has relatively open capital markets. And domestically, people can borrow yen and buy Japanese stocks too, which is what Berkshire Hathaway did, with much success.

The Wall Street Journal [reported](https://www.wsj.com/livecoverage/stock-market-today-dow-sp500-nasdaq-live-08-05-2024/card/why-the-yen-carry-trade-unwind-has-further-to-go-SYsvxWxTvLGLCUsDTDM5) that there was 275 trillion yen worth of cross-border lending at the start of this year, which depending on the exchange rate at any given time is around $1.7 trillion USD worth of yen. Not all of that is used for the carry trade, so the cross-border amount for the carry trade would be somewhat less than that figure. However, there are domestic Japanese firms doing the trade as well, so overall it’s a fairly large leveraged position.

In addition, because volatility has been so low lately, many institutions were “selling volatility”, which means selling options. They collect free money on a regular basis, but whenever there’s a volatility spike they take heavy losses.

During the week leading up to the market shock, U.S. economic data came in soft, and so the dollar index weakened a bit, as the market priced in more expectations of rate cuts by the Fed in the coming quarters. And then the Bank of Japan increased interest rates from 0.10% to 0.25%, which is small but meaningful. This combination helped strengthen the yen vs the dollar significantly (after a long period of weakening).

And when the yen strengthens a lot in a short period of time, the denominator of leverage for all sorts of things, ranging from Japanese stocks to various global assets, is what’s increasing.

So, all those borrowers who were short yen and long other assets, basically got an abrupt punch in the face. Upon taking losses, they had to quickly deleverage their positions. That caused there to be a period of very high volatility, and so leveraged volatility-sellers found themselves taking big hits and having to de-risk their positions as well, which means even more selling. So with many entities all trying to deleverage a the same time, it’s possible to get quite a big downward move in equities and other risk assets.

Some of this is unwound now, in the sense that many types of portfolios now have more risk management in place and are pressing the trade less. But most of it is still there as far as I’m aware. There was no large-scale deleveraging of trillions of dollars worth of yen leverage. And the actual amount of foreign capital (much of which is unleveraged) that is stuffed into U.S. capital markets is measured in the tens of trillions. So, this wasn’t any sort of major rotation yet. It was just a big hiccup from leveraged entities that had to quickly deleverage.

My friend Peter McCormack invited me onto his show for an impromptu [interview](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=I8JzsnZVylY) about the issue, which we filmed on August 6th and released the next day. In that interview, I used the opportunity to push back on some of the public alarmism and overreaction that I was seeing on social media about it.

> **Peter:** Secondly, we’ve talked a lot about this in the past where economies start to fail, that you’d expect to see a more of a shorter cycles and this kind of yo-yoing of rates. Is this what we’re starting to see? Are we seeing something breaking in real time?
>
> **Lyn:** Well, I would phrase it a little bit differently. I think a lot of people- I mean when the yen was weakening people were saying “oh it’s the end of Japan, all the stuff is coming back to hit them now.” And ironically when we had this period of yen strengthening, and the Nikkei down, people are like “oh it’s the end of Japan, all the stuff is coming back to hit them.” So, well, what is it? When the yen weakens is it bad for them, when it strengthens is it bad for them? Like, what exactly are we calling the crisis here?
>

Which is to say that, this was a fairly mechanical shift, there was a deleveraging that happened and there is probably more deleveraging ahead but probably less abrupt, because now the pivot is in place, now the trend change is more obvious. Kind of like the “fear of God” has been put back into anyone who was running a leveraged book. Like, they were reminded “yes there’s risk” and it kind of changes psychology in the market.
>

Their stocks are not particularly expensive, so it’s not like you’re in the late 1980s when they got their famous Japanese bubble. The valuations were silly back then, whereas now the stock market is fairly reasonably valued compared to their cashflows and things like that.
>
> So it’s not surprising that when there’s a 12% drop there’s a bunch of people saying, “wait a second, that was almost entirely mechanical, so I’ll go ahead and buy names that are inexpensive and oversold.” There are unlevered entities that can come in and take advantage of levered entities that basically had to sell, kind of like if you see bitcoin get a liquidation and if you’re unlevered and have some cash sitting around you’ll come in and buy it, so we’ve seen a rebound in bitcoin as well. And so I think that’s normal.
>
> -August 6th interview

I then went on to re-iterate that there is still a lot of global capital stuffed into U.S. markets and so if we get a gradual Fed easing cycle and weaker dollar, we could see a significant rotation in capital flows over years with various effects from that. So it’s not that these things don’t matter, but that it’s not always an emergency.

There were market professionals publicly calling for emergency Fed rate cuts on television in the days after the market event, and things like that, and some of them had to then walk those comments back. But the Fed doesn’t care about one-time 12% down Nikkei moves or 3% down S&P 500 moves. They care about the employment rate, the inflation rate, and disruptions to core market functioning including acute illiquidity crises in Treasury markets, repo markets, and the banking system. They would generally only do an emergency response to direct hits on those core system areas, rather than stocks being down due to a bunch of institutions having to abruptly deleverage their books.

The Bank of Japan’s governor did come out and [reassure](https://www.marketwatch.com/livecoverage/stock-market-today-dow-futures-rise-as-stock-market-rebound-continues/card/bank-of-japan-soothes-nerves-by-saying-it-won-t-raise-rates-when-markets-are-unstable-X9qkZ14O8DkLDeGhhj9U) market participants that they don’t plan to further raise rates during periods of unstable markets, which eased investor concerns a bit.

During true crises, I send out extra reports between normal reports as needed. For example, I sent a bunch of extra reports out in Q1 2020 in response to early pandemic news before the crash, and then sent out more reports as the crash played out along with some of the major liquidity responses and bottoming areas of that crash.

This hiccup wasn’t a true crisis, and so I continued with business as normal. It’s newsworthy to discuss, and an opportunity for traders, but in my view nothing worth abruptly changing core positions around.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The United States’ economy continues to be in a malaise, in the sense that it’s gradually weakening but with no clear sign of recession. Some areas of the economy are doing much better than others, which is partially tied to the big divergence between loose fiscal policy (large deficits) and tight monetary policy (high interest rates and ongoing central bank balance sheet reduction) which affect certain industries differently.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-gdpnow.png)

The Fed Fund futures market is pricing in a likely 25 basis point cut in September, with a smaller possibility of 50 basis points, and then gradually further cuts in November, December, and beyond.

By June 2025, the market is currently pricing 3.50% to 3.75% Fed short-term interest rates as the base case with a probability bell curve around that figure, down from the current region of 5.25% to 5.50%.

In my view, as I described in the [July public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/july-2024-newsletter/), I don’t think rate cuts to that level are going to be particularly stimulating for the U.S. economy as a whole. They could encourage new homebuying activity, but they wouldn’t activate much existing homeowner refinancing. Their impact on large corporations would be minimal, while their impact on smaller businesses could be more substantial.

We’ve now had an economic cycle where we’ve had the first “higher highs” in interest rates in four decades. This upcoming period might be the first economic cycle where we’ll have the first “higher lows” in interest rates in that timeframe. That likely means that there would be less overall refinancing effects.

![Interest Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-interest-rates.png)

Retail sales have been one of the more negatively impacted areas in the past two years from tighter monetary policy. Consumers in the lower half of the income spectrum, which generally don’t have many financial assets, have been struggling in this environment. Consumers in the upper half of the income spectrum, and especially toward the higher end, do have plenty of assets and have done much better. Retail sales numbers in aggregate tend to be geared toward the lower end of the income spectrum, because wealthy people don’t grow their retail spending in a linear correlation with their wealth, and instead tend to divert their purchasing power elsewhere (travel and hospitality, investment assets, etc.)

Nominal retail sales continue to edge higher over time, but inflation-adjusted retail sales (how much “stuff” people actually take home from their dollar-denominated purchases) has been in a downward trend for about three years. This observation will be relevant for the stock analysis section later in this report.

**Fedi Launch**

One of [Ego Death Capital](https://egodeath.capital/)‘s portfolio companies, Fedi, recently launched their app out of beta development into main operation. We’ve been working with them for over two years, and the launch is very exciting.

[![Fedi](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/power-of-ecash-fedi.png)](https://www.fedi.xyz/)

It’s a wallet with in-built chat features and a lot of customization. But more broadly, it allows any community to set up a federated community bank or “mint” in their area, to provide all sorts of monetary services, compute, AI, and chat features.

In other words, a multi-signature custodial wallet can be set up by a number of founders for a given mint, and those founders will serve as a federation of guardians, kind of like a digital board of directors. As long as the majority of them continue to act responsibly, the funds will be safe. It would require a majority of them to collude in order to steal user funds. There are many such mints, and each one is run by a different group of people.

Fedi itself doesn’t custody any funds. Rather, they provide the technology to allow groups of people to create their own mints.

It’s open-source, and each federation can customize their setup to make their mint how they want. Some are simple, and some are more complex. There are a bunch of federations/communities set up in different countries and continents.

And they can operate at different sizes. For example, I could set up a mint for my extended family and friends to use and keep the invite code private so it’s just for us. In other cases, people can set up a mint focused on a given town.

Some mints have a stable balance feature, where users can hold a bitcoin balance and/or a fiat currency pegged balance, so that they don’t have to deal with the volatility. The users of a given federation have a lot of privacy, even from the people running a given federation, which is one of the unique features. This comes from the wallet’s underlying blind signature technology, which was created back in the 1980s by David Chaum and resurrected in recent years to be used with bitcoin wallets by Fedi’s co-founder and others.

The technology even enables offline payments, assuming you at least partially trust the person you’re receiving a payment from (i.e. a customer that you know in person). This is particularly useful in developing countries that can have inconsistent internet in many areas.

Users that want to try it out can go to [Fedi.xyz](https://www.fedi.xyz/). They have a big high-production introductory video, as well as information about the product and downloads for your phone.

You can find a list of public mints to join [here](https://bitcoinmints.com/?tab=mints&showFedimint=true). Most mints are focused on local communities and thus don’t share the invite code publicly. Among the global ones, I like “Bitcoin Fundamentals.” I’d suggest only keeping pocket money on mints in this early stage, as you try out the features.

**BitVM Bridge**

Late last year, Robin Linus published a whitepaper for BitVM, which is basically a way to perform relatively complex computation on the Bitcoin network.

The Bitcoin network is designed to be very simple on the base layer to keep the bandwidth and attack surface as low as possible, but with clever enough techniques, Bitcoin’s relatively simple scripting language can be used to greater effect. And BitVM has been a breakthrough in that area.

But because Bitcoin’s computation capabilities are so constrained, the type of computation has to be very carefully minimized in order to be useful and economical. “Fraud proofs” are one of the best use-cases here. A program can be designed so that it mostly runs off-chain, but where an on-chain “fraud proof” can be submitted if the program is being misused. In other words, the program’s full computation doesn’t have to be handled on chain, which would be impossible, but rather only the tiny part of the program that checks for errors can be run on occasion if there is a need to.

The reason I bring this up is that Linus and others recently published a paper called [BitVM Bridge](https://bitvm.org/bitvm_bridge.pdf). This paper further elaborates on BitVM by providing more specific guidance toward building trust-minimized bridges between the base layer Bitcoin network and second layers built on top of it, with peg-in and peg-out capability.

Right now, Lightning is arguably the only true second layer solution on bitcoin. It uses a series of smart contract channels to form a web of payments. It can be used in a fully non-custodial manner.

In addition to Lightning, there are side-chains and things like that, which are mostly built around custodial federations. For example, the Liquid Network is a sidechain that allows people to peg in BTC, and receive LBTC tokens in return, which are collateralized by that BTC that the federation now holds in their multi-signature wallet. LBTC has much faster block times (1 or 2 minutes) than BTC, is more private, and is more programmable. However, LBTC is not quite as safe as base layer BTC, since it’s somewhat custodial. It is run by a large global federation, where 15 different companies hold keys to the Liquid Network’s multi-signature wallet. If a majority of them ([11 out of 15 in this case](https://help.blockstream.com/hc/en-us/articles/900002386446-How-does-the-Liquid-Federation-s-multisig-work)) are malicious or compromised, then they can steal the underlying BTC collateral and thus render the LBTC worthless. Assuming the network is operating correctly, LBTC can normally be pegged out (converted back) to BTC at any time.

That’s a reasonably good trust model; the probability that 11 out of 15 reputable companies would conspire to steal the money is low, especially because at that scale they’d also be subject to off-chain legal action. And they are spread out among multiple countries such that no single government could easily force a majority of them to turn over the funds. So it’s not meant to hold your life savings there, but rather to use for moderate amounts of money when you want to take advantage of Liquid’s features.

However, BitVM Bridge can potentially improve a trust model like that even further. Federations can incorporate a “fraud proof” and thus become “optimistic rollups”. This has already been demonstrated to good success on some of Ethereum’s second layers.

An optimistic rollup federation is called “optimistic” because it normally runs through its operations without much hassle, and it’s assumed that the federation is acting correctly. However, if at any point one of the layer’s operators tries to do something that goes against the rules of the federation like submit a fake piece of cryptography to incorrectly withdraw funds, someone can submit an on-chain fraud proof to challenge it. And at that point, as long as *at least one member* of the federation is still honest, they can preserve the correct functioning of the layer and prevent things like theft of the collateral.

In other words, the improvement here is that rather than requiring 11 out of 15 federation members to be malicious or inactive in order to steal the collateral from the federation, it would require all 15. And federations can be made larger than that. In practice from what has occurred on Ethereum, it tends to be the case that the fraud proof never actually has to be used, because the mere fact that it *could* be used prevents any federation operators from trying to steal funds from that layer’s wallet.

There are, however, some limitations to the technology.

Firstly, the on-chain fraud proof requires one or more very large transactions (comparable to thousands of normal transactions), which can be expensive in terms of fees. Therefore, this fraud proof technology would only be applicable for large federation layers that have a ton of collateral to protect, rather than small federations.

Secondly, it’s a far more complicated design than a normal federation, in terms of software. This means that there’s potentially more attack surface for bug exploits. Across the broader crypto ecosystem, there have been multiple bridge hacks, where someone finds a way to bypass the normal peg-out process of a given cryptocurrency layer and drain funds from that layer. So, compared to a normal federation, an optimistic rollup might have higher hack risk, in exchange for having a smaller trust assumption for the actual federation operators.

In that sense, I generally prefer the trust model of there being multitudes of small federations (such as what Fedi enables) that one can use rather than one big optimistic rollup federation, but there is a role for both models and we’ll see over time what takes hold. The smaller ones are potentially more useful for payments, while the bigger ones are potentially more useful for DeFi and tokenized real-world assets. The Lightning network can connect them all together anyway.

Overall, the potential for bringing optimistic rollups to the Bitcoin network is a positive development, and worth monitoring as it progresses. There are multiple companies and open-source developers working on ways to make the fraud proofs as small as possible, and to make the concept as robust as possible.

A well-functioning second layer potentially allows for relatively fast and private payments, smart contracts, and other capabilities. This is already possible on multiple layers including RSK, Liquid, and others, but many people consider optimistic rollups to be improvements to their trust models for particularly large federations.

## Deep Dive: Ulta Beauty

Ulta Beauty (ULTA) is an $18 billion growing retail company that focuses on beauty products. They were founded in 1990 and went public in 2007, and they’ve been a strong performer since that time. I’ve been watching it for years, and I’ve finally become bullish on it after a significant price drop.

With over over 1,350 stores and a strong online presence, they are the largest retailer that specifically focuses on beauty products in the United States, which gives them considerable economies of scale within the industry.

Here’s how the company describes their key aspects in their latest 10-k filing:

> **One-of-a-kind Assortment.** We offer guests a differentiated assortment of approximately 25,000 products from approximately 600 established and emerging beauty brands across a variety of categories and price points. We believe we offer the widest selection of beauty categories, from mass to prestige price points, across cosmetics, fragrance, haircare, skincare, bath and body products, professional hair products, and salon styling tools.
>
> **Store Footprint.** We operate more than 1,350 stores predominantly located in convenient, high-traffic locations. With a bright and open store environment, we make it easy for guests to discover new products and services. Our store design, fixtures, and open layout provide the flexibility to respond to consumer trends and changes in our merchandising strategy. We also offer beauty services in nearly every store, including a full-service hair salon and a BenefitTM Brow Bar. In addition to our free-standing locations, through our partnership with Target Corporation we have more than 500 Ulta Beauty at Target shop-in-shops which provide guests with a highly-curated, prestige beauty assortment in a unique and elevated presentation in 1,000 square feet of dedicated space within certain Target locations.
>
> **Leading Digital Experiences.**Through our website, Ulta.com, and our mobile applications, we offer guests convenient, interactive, and personalized digital experiences. Our digital channels enable always-on shopping and discovery, and our diverse fulfillment options, including buy online pick-up in store, buy online pick-up curbside, ship from store, ship from distribution center, and same-day delivery, provide guests with value and convenience. In addition to e-commerce platforms, we offer guests a variety of unique digital experiences, including virtual try-on and skin analysis tools, which leverage augmented reality capabilities and artificial intelligence tools to provide guests with personalized experiences.

>
> **Great Guest Experiences.**We cultivate human connection with warm and welcoming guest experiences across all of our channels. Our knowledgeable and approachable store associates, our differentiated service offerings, and our efforts to create relevant, compelling digital content are competitive advantages and enable us to build strong engagement with guests.

Ulta estimates that the beauty category is a $112 billion industry, and that even though they’re the largest specialty retailer in the industry, they only account for 9% of the industry’s market share. This gives them a considerable runway ahead for growth:

![Beauty Category](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-beauty-category.png)

*Source: April 2024 Ulta Investor Presentation*

Ulta operates in both the ecommerce category and the specialty beauty category, and they have some partnerships with mass merchandisers to extend their reach into those environments as well.

What makes a specialty beauty retailer useful, is that they can provide advice and styling services. Many locations have in-house beauty specialists that will do someone’s hair or makeup for them, or teach them how to do makeup themselves, or give them expert advice and answer questions about product selection. And many people who buy beauty products want to see the products in person prior to purchase.

So at Ulta’s scale, they can compete approximately with the massive retailers on price, while also maintaining a distinction that keeps them useful even aside from price due to the in-house expertise and services. Ulta may not be nearly as big as Walmart (WMT) or Amazon (AMZN), but in the beauty category specifically they are up there on that level, and so they are among the biggest buyers of the products that they sell, which gives them a lot of pricing power.

Ulta has a loyalty program that is more powerful than most I’ve seen. A whopping *95% of their sales* are to loyalty members, which other than during the pandemic in 2020, continue to increase. They have 43 million members currently, and they represent a strong base of repeat buyers.

![Ulta Members](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-members.png)*Source: April 2024 Ulta Investor Presentation*

Back in 2019, Ulta was considering an expansion into Canada, but dropped those plans during 2020 amid the pandemic. As things have normalized, they’ve decided to [expand into Mexico](https://www.retaildive.com/news/ulta-beauty-expands-mexico/710428/) in 2025. They have strong performance in their border stores, and expect that their brand will perform well within the Mexican beauty market. Canada is still on the table in the long run as an expansion opportunity, as are other countries.

I’m optimistic on Ulta’s ability to expand to both Mexico and Canada over time. Mexico has 127 million people, and Canada has another 39 million people, which together represent a nearly 50% potential expansion of Ulta’s addressable market in terms of population, and about 15% in terms of nominal GDP (or upwards of 20-25% if we get a weaker dollar cycle).

![Ulta FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 24.56x price/earnings in this case due to its growth)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Investors understandably don’t like the earnings growth slowdown over the past year, and so they’ve sold the stock to cheap levels. I view this as a transition from a growth stock to a value stock.

If Ulta were experiencing this in a vacuum, I’d be concerned about the attractiveness of the business. However, as previously described in the macro section, the whole retail sector has been under pressure lately. A slowing consumer, and inflationary pressures still keeping their costs up, have been a difficult environment to navigate recently. And this is in the context of having had a *massive* surge of growth in 2021 and 2022. Here’s the retail sales chart again:

![Retail Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-retail-sales.png)

So as a base case, I don’t have fundamental concerns about Ulta’s business. At under 15x earnings, I view this growth slowdown as a buying opportunity for value investors.

Revenue growth, while slowing, has still been solid:

![Ulta Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-revenue.png)

The company is trading at EV/EBITDA valuation levels that it has only reached during the 2008 financial crisis and the 2020 pandemic crisis:

![Ulta EV EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-ev-ebitda.png)

Ulta has consistently maintained returns on invested capital of over 20%, which is very strong:

![Ulta ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-roic.png)

And since their returns on invested capital are so high, they only need to reinvest a portion of their profits back into growth opportunities. Therefore they plow a lot of their free cash flow into reducing their share count, which helps improve all of their per-share metrics and gives shareholders a larger ownership stake in the company over time:

![Ulta Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-shares.png)

Lastly, the company is **completely free of long-term debt**.

They lease their stores rather than own the real estate (that’s part of their asset light model), and so they do have about $1.9 billion in lease liabilities. They also have some other liabilities (accounts payable and such), but overall they’re operating with a rather unlevered balance sheet:

![Ulta Assets and Liabilities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-ulta-assets-liabilities.png)

In summary, Ulta is a well-run company with good forward prospects, a cheap valuation, and a strong balance sheet, which happens to be operating in a difficult macro environment. I consider it a long-term buying opportunity at these levels, and will continue to monitor the company over time.

**Risk Analysis**

Although I think Ulta is cheap here, and with a good long-term risk/reward ratio, there are some key risks to be aware of.

If the United States goes through an outright recession within the next year, I wouldn’t be surprised to see the stock dip to lower lows. It’s already basically priced for a recession, but could be worse.

Domestically, Ulta faces ongoing competition from mass retailers and from their biggest specialty rival, Sephora. Sephora is owned by the enormous French LVMH luxury conglomerate, and represents a considerable rival that can compete with Ulta for both market share and for partnerships with mass retailers.

The company’s expansion into Mexico might not be as successful as they expect it to be. It’s a different jurisdiction and culture, and so there will be some degree of uncertainty over the next few years until we get more data on how that’s going once they begin in 2025. However, they are partnering with the Mexican distribution company Grupo Axo to keep their expansion capital light and able to move fast. Grupo Axo can also potentially help them expand into Chile, Uruguay, and Peru over time if the expansion into Mexico works out.

Ulta’s stronger-than-average balance sheet also helps to mitigate all of these risks. Paying a 15x earnings multiple for an all-equity business and a strong history of growth, means that quite a bit would have to go wrong in order for the investment to lose money in the long run.

## Final Thoughts: Gold at New Highs

Gold settled at a new all-time closing high of well over $2,500 USD:

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-gold.png)

I continue to have a long-term bullish view on gold, which I initiated back in 2018 at about half the current price. With the Fed likely to have more dovish monetary policy next year while the country continues to run very large fiscal deficits, I think the structural case for gold remains strong.

There’s initial evidence that U.S. stocks might be rolling over relative to gold for only the fourth time in the past century:

![SPX to Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-18-spx-gold-attribution.png)

Over a long enough timeframe, stocks greatly outperform gold more than the chart above implies, because stocks pay dividends along the way as they zig-zag up and down relative to gold in terms of price. However, there were certain decades like the 1930s, 1970s, and 2000s where gold greatly outperformed stocks. I wouldn’t be surprised if the decade ahead is one of them.

Either way, I consider it useful to replace a small slice of bonds in a portfolio with gold, as doing so historically improves the Sharpe ratio of a portfolio. Given how indebted most major countries are today, they are likely to encounter more waves of currency debasement in the years ahead, and central banks have been gradually rotating toward becoming gold buyers at scale in lieu of Treasury securities, around the margins.
