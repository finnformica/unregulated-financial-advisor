## Macro View

With a withdrawal of fiscal stimulus and a tightening of monetary policy, the demand side of the economy has been rather weak. This is increasingly being reflected in the performance of risk assets.

![S&P 500 Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-spx-chart.png)

Meanwhile, there are a lot of imbalances in the global financial system, mainly revolving around inflation and currency.

**Currency Problems**

The Bank of Japan is doing firm yield curve control to peg their 10-year government bonds at 0.25% or lower. The mechanism for this is that they have an unlimited bid to create new bank reserves and buy bonds at the price that they set. They can override all market participants thanks to the monopoly ability to create new base money.

However, if the BOJ holds bond yields below a market-clearing rate, it means that market participants will sell the assets and this will be expressed through currency markets. And so, the yen has sharply devalued vs the dollar:

![JPYUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-jpyusd.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

There is no ability for the bond market to “break the Bank of Japan” or anything like that directly; the BOJ has complete control over bond yields denominated in their own currency due to unlimited balance sheet capacity. However, if the yen weakens enough, it might convince BOJ policymakers to change their approach and adjust the yield cap.

The BOJ could have chosen a higher yield cap, like 0.50% or 0.75% or 1.00%, which would have made it less costly on the currency to defend. With their debt as high as it is, they have no choice other than to keep yields low, but I would argue that they chose too low of a yield cap. So, this is basically an unforced error, unless significant currency devaluation was indeed their intention.

Meanwhile, the euro is suffering due to a mostly self-imposed energy crisis. Over the past decade they shut down nuclear plants and decided not to diversify their natural gas import mix very much. Natural gas prices in Europe spiked to being 15x higher than in the US during late 2021, prior to any war. Then, Russia’s war on Ukraine in 2022 along with the associated breakdown in geopolitical relations led to further uncertainty regarding Europe’s ability to get natural gas at anything resembling reasonable prices.

![European Natural Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-europe-natural-gas.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Russia has been demanding over the past month that European companies pay for gas in rubles, by opening Russian bank accounts. The European companies can still fund the accounts with euros, but the point is that the funds are held in a Russian bank and converted to rubles, rather than Russia having bank accounts in Europe that can be frozen by European policymakers.

At first, many European nations refused, but with Russia now cutting off gas supplies to some countries, Bloomberg and other outlets have been reporting in recent days that a number of large European energy providers are indeed opening up Russian bank accounts out of necessity.

To fix this energy situation in the long run, Europe has to build a lot of LNG capacity (which takes years), as well as pipelines from North Africa and such. And Europe would have significant problems if the European Central Bank were to stop buying government bonds for a long period of time; heavily indebted southern European countries like Italy are reliant on the ongoing monetization of their government debt. This puts the ECB in a tough spot when it comes to dealing with inflation.

Additionally, Chinese policymakers performed a yuan devaluation in mid-April, after a period of significant yuan strength. Ongoing lockdowns continue to stagnate their economy, but their policymakers have spoken a number of times about stimulus they will do this year. One move was that they performed a mild but sharp devaluation on their exchange peg:

![CNYUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-cnyusd.png)

This currency devaluation (particularly what’s going on in Europe and Japan) is cumulatively resulting in a stronger dollar than I anticipated. I was correct about inflation and energy shortages materializing, but the degree to which foreign sector policymakers impacted their energy security and currency in some of these countries is hurting their exchange rates more than I was expecting as my base case. I thought we’d get a lower high in DXY rather than a new cycle high:

![DXY Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-dxy-chart.png)

What’s happening with the dollar, however, is not good for the US either. It’s indicative of the global financial system not working smoothly. With so much dollar strength, the foreign official sector is not buying US Treasuries; they hold fewer Treasuries than this time last year despite the fact that the US is running record monthly trade deficits and thus is sending them a ton of investable dollars:

![US Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-us-trade-balance.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This widening of the US trade deficit was a significant contributor to the recent negative real GDP growth print that recently came out for Q1 2022.

Plus, when S&P companies report their quarterly numbers, the overseas revenue will translate into fewer dollars due to the relative strength of the dollar. Companies will have a large number of “unfavorable exchange rate” comparisons. If the dollar gets too strong, it can also put pressure on emerging markets (due to them having so much dollar-denominated debt, mostly owed to lenders in Europe and China and Japan and elsewhere), which can soften global growth and consequently can soften US export volumes.

Due to this dynamic, strong dollar periods (blue/red line below) are highly correlated with stagnating US corporate profits (green line below):

![Dollar Profits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-dollar-profits.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As a few analysts, especially Luke Gromen, have pointed out that US stock prices are tightly correlated with US Federal tax receipts, and lead them. Any lull in stock price growth results in a reverse wealth effect, with less consumer spending, less GDP activity, and less realized capital gains taxes. Here’s the year-over-year percentage change in the Wilshire 5000, along with the year-over-year percentage change in tax receipts:

![Wilshire vs Tax Receipts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-wilshire-tax-receipts.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Lower tax receipts results in larger deficits, which requires more Treasury debt issuance, which is a problem in a strong dollar environment because the foreign sector doesn’t usually buy a lot of Treasuries in strong dollar environments.

The Fed can of course step in to buy Treasury securities if necessary, and I think they’ll be forced to at some point, but to do so during a high inflation environment would represent a severe degradation in central bank independence, similar to the 1940s. Alternatively, the Fed could use other levers like changes in the supplementary leverage ratio to get banks to buy more Treasuries as well.

**Bond Problems**

This has been the absolute worst start to a year for Treasury bonds in decades. The 10-year yield started the year a bit over 1.5% and by mid-April reached nearly 3%, which means bond prices went down considerably. It’s worse for the ultra long duration ones:

![Bond Drawdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-bond-drawdowns.png)

The performance is even worse in inflation-adjusted terms.

Usually bonds serve as a ballast to stocks, but that doesn’t work out in a stagflationary environment. Something like a stock/commodity/gold/cash portfolio historically works better during stagflation.

This year-to-date period has been one of the worst 4 months on record for a 60/40 stock/bond portfolio. We have to go back to 2008 to find a similar period. The fact that bonds are selling off with stocks will further impact tax receipts over time.

I have maintained zero long-duration bonds in my portfolios due to this risk. I’ve been a bond bear mainly due to inflation, let alone what can happen with prices if yields do rise in response to that inflation. However, the 10-year yield piercing above 2.5% has been higher than my base case, even as an inflation bull and a bond bear.

Due to how financialized the United States is, a drawdown in the stock market generally leads to less consumer spending (due to a reverse wealth effect), and consequently lower GDP growth and lower tax revenue. If bonds aren’t serving as a ballast for that, then the drawdown can be more impactful. The same is true for many other countries, but it is more often about their housing market rather than their stock market.

As someone who has completely avoided long duration Treasury bonds for years, I’m actually somewhat sympathetic to them at this time on the long side. The degree to which they have crashed is unusual, and with decelerating economic growth, there is a decent case to go long for a decline in yields (and thus a bounce in prices) here.

However, if we do see a reversal on bond yields, I think being long gold is another way to play it, which is in line with my existing positioning. Before I’d consider buying long-duration bonds, I’d want to see more of a reversal in the price trend.

**US Business Cycle Analysis**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Rather than describing the economy in narratives or qualitative terms, or cherry pick data to fit a particularly bullish or bearish view, it quantifies a broad section of data.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 and Q4 had decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation. The first half of 2022 appears to be continuing the trend of decelerating growth and with still-hot inflation.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin continues to have an unclear price trend. The supply-side continues to be very tight due to highly-convicted long-term holders, but the demand-side continues to be weak, which is typical in economically-decelerating risk-off macro environments.

As a result, more and more coins are shifting from liquid fast-money hands to illiquid long-term holder hands. This potentially sets up the next supply shock and upward price action, but the timing for that with demand being so weak remains unclear. **Conflicted Soft Fork**

There is a significant disagreement in the Bitcoin ecosystem about a potential soft fork. Unlike hard forks, soft forks are backwards compatible and optional to use, but they still do involve changes to a subset of the protocol.

This soft fork would add covenants to bitcoin, meaning that certain addresses could be restricted based on programmed rules between parties. This can expand the smart contract capability and scaling capacity on bitcoin with some novel use-cases, but can also reduce fungibility of coins. More importantly, the ideal way to activate a soft fork and the speed with which this one was proposed to be activated, are controversial aspects being discussed in the community, in addition to the technical details of the soft fork itself. Many people who have been in the bitcoin ecosystem for 5-10 years are on opposite sides of this debate from each other.

Overall, it’s a significant risk worth monitoring, but a soft fork that lacks mass user consensus is likely to end up taking more time to implement, if it’s implemented at all.

**Client Upgrade**

Bitcoin Core was recently upgraded to version 23.0. In addition to upgrading via optional and backwards-compatible soft forks, the Bitcoin network also updates via optional and backwards-compatible routine node client software updates, such as this one. Many node operators don’t update immediately; they watch for a while to ensure the new version is free of bugs before upgrading. As a result, the node network at any given time has a bunch of different (but compatible) versions running at the same time.

NYDIG’s April 29th weekly research report had a good highlight of this upgrade:

> Perhaps one of the more important updates in this release makes Bitcoin more resistant to censorship. Historically, Bitcoin nodes have communicated with each other as default through port 8333. Ports allow internet traffic to be segregated like lanes on a highway, facilitating more efficient communication for specific applications. For example, SMTP (email) is sent through port 25. The issue with sticking to a specific port is that internet service providers (ISPs) can easily censor their traffic. With that in mind, the latest update allows new Bitcoin nodes to more easily utilize non-default ports, thus making it harder for ISPs to censor Bitcoin internet traffic.

NYDIG continues to be one of the most important companies in the bitcoin ecosystem in terms of both size and impact, despite the fact that many retail investors are unfamiliar with it. The company is mostly focused on businesses and institutional clients, although some of its subsidiaries are aimed towards retail.

## Ethereum Note

Ethereum 2.o staking has a rather significant centralization problem occurring, even faster than I would have guessed. There’s a network effect stacked on top of an economy of scale that is drawing a higher and higher percentage of staked ETH into one protocol.

Alex Berge [was one of the first to point this issue out](https://twitter.com/bergealex4/status/1410761639226318852) in detail last year, and he did so before it became a huge measurable problem, from first principles analysis. It reached my attention at the time, but I wasn’t sure how quickly this issue would turn into a practical problem.

Then, Dap [wrote an article for SureSats](https://www.suresats.com/post/ethereum-s-centralization-dilemma-through-lido-staking) about this a couple weeks ago, updating the issue and showing how quickly it’s indeed centralizing due to compounded network effects as Alex had anticipated. I read the article, checked a few points out, and [wrote a tweet thread about it](https://twitter.com/LynAldenContact/status/1516467948868804610). A couple days later, Fortune Magazine picked up the story and [wrote about the issue](https://fortune.com/2022/04/21/ethereum-merge-threat-lido-finance-proof-of-stake-dominant-defi-platform/) with a link to my thread and a few other threads on the subject.

I’ll try to summarize it in a bit more detail here.

**Liquid Staking Derivatives 101**

To run an Ethereum 2.0 validator, you need 32 ETH. If ETH is $3k per coin for example, that would be $96k to run a validator. Most people don’t have that amount. Only wealthy people can run Ethereum validators.

So, third parties can stake ETH for normal customers in smaller amounts. Exchanges like Coinbase and Kraken, for example, can take a customer’s ETH with their permission and stake it along with other peoples’ ETH. They run many full validators, and their customers basically have an off-chain claim to a partial validator. The problem with this is that large centralized entities end up controlling a lot of the staked ETH in a custodial fashion, which means they have a lot of influence over which transactions are added to the blockchain.

Smart contracts also popped up to do this, with liquid staking derivatives. The biggest one is called Lido. You can deposit your ETH coin into Lido’s smart contract, and receive a stETH coin in return, which represents your claim on that ETH. This is an extremely attractive solution from a profit point of view because it lets users earn double the interest on their ETH.

Theoretically, staked ETH is supposed to have an opportunity cost compared to using it to earn yield in a DeFi protocol. Users give up DeFi yield, in order to stake their ETH and earn a yield from validation. However, with a liquid staking derivative stETH, a user can have their cake and eat it to. They can deposit their ETH into Lido, receive stETH as their claim on it, and earn the staking yield. Then they can take that stETH coin (which is equal in value to one ETH), and deposit it into a DeFi protocol to earn additional yield on that stETH.

Basically, with this liquid staking protocol solution, users get liquidity similarly to unstaked ETH, while actually staking. And liquidity is a major network effect; a stETH token is a lot more desirable than the derivative coin of a much smaller staking provider, which begets more people staking with Lido and using stETH. Technically anyone can create a new liquid staking protocol, but in practice, it concentrates towards one. Lido has about 90% of the liquid staking market share despite the existence of multiple competitors.

![ETH 2 Liquid Staking Balances](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-eth2-liquid-staking-balances.png)

*Source: [Dune Analytics, @eliasimos](https://dune.com/eliasimos/Eth2-Liquid-Staking)*

More concerningly, Lido now has nearly 30% of all staked ETH and climbing.

Additionally, processing transactions on a smart contract blockchain offers a lot more arbitrage opportunities than processing transactions on something simple like Bitcoin. Miners/stakers can run complex software analysis to find arbitrage opportunities in terms of how to order their transactions on smart contract blockchains, and this naturally comes with economies of scale. This is called miner extracted value or “MEV”. The combination of liquid staking providers with MEV represents a powerful set of network effects that are likely to quickly centralize the transaction processing of the system post-Merge, unless something is changed.

Bitcoin miners don’t have this issue anywhere near the same degree. Mining has some economies of scale, but it starts to top out pretty quickly. In other words, it’s more cost effective to have 5% of the Bitcoin mining market than only 0.5% of the market due to various cost synergies and economies of scale, but there’s no significant benefit to a miner as they keep going higher than that, and so the ability to reach higher and higher is constrained.

Liquid staking, however, has an actual self-reinforcing network effect rather than just an economy of scale from its MEV, and this can trend towards controlling the majority of the market unless big players deliberately go against their own economic interests and avoid using it.

The risks of this are pretty apparent. The blockchain becomes more centralized, and a large number of staked ETH ends up in a single smart contract that, if it is exploited/hacked in some way, would result in billions of dollars worth of lost funds.

## Fiserv Analysis

Fiserv (FISV) is a Wisconsin-based Fortune 500 company that provides financial technology to financial institutions. The current market capitalization is over $60 billion.

Their most important business line is that they are a leading point-of-sale capability provider. They allow small, medium, and large businesses to accept online and offline payments in various forms. This is Fiserv’s growth engine.

Whenever a business wants to do transactions with the world, they need to integrate point-of-sale-technology. As a small business operator myself, I use Stripe and PayPal as my two point-of-sale providers for example, but there are many to choose from. Once chosen, they tend to have high switching costs, since it’s a hassle to switch out your entire backend system. In several years of doing business, how many times have I had a problem with my point-of-sale system? Only a few times. How many times have I looked into competitors? Zero. I just pay the fees every year like clockwork as a business expense. It’s one of those things you consider changing maybe once or twice a decade when you refresh your whole software/accounting stack, but so far none of the few issues I’ve had warranted that.

Additionally, Fiserv provides technology for banks and credit unions to run their services for customers. Given the size of their market share, if you’re in the US there’s a good chance that your bank or credit union uses Fiserv in their backend. Here’s how the company describes it:

> We provide account servicing and management technology products and services to our depository institution clients, as well as a range of integrated, value-added banking products and services. Account processing solutions enable a financial institution to operate systems that process customer deposit and loan accounts, an institution’s general ledger, central information files and other financial information. These solutions also include security, report generation and other features that financial institutions need to process transactions for their customers, as well as to facilitate compliance with applicable regulations. Although many of our clients obtain a majority of their processing requirements from us, our software design allows clients to start with one application and, as needed, add applications and features developed by us or by third parties. We support a broad range of client-owned peripheral devices manufactured by a variety of vendors, which reduce a new client’s initial conversion expenses, enhance existing clients’ ability to change technology and broaden our market opportunity.
>
> -Fiserv 2021 Annual Report

This is a product with even higher switching costs. A bank doesn’t switch out their entire backend, with all of the expense, risk, and hassle that comes with that, without cause. This acquisition is what brought Fiserv’s financial institution backend business together with the point-of-sale industry.

Back in 2019, Fiserv acquired First Data, which was one of the largest fintech acquisitions in history. First Data is a massive point-of-sale technology provider, and handles over 40% of US credit and debit transactions.

The company took on significant debt, but this came with a significant revenue and profit increase as well.

The mid-point of guidance for 2022 was also slightly better than consensus analyst expectations. The stock did great on April 27th and 28th, but then tanked with the rest of the market on April 29th, which gave up most of its post-earnings gains.

**Risk Analysis**

Fiserv has strong market share among financial institutions, which comes with high switching costs. However, Fiserv still has to keep up with trends over time, and they face particular competition in the point-of-sale category which is an important area of growth for them via their Clover and Carat business lines. Block (SQ) in particular is a significant competitor, as are many others.

The payments industry in general continues to grow, because digital payments continue to take market share over physical cash payments, and total payment volume grows roughly in line with nominal GDP.

However, cryptocurrencies are also a competing payment system, and these assets go *around* the banking system once they get past the onramp phase. In general, they’re growing faster than the broader payments industry but from a much smaller base. This presents another set of risks to banks and point-of-sale technology providers.

Fortunately, Fiserv is on top of this trend. Last year, Fiserv partnered with NYDIG to allow banks to offer bitcoin-purchasing solutions to customers, as well as some other cryptos. This is something I reported on last year, and [here’s how they describe it](https://www.fiserv.com/en/solutions/open-banking/cryptocurrency-solutions.html):

> Fiserv can equip your organization with the ability to offer cryptocurrency and cryptoasset data aggregation, making it possible for your accountholders to buy, sell, hold and manage bitcoin within existing digital banking capabilities.
>
> The largest banks and fintechs already offer cryptocurrency to wealthy consumers, but banks and credit unions are uniquely positioned to offer cryptocurrency to mainstream consumers through trusted brands.
>
> Fiserv is a global leader in payments technology and can help organizations build an omnichannel experience with new and emerging pathways like cryptocurrency.

Later this week, I’ll be speaking at the Wharton Institutional CEO Crypto Forum, and I noticed that Byron Vielehr (Chief Strategy Officer of Fiserv) is listed as a speaker there as well. His topic is “The Future of Bitcoin, Banking, and Payments” along with NYDIG’s CEO Robby Guttman. This is clearly an area that Fiserv is aware of, to avoid getting disrupted by as a legacy player.

I’d be cautious of any payments company that doesn’t have bitcoin on their radar in at least some small way. Block, Fiserv, and PayPal (PYPL) all do, and have already integrated it into their product/service offerings in various ways, with probably more integration to come. I suspect that Fiserv will eventually integrate the Lightning network into their point-of-sale business when the time is right, but to my knowledge they haven’t said anything about that yet.

Probably the biggest overall risk for Fiserv would be a stagnation in growth. If their growth comes in lighter than expected over the next 3-5 years, particularly from their point-of-sale business lines, their earnings multiple could contract from the high teens to the low teens. As a result, the stock could be rangebound for years. That’s not my base case, but it would be my bear case.

In addition, Fiserv stock will likely to be rather correlated with the S&P 500 over any short time frame. To the extent that broad equities continue to sell off, I would expect Fiserv to maybe hold up a bit better, but to go in the same general direction.

## Final Thoughts: Still Defensive

In my December 12th, 2021 report, I laid out my defensive view for 2022:

> Ever since the global financial crisis, US stocks have been very reliant on persistent stimulus, either monetary or fiscal, in order to keep going up.
>
> This chart is cluttered but quite important in my opinion. It shows the Wilshire 5000 price index (the closest proxy for the broad US stock market) and the Fed’s balance sheet. I’ll walk through it in the following paragraphs:
>
> ![Stimulus Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-stimulus-annotated.png)
>
> *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed, annotated by Lyn Alden*
>
> In 2010 (first green box), the stock market had partially rebounded from its 2009 depths. The Fed had performed emergency quantitative easing (QE1) during the crisis, but had not been actively stimulating recently. The Fed’s balance sheet went sideways, and the stock market chopped along sideways as well. Then in late 2010 (the end of the first green box), the Fed announced QE2 and both the Fed balance sheet and the stock market began going up together.
>
> From late-2011 to 2012 (second green box), the Fed held their balance sheet flat and the stock market chopped along sideways again. Then after that green box, they did QE3 from 2013 to 2014 to expand their balance sheet a lot, which coincided with another bull market period.
>
> From 2015 to 2016 (third green box), the Fed once again held their balance sheet flat and the stock market chopped along sideways again. This was a bigger deal because the dollar strengthened, and several emerging markets including Brazil in particular entered very serious recessions. The price of oil crashed and remained down for years as global growth slowed. The US experienced an industrial recession (which was not quite big enough to cause a full recession), in large part because of how weak the energy sector was, which cascaded into weakness in manufacturing to supply the energy sector.
>
> The year 2017 (purple circle) was an exception. The Fed continued to hold their balance sheet flat and began gradually raising interest rates, but Donald Trump was elected to be president in November 2016 and was sworn in to office in early 2017, with a Republican majority in both houses of Congress. The stock market quickly began pricing in tax cuts including corporate tax cuts in particular, which indeed materialized a year later. This was a form of fiscal stimulus; tax cuts without associated spending cuts, resulting in a bigger fiscal deficit, which was good for the stock market. Corporations were able to repatriate hundreds of billions of dollars from overseas and use the majority of it for share repurchases, which was great for stock prices but didn’t meaningfully impact GDP growth or employment.
>
> From 2018 through much of 2019 (fourth green box), there wasn’t any more fiscal stimulus coming down the path. The Fed continued raising rates but also began performing quantitative tightening, which means shrinking their balance sheet. The stock market once again entered a very choppy sideways period. By 2019 it was clear that the global economy was slowing, the US yield curve inverted, and the Fed slightly reduced interest rates.
>
> From late 2019 onward, the Fed has been performing massive monetary stimulus. This was initially triggered by the repo rate spike in September 2019, but then was kicked into high gear by the COVID-19 global lockdowns to address the illiquid Treasury market. The largest fiscal stimulus and monetary stimulus in modern history occurred together, and this was massively beneficial to the stock market.
>
> With fiscal stimulus winding down, and the Fed now tightening, the base case would be for a volatile market again as we enter another probable “green box” in 2022. Equities have been very reliant on these ongoing stimulus efforts. Plus, equity valuations are much higher now, and 2021 already had record inflows into equity at a scale that exceeded the prior twenty years combined. Everyone piled into equities. It *could* be different this time, but that’s not the assumption I would go with. The caveat is that fiscal stimulus from a Build Back Better bill might serve as another purple circle, but that remains to be seen, and much of that money is not very fast-acting like stimulus checks or corporate tax cuts are.
>
> So, I am looking for a defensive posture around the margins.
>
> -Lyn Alden, December 12, 2021

This has so far played out rather oddly close to as expected, albeit with some assets performing even worse than I thought so far, particularly growth-oriented equities and long-duration bonds. Monetary policy tightened, fiscal stimulus dried up, and risk assets went down. Here’s a chart update:

The Russia/Ukraine war certainly added some fuel to the fire by adding additional inflationary pressures to an already inflationary cocktail, and the spiking dollar provides additional challenges to most risk assets. But a lot of this poor performance in risk assets is from decelerating US and global growth.

Year-to-date, the energy sector has done very well, gold is up a bit, defensive sectors like utilities and healthcare are holding up okay, the S&P 500 is down moderately, the Nasdaq and bitcoin and long-duration Treasuries are down significantly, and Ark-style hypergrowth stocks are crushed:

**Sharp Reversal Risk**

A lot of things in the market look very stretched, in various directions. The speed with which bond yields went up, mortgage rates went up, the dollar index went up, the US trade deficit worsened, hypergrowth stocks went down, and so forth is worth watching.

Some of these things could have a sharp reversal bounce, even if the trends do continue after that. Something in markets could “break” in terms of liquidity in the months ahead due to such unusual action, resulting in some sort of policy reversal.

Diversification is the best defense here, in my view. Having some risk exposure captures upside in case of a sharp reversal. I like cyclical value stocks, bitcoin, and a few growth stocks for that. A cash position provides defense against further volatility, as well as fuel for rebalancing into any price capitulations that may occur. A base of moderately defensive assets, such as dividend-paying value stocks and gold, helps provide a portfolio with a ballast.

On one hand, sentiment appears to be rather overdone to the downside. The chorus of people mocking the bond bulls, the Ark bulls, and calling for very doomy bear action in general across a wide range of assets is reaching a fever pitch.

On the other hand, macro indicators do still continue to suggest defensiveness going forward. Nothing has really improved on that front. The rate of US and global economic growth is decelerating, while the Fed and many other central banks continue to tighten monetary policy into that weakness, in an effort to subdue inflation by reducing the demand side.

Perhaps the pain trade is sideways stagnation, with neither a major asset price capitulation as bears expect, nor a strong recovery as bulls expect. Asset prices could bounce back up, roll back over, go sideways, and shake a lot of traders out. But I don’t really try to predict short-term price movements.

And I think it comes down to managing timeframes. Despite still being defensive, I can acknowledge oversold conditions and thus not be surprised by sharp bounces. Despite being value-tilted, I can acknowledge oversold growth factors and thus not be surprised by counter-rallies there. Despite expecting inflation and a commodity bull run throughout the better part of the 2020s decade, I can acknowledge potential disinflationary cycles and pullbacks within that overall trend.

My focus for now is to continue emphasizing high-quality assets at reasonable valuations, while monitoring the growth acceleration/deceleration cycle. Healthcare stocks, energy pipelines, cash-equivalents, and gold continue to look decent for both the intermediate term and the long term. I like bitcoin with a multi-year view but am uncertain over a 3-6 month timeframe due to its historical performance in risk-off conditions. I mostly avoid the fast-growth unprofitable stocks, but there are a few that I have as small positions in my portfolio and I am bullish on them as a diversifier for the long term as well.
