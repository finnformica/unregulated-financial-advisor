## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity indicators in the United States continue to be a relative tailwind for risk assets, although sentiment is beginning to get rather stretched:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-domestic-liquidity.png)

There are various measures of financial conditions, and most of them look good at this time. The Chicago Fed National Financial Conditions Index, for example, continues to be loose. For this index, positive means bad/tight financial conditions, and negative means good/loose financial conditions.

For the charts of financial conditions below, I have a long-term chart over the past five decades, and a 10-year chart that zooms in on the green box of the long-term one:

![Financial Conditions](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-financial-conditions.png)

The above charts show how tight conditions became in the 1970s and early 1980s as policymakers sought to control inflation. In the modern era with so much public debt and other forms of leverage, it’s a lot harder to tighten that much without it becoming counterproductive. In 2022, the Fed aggressively tightened monetary policy, and financial conditions tightened relative to their extremely loose 2021 levels, but still remained fine. And since early 2023, financial conditions have been loosening again, despite the Fed trying to remain hawkish.

While any particular measure of liquidity or financial conditions has limitations, they are still helpful. The following chart shows financial conditions relative to year-over-year S&P 500 performance. As we can see, they tend to be quite inversely correlated. Worsening financial conditions (rising blue line) correlate with weak equity returns (falling red line), and improving financial conditions (falling blue line) correlate with strong equity returns (rising red line):

![Financial Conditions vs SPX](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-spx-vs-financial-conditions.png)

If we look at bank liquidity (measured below as cash as a % of total assets), it bottomed during the March 2023 banking crisis and has been fine since then. At that time, the Treasury and Fed blinked and quickly responded to put out those various bank fires before they spread more broadly to the rest of the financial system:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-bank-cash-annotated.png)

The dollar index has been gradually weakening lately. It’s still relatively strong on an absolute basis, but since its autumn 2022 peak it has been making lower highs and lower lows. All else being equal, a weakening dollar index is good for global liquidity because trillions of dollars in foreign debt are denominated in dollars, and a weakening of dollars helps alleviate their debt burden relative to their cash flows that are often denominated in other currencies:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-dxy.png)

Overall, liquidity conditions look constructive, but prices for many assets have probably front-run that liquidity to a certain degree, and so I have very mixed views on the next several months across a range of asset classes. I prefer sectors that directly benefit from a re-acceleration theme, such as the energy sector.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin temporarily but firmly broke out to new highs, and spent four days above $70k with a peak over $73k.

It has since gone through a sharp correction to briefly below $65k, liquidating some of the leverage that was building up on it. Bitcoin historically has very sharp corrections during its bull markets, which resets sentiment and shakes out overleveraged entities. I warned about this type of sharp corrective price action in my prior premium report.

There are plenty of signs of locally overbought sentiment, and I have no opinion of what the price will do for the next several months.

However, I still view this price cycle as being relatively early, and expect more gains over the next couple years. Global liquidity continues to be generally supportive, although not in full break-out mode yet:

![Bitcoin vs Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-bitcoin-vs-global-liquidity.png)

The realized price (average on-chain cost basis) is just starting to inch up. The market price is elevated relative to realized price which is normal in bull markets, but the realized price still has quite a bit to go up in this cycle, in my view:

![MVRV Z-Score](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-mvrv-zscore.png)

Raging bull markets bring in new demand for bitcoin, but also mobilize some of the long-held supply to come back to market and meet that demand. When price surges 3x or 5x or 10x or more, it increasingly unlocks long-held coins to be sold. The recent bullish price action has unlocked only a tiny percentage of long-held coins, which indicates that the cycle is early and the recent price action was insufficient to trigger almost any selling:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-hodl-wave.png)

I generally view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into bitcoin as being ideal for many people that want a position. Or starting with a lump sum to establish a nontrivial position size, and then dollar-cost averaging into it after that.

**MicroStrategy’s Price Surge**

MicroStrategy (MSTR) initiated a bitcoin holding strategy on August 11, 2020, and I added it to the Newsletter portfolio within a week (August 18, 2020). I later added it to the No Limits portfolio and the Top 12 Stocks List as well, and mentioned it on social media.

The stock is up over 1,100% since August 18, 2020. Nvidia (NVDA) had more than 10x better performance than the S&P 500 or Nasdaq 100, and MSTR has nearly doubled NVDA’s performance:

![MSTR Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-mstr-performance.png)

I discussed bitcoin and MicroStrategy on Fox Business last week [here](https://www.foxbusiness.com/video/6348685119112).

![Fox Business Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-fox-business-tweets.jpg)

As of Friday’s close, it is up $1,782/share. But now it’s time to re-assess the stock’s sentiment and valuation.

MSTR holds 205,000 bitcoin, which at current bitcoin prices is worth about $14 billion in total. The underlying software business was valued at under $1 billion in early 2020, which we can round up to probably $1 billion or more now. The market capitalization after this price surge is about $30 billion, and the enterprise value is well over $32 billion. The company is now trading at a big premium to the bitcoin it holds and relative to the value of the software business.

The company’s management has been excellently allocating capital based on their changing stock valuation over time, and has grown their company tremendously because of it. They can issue normal debt, convertible debt, or equity as market conditions indicate, and have been doing so. Whenever the stock trades well above its net asset value, it basically represents excess demand for bitcoin, so the company issues new capital to absorb that demand with the right type of security, and buys more bitcoin with it. This increases the per-share amount of bitcoin. When market conditions are not favorable (i.e. the stock is not trading at a premium to NAV), they hold back with new capital issuance until conditions become more favorable.

But investors have to be careful at these levels. Buyers of the stock are paying a big premium relative to bitcoin. I do think the company is justifiably worth more than its pure net asset value, but 2x net asset value is quite rich.

Closed-end funds have a less flexible capital structure and often trade at a 10-20% discount to net asset value. Some of the best-managed and unique ones can trade at a 10-20% premium to net asset value. MSTR is in the best-managed category and has a more flexible capital structure than closed-end funds, so a 20% premium would be justifiable, in my view. I’ve happily held it during periods of 20%+ premiums over the past three and a half years. But a 100% premium is a lot.

Probably a factor that is helping MSTR to surge to such high levels is that nearly 20% of the shares are currently sold short by bearish traders or hedgers. Conditions for a short squeeze have likely been occurring, in other words. And during a short squeeze, major valuation dislocations can temporarily occur.

So I am of two minds about MSTR. I’m not bearish on it, because I’m long-term bullish on their underlying asset (bitcoin) and management’s ability to allocate capital to garner a premium on top of that asset. But at current levels, I prefer bitcoin to MSTR, due to the latter’s unusually large premium to net asset value.

I advise caution at these levels, given the atypically large premium to net asset value.

## Oil Services Deep Dive Analysis

I’ve been strongly bullish on energy producing stocks as growth and income investments since [mid-2021](https://www.lynalden.com/oil-and-gas/), and my preferred way to play the sector has been to own energy producers.

This is because in the post-2020 environment, energy producers have been cheap, highly profitable even at rangebound oil prices, conservative with their capital allocation, and are paying much of their free cash flow out in the form of dividends, share buybacks, and net debt reduction. So far, the oil producer thesis continues to pay well from that mid-2021 period:

![IXC vs Oil](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-ixc-vs-oil.png)

Going forward, I continue to prefer oil/gas producers as my primary ways to express a bullish energy thesis.

Premier U.S. large-cap integrated producers can be purchased for under 12x earnings. European and Canadian large-cap producers can often be purchased for under 10x or even under 8x earnings. Smaller domestic and foreign producers have more risk but in particular can be analyzed for their likelihood of being acquired at a premium.

I intend to buy and hold energy producers for the foreseeable investment time horizon, at least until there is a major energy capex cycle and the sector becomes crowded, which I expect to take many years.

The overall thesis on energy is based on 1) supply growth will be tighter going forward than it was in the 2010s decade, 2) demand will remain structurally resilient, and 3) the sector is uncrowded and inexpensive even as it showcases improving capital allocation policies relative to their prior decade.

One way to protect that thesis is to own some companies that benefit from greater oil production. If more oil supply comes online than anticipated, it could suppress oil prices and oil producers’ profits to some degree, but would also likely benefit oil drilling service companies. So, owning a smaller share of oil drilling service stocks alongside a larger share of oil producer stocks may not be a bad idea.

**Valaris**

Valaris (VAL) is a stock that I added a tiny position to back in October 2023, and it has been rather flat in that short time so far.

Valaris owns a fleet of drilling ships and drilling jackups, and has the largest fleet of them in the world. The predecessor company declared bankruptcy in 2020 amid the biggest-ever abrupt global contraction in oil demand driven by lockdowns, and the company emerged from bankruptcy in spring 2021 with new management, new owners, and a strong balance sheet.

I view the year 2020 as having been the end of the 2010s bear market in oil. Many companies got washed out in 2015/2016, and then another wave of them got washed out in 2020. The sector has emerged a lot stronger on the other side of that. Energy assets have rotated to new holders, and in many cases equity holders have been wiped out and debt investors have become the new equity holders.

The business model of Valaris is that they provide offshore drilling rigs and crews to operate them, and prices regularly adjust based on global supply and demand for these rigs. The major customers of Valaris are Exxon Mobil, Chevron, BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, Eni, Equinor, Occidental, Petrobras, and Saudi Aramco, with BP being the biggest customer representing 11% of Valaris’ revenues.

The company has a clean balance sheet but a lot of operational leverage, meaning that they have a big fixed cost of rigs and overhead, and so relatively modest changes in day rate prices and fleet utilization can sharply increase or reduce their net income.

![VAL Operational Leverage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-val-operational-leverage.png)

*Source: VAL February 2024 Investor Presentation*

Day rates for drillships and jackups have been strongly upward over the past few years. This chart is for drillships:

![Day Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-day-rates.png)

*Source: Valaris February 2024 Investor Presentation*

The thesis behind Valaris is straightforward. They own the leading fleet of drilling rigs of various types, and they are held on the balance sheet at values that are well below replacement cost. The major shipbuilders don’t have capacity to build significant numbers of new ones anytime soon, and it takes years for that contracting and construction cycle to occur. Offshore drilling has been lackluster over the past decade amid the rise of shale oil and a bear market in oil prices, but with shale oil maturing, offshore is likely going to be a big source of new production over the next decade, and a sizable capex cycle will be necessary. The supply/demand situation for drilling rigs is likely to keep improving, and along with that the day rates and profitability of companies like Valaris should benefit. Valaris intends to return a large portion of free cashflow to shareholders.

Normally I don’t like investing in zero-moat companies such as ship owners. But the cyclical nature of this industry and the capacity tightness for shipbuilders makes it a unique situation for a small position. As long as offshore drilling activity remains decent (the key risk), Valaris has basically an invincible economic moat for a number of years until major capex can occur. Their fleet is a key lever for global oil supply growth to continue, at least for a multi-year window of time. Day rates can rise dramatically in the coming years due to supply inflexibility and high replacement costs.

Or as they concisely describe the situation in their most recent 10-k filing:

> Demand for offshore drilling services continues to improve as evidenced by increasing global utilization and day rates for offshore drilling rigs. In recent years, oil prices have experienced significant volatility as a result of the global COVID-19 pandemic, production disputes among major oil producing countries and various other factors. This volatility meaningfully impacted both the supply of, and demand for, offshore rigs. Since 2021, oil prices have become relatively more stable due to, among other factors, rebounding demand for hydrocarbons, a measured approach to production increases by OPEC+ members, reduction in supply due to Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent sanctions placed on Russia, and a focus on cash flow and returns by major exploration and production companies. In 2023, prices have remained at levels that are supportive of offshore exploration and development activity. The more constructive oil price environment has led to an improvement in contracting and tendering activity for our industry.
>
> Rig attrition in the industry over the last decade, particularly for floaters, has resulted in a smaller global fleet of rigs that is available to meet customer demands. Consequently, our outlook for the offshore drilling business is positive

**Halliburton**

Halliburton (HAL) is the second largest oil service company in the world, and along with the oil majors they have no shortage of controversies in their long history. But much like other companies in the industry, their management and ownership has rotated over time, although not to the complete extent that a bankrupted company like Valaris has gone through.

Halliburton provides onshore and offshore services, and are key provider of services for shale oil production. Their 10-k concisely describes their two divisions:

> *Completion and Production* delivers cementing, stimulation, specialty chemicals, intervention, pressure control, artificial lift, and completion products and services.
>
> *Drilling and Evaluation* provides field and reservoir modeling, drilling, fluids, evaluation and precise wellbore placement solutions that enable customers to model, measure, drill, and optimize their well construction activities.

Advanced technology is increasingly important for oil companies to find and develop unconventional oil sources. I expect that the market leaders in oilfield services will consolidate their market share and continue to be the market leaders, largely because they will have the best access to technology and the economies of scale to use it to its full potential.

Halliburton and Valaris both have significant geographical diversification, because they service oil producers wherever the oil is in the world. Here is Halliburton’s geographical breakdown:

![HAL Revenue Geography](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-hal-revenue-geography.png)

*Source: Halliburton Fiscal Year 2023 10-K*

Halliburton has a strong balance sheet but cyclical cashflows, and so they have a moderate BBB+ credit rating. They are currently quite profitable and have minimal debt maturities until 2030:

![HAL Debt Maturity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-hal-debt-maturity.png)

*Source: Halliburton Fiscal Year 2023 10-K*

The company’s income dried up during the two major phases of the recent bear market, but they have since recovered. During that unprofitable period, net debt increased substantially and equity value was nearly wiped out, but since then the company has been in a strong recovery with good profits and significant debt reduction. Net debt is now equal to only about two years of net income, which is pretty solid as long as positive income continues (as I think it will):

![HAL YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-hal-ychart.png)

My expectation for the company is in line with consensus analyst estimates. Halliburton has been in a long bear market along with the rest of the energy industry, and I believe that 2020 marked the bottom. In line with that, I expect ongoing recovery of industry profitability, and eventually a longer-term breakout.

![HAL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-hal-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/cash-flow ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/cash-flow in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Overall, I think it’s justifiable to have a small allocation to drilling assets like VAL and oilfield service companies like HAL in the current energy supply/demand environment. I would be cautious about position sizing due to the cyclical nature of these industries, but I expect overall risk-adjusted returns to be attractive with a five-year view as a small portfolio slice.

## Final Thoughts: Chinese Stocks

Chinese stocks have put in a higher low relative to 2022 during their recent capitulation in terms of dollar-denominated price, RSI, and MACD:

![MCHI Monthly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-17-mchi-monthly.png)

It’s increasingly likely (but not certain) that Chinese stocks have put in their cycle lows in dollar terms. The Chinese government is starting to directly target their stock price levels and adjust their policies around supporting them.

Chinese stocks have a lot of geopolitical tail risk, especially for American investors. But the reason I highlight them is because their levels are an indicator of shifts in global liquidity. China has contributed to the recent period of weak global growth and commodity demand over the past couple of years. Specifically, their negative wealth effect from their real estate sector (primarily) and stock market (secondarily) have contributed to their weak consumer demand.

To the extent that Chinese assets start holding the line (in significant part due to a shift in government policies from crackdowns to support), that may begin to notably affect global liquidity and reinforce the potential for global re-acceleration of economic growth and price inflation. So, even if one has no interest in owning Chinese assets, paying some degree of attention to them is prudent due to the macroeconomic impact they can have on the global economy and global liquidity.
