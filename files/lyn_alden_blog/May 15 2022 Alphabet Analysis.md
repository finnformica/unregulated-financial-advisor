# Alphabet Deep Dive Analysis

*May 15, 2022*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report covers macro aspects of the US economy, their global implications, and the trade-off between inflation and recession (which has no clear answer in a stagflationary environment).

It then moves into a bitcoin and crypto section to discuss the Terra/Luna breakdown which was foreshadowed over the past 6 weeks in prior reports. I also published a [public article](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/) about this event.

The equity section focuses on Alphabet, the parent company of Google and Youtube that has run into a significant price correction.

## Macro View

This continues to basically be the Fed’s playbook on how to tackle inflation:

![Inflation vs Recession](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2022-5-inflation-vs-recession.jpg)

The Fed is aiming for a “soft landing”, meaning they want to thread the needle to trim demand growth *just enough* so that it’s still softly positive. This can theoretically take the edge off inflation without resulting in a recession.

The problem is, soft landings are historically rare. The fact that inflation is this high, with this much debt in the financial system, makes a soft landing particularly hard to do. And yet they’re still publicly hoping for it. Part of their job is managing public expectations, but they’re starting to sound less confident about it.

Fed Chairman Jerome Powell said in an interview last week that “the question whether we can execute a soft landing or not, it may actually depend on factors that we don’t control”.

Similarly, Treasury Secretary Yellen last week said that the Fed would be need to be “skillful” and “also lucky” to have a soft landing.

Translation: we’re probably not going to get a soft landing.

Until we see structural improvements on the supply side (supply chains and commodities), the three choices are between 1) high inflation or 2) recession or 3) both. In order to experience the fourth possible option of proper disinflationary growth, we would need abundance on the supply side, and that doesn’t seem to be happening any time soon.

Currently, the US Federal Reserve is choosing option two. They’d rather have a US recession than fail to tackle persistent inflation, after having called it transitory in 2020. I think eventually they’ll have to reverse course on that plan, though. When there is this much debt in the system, there are few options other than inflating out of it.

**Latest Inflation Numbers**

April’s inflation numbers came out last week. Consensus economists were expecting 8.1% year-over-year inflation, but the official print was closer to 8.3%. It’s down from the March peak of 8.5%, but above consensus expectations.

![CPI YOY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-cpi-yoy.png)

*Chart Source: [YCharts](https://ycharts.com/)*

The supply-side continues to be very tight. Oil producers aren’t producing more (forward futures pricing is basically telling them not to), and there’s an increasingly acute diesel shortage in the northeast USA. China’s lockdowns continue to contribute to broad supply chain problems.

On the other hand, with fiscal stimulus gone, the US consumer demand side is weakening. This is increasingly becoming a stagflationary environment rather than an inflationary environment, with reduced economic activity but sticky levels of high inflation.

This potential local top in headline CPI is something I’ve been tracking for a while:

> From base effects in the CPI calculation, there’s a decent chance that we’ll see a local top in official CPI sometime in Q1 2022 within the 7-9% year-over-year range. As we look later into 2022 beyond that point, the future is less clear and depends on certain variables.
>
> -Lyn Alden, December 12, 2021

As of this writing, CPI did hit a local top in the final month of Q1.

I updated this view a few times during the quarter, based on Russia’s war, and began outlining possible scenarios where CPI could go above my 9% local ceiling and for longer. Some of those scenarios are still on the table- the supply of energy in particular remains a significant concern. As of today in mid-May, gasoline prices are higher than last month and this will show up in the May CPI report.

I continue to have an inflationary outlook for the 2020s decade, but it won’t be a straight line, and as we are seeing at the moment, inflation can reach local tops.

In addition, some inflation is simply replaced by shortages. For example, there’s a shortage of medical dyes (used for medical imaging) and a lot of other medical items that most of us don’t think about. This is because they’re made in China, which is currently going through a massive supply chain bottleneck due to long-duration lockdowns.

**Treasury Bonds: Getting Bullish?**

As an inflation bull for a few years now, I haven’t touched long-duration Treasuries throughout this cycle.

However, I dare to say that I’m getting somewhat tactically bullish on them here, with a 6-12 month view. I think they’re oversold, and there is evidence that yields are catching a breather. Maybe that statement will look silly in a couple months and the 10-year will be sitting at 3.5%, but I think the risk/reward is starting to tilt in their favor.

US economic growth is decelerating, and with the way credit markets are behaving, increasing evidence suggests that the Fed will have trouble tightening monetary policy into this macro situation beyond a certain point, despite still-high inflation.

## Crypto Note

The broad cryptocurrency industry has been slammed by the complete collapse of the nearly $60 billion Terra/Luna ecosystem within a matter of days. Approximately $40 billion in LUNA coins and nearly $20 billion in their TerraUSD “UST” algorithmic stablecoin have been obliterated.

![Terra/Luna Market Cap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-luna-marketcap-1.png)

*Chart Source: Coin Market Cap*

Basically, the problems I wrote about regarding Terra/Luna on April 3rd and May 1st played out almost immediately, starting on May 7th, seemingly due to a well-capitalized attacker. It’s painful to see events like that happen to people and it’s an example of why I tend to be critical on most crypto projects. A lot of these types of tokens were marketed to retail investors during the Super Bowl.

Predicting something like this is similar to predicting a forest fire. Someone can point out that conditions are dry and ripe for a fire, but one can never guess exactly when lightning will strike a tree and get it started. It could be days, weeks, months, or even years later.

Terra/Luna was inherently unstable by design, and liquidity was being sucked out of the market in a declining-PMI environment, which are dangerous conditions for an unraveling. A number of issues were materializing over the coming months to lay bare some of the issues.

Along the lines of how I described it might happen, Terra/Luna encountered a death spiral, its peg broke, and they were forced to use their bitcoin reserves in an already-weak market, contributing to an industry-wide capitulation. More than half a trillion dollars in broad crypto market capitalization evaporated within a single week. Luna coins subsequently hyperinflated.

From April 3rd:

> Some of this recent bitcoin breakout was probably due to over $1.3 billion worth of bitcoin purchases by the Luna Foundation Guard, which plans to buy $3 billion in bitcoin for reserves, and to eventually increase that reserve to over $10 billion.
>
> I’ll rewind a bit and explain what the heck that means.
>
> Terra is a proof-of-stake smart contract cryptocurrency based around algorithmic stablecoins, and particularly around TerraUSD or “UST” which is their dollar stablecoin. Unlike USDT or USDC, which are custodial stablecoins (where centralized entities hold fiat dollars as assets and issue redeemable stablecoin tokens representing those assets), UST is an algorithmic stablecoin. This means that it approximates the value of a U.S. dollar but does not hold U.S. dollars.
>
> The way this works is that Terra’s primary token, LUNA, serves as the volatility offset for UST. If UST goes above $1, there is an arbitrage opportunity to burn LUNA and create more UST. If UST goes below $1, there is an arbitrage opportunity to create LUNA and burn UST. UST is supposed to remain around $1, while LUNA is allowed to be volatile. The more demand there is for UST over time,  the higher UST and LUNA market cap should go. It’s like a central bank that tries to incentivize market participants to do both sides of open market operations.
>
> However, if LUNA’s price does not keep pace with UST market cap expansion, then UST can become less and less “backed” by LUNA over time. That’s a general trend we’ve been seeing since late 2021 when UST demand started to take off. At this time, it’s still over 200% backed, but declining fast.
>
> ![UST LUNA Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-3-ust-luna-ratio.png)
>
> Terra is now the second biggest ecosystem in DeFi based on total value locked:
>
> ![DeFi Market Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-3-defi-market-share.png)
>
> *Chart Source: [DeFi Llama](https://defillama.com/)*
>
> The catch? Well, the huge demand for UST is almost entirely driven by unsustainably high yield farming opportunities, much like other DeFi ecosystems. Investors have been able to earn nearly 20% yields on UST in Terra’s Anchor protocol due to various arbitrage opportunities, and that looks like it is starting to dry up. If that VC-supported yield opportunity dries up, UST demand will likely decline. If UST demand shrinks, it could cause a negative feedback loop and liquidity problems for both UST and LUNA, also known as a “death spiral” where tons of capital pulls out of the Terra ecosystem, which crashes the price of LUNA and then eventually breaks the UST peg. If that occurs, it’s functionally very similar to an emerging market currency crisis.
>
> Unlike bitcoin which has no centralized foundation for it, most smart contract blockchains have specific for-profit or nonprofit organizations that serve as their central promotion and development hubs. Terra has Terraform Labs. Solana has the Solana Foundation. Ethereum has the Ethereum Foundation. Avalanche has Ava Labs. These are generally founder/VC-supported entities, with specific leaders and employees trying to promote and develop their ecosystems, and they often use token pre-mines as starter capital.
>
> Terraform Labs and other parties raised capital to set up the Luna Foundation Guard, which will act as a second layer of defense for the UST/USD algorithmic peg rather than that peg relying entirely on LUNA. As [they describe it](https://lfg.org/missionandvision/):
>
> *“In connection with its stewardship and support of the Terra ecosystem in general, LFG is establishing a decentralized UST Reserve protocol – a non-profit initiative to provide a further layer of support to ensure that UST maintains its peg. In an event where the market price of UST materially deviates from the USD peg, holders of UST will be able to close the arbitrage and bring the market price of UST back to the peg by swapping UST for major, non-correlated assets like BTC that capitalize the reserve. The reserve functions as a release valve for swelling pressure to exit UST to LUNA on-chain, dampening the reflexivity of the system by reducing the dilution of the LUNA supply during severe contractions and restoring the peg in real-time and maintaining an alternative arbitrage opportunity outside of the Terra protocol itself.”*
>
> *-LFG*
>
> One way to think of this is that it’s similar to a country’s sovereign reserve. During good times, an emerging market can use accumulated trade surpluses or currency sales to increase its reserves of foreign assets, such as gold or dollars or euros. Then, if the country later experiences a recession and currency crisis, it can defend the value of its currency by selling some of those reserves that it previously stockpiled, and using the proceeds of those sales to buy back some of its currency units from those that are selling it.
>
> On one hand, Terra buying a lot of bitcoin is bullish for bitcoin… at first. LFG could have bought USDC or USDT as reserves. They could have bought ETH as reserves. They could have bought a mix of these assets as reserves. But instead, they bought BTC as reserves, having judged that to be the best one for the purpose of holding perpetual, decentralized, pristine collateral with minimal counterparty risk. This further validates the argument that bitcoin is the best digital reserve asset. And now, the bigger the Terra ecosystem gets, the more demand they expect to have for their BTC reserve. Eventually as they expand onto other platforms, they intend to add small amounts of other tokens as reserves. For example, to the extent that they expand usage of UST into Solana’s ecosystem, they expect to bring some SOL into their reserves. Their stated expectation is to have BTC be the primary reserve asset along with LUNA, and smaller allocations to other tokens.
>
> On the other hand, this presents a future risk for the bitcoin price. If Terra encounters a problem and is forced to sell a lot of bitcoin to defend its UST peg, it will be detrimental to price in a similar way that their current buying is favorable to the price. Terra’s unsustainable Anchor protocol with 20% yields results in a lot of UST demand, and with this new reserve practice by Terra, UST demand now results in BTC demand. This can be thought of as a source of indirect, artificial, or unsustainable BTC demand, which should eventually dry up. Active bitcoin traders should keep an eye on Terra’s LUNA and BTC reserves relative to UST market capitalization because if it starts to break down and they are forced to defend the UST peg, we could see tens of thousands of coins worth of rapid BTC selling pressure. I’m not saying that will happen but it’s a new factor to monitor going forward.
>
> -Lyn Alden, April 3rd 2022

Once it began, it’s amazing how fast it fell. There were people who pointed out these types of vulnerabilities to Terra/Luna’s founder all the way back in 2021. The founder was known for jokes and insults against people critical of the design, right up until the start of the protocol’s complete failure.

From there, the contagion spread. A number of crypto funds had a lot of exposure, and were suddenly over their skis. It caught a lot of VCs, retail investors, and analysts totally off guard.

Mike Novogratz, the billionaire CEO of Galaxy Digital, got a big tattoo of Luna several months ago and described himself as a #LUNAtic at the time, which is the term that Luna bulls use for themselves. Remi Tetot, a co-founder of Real Vision, disclosed that he had 100% of his crypto portfolio in Luna and held it all the way down as it hyperinflated. Zhu Su of Three Arrows Capital was significantly invested in it as well, although he was more diversified. Binance had a significant stake also. A bunch of big pools of VC capital had exposure to it, along with an army of retail speculators.

A lot of other malinvestments began to lose value as Terra/Luna death-spiraled. People sold every type of crypto, expensive illiquid jpegs of Apes began to decline in price, etc. Everyone suddenly had to de-risk, re-assess, and re-value everything they own.

As liquidity was sucked out of the market at a rapid rate, Tether’s dollar peg was tested with a high volume of redemptions. Unlike TerraUSD which is an algorithmic stablecoin, Tether is a fiat-collateralized stablecoin, and it seemingly got through this test:

![Tether Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-usdt-price.png)

*Chart Source: Coin Market Cap*

However, the Tether peg is still worth watching for a bit, especially because in this extreme environment, it’s a measure of how distressed the market is and how much liquidity is being sucked out. So far, over $7 billion in Tether has been redeemed out of $83 billion. This is the biggest redemption in Tether’s history in absolute terms, but so far it’s only the second biggest by percentage terms.

I don’t have the same level of solvency concerns for Tether as I had for Terra/Luna, although Tether’s collateral is too opaque for my taste. I think Tether should eventually convert to a 100% cash and T-bill collateral model, like Circle has done for their USD Coin.

Basically, the cryptocurrency ecosystem was shaken to its foundations last week, and continues to be tested.

It’s too early to say for sure, but I think this may end up being the high water mark for speculative altcoins, referring to the inflation-adjusted market capitalization of most things in the space outside of bitcoin, fiat-collateralized stablecoins, maybe ethereum, and a few other types of projects. It was driven by the mother of all macro liquidity injections (2020/2021), and by the time the next bullish crypto cycle comes around, regulators will probably have more measures and requirements in place that issuers have to meet in order to sell new tokens that pass the Howey Test to the public on large regulated exchanges.

So, blowing a bigger bubble than this one would be quite hard to do in the future. I’d never say never, though.

## Alphabet Stock Analysis

Alphabet (GOOGL) is the parent company of Google, Youtube, and a number of other companies. Here’s the breakdown of their segment revenues:

![GOOGL Segment Breakdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-googl-breakdown.png)

*Source: [Q1 Press Release](https://abc.xyz/investor/)*

The combination of Google Search, Google Network, and Youtube continues to be profits powerhouse. The Cloud segment provides a small amount of diversification, as does their group of “Other Bets” including research into self-driving that so far has not been a profit center for the company.

As primarily a software company, Alphabet has relatively little exposure to China, which gives it some defense against the supply chain breakdowns we’re seeing at the moment from that region.

**Growth and Valuation**

The company had an artificial growth spurt in 2021 due to so much fiscal stimulus working its way through the economy. The broad money supply increased rapidly, and Alphabet with its leading position captured quite a bit of that. The 2022 year is kind of the hangover year, where analysts are expecting flat performance.

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-googl-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Most people wouldn’t guess it, but Alphabet is trading at a lower price/earnings multiple than Procter and Gamble (PG). Alphabet is at around 21x, while Procter and Gamble is at about 26x. It’s also notably cheaper than Costco (COST), and is trading at a similar price/earnings multiple to Walmart (WMT) and many utility companies.

The company’s price/sales ratio is currently near the middle-to-lower end of its historical range:

![Alphabet Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-googl-revenue.png)

The same is true for EV/EBITDA and price/cash-flow. By most metrics, Alphabet is really not that expensive:

![Alphabet Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-googl-valuation.png)

The company has one of the strongest balance sheets in the world, with over $130 billion in cash and relatively little debt. Their balance sheet is rated AA+ by S&P, which gives them one of the strongest ratings in the market.

![Alphabet Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-googl-balance-sheet.png)

The company is firmly out of its period of stockpiling cash and focusing on growth at all costs. Now it’s shoveling a lot of its cash towards share reductions. I would like to see a small dividend as well- for them to go the route of Apple (AAPL) basically.

![GOOGL Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-15-googl-shares.png)

**Risk Assessment**

“Web2” companies like Alphabet make their money by exploiting privacy. Most people are the product, meaning they use Google services for free, but in exchange their data is tracked and given to others in order to show them targeted ads.

This practice is getting increasing scrutiny by policymakers, and there are a number of “Web3” technologies that, at least in principal, aim to let users monetize their own time and their own data. An investor in Alphabet should be on the lockout for these types of regulations or technologies becoming more meaningful relative to Alphabet’s size.

YouTube has robust growth, but does face some competition from TikTok. This should be monitored, but so far TikTok seems to be affecting YouTube less than some other social networks.

Overall, I think the company has a decent amount of structural growth left ahead of it, and could one day even catch up with Apple’s market capitalization. The combination of organic growth, inflationary price increases, and share repurchases should give shares decent long-term (3-5 year) appreciation potential compared to dollars and Treasuries.

However, if growth decelerates more than expected and fails to pick up in 2023 and 2024 as a result of recession, even the current price/earnings multiple of 21x might be a bit rich. The macro forces at play over the next year are significant headwinds.

## Final Thoughts

This continues to be a challenging macro environment. The US economy and large portions of the global economy are slowing, while the Fed and other central banks tighten monetary policy into that slowdown to try to contain inflation. This continues to point towards a “risk off” environment.

On the other hand, a number of assets do appear to be oversold, and any relief from Treasury yields could give equities room for a bounce. Excessive valuations have been removed from many equities, although some areas still look somewhat expensive. Crypto markets already crashed rather significantly, and sentiment across the board is very bearish, which tends to be a decent contrarian signal.

Energy remains a key concern. Oil production remains tight, whether you look at North American shale or OPEC+ producers. In order for a noticeable amount of new supply to be brought online, oil futures looking out 3-5 years probably need to go up quite a bit.

“Normal” rules about monetary tightening point towards much higher interest rates. The Taylor Rule, for example, suggests that the Fed’s short-term interest rate should be in the high single digits. However, it’s very hard for policymakers to bring rates that high with so much public and private debt in the domestic and global financial system. With the dollar as strong as it is right now, it is holding down global growth and for the most part preventing the foreign sector from accumulating Treasuries, which means Treasuries have to be financed domestically.

I continue to view this as a defensive environment overall, although I’m happy to lean into small pockets of risk and rebalance into the oversold conditions.
