## Election Update

The U.S. election could face a lengthy period of contestation, but at this time, it appears that Joe Biden will take the presidency in January with up to 306 electoral college votes. He’s the presumptive president-elect, although it doesn’t become official until mid-December.

On the day after the election, it seemed clear that the Democrats, however, would not take the Senate, resulting in a divided government with a blue President, red Senate, blue House, and red Court. That became a bit less clear later in the week, as Georgia threw a curveball into the Senate race with two runoff Senate races in the months ahead. Democrats still have a narrow path to control the Senate, in other words, and betting markets place their odds of it at 20-25%.

For now, I’m operating with the assumption of a Biden presidency and a Republican-controlled Senate, because that is the most probable outcome at this point. However, I’ll keep monitoring the situation.

As soon as the potential blue wave seemed dead on Tuesday night, a renewed disinflation trade took hold, as markets probably expect Biden and McConnell to spend the majority of the next two years in gridlock. Treasury yields fell sharply, the dollar rallied, and tech stocks soared, in overnight trading. That continued the recent momentum of a disinflationary macro backdrop, with slow growth, and investors piling into the handful of companies that have growth, regardless of valuation. With a red Senate, investors were also likely reducing their probability assessment of major anti-trust action against Big Tech, which is something the blue House has pushed recently.

This trade eased a bit later in the week. Precious metals rallied, the dollar fell, and Treasury yields popped back up. Bitcoin in particular has been soaring, and is now up about 120% since I recommended it in the April 12 report. I initially suggested Bitcoin at $6,900 and went long myself a week later at the same price, and now it’s about $15,000. I still see a solid runway ahead for it in 2021, although we’ll of course have various pullbacks along the way.

The risk to healthcare stocks decreases further still, if Republicans maintain control of the Senate. The United States is an outlier compared to other countries, with by far the most expensive healthcare system in the world per capita, despite not being ranked in the top 30 countries for either high life expectancy or low infant mortality. **Commodities**

## Itochu Corporation (ITOCY) Deep Dive Analysis

Before I dive into the Japanese conglomerate Itochu Corporation (OTC: ITOCY) which will be unfamiliar to many readers, it helps to provide some background context on the American conglomerate, Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B).

Warren Buffett, the billionaire investor that runs Berkshire Hathaway, has mostly been out of the spotlight this year. He’s 90 years old, his investment track record over the past 15-20 years hasn’t been great compared to the broad market, and many investors have questioned whether he “still has it”.

In particular, other than an extremely large position in Apple (AAPL) that has done very well, he has a somewhat poor track record with technology stocks, and that’s the sector that has dominated equity performance over the past decade.

Normally, he builds up cash during bull markets when stocks are expensive, and deploys it into bear markets to buy cheap stocks and to make large acquisitions or profitable bailouts of other firms.

For the past several years, he has said that most companies are too expensive for his taste, and so he hasn’t been doing many acquisitions or buying many stocks, and has just been building up a huge cash hoard. Now that we finally had a massive recession after ten years without one (the longest economic expansion in American history), he still hasn’t deployed much capital, likely because with so much fiscal stimulus and central bank intervention, he hasn’t had many opportunities to bail companies out and/or buy them. He said that at first when the pandemic shutdown started, he began receiving some calls for him to provide capital to troubled companies, but then when the Fed sprang into action and started artificially pushing down corporate bond yields, those calls stopped.

However, Buffett does have a clear “hard asset” theme going on this year, and I don’t think he’s out of tricks yet.

In response to the pandemic in 2020, Buffett completely sold out of his airline stocks, and trimmed or eliminated many of his bank stock positions (but maintained or increased some of his core bank positions, like American Express, US Bancorp, and Bank of America).

He then deployed $10 billion to buy a big set of natural gas pipelines from Dominion Energy as a private wholly-owned transaction, which was his biggest acquisition in years.

Next, he added a $500+ million (small for him) position in Barrick Gold (GOLD). Rather than him personally, this investment might have been from one of his two investment lieutenants, although it made headlines due to Buffett’s historical dislike for gold investing. That was Berkshire’s only new stock add in Q2, 2020, so it stood out as interesting despite its small allocation.

Then, 2-3 months ago, Berkshire revealed that they spent over $6 billion buying a 5% stake in each of Japan’s five largest trading companies, or *Sogo sosha*, which deal with commodities and industrials. Berkshire accumulated the purchases over the past year and announced it in autumn 2020. This was a pretty big transaction, and based on the press release, Buffett was directly involved. The press release said they plan to hold for the long term and might buy more, up to 9.9% of any or each trading company.

Most of Berkshire’s acquisitions and stock holdings are U.S. companies, so this sizable foreign purchase also stood out as interesting.

It’s impossible to say for sure what Buffett was thinking with his Japanese trading company purchases, but a few things are clear. By buying 5% stakes in each of the top five, it was not a company-specific bet. It was more of a macro bet, which would benefit from positive Japanese economic performance, from strong commodity and industrial performance, and/or from a weaker dollar or a long period of global reflation in general. These stocks pay dividends, have wide economic moats, and are quite cheap, which makes them classic Buffett buys.

**I am One with Infinite Missions**

There are seven Japanese trading companies, of which Berkshire bought into the biggest five. They date back to the 1800’s or before, with several of them being linked to the *Zaibatsu* of the pre-WWII Empire of Japan, although Itochu Corporation has different roots.

Japan is a resource-starved island country, so importing raw materials has long been a key part of the economy and national security. These trading companies serve a vital role in that. However, they organize themselves as conglomerates rather than pure-play commodity companies.

Itochu Corporation’s guideline of conduct is “I am One with Infinite Missions” which pretty much sums up how these trading companies operate.

In the United States, this business model would generally be frowned upon as an inefficient and haphazard collection of unrelated businesses that should unlock shareholder value by spinning off non-core assets and picking an industry to specialize in. Like, why should the same company have interests in a chain of Japanese convenience stores, desalination plants in the Middle East, and coal mines in Australia?

However, Japanese business culture is different, more prone to diversification and conglomerates, and these trading companies represent the pinnacle of that. The trading companies have economies of scale and internal data that give them an information advantage, and many of their internally-owned businesses supply each other and work closely together.

A Japanese trading company has operations in commodity acquisition, often with various operations around the world, as well as logistics and supply chain infrastructure, and then generally industrial manufacturing/engineering businesses, and more. Many of them then invest in domestic Japanese real estate, as well as other businesses ranging from retail to healthcare. They tend to be rather high-paying and prestigious places to work, so they attract top professional talent.

Japan has the largest absolute net international investment position “NIIP” of any country in the world, meaning they own more foreign assets than foreigners own of their assets, by a wide margin. If we adjust that for “net international investment position as a percentage of GDP”, Japan is not quite the highest, but it’s still near the top of the list, and is the highest among large countries:

![Net International Investment Position](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-8-net-international-investment-position.png)

The United States is the world’s biggest debtor nation in absolute terms, and one of the biggest in % of GDP terms. Americans own $29 trillion in foreign assets, while foreigners own $42 trillion in U.S. assets, including large portions of our government debt, corporate debt, equity shares, private business interests, and real estate. The chart above is as of the end of 2019 (pre-pandemic) and it keeps getting worse, so the U.S. net international investment position is now lower than -60% of GDP.

Japan, being the world’s biggest creditor nation, is in the opposite position. Although foreigners including Americans own some Japanese assets, Japan owns far more foreign assets. Between their government holdings, pension holdings, corporate holdings, and household holdings, the folks of Japan own stocks, bonds, private business interests, and real estate around the world.

Creditor nations like Japan build up positive NIIPs through years of accumulated trade surpluses; producing more than they consume. Debtor nations like the United States build up negative NIIPs through years of accumulated trade deficits; consuming more than they produce.

Japanese trading companies are a contributor to and manager of Japan’s positive NIIP. They own strategic commodity assets around the world, and interface with the rest of the world through trade, producing/importing materials that Japan needs and completing/exporting finished goods and projects to other countries.

I researched them over the past couple months, and the one that stands out to me is Itochu Corporation. My second favorite is Mitsui & Co.

**Itochu Corporation Overview**

Founded in 1858, Itochu Corporation is one of Japan’s major trading companies. Their roots are in the textile business, but now they have diversified metals and mining, energy production, engineering, real estate, and convenience stores.

They earned over 500 billion yen in income for 2019 and 2020, which is slightly under $5 billion USD, and therefore quite a lot of money. They expect 2021 income to be more like 400 billion yen, due to pandemic impacts, although that’s probably a lowball hurdle for them to step over and beat expectations. Their year ends in March of each year, so 3/4ths of their 2021 fiscal year is actually 2020, during the pandemic, and the same is true for their 2020 fiscal year actually being mostly 2019.

They own or are involved in businesses ranging from Japan’s second largest chain of convenience stores, to uranium interests in Canada, to desalination plants in the Middle East, and geothermal energy plants in Indonesia.

Itochu has outperformed both the S&P 500 and the Japanese stock market over the past 1 year, 3 years, 5 years, and 10 years. Here’s the ten year performance chart, under Okafuji’s leadership:

**Company Risks**

Although I consider Itochu to be a relatively conservative stock, it does have some risks that investors should be aware of.

*Financially Opaque*

Japanese trading conglomerates are not particularly easy to analyze, because they have so many moving parts. Analyzing Itochu is like analyzing eight different businesses, basically. Their financial documents do a fine job of breaking down their various business lines, but there is inherently less information about each business line than there would be if that business was a stand-alone public company.

Therefore, high quality management is very important for investors to consider. Itochu has developed a good track record of managing its businesses well and reporting accurately.

*Commodity Prices*

The results of Itochu’s resource businesses are tied in part to commodity prices. A big part of the company’s expected decrease in profits from 500 billion yen to 400 billion yen this year, is due to their resource businesses.

Itochu is more buffered against commodity price declines than pure commodity producers, but in terms of 5-10 year growth potential, Itochu would likely do better in a rising commodity price environment than a weak commodity price environment.

*Economic Cyclicality*

Itochu benefits from a stronger Japanese economy, and more broadly a strong global economy. Much of what it deals with are necessities, but even some of those necessities can have some degree of cyclical demand. Convenience stores, real estate, commodity usage, industrial logistics, and other things tend to do better when the economy is hustling; not when it’s stagnant or shut down in a pandemic, for example.

*Currency Exposure*

Itochu reports results in yen. Investors in Europe, the United States, or elsewhere therefore have some currency risk when investing in Itochu. The opposite is true as well; if the dollar falls vs the yen, for example, then Itochu’s results would be even better in dollar terms. I consider geographic diversification and currency diversification to be important.

Japan has a positive current account balance and a developed financial system. They have a lot of sovereign debt, but large swaths of it is already owned by the Bank of Japan. The yen is often viewed as a safe haven currency due to its political stability. I expect many currencies around the world to continue to devalue vs hard assets over the next decade, including the yen. However, I don’t view the yen as a particularly risky currency, mainly due to Japan’s favorable trade situation and high degree of productivity.

Lately, the yen has been strengthening vs the dollar:

![JPY USD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-8-jpyusd.png)

*Chart Source: XE*

The yen has strengthened vs the dollar from the 1970’s to the 1990’s, and since then has been range-bound against the dollar, mostly within a range of 75-to-1 to 125-1. At 103, it’s currently right in the middle of that range, and has been in a multi-year upward trend.

## Summary

Overall, this tentative election outcome has only mild impacts on my long-term outlook for various stocks and asset classes.

All else being equal, a divided government outcome moderately reduces my outlook for stimulus size and inflation timing, slightly increases my already-favorable outlook for healthcare stocks, and leaves my already-favorable outlook on top-quality commodity stocks and international stocks unchanged.

Itochu Corporation represents a blend of quality and value, with an emphasis on international equity and commodity exposure, which combines a number of my long-term themes in a nice package for a long-term compounder and dividend grower.
