## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity has been flat lately based on most indicators.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Federal Reserve keeps reducing their balance sheet, which is negative for liquidity, but the Treasury General Account has been flat, and the reverse repo facility has been declining, which is positive for liquidity.

However, the rate of decline for the reverse repo facility has slowed down, which has contributed to the lack of domestic liquidity growth lately:

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-reverse-repos.png)

Global liquidity indicators have been up slightly in the past couple weeks, mainly because the dollar index has slid off of its recent highs:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-dxy.png)

Overall, the liquidity environment is mixed but constructive, in the sense that it’s not really a headwind at the moment but it’s not a huge tailwind either. Fundamentals really matter when selecting investments in this environment because there’s not enough liquidity to push everything up without discretion.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Conference Board’s leading indicators have been turning up from low levels recently, but it remains to be seen how durable this re-acceleration will be. It’s still in a fragile stage.

![US Leading Economic Indicator](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-us-lei.png)

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow estimator model from raw data has declined over the past week, but this data set is noisy and should be watched over the coming weeks:

PMIs consolidated last month but continue to show signs of resurgence. Much like other indicators, they are still in the unclear phase, but in my view have more upside potential than downside potential unless we start to see firm evidence that suggests otherwise:

**China Business Cycle**

China’s economy is larger than the United States’ economy in some ways and smaller in other ways, but overall is similar in size, and it therefore has major impacts on the global economy.

Right now, China’s economy is about as disinflationary as it could be for the global economy. They are running tight fiscal policy and loose monetary policy, which is the opposite of what the United States is running. The credit impulse is middling, and I think policymakers have a pretty solid floor under it but no strong willingness to shoot it back up:

![China Credit Impulse](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-china-credit-impulse.png)

They have been deliberately cracking down on bubbles in their real estate sector, which by extension also stifles their consumer spending as people deal with a negative wealth effect. But at the same time, they are continuing to encourage and stimulate their industrial sector and try to keep a floor on their stock market. This combination means that Chinese imports of raw materials are stagnant, which is disinflationary for the world, and yet their output of manufactured goods is strong, which is also disinflationary for the world.

The opposite scenarios would be inflationary: if China’s domestic economy were to be booming it could drive inflation to the world via greater price pressure from imports of raw materials, or if their industrial sector was disrupted it could drive inflation to the world through supply constraints.

However, this disinflationary combination is subject to change, and goes up against other inflationary components of the global economy. Right now, the world is in reacceleration mode from low levels, but it’s fragile and subject to changes in global liquidity with regard to how much momentum it can maintain. Inflation may be accelerating just as quickly as fundamentals are, which isn’t great:

![Global PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-global-pmi.png)

My thesis for a while has been that inflation is at a high risk of returning during the next economic acceleration, and that remains my base case for now. Inflation has been held down by global demand softening, so if there is any significant degree of global demand increasing again, the tight energy markets and increasingly complex supply chains are still friction points contributing to higher price levels. Early signs of economic reacceleration have already been correlated with early signs of inflation coming back as well.

On another note, China is now exporting more to developing countries than developed ones:

![China Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-china-exports.png)

This is partly from BRICS-type of countries gaining overall market share over time. After all, they are the major population centers of the world, and so as they reach even marginal percentages of development that the developed world enjoys, their total economic size will be massive.

However, it’s also from supply chain rotations. China is exporting more to the countries that export to the United States, and so for an increasing number of product lines there is one degree of separation which helps soften some tariff issues or other geopolitical tensions.

A lot of people talk about the United States decoupling from China as though it’s a simple thing to do, but it would likely require a decade or more and trillions of dollars of long-term investment to replace the infrastructure assets, energy production assets, and human capital located in China, before any meaningful amount of decoupling would be possible.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has briefly broken over $68k and is showing significant strength as it consolidates near all-time highs of $69k. It has already surpassed its all-time highs in most currencies other than the U.S. dollar, and has broken above its dollar highs in monthly terms and in terms of market capitalization.

![Bitcoin Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-bitcoin-price.png)

In the first couple weeks of the spot bitcoin ETF launch, a lot of funds flowed out of the Grayscale ETF (GBTC) and into the newer cheaper spot ETFs (IBIT, FBTC, etc). The funds that were previously in GBTC were kind of “trapped” by a discount to net asset value, and the conversion from a trust to an ETF closed that discount and enabled those funds to get out into better vehicles.

Over the past month, that effect has been slowing down, and incoming fund flows are still solid, meaning that the total number of coins held in the ETFs is rising. I’ve spoken to a number of executives in the spot bitcoin ETF industry and my understanding from them is that they are still in the early phases of marketing their products to their network of clients. Their clients are various wealth managers that then either market the funds to their clients (“solicited”) or at least allow the clients to get into them if they bring the topic up (“unsolicited”).

![Cumulative Bitcoin Spot ETF Flow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-spot-etf-flow.png)

Occasionally there are bankruptcy sales of GBTC that contribute to a couple days of flat net inflows, but overall the trend seems firmly in the inflows’ favor. And once bitcoin firmly breaks out to new highs in dollar terms, it’ll likely be all over the news and mobilize more capital to come in.

My [first public article](https://www.lynalden.com/cryptocurrencies/) on bitcoin and cryptocurrencies was in November 2017, where I highlighted it as interesting but euphorically-priced, and took no position. It went on to underperform the S&P over the next 2+ years, which wasn’t surprising. I then recommended bitcoin to my premium subscribers in [April 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-4-12/) at about $6,900 per coin and bought it there, and then provided a longer [public bullish rationale](https://www.lynalden.com/invest-in-bitcoin/) for it in June 2020 at a little over $9,000/coin.

I researched it quite heavily throughout that spring/summer 2020 period and shifted from a speculative/cyclical bull to a high-conviction structural bull on the asset within that timeframe where it was consolidating around $10k for several months.

I became an advisor to [Swan Bitcoin](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) in 2020 a few months after they went into business, and joined their board of directors as their third board member in 2021. During the bear market in 2022, I joined the bitcoin-focused venture firm [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/) to advise them and serve on their investment committee from their start as a firm, and last summer I agreed to join as a general partner as they prepared to raise their second fund.

From 2021 through the present, I’ve been very fortunate to attend a number of events and discuss bitcoin with some of the key cryptographers, core developers, second layer developers, institutional capital allocators, company founders, and others that have been involved in the space for a while.

I continue to be bullish on bitcoin with a multi-year view, but have no firm opinion about any multi-month period. And while I like the option for ETFs in various brokerage environments and retirement accounts, I prefer holding the [real thing](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) where possible.

**Supply is Not Yet Mobilizing to Meet Demand**

The more euphoric that the bitcoin market gets, the more it risks getting sharply washed out by leveraged traders or bad dollar liquidity conditions from time to time. Prior bitcoin bull markets regularly had sharp 10%+ and even 30%+ drawdowns before quickly bouncing back to higher highs.

I don’t plan to trade it, but rather just want to make that known so that people don’t get over their skis with leverage or oversized allocations relative to volatility tolerance. Meme coins are rallying, which shows broad signs of local euphoria, and so prudence is important. We’re at a stage where although I am not necessarily calling for a pullback, I certainly wouldn’t be surprised by one, and might even welcome one.

That being said, on-chain indicators still suggest that the bull market is early in the more structural sense, even as it may run into air pockets along the way. Existing supply has been very tightly held against rising price, indicating that much higher prices would be necessary to mobilize more supply to meet the incoming demand.

Bitcoin bulls often point out that ETFs are consuming way more bitcoin than are being mined each week, and the amount of mined bitcoin per week is about to get cut in half in April, as is the case every four years. Demand vastly exceeds supply, in other words. That’s bullish, obviously.

However, new bitcoin is not the only source of supply. At the right price, buyers can get bitcoin out of the hands of existing holders. The problem is that the price needed to do that on a big scale is likely quite a bit higher than the current price.

During bear markets, weak-handed buyers sell, most of the fast money leaves the ecosystem, and existing coins rotate more toward stronger-handed long-term accumulators. By the time new demand comes back, either due to improving liquidity conditions or some other catalyst like the introduction of spot ETFs, it only takes a small bit of demand to move the price up a lot, because such a large portion of the coins are tightly-held and thus illiquid.

However, as price soars well into new all-time high territory, it starts unlocking some of that tightly-held supply. When multi-year holders are up 5x or 10x on their position, some significant percentage of them do start to trim their holdings into that incoming demand and price strength. Sometimes it is because they are rebalancing into other investable assets, or because they want to use some of their newfound wealth to upgrade their physical lifestyle, or for other reasons.

This can be measured in multiple ways, but the 1+ year HODL wave is the most basic one. It shows the percentage of coins that have been held on-chain for one year or more. It increases during bear markets when coins rotate to longer-term holders, and it decreases during bull markets when strong price action starts to coax some of those strongly-held coins out into the hands of new buyers.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-hodl-wave.png)

This is why about $8 billion in ETF flows (along with probably some other billions in exchange inflows, although that is more opaque) caused the market capitalization to increase by about $300 billion. That new inflow ran into very tightly-held coins, and thus to find sufficient liquidity prices had to rise sharply.

As the chart shows, the HODL wave has barely decreased yet; it’s only down by about 2%. Recent bullish price action has not coaxed almost any long-term held coins out of their holders’ hands. And that’s normal for this stage in a bull market where the price is close to prior all-time highs but not way higher yet.

Custodial environments make this metric imperfect at times, but so far I don’t think this cycle is much different than the prior cycle in terms of custody, and so I expect that when the bull market starts reaching its later period, this metric will probably have decreased by some more noticeable percentage, like 5-10% or more.

Another way to look at this is via the market capitalization vs the realized capitalization. The market capitalization is the number of coins multiplied by the current price. The realized price is basically the “cost basis” of on-chain coins in aggregate. It records the dollar price at which each coin last moved from one address to another address on chain, which usually (but not always) represents the transfer from one person to another (or an exchange to a person, or a person to an exchange).

This next chart shows the market capitalization vs realized capitalization ratio. The realized capitalization (blue line) is starting to rise, but hasn’t really had any significant run yet, and the overall multiple of market capitalization to realized capitalization still suggests that bitcoin is early in a bull market phase. When market prices are way above aggregate cost basis is usually when stronger hands start to bring some of their coins to sell into all the incoming demand. That hasn’t really materialized yet.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-mvrv.png)

Lastly, the RHODL ratio kind of combines those two metrics. It takes into account age of coins and their cost basis. It’s also a bit more conservative because it has an in-built calibration to assume more hodling behavior over time.

> This indicator uses a ratio of Realized Value HODL Waves.
>
> In summary, Realized Value HODL waves are different age bands of UTXO’s (coins) weighted by the Realized Value of coins within each band.
>
> The Realized Value is the price of UTXO’s (coins) when they were last moved from one wallet to another.
>
> RHODL Ratio looks at the ratio between RHODL band of 1 week versus the RHODL band of 1-2yrs.
>
> It also calibrates for increased hodl’ing over time and for lost coins by multiplying the ratio by the age of the market in number of days.
>
> When the 1-week value is significantly higher than the 1-2yr it is a signal that the market is becoming overheated.
>
> –[Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/rhodl-ratio/)

![RHODL Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-rhodl-ratio.png)

By this metric, the current bull market is more in its mid-stage. It’s still early compared to most bull markets, but the 2020/2021 bull market was a stunted one and it is already at mid-stage compared to that one.

In 2020 when it reached this RHODL ratio, the price was about $23k and eventually went up to $69k (a stunted run).

In 2017 when it reached this RHODL ratio, the price was about $1,200 and eventually went on to hit $20k.

In 2013 when it reached this RHODL ratio, the price was about $90. It consolidated for a bit, and then soared to over $1,100.

Overall, I have no firm opinion about what price level bitcoin will reach in this cycle. However, I do still think the majority of evidence indicates that whatever size this cycle ends up being in terms of price action, that it’s still fairly early in that run. I expect violent corrections from time to time, but until I see signs that older coins are coming to meet incoming demand at scale, I lean significantly bullish.

**Bitcoin Atlantis**

This past weekend, Madeira hosted the “Bitcoin Atlantis” conference. Madeira is an autonomous region of Portugal that consists of a small island chain off the cost of Morocco.

![Madeira](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-madeira.jpg)

Madeira has become a “bitcoin hub”, meaning it is a region that has attracted a significant number of bitcoin users in one place and has a higher rate than average for merchant adoption (i.e. accepting bitcoin for payment). The [FREE Madeira](https://www.freemadeira.com/goals) nonprofit organization focuses on bitcoin education and growing adoption in the region.

The thing I like most about these conferences is seeing people from so many countries in one place, and with so much optimism and hope among them in a world that currently has a lot of dark things happening. They all have different backgrounds but come together to embrace something they agree on. Within one hour of walking around the conference, I had people from Bolivia, Colombia, Saudi Arabia, Spain, Bulgaria, Australia, India, Turkey, Germany, Portugal, Malaysia, France, the Netherlands, and elsewhere all enthusiastically come up and say hi. People from dozens of countries were represented here.

I participated in a number of panels and a book signing, but my favorite was probably the panel that focused on the [intersection of open source money and human rights](https://www.youtube.com/live/bikLdxCKQjc?si=mwM7J-ZUYTr7tWM-). In addition to me, it consisted of Alex Gladstein (the Chief Strategy Officer of the Human Rights Foundation), Farida Nabourema (the Togolese democracy advocate and organizer of the annual Africa Bitcoin Conference), and Jack Dorsey (the co-founder and former CEO of Twitter, and co-founder and current CEO of Block). Most people are focused on price during bull markets, but how the asset is used and perceived in inflationary environments or authoritarian environments is of more foundational interest to me.

![Freedom Panel](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-bitcoin-atlantis-pic.jpg)

*(Preparing backstage)*

Farida is always a powerful speaker because she goes into depth on the monetary problems that Africa has faced, both from outside colonialist forces and from within by corrupt rulers. Africa has over 40 different currencies, most of which are rapidly debasing, which makes saving very hard and fills cross-border commerce with tons of extra frictions and costs. In a number of countries, including where she is from, bank accounts of protestors are regularly frozen outside of the rule of law or within an authoritarian context, which in addition to the rapid debasement of the currencies and frequent use of capital controls has led a lot of them to make use of bitcoins and stablecoins to go around their top-down systems and take power back for themselves.

I also participated on a panel with Michael Saylor, Preston Pysh, and Lawrence Lepard that [discussed the role of institutions on bitcoin over the next decade](https://www.youtube.com/live/SKAHlCpwsLc?si=-CeQ8pRG71MVIxr8&t=29035), which I think went very well. Michael’s description of a “ten year gold rush” for institutions from 2024 to 2034 is a powerful one, in which he refers to the time that the spot ETFs were launched (2024) to the time when 99% of its coins mined (2034).

Some of us also did a [Q&A panel](https://www.youtube.com/live/h2ljazAVDUE?si=e8KZpUhHLRkmZoEa&t=2600) on the third day of the event.

## Realty Income Deep Dive Analysis

Over the past year, pretty much the only investments that have mattered were tech-related. Large profitable tech monopolies, AI-related companies, and bitcoin have soared in price, while much of the rest of the U.S. stock market and foreign markets have trended sideways.

Some other large-cap growers have been pulled into the bullish vortex as well, including Costco (COST) which I have highlighted a few times that now sits at 52x earnings.

My preferred investment in that area has been bitcoin and bitcoin proxies such as MicroStrategy (MSTR), and I also own a handful of mega-cap tech/payment stocks that I don’t view as being overvalued.

The more this divergence increases, the risk/reward on many of those types of assets begins to look less attractive (other than bitcoin, in my view), and I’m inclined to look elsewhere to see what is still a good long-term value.

The energy sector has been my preferred contrarian play due to its cheap pricing, strong balance sheets, high rate of capital return (dividends and buybacks and debt reduction), and improving industry supply/demand fundamentals. But outside of that sector, I also try to look around to see what else is available.

One option is emerging markets, which I have some exposure to. However, Chinese investments come with geopolitical tail risks, so I’m cautious in my weightings there.

Realty Income (O) is looking increasingly interesting to me. I wouldn’t call it high conviction at this time, but it’s a place where I feel comfortable deploying conservative income-focused capital as a ballast while I put most of my attention on more fundamentally volatile sectors.

The price is closer to its 2020 lows than it is to all-time highs, even as fundamentals continue to do reasonably well. The issue has mostly been one of valuation:

![O FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-o-fastgraph.png)**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* + black line: the current and historical stock price
  + blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/cash-flow ratio
  + orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/cash-flow in this case)
  + white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
  + dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

The price to tangible book value ratio is now below its long-term average:

![Realty Income Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-o-book-value.png)

Realty Income is one of the largest real estate investment trusts in the world, has one of the highest credit ratings among REITs, and has over 25 consecutive years of annual dividend increases.

Most of their real estate is not related to offices or other problematic areas. It instead mostly consists of restaurants, grocery stores, convenience stores, pharmacies, casinos, logistics centers, dollar stores, home improvement stores, fitness centers, auto centers, and other areas that are resistant to disruption from ecommerce and remote work trends.

![Realty Income Exposure](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-realty-income-exposure.png)

*Chart Source: Realty Income Q4 2023 Presentation*

Their geographical presence consists of over 15,000 properties spread across North America and Europe. Their main focus is on free-standing single-tenant buildings, and they usually do triple-net lease agreements meaning that the tenant pays for property taxes, insurance, and maintenance. Realty Income has a good track record of raising rents along with inflation, and using its strong balance sheet to perform acquisitions which then benefit from refinancing under Realty Income’s safer diversified portfolio structure.

When bought at below-average valuations, Realty Income has historically been a buy-and-hold income-producing investment. Expected annualized forward returns are in the 7% to 11% range compared to 10-year Treasuries which yield under 4.3%, which I think represents a reasonable risk premium.

**Realty Income Risks**

The first main risk to Realty Income relates to valuation. It has become historically cheap, but it’s not all that cheap relative to how high interest rates are.

Realty Income’s management goal is to grow its dividend payouts by 4-5% per year. With a dividend yield of over 5.8%, that means that the company is targeting 9.8% to 10.8% total returns, assuming constant payout ratios and valuation.

Realty Income has been able to make very good acquisitions in the past, and we can see gradual and step-wise increases in their total scale over time:

![Realty Income Assets and Liabilities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-o-ychart.png)

If opportunities dry up, then Realty Income’s growth rate could slow down. If that happens, then a dividend yield in the ballpark of 7% would likely make more sense if 10-year Treasury yields are in the 4.0% to 4.5% range. In other words, there are some stagnation scenarios that can result in a lower valuation, even though the company is already trading at a below-average valuation.

Higher interest rates put some valuation pressure on Realty Income itself, but those higher rates put even more pressure on REITs with weaker balance sheets, which should give Realty Income a decent pipeline of acquisition targets in the years ahead.

The secondary risk is that if they do not maintain their track record of staying on the right side of disruptive technological trends, their business could be more disrupted than just stagnation. I don’t think this is likely to be the case in any sort of investable time horizon (10 years or so), but it’s a risk that needs to be considered.

## Final Thoughts: Gold is Trying to Break Out Again

Somewhat lost amid the current investor and media focus on bitcoin, gold has made a run for $2,100 and is hanging around all-time highs. It is firmly at all-time highs in most other currencies, and if it breaks out in dollar terms, I would expect significant upward momentum from that point as a base case.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-3-5-gold-chart.png)

I continue to have a preference for gold over long-duration Treasuries, and view it as a useful slice in a diversified portfolio. T-bills, TIPS, and gold are my preferred holdings for the most conservative side of a portfolio, rather than T-bonds.

Bitcoin is increasingly the hard money of choice for people and institutions given its superior portability and lower rate of supply inflation, but at the sovereign level they still tend to favor gold as their preferred reserve asset due to its larger market size and longer track record.

Gold used to be the dominant form of money. During the past 53 years where fiat currency systems became dominant, gold instead served the role as a sort of financial system backup. If major sovereign currencies and bonds and banking systems ever become severely impaired, then gold held outside of the banking system was the go-to choice for many people and institutions including central banks.

Gold’s main properties are that it is resistant to debasement and can be held self-custodially unlike stocks or bonds, but its downsides are that it doesn’t inherently produce growth like high-performing stocks do, it isn’t very portable across borders, and verifying it down to its core is generally a slow and expensive process.

As long as most of the world keeps being connected by electrical grids and telecommunication systems, then I think bitcoin or some future version of it will become a very large form of money over time. In other words, open-source, cross-border, divisible, portable, supply-capped, energy-backed money with the largest network effect, deepest liquidity, and most secure/simple design.

Foundational protocols like Internet Protocol, Ethernet, and Universal Serial Bus tend to last a long time once established and are upgraded slowly over time in backwards-compatible ways. At 15+ years and running, I think Bitcoin as a protocol is likely in that category by this point. It’s the dominant protocol of value, although it still faces some technological and geopolitical tests ahead on its way toward full maturity.

In that context, gold can retain its historical backup role, but more-so in terms of protection against cyberattacks, long-term power outages, de-growth scenarios, and other technological catastrophes. It remains the best form of offline money, but as long as we live in an increasingly digital world, its physicality is a weakness as much as it is a strength.
