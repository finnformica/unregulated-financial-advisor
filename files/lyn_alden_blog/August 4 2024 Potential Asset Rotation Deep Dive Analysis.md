## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity indicators continue to run almost perfectly sideways, in large part because the Treasury and Fed have been in lockstep regarding this:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-domestic-liquidity.png)

![Domestic Liquidity Net](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-domestic-liquidity-net.png)

Notably, Stephen Miran and Nouriel Roubini recently [published a paper](https://www.hudsonbaycapital.com/documents/FG/hudsonbay/research/635102_Activist_Treasury_Issuance_-_Hudson_Bay_Capital_Research.pdf) about “Activist Treasury Issuance” which is their term that refers to the Treasury Department taking unusual actions to offset the Fed’s tightness. Both Miran and Roubini are economics PhDs and worked in various capacities with the Treasury Department in the past.

In the paper, they argued that the Treasury has been shortening the average duration of its debt issuance via excess T-bill issuance, and thus offsetting some of the monetary tightening that the Fed has been doing.

It’s normal for the Treasury to issue an excessive ratio of T-bills during a crisis such as in 2020, but it’s highly unusual for them to be doing it in relatively benign economic and market conditions like we’ve had over the past couple years. I’d argue that it’s a symptom of the ongoing fiscal dominance that the United States finds itself in.

T-bills are more “money-like” than longer duration T-bonds, and so when a government finds itself issuing a ton of debt at a time when neither their central bank nor their primary foreign investors are currently buying that issuance, they can issue more short-duration debt. Short duration debt, being of limited volatility, can be held by investors similar to how they would hold cash.

Here’s Miran and Roubini’s executive summary:

> By adjusting the maturity profile of its debt issuance, Treasury is dynamically managing financial conditions and through them, the economy, usurping core functions of the Federal Reserve. We dub this novel tool “activist Treasury issuance,” or ATI. By manipulating the amount of interest rate risk owned by investors, ATI works through the same channels as the Fed’s quantitative easing programs. ATI has been a major market driver over the past year and we expect it will continue to play a significant role in the years ahead; ATI may become a regular element of the policy toolbox, driving political business cycles in the market and the economy.
>
> We calculate that ATI has reduced 10-year yields over the last year by roughly a quarter of a percent, providing similar stimulus as a one-point cut in the Fed Funds rate, the central bank’s primary policy tool. Combined with higher neutral rates, ATI implies the total stance of both monetary and issuance policy, considered together, is roughly neutral—in other words, ATI has interdicted the Fed’s attempt to restrain the economy, helping explain inflation persistence and upward nominal growth surprises over the past year.
>
> If ATI is unwound via terming out $1 trillion of excess Treasury Bills, we expect it to temporarily boost 10-year yields by 50 basis points, before settling into a permanent 30-bp increase, with corresponding price changes in risk assets. A 50-bp increase in the 10-year yield will have similar economic effects as a two-point hike in the Fed Funds rate. If ATI is not unwound but becomes a permanently employed policy tool, we are likely to see higher equilibrium inflation and interest rates priced in over time due to political business cycles becoming reality.

Regular readers of my work will be quite familiar with this concept. I start most of my reports by discussing liquidity conditions, including what the Treasury has been doing with its excess T-bill issuance. And at the start of this year, I dedicated my [January 2024 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/january-2024-newsletter/) to this phenomenon of the Treasury offsetting the Fed. Here was a key snippet:

> Ever since the fourth quarter of 2022, and extending all throughout 2023, the U.S. Federal Reserve and the U.S. Treasury have been operating opposite policies from each other. And the Treasury has had to pull two different levers to keep up with the Fed.
>
> -From September 2022 through May 2023, the Treasury drained their own cash reserves back into the financial system, which offset the Fed.
>
> -From June 2023 to the present, the Treasury rapidly increased their T-bill issuance as a percentage of their debt issuance and thus drained reverse repos back into the financial system, which offset the Fed.
>
> This following chart shows the Fed vs Treasury liquidity actions in year-over-year change terms. For this chart, higher means more liquidity, and lower means less liquidity. The Fed in blue has been doing its part to reduce liquidity in the financial system since early 2022. The Treasury in red (reflecting both their own cash balances and their ability to affect the reverse repo facility based on their ability to issue T-bills) has been doing its part to increase liquidity in the financial system since late 2022.
>
> ![Net Liquidity Factors](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/january-2024-newsletter-net-liquidity-annotated.png)
>
> The net result is that domestic liquidity was decreasing until Q4 2022 but then began to flatten out and turn slightly upward when the Treasury began its inverse operation:
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/january-2024-newsletter-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> -January 2024 Newsletter, “Fiscal and Monetary Divergence

While I’m more focused on the liquidity impacts, Miran and Roubini’s paper attempts to quantify the effect by comparing it to interest rate changes. They argue that the Treasury’s excessive T-bill issuance has been the equivalent of the Fed cutting interest rates by 1%. And they argue that if this were to be unwound, it would temporarily be similar to a 2% rate hike by the Fed.

I don’t have any particular view regarding if their interest rate comparison is accurate or not, but in my view it’s less about rates per se, and more about liquidity and smooth financial plumbing, of which rates are only a part.

Meanwhile, global liquidity is rangebound, but I continue to have a view that it’ll likely grind higher over the next 18 months. If we get some of the capital rotations discussed later in this report, then that could be a catalyst for such a move higher, which can feed on itself. I don’t have any firm conviction for the next quarter.

**U.S. Economic Weakening**

In the latter half of this past week, two very weak economic reports were released.

The first weak report was that the manufacturing PMI came in well below consensus expectations. The manufacturing part of the economy has been weak for over a year and a half, had been kind of stabilizing, but recently has deteriorated again:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-pmi.png)

The second weak report was that payrolls came in sharply below consensus. Fewer jobs were created last month than expected, and the official unemployment rate jumped from 4.1% to 4.3%.

![Nonfarm Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-nonfarm-payrolls.png)

And the problem is that the payrolls have thus far been the *good* number. A couple months ago I highlighted the widening gap between payroll numbers (surveys of businesses) vs surveys of households regarding employment:

> There is currently conflicting data between the household survey and the payroll survey for overall U.S. employment trends, which is a data quality issue that complicates analysis. The payroll survey continues to show strong numbers, while the household survey has been notably weaker, with a widening gap. The payroll survey counts people with multiple jobs, whereas the household survey does not, and there are a variety of other differences as well, which post-pandemic remote work and gig work might be amplifying to some degree.
>
> -June 9th premium report

Dario Perkins recently provided a chart of that divergence on social media. Both of these lines are supposed to measure changes in employment levels for the country:

![Dario Perkins](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-perkins-tweet.png)

Household employment data had already been stagnant, and now the better-performing payroll data came in surprisingly weak for the past month. And a lot of this is vulnerable to future data revisions from the reporting agencies; recessions are generally declared in hindsight with some subset of the data revised down later.

Currently, the unemployment rate is low in absolute terms, but with the sharp move up from 3.4% to 4.3%, it has moved sufficiently enough to be associated with recession concerns. Given some of the structural tightness in the labor market and large structural fiscal deficits which are stimulatory for certain industries (i.e. fiscal dominance), my base case would be to expect a milder peak in the unemployment rate than the average recession, akin to the early 1970s or the early 2000s.

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-unemployment-annotated.png)

In other words, I’m in a camp that expects a mild recession, at least by some metrics. Recessions in emerging markets tend to have different characteristics than recessions in developed markets (e.g. more stagflationary, more mixed signals or divergent industries that are booming or suffering, more capital outflows). But a recession in a developed market that is in a state of fiscal dominance tends to have certain “emerging market lite” characteristics.

As soon as the payrolls report came out, Treasury yields and the dollar index dropped sharply. The futures market is now pricing in a high likelihood of a 0.50% interest rate cut (a “double cut”) at the Fed’s September meeting. Stocks performed very poorly on the day as well.

**U.S. Business Cycle Summary**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy was decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, entered stall speed for a while, began to show signs of stabilization, but recently weakened again. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as most of them are now except for construction spending and to some extent retail spending), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms. Construction spending has been getting a big fiscal boost from the Inflation Reduction Act.

![Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Along with stocks and most volatile assets, bitcoin has not fared well amid the recent spike in volatility across asset markets.

It continues to be chop along in a range that it’s been in since March of this year, and has fallen toward the lower end of that range.

![Bitcoin Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-bitcoin.png)

**Bitcoin Conference 2024**

For the past few months, I have been reporting on how the broad crypto industry, including some elements of the bitcoin industry, have raised a lot of money to support pro-bitcoin/crypto congressmen and senators (for both parties), including supporting Donald Trump for the presidency.

Within that context, and given that Bitcoin Magazine’s annual conference is the largest bitcoin conference in the world (and by extension, one of the most widely-attended tech/finance conferences in the world), it was very political this year. In addition to many technical and business speakers, this year they had Donald Trump as their headline speaker, in addition to Robert Kennedy Jr and a large number of sitting senators and representatives.

Donald Trump, Robert Kennedy Jr, and Senator Cynthia Lummis all called for the United States to build a strategic bitcoin reserve, similar to its gold reserves.

-Surprisingly to some, given his normal persona, Trump’s proposal was the smallest of the three, as he merely vowed to stop selling the 200,000+ coins that the United States already has, in addition to repeating some of his prior promises to support the industry. His keynote speech was instead more similar to one of his rallies, and he demonstrated less familiarity with the subject and with his audience than many other politicians there, but he hit on a number of key points and has the biggest public reach of any speaker there. He framed himself as a pro-energy, pro-AI, pro-bitcoin candidate and said the bitcoin industry looks like the steel industry from a century ago.

-Kennedy’s proposal was the most aggressive, calling for the United States to keep its existing coins and acquire 550 coins per day until it reaches at least 4 million (roughly 20% of coins), which would be very hard to do as the price appreciates from that amount of persistent buying. He also called for effectively ending taxes on bitcoin, which makes it functionally similar to a second legal tender. Over the past year, he and his campaign have done a lot of outreach to people who work in the field. I don’t reach out to politicians since that’s not my focus, but due to outreach from a mutual contact on behalf of Kennedy’s team, I did a private group call with Kennedy and some of his team to answer bitcoin-related questions a while ago.

-Lummis’ proposal called for establishing a reserve of 1 million coins, and provided the most specifics since she actually submitted [a bill](https://www.lummis.senate.gov/press-releases/lummis-introduces-strategic-bitcoin-reserve-legislation/) in the Senate to do this (which likely won’t pass as-is, but rather sets a precedent). Her bill included specific details regarding the U.S. federal government creating geographically-distributed state-of-the-art secure facilities to hold the keys, establishing a minimum 20-year holding period by the government, enabling individual states to make use of the facilities with segregated accounts, mandating cryptographic proof-of-reserves which allows the reserves to be well-evidenced to the public each quarter in ways that gold cannot be, and clarifying that nothing in the act can infringe on individual property rights (which is relevant considering that the U.S. gold reserve was partially built by forcing citizens to hand over their gold to the government). Lummis and her team regularly meet with a number of people who work in the field and are rather well-versed on the subject.

An area I think that went under the radar at the conference were the Democratic politicians. They were in the minority compared to Republicans, but their presence made it effectively bipartisan. Ro Khanna for example gave a fireside chat on the main stage and said, “Bitcoin is about freedom. Bitcoin is about human rights.”

And during the conference, Ro Khanna and 13 other sitting Democratic congress people wrote [a letter](https://x.com/BitcoinPierre/status/1817219938672591310) to the Democratic National Committee saying that the party needs to pivot to embrace the industry better. They called for selecting a vice president candidate and SEC chair that are sophisticated with regard to the industry, called for including pro-digital asset support in their party platform, and called for engaging with industry experts.

During the past several years, much of the Democratic party’s anti-bitcoin/crypto stance has come from certain politicians like Elizabeth Warren, Brad Sherman, and some others. Warren has been influential on the Biden/Harris administration, which has largely been in her camp on the issue. However, an increasing number of Democratic politicians have broken from that outlook, including Chuck Schumer and Nancy Pelosi who have recently voted in favor of bipartisan bills that benefit the industry, which President Biden vetoed. Politico [reported](https://www.politico.com/news/2024/08/01/democrats-kamala-harris-crypto-policy-fight-00172103) on this growing schism within the party, and the question remains whether Harris will do a soft reset toward a more neutral approach than her camp has had recently, or if she will remain with the Warren camp of the party on this topic.

The reason I bring this up is that for years, one of the identified risk factors for bitcoin was government attacks on it and its holders, in a variety of ways. However, government is not a monolith, and recently many politicians have increasingly concluded that money and votes come from supporting the industry, and there isn’t really much money or votes to be gained from specifically opposing it.

In my view, financial privacy in general will be the main battleground that many governments will continue to have a problem with, rather than owning bitcoin per se.

**Who Owns Bitcoin?**

Many people assume that bitcoin owners lean more conservative or libertarian than the general population. Certainly among politicians, there is a much higher rate of embracing bitcoin among those on the right side of the political spectrum than the left.

However, a [recent study](https://www.thenakamotoproject.org/report) has surprisingly found that to not be the case among the general American public. Two professors, Andrew Perkins and Troy Cross, examined a sample of 3,500 people, including bitcoin owners and non-owners, and found that bitcoin owners have a roughly equivalent distribution of political beliefs compared to non-owners.

![Bitcoin Ownership](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-bitcoin-ownership.png)

Bitcoin owners in the study were slightly more likely to skew to both of the tails of the spectrum, but on average land pretty much where non-owners are politically. They also found that bitcoin owners don’t tend to stand out from non-owners in most statistical ways (ethnicity, income, education, and so forth) with the exception that on average they lean younger and more male than the non-owners in their sample.

Unsurprisingly, the biggest measured differences between owners of bitcoin and non-owners of bitcoin were simply in the areas of bitcoin itself. Bitcoin owners were on average more knowledgeable about bitcoin, and had more positive views toward bitcoin such as viewing it as moral, viewing it as having high utility, and believing its set of incentives and stability to be well-designed.

**University of Wyoming Bitcoin Research Institute**

The University of Wyoming [announced](https://www.uwyo.edu/news/2024/08/new-uw-bitcoin-research-institute-asks-big-questions-about-money.html) the creation of a new Bitcoin Research Institute. Many universities now have digital asset research departments, but this one is specifically to study the impacts of the Bitcoin network.

Established via a major gift from institutional investor Mark Casey, the initial director will be Professor Bradley Rettler, and they’ll also have Professor Andrew Bailey join as well. The institute is reportedly interested in academics joining them from other universities as they build out the institute and perform peer-reviewed research on issues related to Bitcoin. Notably, the institute is not focused on technology, but rather is focused on studying Bitcoin’s economic, cultural, and social implications.

**Ethereum ETF Launch With Weak Demand**

Several Ethereum spot ETFs began trading on June 23rd. In addition, the Grayscale Ethereum Trust converted to an ETF which finally enables redemptions from such a high-fee vehicle.

Over $2 billion worth of outflows occurred from the pre-existing Grayscale Ethereum Trust since its conversion to an ETF, and this amount was larger than the inflows to all of the other new and less expensive ETFs combined so far. There was a similar dynamic for bitcoin ETFs where capital flowed out of the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust conversion and into the other ETFs, except importantly their combined inflows greatly exceeded the outflows from Grayscale.

So, the Ethereum ETFs have thus far had a weaker launch than the Bitcoin ETFs, both in absolute numbers and on a relative inflow/outflow basis. It could be attributed to the asset itself, or it could be attributed to the differences in market environment during the launch.

For Bitcoin, eventually the Grayscale outflows slowed down while inflows into the other bitcoin ETFs continued, and we might see a similar dynamic at a smaller scale here as well. It’s hard to say yet. Thus far, the ETHBTC ratio has performed poorly since the launch of the Ethereum ETFs.

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-ethbtc.png)

The ratio continues to show weakness at around 0.46, but the number I’d be on the lookout for is 0.45, since that’s the low for this cycle so far. There’s not much clear support below that level.

One of the fundamental challenges with Ethereum is that it’s kind of sandwiched between the hardness of Bitcoin which is designed for maximal decentralization, simplicity, and immutability on one end, and more scalable smart contract blockchains like Solana and Tron on the other end. Blockchains like Solana and Tron provide similar performance as Ethereum, but with lower fees, in exchange for more centralization.

Ethereum benefits from significant liquidity and developer network effects that can be sustained for a while, but its bulls have to constantly justify their middle approach. It’s a similar dynamic to how both luxury stores and discount stores tend to do pretty well, while middle-of-the-road retailers have the hardest path to navigate.

## Deep Dive: Rotation Analysis

The United States runs structural trade deficits with the rest of the world in aggregate. And, being the global reserve currency with the biggest and deepest capital markets, the rest of the world takes their surpluses and invests them into U.S. financial assets including stocks, bonds, real estate, and private equity.

Over the past 15 years in particular, this has built up to rather imbalanced levels. Countries like Japan and China own a very large amount of U.S. financial assets, as do many others.

There is a giant engine of almost automatic capital that keeps flowing into large-cap U.S. stocks in particular. Foreign capital keeps flowing in, and domestic employee 401(k) paychecks keep flowing in. And a lot of it is passively weighted by market capitalization, and so much of it automatically flows toward the top.

Effectively the way this works is that we buy depreciating goods from abroad and we sell portions of our appreciating capital ownership to them in return. But it feels nice while we do it, at lease for those of us that own assets, because it’s really good for asset prices.

However, China has been reducing its Treasury holdings, and Japan has been using some of its assets to intervene in its currency weakness. In addition, Japan raised its interest rate to 0.25%, which doesn’t seem like much but it’s their highest level in over 15 years.

![Japan Interest Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-japan-interest-rate.png)

Combined with Japan’s structural current account surplus, large amounts of capital allocated to foreign markets, and high levels of productivity, relatively minor hawkish moves from them can be rather supportive of their weak currency. There’s been a significant drop in the USDJPY exchange rate, both as U.S. economic data softens and as Japan gradually takes more measures to support its currency from getting too weak.

![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-usdjpy.png)

Right now, all of those attributes have pushed a lot of capital to one side of the boat. Foreign investors have a very high percentage of their allocations in U.S. assets, which props up our equity valuations and our currency (which in some ways is beneficial, and other ways is harmful), which then feeds on itself.

Many emerging markets have dollar-denominated debt, and so a strong dollar squeezes their financial systems and economies rather hard. As more capital flows into the United States, it can keep the dollar strong and thus keep downward economic and financial pressure on these dollar-indebted countries. That pressure increasingly makes global pools of capital decide to leave those markets to shift toward U.S. markets, which weakens their currencies and strengthens the dollar further.

However, the United States is not invincible. It’s running massive fiscal deficits, which by late 2025 the Federal Reserve will likely go back to assisting with for liquidity purposes. It’s also running a structural trade deficit, partly because of its artificially strong dollar, which has increasingly become a domestic political issue. With interest rates as high as they are and with deficits as large as they are, there’s a growing gap between those who already own assets and those who are effectively locked out of owning assets.

When enough shifts happen, this dynamic that feeds on itself in one direction can instead turn and feed on itself in the other direction. If capital inflows start turning into outflows, it can weaken asset prices and the dollar together. When the dollar weakens, dollar-indebted emerging markets tend to get a fundamental boost, which attracts more capital to them. As they get more capital, it helps support their currency. And that tends to attract more capital.

Many investors have a concern that these large creditor Asian nations could spark a rotation in capital. As they seek to support their own currencies and/or markets, they can stop investing in the United States around the margins, or even repatriate some of their capital.

**The Potential Catalyst**

Global macroeconomic systems are very complex, with more variables than any one framework or model can account for. And so, the way I approach it in terms of managing risk and discovering opportunities, is to identify a base case path, and then keep modifying it or branching from it as new data and updates come in.

Two months ago on Twitter, there was a thread involving financial professionals Michael Arouet and Patrick Zweifel about what could spark a rotation in capital from U.S. to foreign equity markets, like what has happened in the past.

![Developed vs Developing Markets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-developed-vs-developing.png)

In response, I provided a thought process:

![Lyn Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-lyn-tweet.png)

I then provided more analysis about this topic in my [mid-July public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/july-2024-newsletter/). The focus of the newsletter was how, when the U.S. economy softens and the Fed cuts rates, it might not be as stimulating for the domestic economy as people think.

After four decades of lower-lows and lower-highs in terms of mortgage rates in the United States, this was the first cycle where we got a higher-high in mortgage rates. I think there’s a strong probability that we’ll also get a higher-low in mortgage rates, meaning that they’ll go down from peak levels, but won’t get as low as they did in the prior cycle.

![Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-mortgage-rates.png)

Back in April 2024, the New York Fed [released](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/annual_reports) their annual report which reviewed their activities for 2023 and projected how things might go in the future. In the report, they projected that by the end of 2025 they’d likely go back to structural accumulation of Treasury securities but would continue to decrease their holdings of mortgage backed securities:

![UST vs MBS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma-ust-vs-mbs.png)

The Fed phrases this projection in terms of maintaining adequate bank reserves, while also trying to normalize their balance sheet back toward Treasury securities. I’d phrase this a bit more bluntly as an anticipatory projection on their part that they’ll need to quietly support the structural fiscal deficits going forward, which is what my math points to as well by around that same timeframe.

Meanwhile, if we don’t get a lower-low in mortgages rates, there is a limit to how much spending power homeowner consumers can unlock by mortgage refinancing. Other parts of the lending market, such as small business lending, would benefit positively from rate cuts, but in the troubling context of a weakening consumer and weakening job market.

Some of the biggest beneficiaries of rate cuts might actually be emerging market governments and companies. I don’t necessarily mean right away as we deal with market volatility, but as interest rates on the dollar ease, there are a lot of dollar-indebted emerging markets out there that look like coiled springs. It looks similarly in that regard to the early 2000s period, and emerging markets enjoyed a very strong 2003-2007 cycle from an easing dollar.

During strong dollar periods of the 1960s, 1990s, and 2010s where there was also a general trend of disinflation domestically and internationally, a lot of assets tended to move in lockstep. U.S. growth stocks in particular tended to do well. In the 1960s they were called the “[Nifty Fifty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nifty_Fifty)“. In the 1990s they were called the Dotcom Bubble. Lately they’ve been called the FAANG stocks and now the Magnificent Seven. In addition to doing well fundamentally, they often received very high valuations that eventually became excessive. Meanwhile, value-type stocks, foreign stocks, gold, energy, and commodities tended to not do particularly well, both in terms of fundamentals and in terms of low valuations on those fundamentals.

During weak dollar periods of the 1970s and 2000s, that trend reversed. The dollar weakened, which eased debt burdens globally. Inflation increased, energy and commodities did well, gold did well, and value-type stocks tended to do pretty well. The growth stocks from the prior decades tended to still do well fundamentally, but their stock prices would often stagnate for a while as their valuations normalized.

As a basic example, Coca Cola (KO) reached a price/earnings ratio of 56x in the late 1990s selling sugar water alongside the tech bubble. As a blue-chip stable growth stock, it got caught up in the frenzy. Over the next decade and a half, their net income continued to increase substantially, but their valuation fell by more than half down to reality, resulting in a stock price that took that full period to recover. A lot of similar non-tech U.S. growth stocks had a similar pattern, including Walmart (WMT).

![KO Price/Earnings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-ko.png)

Meanwhile, here in the 2020s, a lot of people are asking if the Nvidia’s (NVDA) of the world are a bubble or not, like Cisco (CSCO) in the late 1990s. Meanwhile, I’m instead scratching my head looking at Costco (COST), which is a great company like Coca Cola was in the 1990s, but is trading at over 50x earnings.

![COST FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-8-4-cost-fastgraph.png)

If there’s a catalyst for the start of that kind of rotation, then I’d say that the United States cutting interest rates in response to a gradually weakening consumer, at a time of near-record structural twin deficits as a percentage of GDP, is a strong contender.

Admittedly, I had a similar thought back in 2019, which was the last time that the United States began a rate-cutting cycle. That ended up being incorrect. The U.S. economy was softening in 2019, and the Fed performed moderate rate cuts in the middle of the year. By September, the Fed had to end quantitative tightening and go back to balance sheet growth due to financial plumbing issues partially related to the large U.S. deficit. And indeed the dollar index began softening from that point. I thought that might be the start of a significant rotation.

But, as a curveball, the pandemic happened in 2020. On January 26th, 2020 I sent out an [extra note](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-1-26/) between normal premium reports to alert members about a riskier-than-normal setup with a combination of overbought stocks and worsening virus news out of China. In the months that followed, economies around the world performed lockdowns, trillions of currency units were printed, and so the likelihood of a rotation got thrown out the window for a few years. Europe had an energy crisis (even before the war in Ukraine, which the war then made worse), and then squeezed out poorer countries by buying as much liquified natural gas as they could and outbidding them. Altogether, a variety of occurrences gave the world another five years of this trend continuing, with the U.S. trade deficit widening further, more capital flowing into the U.S., and the biggest stocks going from $1 trillion to $3 trillion companies.

However, in those five years, China became the largest auto exporter in the world. Russia invaded Ukraine, got cut off from the dollar system, and financially unified more with China. China substantially trimmed their U.S. Treasury exposure. More members joined BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization. A lot of central banks of BRICS-type nations bought gold at an accelerated pace to diversity their reserves a bit, and China helped broker some degree of peace and communication between Iran and Saudi Arabia. Political polarization in the United States continued to rise. And U.S. oil production had been growing dramatically from 2012 to 2019, but here in 2024 it’s nearly flat from its 2019 peak, and with faster well depletion rates now.

The question is, will events occur that result in another repeat of that cycle, with everything pushed further than before, and with pieces further along on the chessboard? It’s possible. But I think there’s a higher chance this time that a rotation will take place. I think the extremes to which capital is stuffed into U.S. financial markets are hitting levels that are hard to push for another full cycle, but we’ll see.

To be clear, I’m not saying to rotate out of all the top U.S. stocks and put everything elsewhere. But I am saying that I’d certainly want some style-factor and geographic diversification in my equities outside of just the Magnificent Seven, and that a set of catalysts for such a rotation seems to be there. I’ll be monitoring how this plays out to see what developments may come.

## Final Thoughts: Gold and Conflict

Conflict in the Middle East accelerated in recent days, with the assassination of Hamas’ political leader Ismail Haniyeh in the Iranian capitol Tehran.

In addition, amid escalating strikes between Israel and Hezbollah, an Israeli strike killed a senior commander of Hezbollah in Lebanon, Fuad Shukr. A number of countries subsequently issued emergency warnings for their citizens to leave Lebanon immediately.

In response, the U.S. defense secretary ordered additional naval units and air units deployed to the region, in what seems to be the biggest movement of U.S. forces to that region since the conflict originally broke out in October 2023.

This continues to be a situation that is hard to invest around. I continue to use my three-pillar portfolio concept of 1) good equities, 2) cash-equivalents and short-term Treasuries, and 3) energy producers, commodity producers, and hard monies.

Gold reached a new high in dollar terms last week, and I continue to be bullish on the yellow metal for the years ahead.
