## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be relatively flat:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-domestic-liquidity.png)

The same is true for a broader measure of global liquidity. Dylan LeClaire put out a great chart on this:

![Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-global-liquidity.png)

Liquidity isn’t the only variable that affects broad stock index performance but it’s one of the big ones.

One of the biggest challenges to global liquidity right now is the U.S. Treasury market. Record high ongoing supply of Treasuries are flooding the market, while the Fed is also letting Treasuries mature off of their balance sheet rather than reinvesting them (quantitative tightening). And as yields go up, that contributes to higher interest expense, and thus larger deficits, and thus more Treasury issuance.

As a result, Treasury market volatility is high, Treasury market liquidity is low, and Treasury yields broke out slightly over their autumn 2022 highs:

![10-Year Treasury YIelds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-10-year-treasury.png)

The biggest foreign holders of Treasuries are Japan and China. Japan has been getting more hawkish around the margins, which can repatriate some capital out of the U.S. and back to Japan’s domestic markets. China, meanwhile, has very weak economic results, and [is selling dollar-denominated assets](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-17/china-told-state-banks-to-escalate-yuan-intervention-this-week-llezr1ia) to backstop their weakening yuan. If they end up selling a lot of Treasuries, that can render the Treasury market illiquid (similar to September 2019, March 2020, and September 2022 to varying degrees) and result in a spike in yields.

None of this is particularly good for risk assets (particularly growth stocks or anti-dollar trades like gold and bitcoin) until it reverses toward a better liquidity situation. This is also why I don’t have any long-duration Treasuries in my portfolio. It’s not that I’m particularly bearish on them; I have low conviction either way because it’s largely up to the Treasury Department and the Federal Reserve how they are priced. Instead, I stick to short-duration Treasuries for my defensive capital, which so far has been the right move.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Most economic data has been decelerating over the past 18 months, and many year-over-year indicators are negative in real terms. I provided an economic heatmap in my prior report.

However, the [Atlanta Fed’s model](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow) recently had an unusual spike up in its estimate for this quarter’s annualized GDP growth:

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-gdpnow.png)

Most of that spike up was due to the recent consumer spending numbers. Consumers in aggregate are absolutely on fire, although it’s not evenly spread around.

This is interesting because the venture capital industry is generally dried up, bank lending continues to tighten on small and medium-sized businesses, commercial real estate has major issues, and manufacturing activity is sitting at borderline-recessionary levels:

![ISM Manufacturing PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-ism-manufacturing-pmi.png)

Why the disconnect? [Fiscal dominance](https://www.lynalden.com/july-2023-newsletter/).

Tight monetary policy is putting downward pressure on interest rate sensitive parts of the economy, while huge federal deficits are continuing to stimulate other parts of the economy.

Homeowners with long-duration fixed rate mortgages, large investment-grade corporations with long-duration fixed rate bonds, senior citizens who are receiving large swaths of what has become a top-heavy stimulative pattern (Social Security and Medicare in an aging society)- these groups broadly are doing more than fine.

Exxon Mobil (XOM) for example has around $30 billion in cash and cash equivalents, and $37 billion in long-term debt, most of which is at low interest rates that they locked in years ago. So their net debt is low, and importantly, they’re earning higher interest rates on their cash equivalents than they are paying on their debt. That’s historically uncommon.

And for a company like Alphabet (GOOG) it is even more extreme. They have $114 billion in cash, cash equivalents, and short-term marketable securities, offset by around $14 billion in long-term debt. With a net positive $100 billion or so, higher rates means more income for them, not less.

In other words, if the Federal Reserve wants to stimulate companies like Exxon Mobil and Alphabet, then *raising rates* is one of the ways to do that.

So, we have a tale of two economies. Entities with a lot of short-duration debt that is getting refinanced quarter after quarter at these high rates, or that are unprofitable and rely on the persistent issuance of very high-valued equity (venture capital or unprofitable publicly-traded companies), are generally not doing well. On the other hand, profitable cash-rich entities with long-duration fixed-rate debt are doing just fine.

**Japan Business Cycle**

I’ve been constructive on Japanese equities for a while, and my recent report re-iterated a bullish view on Japanese value equities broadly (even as some industries like the trading companies may be getting a bit ahead of themselves).

Well, the recent GDP report out of Japan was on fire, with a 6% annualized real growth number. Outside of the post-pandemic rebound, that’s the hottest GDP print in a very long time.

As *Reuters* [reported](https://www.reuters.com/markets/asia/japans-q2-gdp-grows-much-faster-than-expected-helped-by-exports-2023-08-15/):

> TOKYO, Aug 15 (Reuters) – Japan’s economy grew much faster than expected in April-June, as brisk auto exports and tourist arrivals helped offset the drag from a slowing post-COVID consumer recovery, although global recession prospects cloud the outlook.
>
> The 6.0% annualised growth in Japan’s economy translated into a quarterly gain of 1.5%, much bigger than median estimates of 0.8% in a Reuters poll and bringing gross domestic product (GDP) to a record high.

![Japan GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-japan-gdp.png)

Japan is even further into fiscal dominance than the United States is. Higher rates may actually be stimulative for them, especially when combined with quantitative easing. This is because their fiscal deficits are much larger than their rate of new bank loans per year, and with so much public debt-to-GDP, higher interest rates increase the interest expense and thus the deficit (which is stimulative) at a bigger magnitude than it affects bank lending.

**China Business Cycle**

China has been a major drag on the global economy in 2023, in large part because they’re running the opposite playbook of the United States and Japan right now. China has low fiscal deficits, and is easing monetary policy.

As *Reuters* [reported](https://www.reuters.com/markets/asia/how-much-worse-can-chinas-economic-slowdown-get-2023-08-15/):

> HONG KONG, Aug 15 (Reuters) – China’s economic activity data for July, including retail sales, industrial output and investment [failed to match](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/china-cuts-key-rates-industrial-output-retail-sales-growth-miss-forecasts-2023-08-15/) expectations, fueling concern over a deeper, longer-lasting slowdown in growth.

It has become so bad that China announced it would stop reporting its youth unemployment metric after it reached record highs, saying they need to review the methodology. Basically, if the data gets bad, they can just stop sharing it. Overall, China’s economy continues to languish.

In addition, one of China’s largest property developers, Evergrande, [just filed for bankruptcy](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/08/18/china-property-developer-evergrande-files-for-bankruptcy-protection-in-us.html). The CCP ironically has been letting market forces play out more than much of the West has as they try to deflate their property bubble, with falling property prices, bankrupt property developers, and so forth.

Basically, the Chinese economy is in a debt-laden deflationary spiral. House prices have fallen, which has impaired household balance sheets. Household balance sheets, having gotten through three years of strict rolling lockdowns with little fiscal stimulus to support them (unlike the United States), are not in great shape to begin with. And so there is stagnation in domestic consumption patterns.

I suspect the eventual result here will be similar to how they got out of near-endless lockdowns: after a certain point the people will get angry and demand a new direction, and the political calculus of the administrators will change in response. In late 2022, an unusually large number of protests in China against the ongoing pandemic lockdowns caused the CCP to change course and begin re-opening. If that happens again, or signs of it begin to grow on social media, there could be some serious fiscal stimulus out of Beijing.

But in the meantime, the direction is stagnant. Since China is the world’s largest commodity importer, that continues to be a drag on otherwise tight commodity markets. This is also why I continue to generally avoid the copper market despite being bullish on copper in the long run, until there is a clear change in global liquidity and economic re-acceleration. The copper-to-gold ratio historically tracks with the U.S. and global purchasing manager’s index rather well, and continues to point down:

![Copper Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-copper-gold.png)

The main area of the Chinese economy that continues to do well is their export sector. In particular, their auto export sector has massively ramped up, and that’s a topic I have been reporting on in occasional reports for months. There’s no sign of that slowing down.

**Europe Business Cycle**

Europe, like China, has been broadly struggling. During the first half of the year, Germany and some other countries were confirmed to be in a technical recession due in part to their energy crisis, and now they’re more in a period of [stagnation](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-07-28/germany-exits-recession-but-economy-only-stagnated-last-quarter). Some energy-intensive companies like chemical producers have reduced their activity in the country and shifted elsewhere.

Germany’s auto exports [are coming in below expectations](https://edition.cnn.com/2023/08/07/economy/germany-recession-car-industry/index.html), in part because China has become a major auto export rival to them. China exports more cars than Germany now, and is rivaling Japan as the world’s largest auto exporter.

In addition to the rise of China’s auto industry, if Germany and other European companies have higher average energy costs than North America or China or elsewhere due to the energy-related policy choices they have made, it’s hard to remain competitive in manufacturing.

**Egypt and the IMF**

Long-term readers know that I spend part of each year in Egypt visiting family and friends, and currently that’s where I am for the rest of the summer. My husband and I treat this as our second home for part of each year.

According to the World Bank, Egypt has a per-capita GDP of $3.7k USD compared to the U.S. that has a per-capita GDP of $70.2k USD.

I track developing country fund flows and macro aspects on a broad scale, but spending time living in different types of places (and bothering family and friends with frequent random financial questions to understand nuances of what’s happening and how they deal with it in the local sense) helps develop a more realistic understanding of topics like money, inflation, geopolitical power structures, and so forth. I go to a couple coastal vacation spots while I’m here each year, but for most of my stay, my husband and I just live in a normal neighborhood of Cairo in my in-laws’ spare apartment.

When we look at the global financial system, it consists of about 160 different currencies that each have a local monopoly over their own jurisdiction, and then have little or no acceptance outside of that jurisdiction. The main exception is the U.S. dollar, which serves as the world reserve currency, and thus serves as the glue that ties all of these currencies together. A handful of other major currencies have a bit of global reach as well, but then the long tail of around 150+ currencies have virtually no international usage. Each person lives and operates in a little currency bubble, with some better than others, and on the international scale it’s basically a barter system.

So, governments and corporations often make international contracts for goods and services and commodities with foreign entities in salable U.S. dollars to solve the problem of barter between 160 unsalable currencies, and hold dollar-denominated reserve assets that they can tap into if needed. Governments and corporations in developing countries (which don’t have a large internal capital base) additionally often have to borrow in dollars, since that’s what prospective international creditors will be willing to offer reasonable terms in.

Like most developing countries, Egypt has a lot of dollar-denominated debt, and occasionally runs into debt crises regarding its ability to service that debt. When that happens to Egypt or other countries, the International Monetary Fund swoops in with dollar loans, but only if those countries comply with the IMF’s terms. One of the key terms tends to be currency devaluation- the IMF wants the loan recipient country to devalue the local currency as a condition to receive the loan. This reduces the income and wealth of the working people, reduces their import capacity, and then (might) improve their export competitiveness, which can (sometimes) help improve their trade balance and help the country earn more dollars to service their debt.

Usually, countries have to keep coming back for more loans and restructuring over the decades. Many countries have received a dozen or two dozen IMF loans over decades, and keep rolling those balances into larger balances. It’s an endless dollar debt treadmill that only a small handful of countries (generally in Asia) have broken out of to build their own major domestic capital base and the ability to fund most of their debt with their own currency. It’s a vicious cycle, and within the past 50 years, very few MSCI-classified developing or frontier countries have ever graduated to being MSCI-classified as developed.

Back in 2016 and then again recently in 2022, Egypt made a deal with the IMF to devalue their currency and agree to other terms in exchange for dollar-denominated loans to service their dollar-denominated debt. In both instances, Egypt cut their currency in half relative to the dollar as a condition to get the loan.

![EGPUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-egpusd.jpg)

The official exchange rate is currently around 31-to-1 Egyptian pounds to dollars, but the black/street market rate is around 40-t0-1 and often higher. People who serve as street brokers to exchange physical dollars for physical Egyptian pounds are often arrested and face prison time; the authorities do not want people to have access to dollars outside of the banking system. The Egyptian banking system is in acute need of dollars most of the time.

In addition to Egyptians having their liquid savings devalued whenever these devaluation events happen, their ongoing wages are denominated in Egyptian pounds and thus get devalued as well. The burden of difficulty is therefore on them to try to renegotiate higher wages after a devaluation. If their currency was cut in half relative to the dollar, then nothing short of a 100% raise would get them back to earning the same number of dollar-equivalents per hour, and of course basically nobody can successfully negotiate a 100% raise. So if they get a raise at all, they get maybe a 10%, 20%, or at most 30% raise, after their currency unit value was cut in half, and thus now they earn much less in terms of global purchasing power on a recurring basis. Over many years, they might or might not eventually get back to their prior level of dollar-equivalents that they earn per hour.

Egypt currently has 37% year-over-year official inflation. But of course that’s a measure of a very broad basket and is generally understated. The price of beef and poultry is officially up 92% year-over-year, for example. Cars and other imports have also generally at least doubled in price (in other words, they’ve roughly stayed the same in dollar terms). But because hardly anyone doubled their wages in local currency terms, from the Egyptian household perspective, most things are way more expensive now and take more hours of work to earn.

Outside of a few hotspot development projects, similar effects happen with real estate as well. Most local real estate has not yet doubled in price even as the currency was cut in half, meaning that the dollar value of most properties is lower than it was two years ago. I’m currently in the process of property shopping, actually.

Rents are in the process of adjusting upward- many people (whether in Egypt or elsewhere) do not understand the specific magnitude of inflation and so the idea of a 30% rent increase is insulting and often gets pushback by renters. It takes time for the new currency reality to trickle out for most types of internal payments (wages, rents, property prices, etc). But as previously described, it shows up quickly in imports and scarce items.

It’s hard for people to accumulate liquid capital in most developing countries of the world, within their various currency bubbles, which is a major friction for development and wealth accumulation. People turn to illiquid assets (real estate), gold, or literally buying physical cash dollars on the black market and holding them (without interest) in their homes. In tech-savvy countries, they sometimes turn to dollar stablecoins and/or bitcoins. The wealthy, who have more money and access, often turn to international assets and accounts, such as real estate, securities, bank accounts, and so forth.

Bitcoin price action was in a near record-low volatility regime for a while, but broke down on Thursday nearly at the same time as the Evergrande bankruptcy news came out. This liquidated a lot of traders and washed out a lot of leveraged longs and open interest out of the market:

![BTC Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-btc-price.png)

As I wrote in my prior report:

> I continue to view bitcoin as having put in its price bottom and being in a position to grind higher to new highs over the next few years. However, the price will likely be very much tied to liquidity conditions, and so I expect considerable volatility and periods of stagnation or correction along the way. I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the most sensible approach for most investors that want a bitcoin allocation.

Picking up a bit of exposure during liquidations tends to be a good move in the long run. I don’t have a firm view on the extent or duration of the current down move in the price of bitcoin because in the intermediate term I think it’ll be largely tied to global liquidity, the U.S. Treasury market, and Chinese stimulus or lack thereof that I discussed in the initial macro section of this report.

I don’t currently use Trading View but last year I had a friend make a chart with some inputs I gave him, since the closest correlate to bitcoin price action I have seen is the rate of change of global broad money (M2) in USD terms. In a recent Twitter discussion where I brought up that chart again, some random observer of the conversion went and made an updated version, which was very helpful. I added a bit of annotation:

![Bitcoin vs Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-bitcoin-vs-global-m2.png)

*Chart Source: [Tomasz Adasdo (@disnej2)](https://twitter.com/disnej2)*

The top green and red candle line is the broad money supply (M2) of the top five currency blocs, with the denominator being the U.S. dollar. In other words, it’s a proxy for global M2 in USD terms. It is tied to both the rate of new money creation, and to the dollar index relative to these other major currencies. It’s a great indicator for both global monetary inflation and global liquidity conditions.

The yellow line is the bitcoin price. In the first few years of its existence it was too small and idiosyncratic, but ever since about about 2013 or 2014, it has been the closest asset that I’m aware of to global M2 in USD terms.

The bottom bar is the year-over-year % change of global M2 in USD terms, which tends to be a closer correlate to bitcoin price action, since markets tend to set prices on the margin as the rate of growth changes, rather than just the absolute level.

So, when I say bitcoin is tied to liquidity conditions, that’s mainly the chart I am referring to.

## Travel Sector Updates

As the macro section discussed, the U.S. economy (and certain other parts of the world), is a tale of two economies. With very high fiscal deficits and public debt-to-GDP, higher interest rates put downward pressure on some parts of the economy, while actually *stimulating* other areas, as higher interest expense and thus larger deficits flow out into the economy.

The U.S. federal government’s fiscal deficit for the first 10 months of the fiscal year (October 2022 through July 2023) [was $1.6 trillion](https://www.marketwatch.com/story/u-s-budget-deficit-hits-1-6-trillion-in-first-10-months-of-fiscal-year-19357a10). That’s twice what it was for the first ten months of last year. The largest component of that increase was the higher interest expense that the government is paying on its debts. So we have to ask the question: who’s on the receiving side of those deficits, and particularly who is on the other side of those interest expenses?

One answer is cash-rich corporations like Exxon Mobil and Alphabet that I previously mentioned. But certain households are another answer.

Consider an upper-middle class 65-year old retiree. They own a home outright with no mortgage, or they’ve locked in a low fixed-rate mortgage. They have plenty of cash, money markets, and bonds/equities. They receive Social Security and Medicare. They’re a mini-version of Exxon Mobil or Alphabet.

As the Federal Reserve raises interest rates, it doesn’t really affect them negatively at all. In fact, their bank accounts and money markets and T-bills start paying them *higher yields*, while any debts they have are locked in at low rates for long durations. So, they have plenty of money to spend on travel, restaurants, and other things. And for some of them, they help their children or grandchildren with expenses as well.

The consensus idea that aging demographics are deflationary makes sense historically when senior citizens on average tended to be impoverished. But at the current time, that’s where large swaths of the U.S. deficits are going toward: Social Security, Medicare, and Interest Expense (with seniors and their proxies such as insurance companies and pensions on the receiving end of much of that interest). Most U.S. household wealth is still concentrated in the hands of the Baby Boom generation and will be for quite some time.

The travel sector suffered for a while, and so there is still pent-up travel demand. That fact, combined with ongoing fiscal dominance directed at people who have the time and money to travel, is rather constructive for the travel industry and restaurant industry. In other words, a core thesis is to own businesses that upper-middle class seniors want to spend money on. They’re the ones that have a large portion of the country’s money, and higher interest rates are actually neutral-to-stimulatory for many of them.

## Final Thoughts: New Book

I have a book coming out by the end of August called *Broken Money*.

It’s about the past, present, and future of money. My goal for the book is for the reader to walk away with a deep understanding of money and monetary history, both in terms of theoretical foundations and in terms of practical implications.

![Broken Money Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/broken-money-feature.jpg)

This has been my main side project over the past year, and I have been lucky to have learned so much from working on it. For those that have enjoyed my content over the years and want to see how things fit into the big picture, this book aims to do that. I start with the very foundation of money and where it came from, and work up from there in an accessible and modular way.

I was also extremely fortunate to have Joakim Book provide editing and research assistance for the project. Joakim is both a financial editor and a monetary historian with degrees in economics and financial history from the University of Glasgow and the University of Oxford, and he reached out when the book was nearly finished after hearing about it from one of my interviews. I wrote *Broken Money* from a blended engineering and financial background with a big historical focus, and I was very happy to have Joakim fact-check my historical observations and economic inferences, as well as bring additional source material to my attention.

-The early parts of the book focus on the past and the present of money and monetary systems, and often contrasts different ways of thinking about money to explore multiple points of view, before providing an answer for how those points of view can be reconciled.

-The later parts of the book focus on the possible future directions that we may move toward as money becomes more digital over time, including open-source money like bitcoin, privately-issued digital money like stablecoins, and government-issued money like central bank digital currencies.

I sent advance manuscripts to a handful of friends and acquaintances last month who were interested in the project and are subject matter experts in various ways including Preston Pysh, Jeff Booth, Alex Gladstein, and Jack Dorsey, and so far it has been very well-received by all of them. I’ll alert subscribers when the book is available for purchase.
