**U.S. Small Business Employment**

Probably the darkest side to this pandemic, economically, was the “K-shaped recovery”, as it is often described. A K-shaped recovery refers to wealthy individuals and large companies bouncing back quickly (the upper right arm of the “K”), whereas lower-income individuals and small businesses keep trending down (the lower right leg of the “K”).

Some of this is from technological acceleration. The virus pulled forward a few extra years of growth from ecommerce and software companies. In addition, anyone who holds a diverse array of assets, and derives income from those assets rather than economically-sensitive or pandemic-sensitive jobs, did pretty well this year.

On the other hand, this was also due to a disparity in financing. Bigger companies have better access to capital markets, can borrow at lower rates, the Fed can buy their bonds and push down yields to artificially low levels, etc. Smaller businesses, like family-owned restaurants and other small operations, don’t have access to as much easy financing, so a lot of them went bust. This gives the opportunity for larger corporate chain restaurants to move in and take market share, which is unfortunate.

As a result, employment from small businesses (firms with fewer than 50 employees) had no bounce yet, unlike the larger jobs picture:

## The Uranium Market

Over the past year, one of the most common requests I receive is to share my thoughts on the beaten-down uranium market. For the past few years I’ve been bullish on gold and silver, and then this year increasingly bullish on copper producers, increasingly bullish on beaten-down high-quality energy producers/transporters, etc.

So, what about uranium?

The uranium industry is a very complex niche commodity industry to supply nuclear energy utilities, it has been in a decade-long bear market, it’s very unloved, it’s rather small at this point and in many cases not very liquid, and the bullish thesis for it to play out is a 5+ year view. However, historically when uranium moves, it can be extremely potent, virtually uncorrelated to everything else, and therefore is a useful portfolio diversifier.

After having watched the industry for a while, and considering the macro situation as my bigger focus, I consider the opportunity for uranium to finally be at a point where I am directly interested, and am happy to write about it and invest in it as a small slice within a diversified portfolio.

The background context is that the price of uranium is currently well below the cost of production, and yet the nuclear utility industry demand faces a widening supply deficit from miners, and so the price of uranium will likely have to rise at some point in order to incentivize new mining supply to come onto the market and meet that demand.

When it comes down to crunch time, an acute supply deficit can’t be allowed to happen, because nuclear energy generates 10% of global electricity. This, naturally, creates a bullish argument for uranium and some of its producers.

**Background on Uranium and Nuclear Energy**

Nuclear energy is a controversial energy source due to some obvious risks and because of its militarized background.

Originally, uranium was mined and used for nuclear weapons, but then the technology was applied more peacefully to utility energy production, with much lower enrichment levels. Nuclear reactors are also used to power flagship navy vessels like aircraft carriers, and could theoretically be used to power other large transport ships like tankers and containerships instead of fossil fuels.

The benefits of nuclear energy are:

* It’s great for continuous base-load electric power.
* It doesn’t emit carbon dioxide or particulates (i.e. quite green).
* There’s a 100+ year ground supply.
* Ships can go years without refueling (or polluting).

The downsides of nuclear are:

* Meltdown risk from malfunctions or natural disasters.
* There is insufficient capital put towards permanent nuclear waste disposal.
* A “not in my neighborhood” public opinion.

Coal and natural gas utility plants have relatively small initial construction costs, but then very high ongoing costs from their fuel source: coal and natural gas, respectively. Both of them emit carbon dioxide, although carbon capture technology continues to improve. Coal also emits a lot of particulates, which pollutes air, land, and sea to unhealthy levels, and without much of a solution to prevent that. A big percentage of the dangerous amount of mercury in the world’s oceans comes from coal-burning.

The economics of nuclear energy are different. The initial construction costs for a nuclear facility total up to a few billion dollars, and the facility takes years to complete. The facility then can last for 30-60 years and provide a ton of energy, and uranium fuel is a very small cost of ongoing operation. A little bit of carbon is emitted from mining uranium (and the same is true for the metals for solar and wind energy infrastructure), but from there, nuclear power plants don’t emit carbon or particulates, although the uranium waste has to be disposed of securely, which is often not done as well as it could be, due to insufficient public infrastructure for disposal.

Wind and solar are mostly-renewable energy sources, but their key limiting factor is that they are intermittent sources of power, and are more suitable for some geographies than other geographies. Expensive and relatively inefficient battery storage technologies can provide power during the nighttime by storing excess daytime generation, but battery technology has developed at a rather slow pace for decades; it has been a very hard field within material science to make big breakthroughs in.

So, the greenest current mix of electricity generation, as measured by particulate and carbon emissions, is to use wind and solar wherever economical, and then support that with continuous baseload power from some combination of hydroelectric dams (which has some of its own environmental issues and requires the presence of major rivers) and/or nuclear reactors. Natural gas with carbon capture technology is then the cleanest fossil fuel for extra baseload power or peaking power, while coal is the dirtiest.

If some of the downsides of electric vehicles get solved (e.g. less reliance on rare cobalt, better range, more stable), then gasoline engines could gradually be replaced for a larger market share of electric vehicles, which would cut down on smog emissions, but then there would need to be more electrical grid generation to replace that gasoline energy.

**Nuclear Energy Safety Profile**

The safety of nuclear energy is a lot like the safety of commercial flight. It’s statistically extremely safe, but when it fails, it has a tendency to fail spectacularly.

Many people are afraid of flying, but the probability of dying from commercial airlines is extraordinarily low. Driving is statistically far, far riskier per mile than flying in a commercial airline, but most of us don’t think much about the risks of driving. This is because an airliner crash, although rare, typically kills hundreds of people and is entirely out of our personal control. And then it makes global news for weeks.

On the other hand, countless car accidents occur every day, trickling in over time, causing tens of thousands of fatalities in the U.S. each year and over a million per year worldwide. So, the number of people that have died globally from commercial air travel over the past 50 years is a tiny fraction of the number of people that die every single year from automobiles.

Similarly, multiple studies show that upwards of 100,000+ people die per year in the United States from the effects of air pollution, with a significant portion coming from burning coal for electricity and smog emitted from gasoline-powered cars, which causes particulates in urban air that can exacerbate health conditions, and increase the risk of heart attacks, stroke, cancer, asthma, cognitive disabilities, and more.

> We estimate that anthropogenic PM2.5 was responsible for 107,000 premature deaths in 2011, at a cost to society of $886 billion. Of these deaths, 57% were associated with pollution caused by energy consumption [e.g., transportation (28%) and electricity generation (14%)]; another 15% with pollution caused by agricultural activities. A small fraction of emissions, concentrated in or near densely populated areas, plays an outsized role in damaging human health with the most damaging 10% of total emissions accounting for 40% of total damages. We find that 33% of damages occur within 8 km of emission sources, but 25% occur more than 256 km away, emphasizing the importance of tracking both local and long-range impacts.
>
> –[PNAS Published Paper](https://www.pnas.org/content/116/18/8775), April 2019

Worldwide, the number of deaths from air pollution is estimated to be in the millions, because many developing countries use dangerous heating sources for indoor cooking, causing severe indoor air pollution that can be fatal if mismanaged.

And that’s before carbon dioxide emissions and their longer-term effects on the climate are considered. Here’s the chart of atmospheric levels of carbon dioxide over the past 800,000 years:

![Atmospheric Carbon Dioxide](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-25-carbon-levels.gif)

*Chart Source: [NASA](https://climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/carbon-dioxide/)*

Meanwhile, nuclear energy hasn’t killed very many people, doesn’t emit carbon dioxide, and is statistically very safe compared to how much power it has produced worldwide for decades. But when something goes wrong with a nuclear facility, it \*really\* goes wrong, and makes world news. Kind of like commercial flight.

Nuclear power began being used in the 1950’s and 1960’s, and new reactors were built heavily into the 1970’s and 1980’s during the golden era of nuclear energy.

In 1979, the Three Mile Island meltdown occurred in the United States about one hour from my hometown in Pennsylvania. This was a contained meltdown, ranked 5 out of 7 on the International Nuclear Event Scale. No fatalities occurred, and subsequent studies showed mixed results but that overall, cancer was not really an elevated issue around the location in the subsequent decades.

In 1986, the Chernobyl nuclear power plant in the Soviet Union had a more catastrophic explosive meltdown; a full 7 out of 7 disaster on the international scale. A couple staff died during the explosion, and then dozens of first responders died from acute radiation exposure. From there, estimates vary wildly about how many people died or were severely affected from moderate radiation exposure over a wide area in the following decades, but the high-end estimates are in the thousands. It also caused environmental damage over a wide area, to flora and fauna.

In 2011, the Fukushima Daiichi reactor had a meltdown from an earthquake/tsunami event. Although it was not as bad as Chernobyl, this was also ranked a full 7 out of 7 disaster due to the severity of the meltdown. There was one acute radiation death, several injuries, and as of yet unknown multi-decade consequences from moderate radiation exposure. This event is recently making headlines again due to Japan disposing of radioactive wastewater into the ocean.

So, dozens have directly died from nuclear reactor disasters over the past 50+ years despite being such a big part of global energy production, and when we include estimated cancer deaths or severe health consequences from those disasters, it probably stretches into the thousands. The radiation effects have done severe localized environmental damage to Chernobyl in particular. This is terrible, and yet also just a tiny fraction of the number of people estimated to die every single year from coal and gasoline air pollution.

But, nuclear energy is inherently scarier, less knowable. I wouldn’t be thrilled if I were to hear that they are building a new nuclear facility 10 miles from my home, for example, and yet it would be statistically way safer than having a new coal facility there.

All three of these failed nuclear energy facilities were built during the late 1960’s and/or early 1970’s, even though the actual meltdowns occurred in different decades. After the Three Mile Island meltdown, nuclear energy started to fall out of public favor, and then Chernobyl and Fukushima contributed to that decline.

Still, research has slowly continued in the field. Modern technology can make safer nuclear reactors; more compact and standardized, with more safeguards against severe failure, vs the 1960’s/1970’s technology which is already a statistically safe energy source compared to many alternatives. Public investment plans could build the safest-possible permanent waste disposal sites.

If society wishes to reduce carbon emissions, dangerous smog, and water pollution, especially in population-dense areas that don’t have a lot of economic wind and solar potential relative to the energy needs (notably Asia), nuclear energy remains one of the possibilities.

However, there’s not much will for that in in the West. The United States isn’t really building new nuclear reactors. Germany has been slowly de-commissioning reactors. Developing markets, though, continue to build reactors, and are why this industry is still a mild growth industry.

China and Russia build nuclear reactors for themselves, and for export, due to their infrastructure and energy technology capabilities, including providing financing and operation. They build nuclear facilities for several countries in the Middle East, South America, and for India. This gives Russia and China some degree of influence with the countries they partner with, being responsible for key energy infrastructure.

So, there are hundreds of nuclear reactors in operation around the world, and dozens currently under construction, particularly in Asia.

**Impacts from Reactor Shutdowns**

*Japan*

After the Fukushima disaster, Japan shut down all of its 50+ nuclear reactors nationwide. This was a big deal, because Japan had 30% of its electricity generation from nuclear reactors, and the share from nuclear was increasing. They filled the electricity gap by building a bunch of coal and natural gas facilities, burning fossil fuels instead.

Starting in 2015, after a variety of safety updates, Japan slowly began restarting its nuclear reactor fleet. Over the next five years, from 2015 through today, they are gradually putting reactors back online.

*Germany*

Germany also began phasing out its nuclear reactors after the Fukushima disaster, although not abruptly like Japan. A decade ago, Germany produced over 20% of its grid energy from nuclear energy, and now it’s down to about 10%, with plans to get to zero after 2022.

The headline story is that, over these years, Germany greatly increased its wind energy production and solar energy production, and that began displacing its nuclear power. This is true, and Germany’s build-out of solar and wind energy is great.

However, the untold part of that story is that Germany still generates a lot of its power from coal-burning, and so instead of reducing existing nuclear generation capacity with their new solar and wind growth, they could have reduced coal-burning instead.

The National Bureau of Economic Research “NBER”, which is a well-known nonprofit U.S. research organization that is one of the key organizations for defining U.S. recessions, published a 2019 economic working paper on Germany’s nuclear phase-out. It was written by a PhD candidate and two professors, from the University of California and Carnegie Mellon. Here was their key summary:

> Many countries have phased out nuclear electricity production in response to concerns about nuclear waste and the risk of nuclear accidents. This paper examines the impact of the shutdown of roughly half of the nuclear production capacity in Germany after the Fukushima accident in 2011. We use hourly data on power plant operations and a novel machine learning framework to estimate how plants would have operated differently if the phase-out had not occurred. We find that the lost nuclear electricity production due to the phase-out was replaced primarily by coal-fired production and net electricity imports. The social cost of this shift from nuclear to coal is approximately 12 billion dollars per year. Over 70% of this cost comes from the increased mortality risk associated with exposure to the local air pollution emitted when burning fossil fuels. Even the largest estimates of the reduction in the costs associated with nuclear accident risk and waste disposal due to the phase-out are far smaller than 12 billion dollars.
>
> –[NBER Working Paper 26598](https://www.nber.org/papers/w26598), December 2019

Germany’s decision to phase out nuclear energy instead of coal was economically costly according to their analysis, mainly in the form of increased mortality from urban air pollution.

*California*

In the United States, California’s electrical generation and distribution systems keep making national news. Despite being a wealthy state, and having among the highest electricity costs in the country, they keep having rolling blackouts from insufficient electrical generation. It has been a problem for over two decades, and has been particularly problematic in 2020.

Over the years, California has emphasized renewable sources, and has decommissioned several fossil fuel generation facilities, and decommissioned a nuclear facility, so the share from nuclear energy is decreasing. However, the state now imports a lot of its electricity from other states (including a chunk from imported coal-power generation, and some from nuclear), and finds its electricity to be both costly and unreliable.

That doesn’t mean renewable energy is impossible; it just means that not planning ahead properly can cause major issues and an unreliable grid, and ironically ending up relying just as much on coal or other dirty sources of energy.

From a cost/benefit/environment/safety analysis, nuclear energy makes mathematical sense for a number of countries, despite the disastrous global headlines that it has created three times in the past five decades.

**The Uranium Supply Deficit**

Uranium was heavily mined in the middle of the 20th century for military purposes, causing big stockpiles of uranium in inventory and in weapons.

Since then, large amounts of old nuclear weapons have been de-commissioned, and this secondary (previously-mined) supply of uranium has been used for nuclear energy to supplement new primary mine production, which is insufficient to cover current utility demand.

![Uranium Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-25-uranium-production.jpg)

*Chart Source: Mining.com*

The price of uranium had a big bull run in the 1970’s as nuclear reactors became popular, but more primary and secondary supply came online and fixed that problem, so the price became stable in the 1980’s and 1990’s.

Then, uranium had a second big bull run in the 2000’s, which eventually led to the price going up more than 10x. More supply came online, which pushed the price down. Then, the Fukushima disaster happened in 2011, and Japan took all of its nuclear reactors offline nationwide, which reduced demand and led to oversupply conditions.

Japan slowly began re-starting reactors, and China and other countries began building new reactors, so demand steadily picked back up.

Uranium consequently was in a bear market from its parabolic peak in 2007, all the way to late 2016, where it found a bottom and started moving back up.

![Uranium Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-25-uranium-price.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Since late 2016, Uranium has been in a mild bull market, with higher highs and higher lows, steadily climbing over time. However, its inflation-adjusted price remains below where it was in the 1980’s and 1990’s, and it is currently well below the cost of most new sources of production.

Currently, nuclear reactors use more uranium than the amount of uranium that is mined each year, and have been for a while. In 2019, 169 million pounds were used, and 139 million pounds were produced, according to Yellow Cake’s October 2020 presentation, citing the research firm UxC. Utility demand in 2020 is estimated to be equal or slightly higher than 2019, while production was lower due to shutdowns related to COVID-19.

So, secondary sources of uranium, from old inventories, are used to fill these annual gaps between mined supply and utility demand, and have been for years. However, by most industry estimates, this secondary uranium supply will become insufficient to fill the gap between production and demand as we head deeper into the 2020’s.

To be conservative, I wouldn’t focus on the upper case demand in this chart; just the reference case demand:

![Deep Yellow Presentation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-25-deep-yellow-supply-demand.png)

*Chart Source: Deep Yellow September 2020 Presentation*

So, by the mid-2020’s, the uranium price most likely has to rise enough to bring online the idled production capacity and then eventually new production capacity.

Many industry estimates place the price required to profitably bring mines into production is upwards of $45-$50/lb. Some of the higher-cost sources of production, for when the expected supply gap grows into the deeper 2020’s, are more like $75/lb.

And those are at today’s dollars. If there is a notable degree of inflation or currency debasement in the next 5-10 years, that can push the future nominal price up further.

The uranium futures market is rather illiquid. Instead, the vast majority of nuclear utility uranium pricing comes from multi-year private contracts with uranium producers, where they agree on a long-term price structure. For each year that goes by, it results in more of these older contracts expiring. Utilities then have to come to market and buy more multi-year uranium contracts.

Nearly 2/3rds of contracted supply coverage for uranium demand by U.S. utility companies expires over the next 5 years, which means utilities will need to come to market and buy.

![Uranium Coverage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-25-coverage-rates.png)

*Chart Source: Yellow Cake October 2020 Presentation*

Currently, the uranium spot price is $29-$30, while the average industry long-term contract price from legacy contracts (where most of the purchase volume is actually happening) is around $35.

Uranium spot prices shot up to $34 this summer when several mines closed due to COVID-19, but since then it has cooled off to $29-$30.

Some large miners, like Cameco, have actually shut down uranium mines over the past few years, and instead began buying uranium from secondary sources at current low spot prices, which they then re-sell to their utility customers at their higher long-term locked-in contract prices. That’s pretty funny, and smart. It makes use of the price differential between their existing contracts and the lower spot price by buying and re-selling secondary uranium preserves their in-ground uranium reserves, and cuts down on existing secondary supplies faster, which brings forward the next bull market.

With uranium holding companies, investors can currently buy physical uranium exposure for a little under $24/lb, due to the fact that they trade at approximately 20% discounts to NAV.

If uranium is $50 by the end of 2025, that’s about a 15% annualized price increase from the purchasable price of $24 now.

If the price reaches $50 by the end of 2023 instead, that would be over 25% annualized gains from now. If it takes until the end of 2027 to reach $50, that would be about 10% annualized gains from now.

But even if it plays out somewhere in those scenarios, it’ll likely be volatile along the way. There could very well be a dead year at some point, as investors just wait.

And then there are the more bullish scenarios where uranium goes far higher, to $75/lb or more. Utility demand is relatively inelastic and a small part of overall nuclear generation costs, so if at any point there is an acute supply shortfall, the price can go vertical, as it has twice in the past.

However, as described in the risk section later, there are some tail risks that could prevent uranium’s almost-inevitable price rise through the 2020’s.

**Ways to Play Uranium**

There are various ways to get exposure to a uranium bull run, if it should occur. However, it’s a small market after a decade-long bear situation, so liquidity can be a problem for large funds while it remains so beaten-down.

Most directly, there are two corporations that simply hold physical uranium. They serve as a sort of physical uranium closed-end fund in that sense, since their sole purpose is to be holding companies for physical uranium. The older/bigger/Canadian one is called Uranium Participation Corp (TSE:U, OTC:URPTF), and the newer/smaller/UK one is called Yellow Cake (LSE:YCA, OTC:YLLXF).

These both have smallish market caps, in the hundreds of millions of dollars range, so trading liquidity can be limited, especially for Yellow Cake.

And then there are big miners, like Cameco (CCJ). This one has a market cap of well over $3 billion, trades on the NYSE, and so has a decent amount of trading liquidly. Another big miner, Kazatomprom, is mostly state-owned by Kazakhstan and doesn’t trade on a major U.S. stock exchange.

Next, there are junior miners, like Deep Yellow (ASX:DYL, OTC:DYLLF). These tend to be sub-$100 million microcap stocks without much liquidity. They’re extraordinarily risky, but have explosive upside potential. Deep Yellow has contrarian financing from places like Sprott Inc., and is using the fact that the uranium mining industry is in shambles to go around and hunt for acquisitions to assemble into a larger company.

And lastly, there’s the North Shore Global Uranium Mining ETF (URNM). Kazatomprom and Cameco collectively represent about 30% of the fund, Uranium Participation Corp and Yellow Cake represent a bit under 20%, and the remaining half consists of smaller miners.

There is also the Global X Uranium ETF (URA), which I don’t like much. It is diluted with some equipment manufacturers, conglomerates, and other non-pureplay uranium companies.

**Uranium Participation Corp**

I like the long-term risk/reward ratio of the uranium holding companies here.

Uranium Participation Corp trades on the Toronto Stock Exchange with the ticker U, in Canadian dollars. It also trades over-the-counter with ticker URPTF, in American dollars. The market capitalization is currently over $500 million, and if there is sufficient investor demand, they can increase their market cap by issuing more shares to buy more uranium.

Their share price should rationally follow their net asset value “NAV” of uranium and their working capital. However, in practice, their share price sometimes trades at a premium to NAV, and sometimes trades at a discount to NAV:

![Uranium Participation Corp Discount](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-25-uranium-corp-discount.png)

*Chart Source: Uranium Participation Corp July 2020 Presentation*

They publish their NAV each month, and as of the beginning of October, it was worth $5.13 CAD. The price of uranium hasn’t changed much through October, so the NAV is still basically the same.

Compared to the current share price on the TSE ($3.98 CAD), it’s currently at about a 20% discount to NAV. So, although spot uranium is $29-$30/lb, investors that buy into Uranium Participation Corp (or the other holding company, Yellow Cake), are basically buying uranium for under $24/lb.

Now, it’s quite possible that the uranium price could fall in the months ahead. So, rather than the share price catching up with NAV, the NAV can catch down with the share price. However, the long-term supply/demand story for uranium looks rather good, and the $23-$26 region has a decent support level.

Basically, shares of Uranium Participation Corp are already priced in such a way that they assume that uranium will continue to fall down to red support areas. The price of uranium would have to go below that region for the NAV of Uranium Participation Corp to go below the current share price.

These holding companies can eventually force their share price up towards NAV if they want to. To do this, they can sell some physical uranium, and use the proceeds to buy back their own stock. However, that likely won’t be necessary; whenever uranium finds a local bottom and has a serious upward move in the years ahead, the prices of these holding companies should do fine relative to the price of uranium over time, and could trade at a premium to NAV once again.

In the meantime, the discount gives investors a 20% fundamental buffer against uranium price declines, in case the uranium price retraces from this entire 2020 bullish move back to support levels.

**Uranium Risks**

The primary benefit of uranium for a portfolio, in addition to probably offering good 5-10 year returns from current depressed levels, is that it’s very uncorrelated to other assets.

Uranium will follow where supply and demand lead, and it’s not very dependent on the macroeconomic landscape. This is because electrical generation is not very economically sensitive or policy sensitive or cyclical, whereas copper demand, oil demand, and gold demand are. So, uranium can go up in an environment even if other things go down.

However, uranium can also go down when other things go up, if the industry faces some problems. Investors should keep these risks in mind.

*4th Nuclear Facility Meltdown*

If there is a reactor meltdown in the 2020’s, policymakers in the United States or France or another country with a large nuclear reactor fleet could force the de-activation of reactors, either abruptly like Japan, or gradually like Germany.

This is the biggest tail risk that could outright kill or delay the next uranium bull market, and should be considered when choosing a position size.

To hedge against that possibility, investors can own silver (for solar panels), and fossil fuel producers/transporters (particularly natural gas and/or coal), because that electrical supply gap from nuclear power going offline would need to be filled quickly.

If a couple reactors unexpectedly go offline for maintenance reasons, it wouldn’t have a big impact on the global supply/demand gap for uranium. This is because there are hundreds of operating reactors, and dozens currently in construction, so any one reactor is a fraction of a percent of industry demand. However, a major meltdown that convinces a large country to shut off its whole nuclear fleet, would be enough to throw off current supply/demand deficit estimates over the next several years.

*Technology Breakthroughs*

Most of the estimated supply/demand gap through 2025 is based on operating and under-construction reactors. So, these reactors are unlikely to be influenced by technological changes or competition. There are also some planned reactors in the pipeline, but those are never certain until they begin.

However, the further out we look towards 2030 and beyond, the more possibility there is for industry disruption that could reduce forward plans for new nuclear reactors in developing markets. Better wind and solar technologies, and better batteries for electrical storage, could accelerate the adoption of solar and wind energy even in places where it is less suitable.

There is a plan called the “Sun Cable” to build an absolutely massive solar array in northern Australia (which is particularly suitable for solar), and then run a 2,000+ mile undersea cable to Singapore, to export solar energy from Australia to Singapore. It would cost tens of billions of dollars and take years to complete (by the late 2020’s) but would last for decades. While this is a cool idea (with my electrical engineering background, I love electrical mega-projects like that), it would only power part of Singapore and would require 50 square miles of Australian desert and a massive infrastructure under-taking, so this type of project would be hard to repeat for China and India’s massive energy needs.

*Incorrect Research Estimates*

Uranium markets are somewhat opaque, and estimates about secondary supply could be off by a meaningful margin. Many of the estimates are provided by industry research firms, as well as work done by independent investors.

If there are more stockpiles than currently known, or those stockpiles are loosely held, it could push back the acute uranium shortage that is likely coming as we head into the 2020’s. On the other hand, if there are less stockpiles than currently known, or they are tightly held for strategic reasons, it could accelerate the acute uranium shortage. When it comes to this likely uranium bull market, patience or trend-following are key.

Japanese utilities in particular have a lot of uranium inventory, due to a long period of not using uranium as reactors were shut off and restarted slowly over time. The rate of Japanese reactor restarts will have some impact on overall uranium demand and Japanese utility company decisions about what to do with their existing inventory. This has been a small source of secondary supply on the market, but mostly they are holding their inventory tight and are still signing foreign supply deals.

And this is why it’s impossible to be precise about when the uranium shortage becomes acute. Instead, it’s one of those uncorrelated things to stick in a back of a portfolio and see how it performs over 5+ years, or monitor it and use a trend-following system.

*Counterparty/Operation Risk*

Although the holding companies are relatively low risk, there is still technically some counterparty risk for them to buy and hold uranium. Plus, holding companies charge about 0.8-0.9% per year in storage and management fees, although this is much less important when they trade at a big discount to NAV.

The mining companies have higher risk, because they have more operating leverage relative to the price of uranium. Disruptions can reduce their operations, and cut off their cash flows. If the uranium price stays low for longer than expected, it puts more pressure on some of these companies.

Many of the smaller miners don’t have debt, but they would need to issue more equity to stay in operation/development/exploration, and thus dilute their shareholders.

## Summary

The macro picture remains very unclear, with a gradual rebound in sales volumes and jobs, but still a large degree of economic pain as unemployment remains high. After a sharp rebound in Q3 GDP numbers, to be reported shortly, consensus GDP estimates for Q4 2020 keep coming down as they begin factoring out stimulus.

Share prices will fluctuate in the months ahead. However, by emphasizing quality and looking through some beaten-down areas, we can generally protect a portfolio from whatever happens in the next few months in terms of actual fundamentals. Plus, international diversification somewhat reduces exposure to the stimulus-or-no-stimulus question in the United States. China and Japan, for example, are on their own stimulus schedule.

With 10-year Treasury notes offering yields below 1% (and below the current prevailing inflation rate), buying exposure to uranium at a 20% discount to spot prices and below the cost of new production, while it’s already in a 4-year gradual bull market, and with expected supply deficits by the mid-2020’s that should eventually push the price north of $50 to bring idled and new production online, seems like a smart risk/reward asset to have exposure to.

There’s not a high opportunity cost for buying scarce assets with good forward fundamentals, in other words.

Although not without risk, the risks for uranium are relatively uncorrelated to other portfolio risks, so a small portfolio exposure to uranium holding companies  for the next several years can potentially improve the risk-adjusted returns of a diversified portfolio.
