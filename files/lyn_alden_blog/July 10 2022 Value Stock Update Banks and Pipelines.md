*July 10, 2022*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

## Macro View

The US economy continues to weaken. Leading indicators are pointing to levels that are historically associated with recessions or near-recessions:

![Weekly Leading Indicator](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-wli.png)

*Chart Source: [Advisor Perspectives](https://www.advisorperspectives.com/dshort/updates/2022/07/01/ecri-weekly-leading-index-update)*

Atlanta Fed’s aggregate indicator is pointing towards a negative real GDP print in Q2, which we just finished up but was not reported yet since it takes time to compile the data.

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-gdp-now.png)

*Chart Source: Atlanta Fed*

We already had a negative real GDP print in Q1, so if this model ends up being roughly correct, it will mark two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth.

The one pillar of the economy that continues to hold up decently is the labor market. However, this does tend to be a lagging indicator; we’re often firmly in a recession by the time it has noticeably rolled over, especially after revisions to the data are done later.

Here’s the total nonfarm payroll numbers, represented as % off high:

![Payroll Drawdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-payroll-drawdowns.png)

If I zoom in on the 1970s, which was the last time that we had inflation levels this high, we can see that payrolls often didn’t start falling until well into a recession:

![Payrolls in the 1970s](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-payrolls-1970s.png)

Here’s the employment to population ratio of 25-54 year olds in the past four decades or so:

![Employment to Population](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-employment-population.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Additionally, we need to look at real wages. Although we’ve seen faster-than-normal wage growth over the past year, it has been below the average rise in inflation. Due to changes in the CPI formula since the 1970s, this probably understates the depth of the problem, meaning we’re probably even more negative on this chart at the moment in terms of wages vs the real cost of living:

![CPI Wage Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-cpi-wage-growth.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

People were miserable during inflationary spikes in the 1970s because wages were slower and stickier to move up than price levels, and so they could buy less stuff with their wages. That’s also why economic activity was worse than the unemployment numbers alone suggested; even employed people were getting a trim in their ability to buy real goods and services, especially necessary ones like rent, energy, and nutrient-dense food. That’s happening again now.

If we look globally, the world as a whole is at borderline recession levels. [CrossBorder Capital](https://twitter.com/crossbordercap) does great research on cycles and liquidity in particular, and has been showing near-record low levels of global liquidity lately.

![Crossborder Capital Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-crossborder-capital-tweets.png)

**Dollar Strength and Treasury Troubles**

The dollar has been rather strong in recent months, as the Fed tightens monetary policy more-so than some of its developed market peers. The dollar index, which is very heavily weighted towards the euro and the yen, has broken out to multi-decade highs:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-dxy.png)

However, the sheer scale of that recent rally has more to do with euro and yen weakness than it has to do with dollar strength. For example, the US dollar vs the Canadian dollar has been strengthening a bit from a low base, but with nothing particularly remarkable going on:

![USD vs CAD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-usdcad.png)

The US dollar vs the Brazilian real, the Chinese yuan, or the Australian dollar, is showing a bout of strength in 2022 but nothing really notable there either. The notable aspect of the move is mainly against the euro and yen.

Europe is facing a crippling energy crisis that is worse than the rest of the developed world, and Japan is capping long-duration bond yields artificially low at 0.25%. This has given the USDEUR and the USDJPY currency pairs a huge chance to rally in USD’s favor. There are also of course some specific emerging or frontier market countries having currency problems, such as Sri Lanka, Argentina, Turkey, and so forth.

To a lot of people, this probably sounds like a good thing for the United States. Our dollar is strengthening, which is great for us, right?

Well, not really, as I’ve covered before. When the dollar strengthens notably, the foreign sector tends to get pretty squeezed, because they buy dollar-based commodities and in many cases have dollar-denominated debts. However, they also have tens of trillions of dollars worth of US financial assets, which they accumulated over decades from structural trade surpluses against the US. The US has a deeply negative net international investment position, which we dug into from decades of structural trade deficits with the rest of the world. When the dollar strengthens markedly, the foreign sector in aggregate tends to stop buying and even start trimming their dollar assets, which can render US financial markets illiquid.

This chart shows the portion of US Treasuries that the foreign sector holds in custody at the US Federal Reserve. It represents around 40% of total foreign Treasury holdings, but unlike the full dataset that gets updated monthly with a two-month lag, this dataset is updated weekly. And as it shows, when the dollar strengthens, the foreign sector tends to trim its Treasury exposure:

![USD vs UST](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-USD-vs-UST.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

With the foreign sector currently on a US Treasury buying strike, along with other factors, the US Treasury market is getting rather volatile and illiquid. The MOVE index, which is a measure of US Treasury market volatility, has crept up to decade highs:

![Move Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-move-index.png)

*Chart Source: [Trading View](https://www.tradingview.com/symbols/TVC-MOVE/)*

One thing that the US Treasury Department currently has going for it is that they don’t intend to issue a lot of net Treasuries through September 2022. Their cash reserves are pretty high, and the timing of tax revenues and government expenditures is in their favor during Q2 and Q3 of this year.

However, all of this destruction in asset prices and economic deceleration will likely start weighing on tax revenue, and by Q4 of this year or during the first half of next year, Treasury issuance will likely pick up. This will be when the Treasury is more vulnerable to liquidity problems and lack of foreign buyers, if these types of conditions are still present by that point.

While it’s less directly correlated, the chart of high-yield spreads also shows that a rising dollar tends to coincide with financial stress in US markets. The yields of non-investment grade corporate bonds tend to rise significantly above safe Treasury yields during times of economic deceleration and financial stress, which then puts further financial stress on those types of weaker companies. This usually triggers some sort of policy response eventually:

![High Yield Spreads](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-high-yield-spread.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The US Treasury market and US credit markets are not seized up yet, but they’re getting somewhat shaky in terms of liquidity, yields, and so forth. If the stock market falls, the Fed doesn’t really need to do anything for a while. If credit markets seize up, that generally forces a Fed response within weeks. If the Treasury market seizes up, that generally forces a Fed response within days, since that represents totally broken market functioning at the core of the system.

Markets are starting to price in Fed rate cuts for 2023. Basically, markets now expect the Fed to keep tightening through 2022 but then to begin turning more dovish by next calendar year as economic conditions deteriorate. Given what’s happening with recession indicators, Treasury illiquidity, widening high yield spreads, and other factors, I do think this represents a reasonable base case. The Fed will probably perform significant rate hikes during the July and September FOMC meetings, but the further we go beyond that point, the harder it will be for the Fed to keep tightening policy at an aggressive pace.

The Fed also has other tools that they might end up using by next year if liquidity in the Treasury market doesn’t improve, including supplementary leverage ratio adjustments for commercial banks, which would basically allow them to buy more Treasuries and help re-liquify that market.

One thing I’m watching closely is to see if the 10-year Treasury yield turns into a head-and-shoulders pattern and rolls over, or if it breaks out higher from here:

![10 Year Treasury YIeld](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-ust-10yr.png)

So far it has made higher highs and higher lows. A lower low would be a pretty significant signal. My operating assumption is that this almost 3.5% level was a significant cyclical top in yields. However, I want to see if the market confirms that, since it’s still not assured. Especially if the Fed lets the Treasury market get disorderly, we could see a spike as high as 4% or more.

On one hand, a big chunk of the physical goods inflation seems to be behind us for the moment, and economic conditions are deteriorating. This should push the yield lower. On the other hand, the yield is still well below current inflation levels, energy prices remain the wildcard that under certain scenarios (China fully re-opening, for example) could result in even higher inflation this summer, and the foreign sector isn’t buying Treasuries.

**Europe’s Vulnerability**

Looking globally, I’m less concerned about Japan’s financial and energy situation than I am about Europe’s.

Japan has a lot of problems, but they also have a productive industrial economy, positive net international investment position, and foreign exchange reserves that are equal to about ten years’ worth of the country’s annual oil consumption at current prices.

On the other hand, I don’t think many people fully grasp how much geopolitical leverage Russia has over Europe at the moment, and how dangerous it is if for one reason or another Russia decides to fully exert that leverage.

First of all, Europe’s gas prices are already insane:

![Andreas Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-andreas-tweet.png)

*Chart Source: [@AndreasSteno](https://twitter.com/AndreasSteno/status/1545403397972566018)*

As the chart shows, US natural gas prices are currently 2.5x as high as they were at the start of 2020, while Europe’s natural gas prices are 15x as high as they were at the start of 2020. Even if we ignore the temporary spikes, Europe’s natural gas prices have been hanging around 7.5x what they were at the start of 2020.

Europe’s gas prices don’t have to get worse for this to be untenable. Even staying at the low end of their recent range is untenable. Over time, they have to get more LNG import capacity online, with corresponding LNG export capacity elsewhere, or fall back on more coal burning, or build out some new nuclear reactors, or something. Germany’s structural trade deficit for decades has just evaporated down to nothing, since their energy bills are now so high. And notably, this has been a problem since late 2021, before the war. The war added additional uncertainty to the mix.

Many people point out, correctly, that if Russia were to stop exporting gas to Europe, it would severely harm Russia as well as Europe. Russia is already severely hurting from import restrictions, so maintaining export cash flows is important to them. Russia and Europe in that sense are currently co-dependent on each other, Europe for energy imports and Russia for export cash flows, with both sides now eventually looking to build the infrastructure needed to sell or buy their natural gas respectively from other sources in the coming years.

However, not all times are created equal. Suppose that Russia specifically shuts off natural gas to Germany and Poland for just the month of January, in the dead of winter. That alone wouldn’t impact Russian finances too much, but how would Germany and Poland fare with that happening to them during freezing temperatures?

While natural gas prices remain so high, Europe’s economic competitiveness is declining, Russia is raking in record levels of export cash flows, and Russia maintains significant geopolitical leverage over the central/eastern portion of Europe, during a time when Europe is holding a lot of frozen Russian sovereign reserves due to Russia’s attack on Ukraine.

This remains an evolving situation, with a lot of uncertainty and tail risks in the months and years ahead. I continue to view the 2020s decade as an energy and commodity oriented decade, which is not good for many countries that are structural importers of those things.

## Digital Assets Note

For much of this calendar year so far, I have been cautioning about unclear bitcoin price action over the immediate term due to its historical weakness during economic decelerating environment and tightening liquidity conditions, while still re-iterating my long-term structurally bullish view on the asset. It’s always challenging to manage differing views of the same asset over differing timeframes.

In April, I started warning about a more specific scenario where Terra/Luna potentially encounters a death spiral and is forced to liquidate its bitcoin into a weak market. That ended up happening in May, representing a significant capitulation down to a bit under $30k in price.

However, that Terra/Luna collapse then led to contagion at Three Arrows Capital and some other parts of the ecosystem during the subsequent month, resulting in a second and more opaque capitulation. Three Arrows Capital went bankrupt in June, and a number of lenders liquidated bitcoin-denominated loans to them, and to other entities as well. This resulted in a bitcoin price capitulation to below $20k (including a brief drop during the worst of it to around $17.5k), which was below my base case and not something that was specifically on my radar.

Due to low prices, a significant percentage of bitcoin miners have had to start selling some of their bitcoin to pay their expenses. This has happened during a number of historical capitulations as well. When this occurs, the difficulty adjustment automatically decreases to regain an equilibrium.

Now that this has all occurred, and sentiment is totally washed out, I’m getting pretty bullish again. I still think there is a lot of uncertainty over the next 6 months due to the troubling macro backdrop, but bitcoin is now in a more convincing deep value zone. I’m happy to be a buyer from leveraged whales that are getting liquidated and forced-selling their bitcoin into a weak market.

That doesn’t mean it can’t go lower; there are still scenarios that could liquidate even more major holders. However, the risk/reward ratio is looking very appealing to me here with a 3-5 year view.

Bitcoin is below its on-chain cost basis for the fifth time in its history:

![BTC Realized Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-btc-realized-price.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/realized-price/)*

There has been a spike in the number of smallish holders moving their holdings to cold storage, possibly due to fear of various deposit/lending companies having to halt withdrawals or possibly for other reasons. This chart shows the number of on-chain addresses with at least 0.1 BTC (which means addresses with over $2,000 USD at current prices):

![BTC Addresses](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-btc-addresses.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

Addresses with at least 0.01 BTC increased by about a million in the first half of this year, which is more than 10% higher than the year-end 2021 figure. Addresses with at least 0.1 BTC increased by about 400,000 during the first half of this year, which is also more than a 10% gain. Addresses with at least 1 BTC increased by about 60,000 which is just under a 10% gain.

Notably, the number of addresses with 10+ or 100+ BTC is not at record highs. It’s those smaller sub-10 BTC addresses that are spiking the most. And we have to think globally; how many countries are experiencing currency devaluations, where a small but meaningful percentage of the population may want to hold things like USD stablecoins or bitcoins as part of their holdings? Many people in developing markets can’t easily access things like the S&P 500 or other developed market equities, but they have a smart phone and can buy cryptos.

I spoke with the CEO of Coinkite on Friday, which is a small private company that among other products makes the Coldcard hardware wallet (regarded as a top-of-the-line model, favored by techies in particular). He said that his company experienced somewhat of a spike in Coldcard sales, which is not something they normally see during this type of price weakness. So, that anecdote seems to validate this recent surge in on-chain addresses that I’m seeing in the data.

Separately, the quality of bitcoin-based startups I’m seeing in my [venture capital advisory work](https://egodeath.capital/) is promising. There is plenty of development happening on the Lightning network and elsewhere in the ecosystem that makes me bullish for the next cycle, once the market gets through the next several months of ongoing macro turbulence.

One potential negative catalyst that is getting some headlines is that around 142,000 bitcoins from the 2014 Mt Gox exchange collapse are expected to be released to long-waiting creditors during the second half of this year. It remains unclear what percentage of these many people will decide to sell their holdings. However, even if we suppose they sell half or three quarters of it over the course of months, it would only represent a relatively small percentage of daily trading volume. Nonetheless, this is something to be aware of, and alongside other factors could represent a mild downward variable on price.

I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into bitcoin as part of an asset mix to be the best method of accumulation for most people who want some exposure. Adding an extra couple tranches into it when the price is this beaten down probably isn’t a bad idea either, assuming one is managing their overall position size appropriately and is bullish on the asset.

Swan.com recently released their [July Private Insight newsletter](https://drive.google.com/file/d/19BE5jcOV3CVxoQhz9ZYYASscPp7srXI_/view?_kx=v_mQQkqcP5blZCf0bzf4nxpQUIBr0IGyP9LROajIfo-l-00bdDVdqFYYVOvzeAA1.KzLKES), which is for their HNW clients. I’m able to share it with my private audience as well but please do not share the link publicly.

Many altcoins will probably get a bounce if bitcoin starts to form a price bottom, but I would advise significant caution regarding exposure to them for most people. I still think there is a lot of speculative malinvestment throughout the ecosystem that needs to bleed out over time, even when bitcoin starts to find its footing again at some point.

For the No Limits portfolio, I’m holding the GBTC position at current levels, but am willing to buy an additional final tranche if we continue to have even more weakness later this year.

## Value Stock Update

The financial sector, which is generally a core part of the “value factor” has a had a rough year so far. With economic deceleration occurring, investors don’t want much exposure to companies that have a lot of risk associated with various types of borrowers, which is understandable.

The energy sector is also normally associated with the value factor and has been a top performer year-to-date in 2022, but has been an underperformer over the past month. Nonetheless, I continue to view energy producers, and more cautiously energy transporters, as attractive long-term holdings.

The healthcare sector, which tends to be considered “defensive value” has been among the better sectors year-to-date, since it held up in a flat pattern while the most other parts of the stock market went down. There are some companies in this sector that I continue to be bullish on, while there are others that I am willing to harvest some gains from and invest in more beaten-down value sectors during the second half of this calendar year.

I’m going to use the stock section in this report to do an overview of a number of financial end energy transport companies and re-iterate some of my longer-run bullish views on them, rather than to dive too deeply into any one particular company.

**Bank of America**

Bank of America (BAC) and the financial sector in general have experienced a deep decline lately. However, at these levels I’m once again getting rather bullish on BAC, and the sector as a whole.

BAC, among many other major banks, had their capital eviscerated during the global financial crisis, had to issue a ton of new shares, and start building their share price back from scratch:

![BAC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-bac-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

If we look back at my favorite chart from last year, the kind of event that sunk BAC in 2008 was the initial unwinding of something big. Very big:

![Long-Term Debt Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-longterm-debt-cycle.png)

That 2008 event, similar in scale to the 1929 event, was a massive private debt bubble popping into a disinflationary bust. A lot of investors today are fighting the last battle as they analyze recession risks; they are concerned with a big banking insolvency event and disinflationary bust.

While that’s an understandable concern, I think we’re beyond that point, and more in a stagflationary type of environment now. I’m bearish on many things, but we have to be specific about what we’re bearish about. For example, I’m bearish in inflation-adjusted terms on overvalued stocks with extensive global supply chains and inflationary pressures on their margins.

US banks in general currently have a higher-than-normal share of their total assets allocated into cash and Treasuries. This is partly from regulations, and partly from more conservative lending decisions in general. This chart shows the ratio of total large bank assets held in cash, Treasury/Agency securities:

![Bank Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-bank-assets.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Going into the 2008 crisis, large US banks had under 15% of their assets in cash and Treasuries, while the other 85% was in securities and loans that have credit risk. Fast forward to today, banks have over 35% of their assets in cash and Treasuries, and under 65% in securities and loans with credit risk. And for that loan and security book, there is less exposure to subprime lenders than there was during the prior crisis.

Most analysts prefer to look at capital ratios, but due to regulations, capital ratios almost always look reasonable, even before crises like 2008. Instead, I prefer to look at the total asset book, and see the spectrum of risk they are taking across their assets.

The US and the world are in bad economic shape generally, but the problems are not emanating from the banking system this time. Banks can get caught up in changes involving net interest margins, fluctuating equity valuations on their stocks, changing housing cycles, and all sorts of things, but they’re built to weather a recession now, unlike 2008 when they were at generationally vulnerable levels and were the core of the problem.

BAC is trading at very low valuations, and I think it has plenty of price chop ahead of it, but I’m a long-term buyer at current levels and willing to start gradually dialing up my allocation a bit here.

**T Rowe Price**

T Rowe Price (TROW) is a large asset manager that has managed to remain relevant with good fund returns even in the age of ETFs and alternative assets.

Like many asset manager stocks, their share price just got crushed during this broad sell-off, but now it is back down to attractive levels.

![TROW FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-trow-fastgraph.png)

![TROW Presentation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-trow-presentation.png)

Its share price is back down to late-2017 price levels despite significant EPS and dividend growth since then, and I’m willing to be a long-term buyer here.

**Enterprise Products Partners**

The energy sector just had a sharp sell-off, but that let some steam out of the space that otherwise still has great fundamentals.

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) is a pipeline partnership that, unlike most of its peers, managed to never blow up during the periods of severe turbulence in the energy sector over the past decade. They have 25 consecutive years of distribution increases since they became public, and currently yield well over 7%.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-7-10-epd-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Enterprise is growing in part due to energy exports out of the United States, which the rest of the world needs for the foreseeable future and is willing to pay top dollar for. Enterprise is involved with the transportation of crude oil, petrochemicals, natural gas, and especially natural gas liquids.

For a high-yielder like this, it’s the total return that matters. With over 7% yields and then some slow distribution growth, even if the valuation remains rangebound, total returns during a 5+ year period should be decent. The pipeline industry more broadly has been crushed down from high valuations and deleveraging, and now it’s pretty cheap and lean.

## Final Thoughts

Many people are rightly worrying about whether the US will have a recession or not, or if the US is already in one. For investors, I think this is a good time to focus on profitable value-oriented companies.

Sentiment is extremely bearish, and there are plenty of things to be concerned about. For example, I think the earnings side of many S&P 500 companies will be weak over the next year. Many sellers of discretionary goods will likely have cost pressures that they’re not able to easily pass onto consumers. The global supply chain situation, and the high cost of moving their supply chains to the extent that it’s necessary to do so in some cases, will be a problem for a while.

However, I think there are many good companies at attractive valuations now, that are already priced in such a way that earnings can go down a bit over the next year. Recessions during periods of structural inflation tend to be rather different than recessions during normal disinflationary periods.

Specifically, I continue to be bullish with a 3-5 year view on the energy sector, but would be cautious during brief overbought periods as they come. And while I’m less bullish on the financial sector as a whole, whenever it becomes oversold, I’m willing to pick up shares of some of the higher-quality institutions.

I use a dial-in and dial-out approach for many of my allocations. While I still remain somewhat defensive going into the second half of this year, with allocations to cash and Treasuries and gold in each of my portfolios, I’m also looking to cautiously lean into oversold opportunities as they come.
