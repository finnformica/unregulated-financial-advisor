## Macro View

**US Economic Update**

Economic indicators here in Q2 are currently running pretty hot:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-atlanta-fed-gdpnow.png)

I previously warned that Q2 would be a noisy quarter because in some ways it’s the opposite of the overly-negative Q1. So far, that’s happening and probably even more than I expected.

Back in Q1, many companies front-ran tariffs and imported extra volumes of things, which worsened the trade deficit, and that negatively impacted GDP due to the way it’s calculated. This showed up directly in the deeply negative “net exports” subcomponent of the Atlanta Fed GDPNow calculator:

![Q1 GDP Subcomponents](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-q1-subcomponents.png)

Here in Q2, inventories from all that importing are high, and so now companies are *under-importing* things. This showed up directly in the now greatly positive “net exports” subcomponent of the Atlanta Fed GDPNow calculator:

![Q2 GDP Subcomponents](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-q2-subcomponents.png)

Thus, Q1 wasn’t quite as bad as the numbers suggest, and Q2 isn’t quite as good as the numbers suggest, and instead there’s some degree of noise.

Overall, tariffs are a headwind, the ongoing large fiscal deficits are a tailwind, and so in some ways the economy is like being in a car where the driver presses the gas pedal and the brake at the same time.

The key thing is that under the surface of that semi-neutrality are winners and losers. Some companies/industries are on the receiving side of those deficits more than others. Likewise, some companies/industries are are on the hurting side of those tariffs more than others. Thus, we’ll want to keep this in mind for investment selections. Ideally, we want to position as being on the receiving side of ongoing deficits, while minimizing tariff harm where possible.

The recent nonfarm payroll report came in above consensus expectations, but overall labor activity does continue to soften (albeit very slowly). Pictet Wealth Management’s head of macroeconomic research, [Frederik Ducrozet](https://x.com/fwred), depicted it well:

**US Budget Bill and Trump/Musk Feud**

The biggest macro topic to watch currently is whether the Republican budget bill will pass in something akin to its current form or not. I suspect it will, although I wouldn’t begin to guess the precise timeframe or how much (if any) tweaking it’ll take.

Here was my summary from two weeks ago:

> Last week, the House of Representatives narrowly passed a budget package that extends many tax cuts, adds to some of them, trims some other areas, and raises the debt ceiling by $4 trillion. Among other things, the bill would have the following effects:
>
> * Extend the income tax cuts from Trump’s first term.
> * Increase the deduction limit for state and local tax (SALT).
> * Increase the childcare tax credit.
> * Eliminate taxes on tips and overtime pay.
> * Boost military spending and direct additional funds towards deportation efforts.
> * Tighten funding and increase requirements for Medicaid and food stamps.
> * Tighten student loan assistance.
> * Eliminate tax incentives for EVs and solar/wind energy.
> * Increase the debt ceiling by $4 trillion.
>
> The net result is an increase in the expected cumulative deficit over the next decade. The Wall Street Journal [estimates](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/what-is-in-republican-tax-bill-39809182) it’ll result in a $2.75 trillion cumulative deficit increase, while [other sources](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/gop-tax-bill-reconcilation-deficit-moodys-debt-spending/) point toward between $3 trillion and $4 trillion. This amounts to some hundreds of billions of dollars per year, which may partially be offset by new tariff revenue.
>
> President Trump has urged the bill to be passed, but it has to go through the Senate first, and its fate there is unclear.
>
> -May 25, 2025 premium report

There are some notable developments since then.

-The first development is that the bill is facing some opposition in the Senate, which was fully expected but now we have more color. President Trump supports the bill and his party has a slight majority in the Senate. Democrats mostly don’t support the bill due to certain cuts it makes (e.g. Medicaid, food stamps, solar/wind incentives, etc). A small number of Republicans such as Rand Paul also don’t support the bill in the other direction due to not cutting spending enough and due to raising the debt ceiling so much. As a result, there’s ongoing uncertainty around the timing of the bill’s passage or what sorts of concessions might need to be made to get the bill across the finish line.

-The second and more notable development is that Elon Musk and President Trump erupted into a huge public feud, centered around this bill. Elon Musk has been highly critical of the bill for adding to the already-large fiscal deficit. The feud started with a focus on the bill itself, with Musk quote-tweeting many former posts by Donald Trump and House Speaker Mike Johnson for previously criticizing debt ceiling increases to highlight their inconsistency in now wanting to increase the debt ceiling by a record amount. Musk threatened to try to oust non-fiscally-conservative Republicans in the 2026 midterms. The feud then escalated further, with President Trump saying Musk was wearing thin and then threatening to end government contracts with Musk’s companies, and Musk saying that the reason the Epstein files weren’t released is because Trump is in them. So they’ve gone rather “scorched earth” at this point.

On the surface, I agree more with Musk in the sense that there’s an obvious double-standard in politics. Many of these politicians repeatedly criticize deficit spending and debt ceiling increases when they’re out of power, and then when they’re in power they go right ahead and deficit spend and raise the debt ceiling too.

Under the surface, however, I think Musk’s DOGE budget cut attempt was mistargeted to start with, was not going as expected, and so he had to make a separation at some point to distance himself from it. From a marketing perspective, it’s better for him to be seen as having tried to cut but got run over by a fiscally reckless Congress, than for the program to have been destined to fail from the start due to not tackling the root itself and him misunderstanding the nature of it.

My November 24, 2024 premium report analyzed the DOGE proposal when it started to form and quantified why it likely wouldn’t materially cut the deficit:

> But for this report, I’m going to focus on what I consider the proposal that most directly affects one of my core investment theses: the Department of Government Efficiency, or “DOGE” for short.
>
> Those that have followed my work know that I’ve been emphasizing fiscal dominance since around 2019, along with the broader idea that the U.S. is running unusually large fiscal deficits, is very likely to continue doing so despite various statements by officials to the contrary, and that this has investment implications.  So, my view on this began under the prior Trump term, continued through the Biden term, and as of now I expect it to continue under the next Trump term as well.
>
> Mainly, I’ve mostly avoided the long end of the bond curve (my mid-[2019 article on bonds likely being a bubble](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/) is my starting point for that), and indeed bonds have had basically their worst bear market in modern financial history in these 5+ years. Instead I’ve emphasized stocks, gold, bitcoin, and cash-equivalents or other short-duration paper. This included avoiding long-duration bonds at times where I highlighted risks of economic slowdowns, which goes against most consensus thinking but has been a successful strategy this far.
>
> I continue to expect large fiscal deficits in my forward analysis, which tends to “run things hot” compared to the baseline that we would expect when looking at credit cycles and other private sector metrics alone.
>
> However, Trump plans to set up a presidential advisory committee called the [Department of Government Efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Government_Efficiency) to address that. It’s not going to be an actual department since that requires an act of Congress, but rather it’ll be an advisory group led by Elon Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy. The stated goals are to streamline the government, improve efficiency, reduce the headcount, and cut the fiscal deficits. And notably, they plan to achieve the bulk of the outcome by July 4, 2026, which is symbolic since it’s the 250th anniversary of the country.
>
> The proposed leaders of this advisory committee have said that federal headcount could be reduced to up to 75% and fiscal deficits could be reduced by up to $2 trillion. Here’s the current Wikipedia snapshot, although I’m only using it as a summary source since I’ve seen many of the various social media posts by the advisory committee leaders directly:
>
> *“Musk has suggested that the commission could help to cut the U.S. federal budget by up to US$2 trillion through measures such as reducing waste, abolishing redundant agencies, and downsizing the federal workforce. Ramaswamy also stated that DOGE may eliminate entire federal agencies and reduce the number of federal employees by as much as 75%. Musk has also proposed consolidating the number of federal agencies from more than 400 to fewer than 100.” –[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Government_Efficiency)*
>
> The federal government has somewhere in the ballpark of [4.3 million employees](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/ap_14_strengthening_fy2025.pdf). About 1.35 million of them are uniformed military, and I don’t expect much if any cuts there. Another 550,000 or so (0.55 million) are postal workers. So that’s 1.9 million uniformed military and postal workers, leaving 2.4 million federal employees in other categories.
>

>
> The number of contract employees that serve federal contracts has grown over time, and most sources I’ve seen place that number somewhere around 4 million, or up to 5 million at the high end. The spending on them in 2023 was [$759 billion](https://www.gao.gov/blog/snapshot-government-wide-contracting-fy-2023-interactive-dashboard), of which 60% was spent by the Department of Defense and 40% was spent by every other department combined.

So as a rough number, every 1 million jobs cut could result in $150-$200 billion less in spending. >
> If these numbers seem small, it’s because the biggest spending areas of the government are light on employees and are heavy on transfer payments. Medicare, Social Security, Interest Expense, Veterans Benefits,  and those sorts of programs are where the bulk of spending occurs, and which are very unpopular to cut ([source](https://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-budget/where-do-our-federal-tax-dollars-go)):
>
> ![Federal Budget](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-federal-budget.png)
>
> The Social Security Administration and the Department of Health and Human Services (which houses Medicare and Medicaid) have under 150,000 employees combined despite accounting for a huge portion of federal expenditure. And Interest Expense isn’t a headcount issue either.
>
> Promise 14 at the top of the [2024 Republican Platform](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/2024-republican-party-platform) is:
>
> “FIGHT FOR AND PROTECT SOCIAL SECURITY AND MEDICARE WITH NO CUTS, INCLUDING NO CHANGES TO THE RETIREMENT AGE”
>
> So they can’t really cut Medicare too much, can’t really cut Social Security at all, can’t really cut interest expense, can’t really cut Veterans Benefits, and the rest of the non-Defense stuff is not all that big (i.e. cut a million employees and consolidate some facilities and save $150 billion/year, back of the envelop).
>
> That leaves the Department of Defense as the big serious honeypot for cutting. The problem there is that untangling a web of failed audits that big, with that many entrenched interests, is a Gordian knot. The defense contractors are major political donors. Basically every Congressperson who has a military base or a factory that produces military goods in their jurisdiction (which is a lot of them) is going to push back on eliminating or moving those facilities. The same is true for the smaller civilian facilities. Cutting every $100 billion increment out of the Department of Defense will be like pulling teeth from an angry tiger.
>
> My initial conclusion from this analysis is that I will take the significant “under” on the proposal that up to 75% of federal jobs could be cut, or that up to $2 trillion in deficits could be saved. I will monitor the actions and progress of this and other things that affect fiscal deficits, but I maintain my thesis that U.S. fiscal deficits will likely be large going forward for many years, and that I will continue to use my existing investing framework unless I see significant evidence to the contrary that changes that view.

The same 2024 GOP Platform mentioned above promises to “strengthen and modernize” the military, and, in fact, the CBO projects that defense spending will continue to rise over the next decade.

As a result, here in June 2025, even *before* the new spending bill has been passed, they have already spent more from January through May this year than they did last year. And this upcoming spending bill just reinforces the realpolitik that is at the heart of the issue.

That’s because by far the biggest spending areas are hard to tackle (and politically unpopular to do so), and so DOGE from the start went after the smallest portions of the pie. Meanwhile, nobody wants higher taxes, either.

We’ll see what happens to the bill in the coming weeks and months, but pretty much regardless, I reiterate my view that fiscal deficits will remain large, and that this will have various “run it hot” economic and investment implications going forward. It doesn’t mean we can’t have recessions or bad years for asset prices periodically, but the “flavor” of both bull and bear markets tends to be different when deficits of this scale are being run.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has been consolidating in the low six figures over the past couple weeks. I view it as a healthy consolidation, and as a base case I remain bullish in the months ahead.

I see some people talking a lot about exchange balances going down, and how this might cause a “short squeeze”. In general I fade that view, but let’s analyze that for a moment.

For years, the number of bitcoin in circulation was going up pretty rapidly, and the number of coins on exchanges went up rapidly as well. Well, now about 19.9 million out of 21 million bitcoins have already been mined, and so supply growth is very slow (less than 1% per year). Exchange balances have likely hit their peak at 3 million to 3.5 million coins:

![Exchange Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-exchange-balance.png)

A lot of these coins are transferring to institutional custody balances held by entities like Strategy (MSTR) or iShares (IBIT). And sometimes they’re still held by the exchanges themselves (e.g. Coinbase provides institutional custody services).

I think the more important metric to look at for available coins is the HODL wave. The biggest source of liquid coins on the market comes from prying them out of the hands of longer-term holders who have tons of coins. That mostly happens during bull markets. Give them a 5x or 10x price gain and many of them will trim their holdings, either to rebalance into other assets or to upgrade their consumption level (“buying lambos” is the stereotypical image, but it can be as simple as buying a house and starting a family as they enter their 30s or 40s with money).

Here’s the 1+ year HODL wave, meaning that the orange line represents the percentage of coins that have not moved in more than a year. That metric tends to fall in bull markets, as new buyers come in and some of these longer-term holders sell into that strength:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-hodl-wave.png)

Some people ask “if ETFs and MSTR are buying so many coins into a nearly fixed supply, why isn’t the price going to the moon?” The answer is because these older coin holders are gradually selling, offsetting that buying. It happens every cycle.

In other words, some guy with 500 coins he bought in 2016 is rich now, and he’s selling half of his holdings at $100k+ to the new ETF buyers. There are countless like him, big and small, going back 3 years, 5 years, 10 years, etc.

This is part of the natural distribution cycle that the Bitcoin network has to go through. It often gets criticized by having many of its coins concentrated in the hands of a small number of people. Well, the way to distribute the coins is for those people to keep selling over time, broadening the number of people who hold bitcoin (either directly or through proxies like ETFs and MSTR).

The closest analogy is that startup companies go through a similar holding cycle. In the beginning, a startup company is held by founders and early investors. Then as it goes through more growth rounds of raising capital (series A, series B, series C), the company gets bigger but those holders get diluted toward a smaller share of the network. When it goes public and remains public for a while, many of those founders and early investors will sell some of their massive holdings to the new public buyers and traders. The company becomes very broadly held, with something like Procter & Gamble (PG) being an example of a very old, large, and widely distributed company.

The difference of course is that Bitcoin is an open source network and digital monetary asset, not an equity security. But the overall holding patterns are similar as it arises from a niche network to a widely-held asset.

In other words, there are plenty of coins available to new buyers. It’s just that often the price has to go up a bit to get more of the holders to trim their holdings.

However, the fact that this talk was so popular reinforces my point from my report two weeks ago that there is a narrative pivot about fiscal dominance afoot. What was a relatively niche subject is more commonly referenced now.

Although I’m not the only one, since 2019 I’ve been emphasizing fiscal spending as an increasingly big portion of macroeconomics in modern times, with big ramifications for investing. It has been my primary “north star” when trying to navigate this rather hectic macro environment over the years.

-My 2019 article “[Are We in a Bond Bubble?](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/)” was the prologue. My conclusion was that yes, we’re likely in a bond bubble, that the combo of fiscal spending and central bank debt monetization can be a lot more impactful and inflationary than people seem to believe, and that such a thing is likely coming in the next downturn.

-During the depths of the disinflationary shock in March 2020, I wrote “[Why This is Unlike the Great Depression](https://www.lynalden.com/great-depression/)” which emphasized how massive fiscal stimulus was starting, and would likely get us back to nominal stock highs faster than people think, albeit at the likely cost of high inflation.

-For the rest of 2020, I followed it up with a series of articles “[QE, MMT, and Inflation/Deflation](https://www.lynalden.com/quantitative-easing-mmt-inflation/)“, “[A Century of Fiscal and Monetary Policy](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/)” and “[Banks, QE, and Money-Printing](https://www.lynalden.com/money-printing/)” which explored why the huge combo of fiscal stimulus and central bank support would be significantly different than the bank recapitalization QE of 2008/2009. In short, the thesis was that this was more like inflationary 1940s war finance than deflationary 1930s private debt deleveraging, thus a position of equities and hard monies would be better than bonds.

-By spring 2021, stocks had already jumped a ton and price inflation indeed began to break out. My May 2021 newsletter “[Fiscal-Driven Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/)” described and projected the issue further.

-The year 2022 was the one year where I got quite cautious around the idea of fiscal consolidation and potential recession, as price inflation reached its peak and pandemic-era fiscal stimulus wore off. My January 2022 newsletter “[The Capital Sponge](https://www.lynalden.com/january-2022-newsletter/)” was one of my early framings of the scene. Most of 2022 was indeed a bad year for broad asset prices and the economy slowed considerably, but by most metrics a recession was avoided due to what started happening later in the year.

-By late 2022 and particularly by early 2023, fiscal deficits were expanding again, in significant part due to ballooning interest expense on the public debt amid the rapid increase in interest rates. The Treasury General Account was draining liquidity back into the banking system, the Treasury Department shifted toward excess T-bill issuance which was a pro-liquidity move to pull money out of the reverse repo facility and back into the banking system, and overall it was “game on” again in terms of deficit expansion. My July 2023 newsletter was called “[Fiscal Dominance](https://www.lynalden.com/july-2023-newsletter/)” and I used the “Through the Looking Glass” analogy for the first time, which I brought up again in my recent keynote.

-By October 2023, federal fiscal year 2023 was in the books (which runs from October 2022 through September 2023) with a new nominal deficit increase, and I started my “nothing stops this train” meme (originally from the show Breaking Bad but in this context referring to US fiscal deficits) with this tweet:

![Nothing Stops This Train](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-nothing-stops-this-train-meme.png)

And since then it’s taken on a life of its own. Most memes are one-and-done, but I keep doing this in different formats and it’s become a bit of a game with my social media followers. The Bitcoin Conference asked me to do a talk about it, since the phrase has massively caught on.

![Cypher Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-cypher-meme.png)

Memes have value in that they are highly memorable and bite-sized, especially when repeated in various ways.

But ultimately it’s the long-form analysis that matters, and is what I base my investing on. Throughout 2023 and 2024 the re-expanding fiscal deficit has indeed provided massive fuel for ongoing investment gains, and while 2025 is a somewhat choppier environment due to tariffs and other factors, the ongoing US fiscal deficit continues to be a force to reckon with in any portfolio.

Overall, we can divide this 2019-2025 fiscal era into three periods.

-The 2019-2021 period was about predicting and then responding to massive fiscal stimulus during an economic problem. The system was so leveraged to start with that the outcome was highly predictable from before it happened. Due to the unique nature of the pandemic/lockdowns and the sheer size of the fiscal response it was all more “explosive” than what most of us would have predicted ahead of time, but in terms of sequence it played out as expected.

-The year 2022 was a “pause” where we went into brief fiscal consolidation, or hangover from all that stimulus, and the Fed was simultaneously trying to get inflation under control with high interest rates.

-And then 2023-2025 has been about the longer-term grind of continued large deficits re-asserting themselves. This is largely tied to demographics, the long-term cycle, and the end of the four-decade drop in interest rates.

I view this third era as basically extending for the foreseeable future (i.e. the next decade) as the “new normal”. However, in rate-of-change terms, we will likely have years akin to 2022 where the brakes get put on for a moment, albeit not quite that sharply. It’s a topic I’ll continue to cover closely in the months and years ahead, since it continues to be important for overall investment positioning.

## Emerging Market Foundations

In my prior report, I discussed a “bank and tech” barbell, where on one hand I’m bullish on a lot of bank stocks for value potential, and on the other hand there are some cyclical tech stocks like AMD that were beaten down and that I was also bullish on.

The main area I *didn’t* like for that report is low volatility growth stocks. That part of the market is crowded and expensive, in my view. The market is overpaying for certainty and thus ironically setting itself up for risk there.

The main theme is that ironically, it can make sense to buy into riskier or more volatile things as long as they’re priced well for that risk and as long as position sizes are managed cautiously. In contrast, overpaying to avoid risk ends up being a form of risk itself. The worst type of risk comes from something you thought was safe and that you overpaid for and overbought, whereas things with risk that are known to have risk are manageable.

And so on that note, I continue to view that emerging market equities in aggregate are building some very interesting foundations to eventually break out of. They have a lot of risk in a geopolitically complex world, and they are cheaply priced to account for that risk.

For a while, however, there was no catalyst. Why would global investors rotate from US stocks to foreign ones? But then we got some.

-The first catalyst in 2024 was a central bank cutting cycle. As the Fed and ECB cut interest rates from their highs, it gives emerging market central banks more breathing room, and they generally have higher real rates as they try to hold up their currency against a strong dollar and/or euro.

-The second catalyst here in 2025 is US policy uncertainty. Put another way, as shifting US policies make many countries question how reliant they should be on the US, this includes questions of how much capital they should keep stuffed into US capital markets. They have to account for the tail risk of asset seizures or punitive fees/taxes on withdrawing assets or things of that nature. They also have to account for a shift against US corporate margins in this new world of rising trade barriers and shifting immigration/border practices.

Together, I think those two catalysts are pretty strong and have legs to them. Equities throughout the rest of the world are cheap and beaten down, and ripe with opportunity. And then there’s a virtuous cycle that can further boost emerging markets, because as capital flows into their stocks, it also helps support their currencies in relation to their national dollar-denominated debt.

Overall, emerging markets have been in a weak trend since their peak in 2007, which is now 18 years and counting:

![Emerging Markets Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-emerging-markets-basic-index.png)

When we look at the total return index (which includes reinvested dividends) it’s not quite as bad. But still, a mere 50% gain from 2007 through 2025 is very weak:

![Emerging Markets Total Return](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-emerging-markets-total-return.png)

Although of course developing market equities have been around for a while, “emerging markets” as an asset class started in the late 1980s. MSCI started their index in 1988. Since then, there have been two big EM bull markets. The first one was from the late 1980s through the mid-1990s. The second one was from the early 2000s until 2007.

Similarly, since the Bretton Woods agreement was defaulted on in the early 1970s, there have been three major dollar cycles. The first one was in the 1970s and early 1980s before “emerging markets” were a thing, although developing market equities were indeed impacted. But then, the second big dollar bear market of 1985 into the 1990s, and the third big dollar bear market the early 2000s to 2007, were the primary catalysts for the huge emerging market bull runs.

We can see this most clearly with a log chart:

![Emerging Markets vs Dollar](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-emerging-markets-vs-dollar.png)

My base case is that, as the US enters a prolonged period of large fiscal deficits (which starting in 2026 will likely be accompanied by a gradually expanding Fed balance sheet to support financial sector liquidity as well), it will serve as a catalyst to give emerging market equities another sizable leg upward.

This is naturally a long process. And we had a big false start with the 2019 interest rate cutting cycle that got derailed by all the pandemic/lockdown stuff that followed.

I don’t have a strong view on the dollar index itself, since it’s so heavily weighted between the dollar and the euro. However, I do have a view that we’re gradually rolling over in terms of peak ratios of capital being stuffed into US capital markets, and expect to see more of a broadening out to other geographies in the years ahead.

Those areas have risk, but to my prior point, those risks are priced reasonably. And this is naturally a long process, so I’ll continue to monitor it over time. It is tied, in some part, to the broad trend of US fiscal dominance.

>
> On the monthly chart, it’s nearly as technically overbought as it’s ever been:
>
> ![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gold-chart.png)

As a gold holder and as someone who is bullish long-term, I’d like to be wrong about gold and for it to just keep rocketing higher. But realistically, that’s not my base case.

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-6-8-gold.png)

I don’t know if we’ll hit the 6-12 month consolidation I was concerned about, but we’re approaching two months for starters. And I think that’s healthy. Investors start to forget about it, the speculative positioning goes away, and the metal can build another base to go higher eventually.
