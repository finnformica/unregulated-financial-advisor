**Stimulus Talks**

Much of the world, including the United States, is not on a big stimulus kick at the moment, like we were months ago.

Inflation expectations are currently wobbly, which makes sense when stimulus clarity is lacking. ## Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) Deep-Dive Analysis

Enterprise Products Partners is a master limited partnership that owns and operates a very large network of crude oil, natural gas, natural gas liquids, and petrochemical midstream infrastructure within the United States. They transport energy and chemicals for both domestic use and for global export.

They have among the lowest debt levels and among the best management in the midstream industry, and they make money primarily from product volumes rather than product prices. They currently pay a distribution yield of over 10% with 21 years of consecutive annual distribution increases, and the distribution is well-covered by cash flows and has no plans to be cut.

I previously analyzed EPD with a positive outlook shortly before the pandemic hit, at the start of this year. It ended up being among the worst-performers of all the businesses I’ve analyzed in recent years, not in terms of fundamentals, but in terms of investment performance due to sharply reduced valuations. I mean let’s be blunt, this stock got crushed this year, as it fell from the high twenties to the teens:

![EPD Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-price-yield.png)

EPD’s price was in a 4-year tight consolidation range between $25 and $30 as fundamentals gradually grew, and I thought it would eventually break out to the upside. However, a global pandemic and the biggest energy demand shock in history were not in my base case outlook back in mid-January when I analyzed EPD, and that’s what we got. So, it broke to the downside instead.

The good news is that EPD’s fundamentals (and distribution) held up as expected, even under this catastrophic scenario for the energy sector.

EPD got hit with among the worst-possible of tail risks, which pulled down the forward expected growth rate, but their operations, capital structure, and quarterly distribution have held up strong. The valuation, however, was abruptly cut in half, from historically average to historically dirt cheap.

When something becomes half the price, but not half as good, that’s worth a serious revisit.

It’s worth noting that over the past month, EPD began breaking out vs the energy sector, after spending the year highly correlated with it. This might or might not persist going forward, but it’s interesting:

![EPD Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-returns.png)

My process focuses on the long-run, so whether we see a lower low this year or not, isn’t my primary concern here. My focus is on whether it’s a good 3-5 year investment at current beaten-down price levels, relative to the risks involved. I think it likely is.

**The Energy, Metal, and Chemical Future**

As described by the previous breakdown of their gross profits, EPD is more diversified than most other midstream companies. The narrative these days is that electric vehicles will abruptly replace internal combustion engines and oil demand overall, but EPD offers a lot more than that, and is mostly uninvolved with the automotive gasoline market. They’re involved with plastics, additives, petrochemicals, propane for developing-market energy use, and some natural gas and crude oil transport.

As someone with a background in electrical engineering and an interest in renewable energy, I’d love to see the cleanest possible future, with smog-free cities, clean oceans, and a stabilization in atmospheric carbon dioxide and particulate levels. However, it’s a challenging process to get there in developed markets, and especially in developing markets.

There’s tons of work to be done to make electric vehicles scalable enough to replace internal combustion engines on a mass scale. They need to improve their range, reduce or eliminate the need for cobalt, and massively expand the electrical grid to shift automotive energy transmission away from the gasoline logistics channel and towards further electrification. And global grid power still comes in large part from natural gas, coal, and oil. So, for fossil fuels to be truly removed from the automobile equation, the electrical production for the national grid would need to shift markedly to solar and wind and/or nuclear energy.

And then even if engineers do all that, it still doesn’t replace the need for natural gas liquids and petrochemicals for plastics and a multitude of additives and materials as shown in the chart above. Hopefully material scientists will find ways to rely less on non-biodegradable petroleum-based plastics, but it’s such a broad set of applications to work through.

Plus, real estate heating comes in big part from natural gas and is expected to continue that way for a long time. Natural gas also plays a major role in electricity generation for global electrical grids. While solar and wind are increasing market share in grid energy production from a small initial base, natural gas is also taking a lot of market share from coal in developed markets, which is good because between the two, natural gas is a lot cleaner than coal.

More renewable energy and electric vehicles, in principle, means a shift from oil and coal usage to various semi-scarce metals, like copper, silver, nickel, and others. Renewable energy doesn’t just come from the sun and the air; various metals allow us to capture, store, and use that energy. Electric vehicles use more copper and nickel and silver (and currently cobalt, which is a supply-constrained socio-political problem) than gasoline-powered cars. Solar panels require a lot of silver. Wind turbines use a lot of steel (and ironically, a good amount of plastic). The electrical grid would need a lot more copper, especially in developing markets.

Investors can diversify their commodity exposure and hedge their energy exposure if technology and infrastructure advance more quickly than expected, by owning these metals resources for which demand would outpace supply. I’m pretty bullish on most metals prices and their producers long-term.

There are novel technologies that potentially allow machines to convert carbon dioxide and other inputs into plastic precursors (by using metals like nickel as a catalyst for the chemical conversion…) but they are nowhere near commercial feasibility. And there are plant-based biodegradable plastics for some applications, like the plastic bags we put around vegetables in a grocery store. But the number of specific chemicals and highly-specialized plastics and additives in every aspect of our lives (from the wrappings around wires, to making up part of wind turbine blades, to carpets, to medical protective equipment, to a thousand other things) is very challenging to replace from non-petrochemical sources, although I happily welcome any innovations in the industry.

**The Asian Energy Dilemma**

Asia has broadly been an energy importer, since it’s not a very abundant region for energy resources relative to the population density. EPD has many export terminals on the U.S. gulf coast, with Asia being a big source of demand for those exports.

So, much of the ESG world is talking about replacing oil and gas as though it will happen quickly, when Asia is still growing its oil and gas demand, let alone still building new \*coal\* plants.

Many places in developing southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa don’t have natural gas pipeline infrastructure, and instead burn wood or cow dung or other biomass for cooking fuel, and natural gas liquids (portable energy sources that don’t require local pipeline infrastructure) are a big step up from that, and therefore have fast-growing demand in those regions.

Here’s India’s electricity generation profile, with coal continuing to dominate and grow:

![India Electricity Generation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-india-electricity-generation.png)

*Chart Source: [EIA](https://www.eia.gov/international/analysis/country/IND)*

And that is just electricity generation; it doesn’t include automobiles, cooking fuels, and the need for petrochemicals.

Indian petroleum and other liquids production has been flat at about 1 million barrels per day over the past 20 years, while their consumption has increased from about 2 million to nearly 5 million over that timeframe. So, even as North America and Europe potentially flatten or diminish their petroleum demand, Asia becomes the center of growth for it.

This chart from Peter Zeihan, which shows geographic solar and wind potential within 1,000km of major cities, emphasizes Asia’s heavy reliance on coal-burning and oil and gas energy imports:

![Renewable Energy Potential](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-renewable-potential.png)

*Chart Source: Zeihan on Geopolitics*

Well over 3 billion people with rising per-capita energy usage live in that circle, and there is not a lot of solar or wind potential relative to the size of the population centers there.

Many of the best places for solar and wind worldwide are very far away from cities. However, electricity gets degraded when it is transported over long distances, so it’s not as though we can build hyper-scale solar panels all over the Saharan desert and send that electricity to China and India. The longer the distance, the less of the energy that reaches from source to destination.

Improved superconductor technologies could make ultra-long-distance electricity transportation more efficient, but even with that, to power Asia with wind and solar from far-away lands would require huge infrastructure build-out and doesn’t seem feasible. Breakthroughs in new forms of local nuclear energy production would likely be more feasible than that.

Unless or until novel technology breakthroughs allow otherwise, Asia needs a lot of imported oil and gas energy and are even still building coal plants, as other continents try to make the best use of renewable energy available to them and phase out coal. LNG and NGLs can be shipped to Asia cost-effectively, from EPD pipelines and export facilities, and other exporters.

Despite all of this, Tesla (TSLA) stock is currently worth multiple times the value of the entire American midstream energy infrastructure from all midstream businesses combined (oil, gas, liquids, refined products, petrochemicals), despite earning a small fraction of the revenue, due to the massive confidence that investors have in how rapidly renewables and electrification will replace the need for all of these products across a broad range of uses. I’ll “take the under” on that, within the context of a diversified portfolio.

**Management Quality and Balance Sheet**

EPD has historically had top-tier management among all businesses in the midstream sector. Every time the energy sector and midstream industry blow up, EPD is one of the businesses that is still left standing with its fundamentals virtually unharmed, and they just moderately revise down growth expectations for the next couple years while remaining profitable, until the industry rebounds.

That resiliency is what I emphasized in my previous pre-pandemic analysis of EPD. Since then, 2020 provided another major energy blow-up, and EPD is still left standing again so far. They face a difficult 2021 after a difficult 2020, but they’re still going steady.

EPD’s resiliency is due to the fact that they operate with low leverage and a long-term view, and emphasize conservative operations with high returns on capital, rather than aggressive financing or unnecessarily greedy growth rates. Management owns about 1/3rd of the company, and a member of the founder’s billionaire family still sits as the chair of the board of directors. EPD was also one of the early MLPs to eliminate incentive distribution rights to the general partner, which is good for limited unitholders.

So, this isn’t a management team that optimizes short-term performance or overly-risky investments or other poor compensation-related incentives. They have long-term skin in the game right alongside other common unitholders for steady and high-margin growth and income, compounded over time.

As such, EPD has the second-lowest debt-to-EBITDA ratio of various large midstream companies:

![Midstream Sector Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-midstream-sector-debt.png)

During 2020, EPD was able to further reduce the interest rates on its debt and increase duration. Their debt now has a 4.4% average yield with a 20-year average duration.

This also gives them a decent inflation hedge if the macro environment should come to that, because many of their fee contracts are inflation-hedged, while their debt is ultra-long-duration and 99% fixed-rate.

**Growth and Dividends**

EPD makes most of its money (88%) from transportation fees, which protects them from changes in commodity prices.

However, a major decline in the price of energy puts many weaker energy producers out of business and results in less supply, which means that EPD faces some counterparty risks with the producers that it works with, and therefore has its forward growth potential impaired to some extent.

So, EPD is resilient to changes in energy prices, but not totally immune to them.

Here is their chart of oil prices on the left axis, vs EPD cash flow and distributions per unit on the right axis:

![EPD Historical Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-performance.png)

*Chart Source: September 2020 EPD Investor Deck*

EPD currently pays a distribution yield of well over 10%, and it’s fully-covered by cash flows. There are other MLPs with even higher distribution yields, but EPD’s is among the most well-covered and least-likely to be cut within the sector.

EPD has raised its distribution for 21 consecutive annual years. They usually raise it slightly every single quarter. In 2020, they increased the distribution in the first quarter to $0.445/share, but have prudently held their quarterly distribution payout flat in the subsequent quarters including through to the recent Q4 announcement. This still leaves their annual consecutive growth record intact, since the total 2020 payout is above their total 2019 payout.

Currently, EPD has a 1.6x distribution coverage ratio, which is higher than most other MLPs, and above-average for EPD’s history, which means the distribution should be very well-covered by cash flows, and about as well as it was pre-pandemic. This is because, in recent years, EPD became mostly self-funding, meaning they operate with a lower distribution payout ratio and no longer need to issue units to fund growth, and instead are able to fund growth internationally and even use buybacks to reduce their unit count.

They used to rapidly issue new units to grow (which is a principle benefit of the MLP structure as originally intended decades ago), but with a maturation of the North American energy sector after a few decades of massive infrastructure growth and production increases, the emphasis in the midstream is now more focused on capital return. The pandemic has somewhat accelerated this trend, which was already in place: less growth, stronger balance sheets, more capital return.

EPD has noted multiple times that American (and global) energy production is decreasing in 2020 and will likely remain low in 2021, as bankruptcies and consolidations occur among producers. EPD management focuses on working with strong counterparties, and making adjustments to their capex plans as necessary.

Looking at exports from EPD facilities, they bottomed in May of this year, and gradually recovered through the summer.

In September, due to changes in production growth forecasts, EPD cancelled their “Midland-to-ECHO 4” crude oil pipeline project. They will instead use existing pipelines to carry out the transportation agreement that this cancelled pipeline would have done, so it increases capacity-utilization of their existing nearby infrastructure, which is good. This cancelled project reduces their planned 2020-2022 growth capex by $800 million, but still leaves $5.3 billion in growth capex during that period.

That’s an example of their topline growth rate being moderately impacted by this pandemic and oil demand destruction; it’s not great, but it’s not terrible either. It just shifts some of their per-share growth potential from the top line to capital returns instead. Management reported that with the savings from this capex reduction, they can potentially use it for some combination of buybacks and/or debt reduction, which also infers strong distribution safety for the current payout, and likely continued growth of that per-share distribution in 2021.

Management has specifically pointed out that because EPD units now have a 15%+ cash flow yield due to extremely low valuations, growth capex expenditures with a historical 12% average return on invested capital have a high hurdle compared to simply using cash to back units at current valuations.

**Valuation Analysis**

EPD is currently trading at a sub-6x price to operating cash flow ratio, which is roughly tied as their lowest-ever valuation in history:

![EPD Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-price-to-cfo.png)

They traded at this valuation briefly near inception, and again briefly during the global financial crisis, and both instances were extremely good buying opportunities.

Times are different now because growth prospects are lower, but this is offset by the fact that EPD is more self-funding than ever, has a higher distribution coverage ratio than ever, and is slightly reducing units rather than rapidly issuing units.

Here’s the F.A.S.T. Graph, which uses adjusted earnings rather than operating cash flow:

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-fastgraph.png)

My previous pre-pandemic valuation model of EPD used 4% forward long-term annual distribution growth as its base case, which was slightly lower than their 10-year history of about 5%. This assumed about 2% annual volume growth and 2% price growth. I was writing about recession risk at the time, but not yet considering a global pandemic.

Now, I should cut that long-term annual growth estimate to 3%, given the circumstances.

But I won’t. Instead, I’ll go a step further and get really bearish this time. I’ll assume 0% volume growth ever from here, 1% annual pricing growth on their contract fees, and 1% EPD units repurchased per year. Considering that they have $5.3 billion in growth capex planned for 2020-2022, zero volume growth perpetually is unlikely, but let’s say more reductions in growth capex occur, and some counterparty risk occurs, and volumes permanently flatline across their diversified infrastructure base for the long run.

If that were to happen, EPD could use the savings from growth capex and instead divert the rest of its cash flow (with a 1.6x distribution coverage) to buying back about 5% of shares per year, or further reduce debt and interest expense, which would allow them to grow their per-share distribution (which currently yields over 10%) regardless.

But let’s say enough bad things happen that they can’t do that either; they neither grow volumes, nor buy back more than 1% of shares per year, and only increase fee prices by 1% per year, and thus some of their excess cash flows disappear each year without productive use, due to some tail risks repeatedly hitting them. And let’s say the valuation stays near its all-time lows and never gets back above a price to cash flow ratio of 6x. This is among the worst possible outcomes, in other words.

In that dreadful scenario, the fair price for the units to achieve 10% annualized returns over a decade would be about $20/share (vs the current price of $17):

![EPD StockDelver Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-stockdelver.png)

On the other hand, if we allow for something resembling a base case, and say that long-term volumes will grow by 1%, pricing will grow by 2%, they’ll buy back 2% of units per year in lieu of weak growth capex, and price to cash flow ratio will go back up to a mid point of 8x (well below their 11x historical average, but up from their current sub-6x level that is near the all-time low), then the fair price is $29, which is what it was pre-pandemic when I previously analyzed it:

![EPD StockDelver Model Base Case](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-10-11-epd-stockdelver2.png)

Ultimately, despite a global pandemic, nothing really changed about my fundamental EPD analysis other than knocking off about 1% off from its annual expected growth rate, and examining some more bearish outcomes than that. They’ll probably do less capex and more buybacks over the next ten years, which accelerates the path of self-funding and higher capital return that they were already shifting to anyway.

Basically, at these bombed-out low valuations, EPD offers the prospects for better-than-10% returns even if most things go poorly for them, and potentially 15%+ annualized returns under a base case scenario. Those are 10-year estimates, and those results could be front-loaded over the next 3-5 years if some of the most bearish global macro outcomes don’t occur.

**Company Risks**

This report included risk analysis throughout its various chapters, but we’ll summarize them here. In addition, although we examined a rather bearish scenario, there are some more extreme bearish scenarios that could result in more serious business damage up to and including a distribution cut.

*Counterparty Risk*

EPD is insulated from changing energy prices, but not totally immune to changing energy prices. If U.S. energy production falls off a cliff faster than even most bear cases expect due to grossly unprofitable drilling, EPD’s transport contracts would be impaired and cash flows would fall more than expected.

A reduction in U.S. shale production is already part of my base case and part of EPD management’s base case until 2022. U.S. production is already down from a peak of 13 million barrels/day less than a year ago, to 10-11 million barrels today. Even if this gradually winds down further, EPD should be fine, but if it quickly collapses, EPD would face severe headwinds. In recent months, it has rebounded a bit, from a low of 10 million barrels/day.

EPD mitigates this counterparty risk by focusing on the Permian, which is the best acreage for production, and working with strong counterparties rather than the smaller insolvent sort. Their risk/reward hurdle rate for building a new pipeline is always high, which mitigates risk.

On the other side of this, EPD has made big investments for ongoing export to Asia and eventually Africa. If product demand collapses from those regions, it puts EPD at significant risk.

*Regulation Risk*

Depending on election outcomes, there could be more restrictions on shale production of U.S. oil and gas, which could affect EPD volumes over time. However, that would make the U.S. more reliant on foreign oil.

My hedge for this scenario is to own some foreign oil producers. If U.S. were to substantially reduce supply for political reasons, it would likely prop up global energy prices, and benefit non-US producers.

I specifically like the diversification of owning both U.S. energy transport networks such as EPD (benefiting from equal or more U.S. volume) and foreign producers (benefiting from equal or less U.S. volume), for that barbell risk management approach. And for both types, I emphasize quality.

*Valuation Risk*

The main risk EPD investors were hit with over the past year was valuation risk. Pre-pandemic, it was already trading at a valuation that was in-line with its historical average, well below any extremes it occasionally experienced. It has since been cut to a valuation that is among its lowest in history.

This current low valuation, however, reduces further valuation risk. If EPD keeps chugging along fine fundamentally, it’s challenging to see valuations go much lower than this. However, in an extreme scenario, it’s always possible that they could fall to, say, a 4x price to cash flow ratio instead of the 5-6x price to cash flow ratio that they’re at recently. The distribution yield would be extremely high.

I could imagine that ultra-low valuation event potentially occurring if there is no second-round stimulus for a prolonged amount of time, the economy rolls back over, a deflationary shock occurs, and risk assets sell off broadly. It’s not my base case, but it’s a possibility.

Due to broad ESG mandates where many investors want to avoid energy companies entirely, it’s possible that midstream valuations could remain below-average in perpetuity. If the fundamentals remain decent while valuations remain compressed, then the likely outcome would be what ironically happened with the tobacco industry: a multi-decade period of investment outperformance due to persistently low valuations and capital return that makes use of those low valuations (high yield dividend reinvestment and buybacks).

*Technology Risk*

If extremely rapid electric vehicle adoption occurs, and if grid energy stops using fossil fuels quicker than anyone expects, and if multiple petrochemicals and materials are globally replaced with alternatives, and if Asian energy demand collapses or they rapidly find other alternatives, EPD’s diverse set of product infrastructure would be impaired. There are a lot of “ands” there, which shows the beneficial nature of EPD’s product diversification.

As described in previous sections, this broad multi-product permanent volume reduction seems very unlikely, due to a number of technological problems that would need to be rapidly solved to make EPD’s broad infrastructure obsolete.

We’ll see renewables grow as a percentage of global electrical grid production, and we’ll see electrical vehicles take market share from international combustion engines. However, with so many uses for natural gas liquids, natural gas, crude oil, and petrochemicals, including growing Asian demand for them, EPD is strongly buffered from most technological scenarios over the next decade or more.

*Distribution Cut Risk*

Despite a 10%+ yield that could appear on the surface to be a sucker’s yield (i.e. a distribution with a high risk of being cut), EPD currently has no management prospects for a distribution cut, with 1.6x distribution coverage and no catalyst on the horizon that should result in a cut. However, with all of these risks considered, it’s always a possibility to consider.

Anyone actively relying on distributions should have diversified sources of those distributions, so that even an unlikely distribution cut is not catastrophic for your investment income needs.

There are some tail risk scenarios as previously described that could make even EPD cut their distribution, but they would involve some of these tail risks occurring, like a sustained product demand collapse from Asia, or a very sharp reduction in North American energy production that is far worse than what already occurred.

At this valuation and capital structure, EPD and their distribution are protected against perpetually flat supply or demand, but not a permanent and rapid collapse in supply or demand.

## Summary

The macro environment remains uncertain, with some semblance of stability, but heightened risk of economic slowdown. Stimulus remains a big unknown variable. I have a long-term view, but the next 3-6 months is uncertain to an unusual degree.

An emphasis on high-quality companies reduces the risk somewhat. Strong balance sheets, recession-resistant business models, and select sectors that have already been obliterated, are areas I’m interested in. I’m being very selective; I’m not running into airlines, cruise ships, or hotels, for example. Targeted energy producers or transporters interest me a ton down here for the long run, as well as certain targeted banks, airports, and other areas that have been impaired but not crippled.

For EPD, their fundamentals have performed mostly in-line with expectations this year based on my previous analysis, with only moderate reductions, despite a global pandemic and the biggest oil demand reduction in modern history. However, with the unit price and valuation nearly cut in half in this period of bombed-out energy sector sentiment, the potential forward returns from EPD units have improved notably.
