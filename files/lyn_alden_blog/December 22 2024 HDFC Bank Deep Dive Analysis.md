## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be precisely rangebound, as the Fed continues to perform quantitative tightening but funds continue to drain out of the reverse repo facility and back into the financial system, offsetting that quantitative tightening.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-domestic-liquidity.png)

Speaking of the reverse repo facility, it’s back below $100 billion for the first time since 2021, and draining at a more steady pace again. Once this runs out, there will be less liquidity available to offset the Fed’s ongoing quantitative tightening, and the pressure to stop that quantitative tightening will start to mount:

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-reverse-repo.png)

Bank cash levels as a percentage of total assets are back down to near where the March 2023 regional bank crisis occurred, but above the levels where the September 2019 repo spike occurred.

![Bank Cash vs Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-bank-cash-vs-assets.png)

The end-of-quarter period tends to put pressure on balance sheets across the financial system. The end of Q3 2024 was the first instance since 2020, however, where the Fed’s repo facility was tapped:

![Repo Spike](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-repo.png)

That $2.35 billion Fed standing repo facility usage wasn’t a lot, but since it was the first in a long time, it was an important signal. As a base case, we should expect higher Fed repo usage figures at the end of Q4, as a sign of growing balance sheet pressures in the system. Indeed, the New York Fed is already [gearing up](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/opolicy/operating_policy_241219) for pressures during the period from December 30th through January 3rd.

The Fed maintaining a standing repo facility gives financial institutions more flexibility and potentially delays the day where the Fed will have to stop reducing its balance sheet and switch back to expanding its balance sheet. In the meantime, the more that this facility is tapped, the more it serves as a public signal that balance sheets are tightening up and that the Fed will end up changing course. I continue to expect that within 2025 they will end quantitative tightening.

Global liquidity continues to be pressured from the post-election strong dollar move. In my prior report on December 8th, I said it looked like the recent dollar strength might be coming to an end, but that comment aged poorly. In the two weeks since then, the dollar index shot up from 106 to 108, reaching a new 12-month high, and part of this was due to the Fed’s hawkish 2025 forecast last week. I expect global liquidity to remain somewhat pressured in Q1 2025.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-dxy.png)

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Q4 quarter is coming to an end, and current data suggests that it was a pretty strong one, with annualized real GDP growth of above 3%:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-gdpnow.png)

The biggest U.S. economic uncertainty I have for the intermediate term is about the potential upcoming tariffs. We don’t really know to what extent tariffs will be implemented or used as a negotiation tactic, and the magnitude of those can have significant impacts on both domestic and global economic performance, as well as on inflation.

My concern is that we’ve gotten the post-election sentiment boom, but that some of the headwinds from policy changes might start to hit in the first half of 2025. This is an area I’ll monitor pretty closely, especially starting after the upcoming inauguration.

**Inflation and Interest Rates**

During this past week, the Fed cut short-term interest rates by 0.25% down to the range of 4.25% to 4.50% (effectively 4.33% in practice), which was expected by the market.

The Fed’s 2025 projections, however, were the interesting part. They give a projection every other meeting, which equates to once per quarter, and their most recent projections were in September. Back then, their median expectation was for PCE inflation and Core PCE inflation to be 2.1% and 2.2% respectively in 2025. However, just three months later here in mid-December, they revised both of those numbers up to 2.5%, which is a material increase. They also boosted their 2025 median interest rate forecast from 3.4% to 3.9%.

The market perceived this as quite hawkish. Stocks, gold, bitcoin, and long-duration bonds sold off on the news right when the Fed materials were released.

While this revision within the span of one quarter is pretty big, nothing about this is particularly surprising from my perspective. I keep pounding on the “fiscal dominance” theme, whereby ongoing 7%-of-GDP deficits have significant implications for nominal GDP growth and baseline inflation.

The Fed tried hard to market the idea that inflation was transitory, and yet even by the end of 2025 they don’t expect inflation to be back down to their target, despite the fact that the supposed transitory catalysts occurred from 2020 to 2022. This is because the rapid growth in money supply, driven largely by monetized fiscal deficits, is still working its way through the system, and still being supported by large ongoing fiscal deficits.

And ironically, by keeping interest rates higher for longer, the Fed is helping to fuel those deficits, because a big chunk of those deficits relates to interest rate expense. The ongoing “running hot” aspect is not due to high levels of bank lending and overall credit formation, which is normally what the Fed is targeting with its monetary policy. In fact, some of those credit-formation indicators were in outright recession territory in 2023 and 2024. Instead, the fiscal deficits are the big fuel source.

Even as the Fed potentially stays higher-for-longer in 2025 in terms of interest rates, as I mentioned earlier, I think they’ll likely find a floor on their balance sheet in 2025 and start heading sideways-to-up from there.

**China’s Bond Yield Contraction**

Just within the month of November, China’s 10-year government bond yield has fallen from over 2% to about 1.7%. It might not sound like much, but that’s an enormous and sharp move:

![China 10Yr](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-china-10yr.png)

![China 10Yr Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-china-10yr-longterm.png)

On one hand, this reduces the expense of Chinese government borrowing. Lenders are willing to give China’s government a lower borrowing rate on their debt than the United States’ government.

On the other (and more important) hand, this is a strong market signal that China’s economy continues to stagnate and needs to rebalance its export-vs-consumption situation. I continue to be of the view that Chinese fiscal stimulus will be less than what people hope for at any given time, but will be recurring in the sense that whenever certain red lines are crossed or things become disorderly, they will likely act. And this is a potentially disorderly signal from the market that their economy continues to be extremely weak.

Specifically, China’s export sector is strong, but their domestic consumption is weak, because householders are dealing with deflating property prices and high uncertainty about the economy. And so I would expect China’s economy to continue to stagnate unless or until they address the consumption side more. I think they will do that more over time, but that it’ll take multiple pain points along the way for them to do so.

**MicroStrategy, and Bitcoin in Corporate Treasuries**

MicroStrategy (MSTR) was recently added to the Nasdaq 100, which means it’ll be bought by the QQQ ETF and other passive vehicles for investors and institutions to access Nasdaq exposure. Along with the major spot bitcoin ETFs, the company continues to be a major way for traditional finance to access bitcoin exposure.

In addition, on December 15th FASB’s new fair value accounting rules for bitcoin went into effect. Previously, bitcoin was treated in a rather draconian accounting way; any drops in price by corporate holders of bitcoin had to be listed as losses, but any gains in price couldn’t be listed as gains. Now, both losses and gains are accounted for, which makes sense.

Microsoft (MSFT) had a shareholder proposal to adopt bitcoin as one of its Treasury reserve assets, but shareholders in aggregate voted against it, as I expected. However, as I mentioned when I previously reported on this, the mere fact that it’s coming up in shareholder proposals is a pretty big deal. Here’s what I wrote about it back in November:

> A shareholder proposal was submitted to Microsoft to add bitcoin to their balance sheet, to which the board of directors recommended voting against the proposal. As a recent update to that, Michael Saylor (executive chairman of MicroStrategy) will give a short 3-minute presentation to Microsoft’s board on why they should approve the proposal.
>
> While the base case is that the proposal won’t pass, it’s pretty powerful to have that interaction occur. MicroStrategy is one of the few stocks that has outperformed Nvidia. Since MicroStrategy adopted their bitcoin strategy in August 2020, their stock is up over 30x which is *insane*(in comparison, Nvidia is “only” up 12x during that time). I’ve been holding it since August 2020 in some of the model portfolios, although I occasionally trim a bit out when it becomes excessively overweight.
>
> -November 24th premium report

Similarly, a [shareholder proposal for Amazon](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/amazon-buy-bitcoin-shareholders-164204047.html) (AMZN) suggests that the company evaluates holding at least 5% of its corporate treasury in bitcoin. I continue to expect that any of these proposals regarding bitcoin treasuries at large corporations will be shot down. Shareholders rarely vote against director recommendations.

Large companies are unlikely to be the early adopters for this. It’ll be companies like MicroStrategy, Metaplanet, Semler Scientific (SMLR), and others that people generally haven’t heard of that take the initiative to add bitcoin to their balance sheet and start outperforming. Entities that face challenges on their income side in terms of growth, can instead turn to their balance sheet side, and generally it’s the smaller and more contrarian companies that will do this.

**Quantum Resilience**

Many of the things we rely on, ranging from the way the Internet works to the security of our bank accounts, come down to unbreakable encryption.

Over the next decade or so, the emergence of quantum computing might begin to challenge some of these existing encryption algorithms that we rely on. Sensationalist headlines often overstate them, but there are gradual improvements in quantum computing research and tests over time, which may lead to large and practical enough quantum computers that can break our current encryption methods.

As a result, many organizations suggest shifting to quantum-hard cryptography by 2030 or 2035, to get ahead of the potential emergence of such encryption-breaking computers.

The Bitcoin network is no different; there have been and will continue to be developer discussions on the best way to upgrade its signature scheme to be resilient against quantum computers. The core SHA-256 hashing algorithm is already quantum resilient, but the transaction signing methods are not, and so those can be replaced or augmented by quantum-resilient signing methods. Specifically, the issue with the current signing methods is that if you know someone’s public key, a sufficiently advanced quantum computer could use it to derive someone’s private key, which reduces the security of the network and requires users to eventually adopt methods that prevent that.

Here, for example, is a new proposal by Spiral developers (Block Inc’s open-source R&D division) to make Bitcoin more quantum resilient:

[Trivial QC Signatures with Clean Upgrade Path](https://groups.google.com/g/bitcoindev/c/8O857bRSVV8/m/4cM-7pf4AgAJ?pli=1)

The general sentiment among bitcoin developers currently is that quantum-resilient signatures are not currently needed (and there is a cost to having them), but that there should be some readiness paths sorted out so that as the world gets closer to potentially needing them, they’re already ready to go and uncontroversial among users.

Perhaps the hardest part of the discussion is around whether to make the upgrade path opt-in or mandatory. Certain upgrade paths can enable quantum-resilient signatures as a new optional method among many, while others can actually lock any funds that are *not* using quantum-resilient signatures, after giving them time to shift over.

The reason that this is relevant is that hacked funds from non-quantum-resilient signatures can impact supply and pricing across the network. For example, Satoshi Nakamoto’s million unmoved coins, and other lost or abandoned coins from people that lost their private keys in the early days before bitcoin had any value, could eventually be taken by those with quantum computers, resulting in a few million previously locked-up coin supply becoming available to spend and negatively impacting price for a period of time.

This is most likely going to be a topic for the 2030s, but I continue to monitor the network’s readiness for those future challenges over time. I’m confident that the Bitcoin network will successfully upgrade to become quantum-resilient when the need arises for it, but there could very well be bumps along the way as it does.

## Deep Dive: HDFC Bank

India continues to be a bright spot for global growth. The [IMF](https://www.imf.org/external/datamapper/NGDP_RPCH@WEO/OEMDC/ADVEC/WEOWORLD/) has an anticipation of 7% inflation-adjusted GDP growth for India over the next year, followed by 6%+ annual growth per year as we head deeper into the 2020s.

![IMF Economic Growth Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-imf-economic-growth-map.png)

HDFC Bank (HDB) of India seems to be past the worst of its acquisition-related slump, and is worth a revisit.

The company, founded three decades ago in 1994, is the largest bank and financial services company in India, with a strong presence in banking, asset management, insurance, and other areas. Despite solid growth, the stock price has consolidated significantly in dollar-denominated terms in recent years, as it has went through a number of changes.

I could be wrong of course, but I do believe it’s increasingly likely that the bottom is in (specifically, the double-bottom of 2022 and 2024) and that the stock is likely to grind higher from here over time.

![HDB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-hdb-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 16.56x price/earnings in this case, based on their growth rate)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

The biggest event for HDFC Bank over the past few years has been the merger with their parent, the Housing Development Finance Corporation. The HDFC was the parent company of HDFC Bank and owned a significant percentage of its shares, although unintuitively HDFC Bank itself was larger than the parent company HDFC.

In spring 2022 it was announced that they would merge, and simplify and consolidate their overall structure. In summer 2023, the merger was complete. However, this was the biggest merger in Indian history, and the process of fully merging the two entities has extended well beyond the formal completion date. The negative result is that there has been a consolidation and slowdown in fundamentals, although it seems likely that the worst is behind them now.

The main issue, among other issues, is that the merger increased the loan-to-deposit ratio of the bank. Deposits are generally the cheapest type of capital for a bank, and the HDFC parent company has used more expensive types of capital than deposits to fund its activities. Over time, the combined bank plans to get back to its pre-merger loan-to-deposit ratio, but this its expected to take 2-3 more years. Along the way, it means the bank will continue to have sluggish loan growth, as its base of deposits grows more quickly and catches up with its loans outstanding.

We can see this issue most clearly when looking at the bank’s yield on assets relative to its cost of funds. Since the merger, the yield on assets increased slightly from 8.1% to 8.3%, while the cost of funds increased substantially from 4.0% to 4.9%, which reduces the overall net interest margin of the bank even though the bank as a whole is larger, with more assets and liabilities.

![HDB Yield Cost Divide](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-hdb-yield-cost-divide.png)

*Source: FY 2025 Q2 Earnings Presentation (October 2024)*

Here’s a chart that shows the jump up in assets and liabilities from the merger/acquisition:

![HDB Assets and Liabilities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-hdb-assets-liabilities.png)

Overall, the bank has a broad and safe set of asset exposures. They hold a diverse array of different assets, and have a diverse array of different income sources.

Their loan book is quite spread out, with the largest area being consumer loans, of which a significant chunk is tied to mortgages.

![HDB Loan Distribution](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-hdb-loan-distribution.png)

**Valuation**

The company is trading at about 21x earnings, which is expensive for a typical bank, but reasonable for HDFC’s rate of earnings growth and India’s overall rate of economic growth. They’re trading below their historical valuation average as shown by the F.A.S.T. Graph earlier, and I think that’s reasonable as the company becomes increasingly large and growth decelerates over time.

Financial services companies should also be valued by their price-to-book ratios, unlike many other industries where that metric is less relevant. At 3.6x the bank’s price/book ratio is pretty high in absolute terms, but low relative to its historical average, and with a rapidly-increasing book value overall:

![HDB Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-hdb-tangible-book-value.png)

Shares for the bank are held significantly by foreign investors, which include the FIIs/FPIs and ADR segments of the pie chart below. Domestic mutual funds, domestic individual investors, and other domestic financial services companies round out slightly less than half of the holdings:

![HDFC Bank Ownership](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-hdb-shareholders.png)

*Source: FY 2025 Q2 Earnings Presentation (October 2024)*

Overall, I expect that the valuation will gradually come down over time, but that this will be more than offset by growth, such that in five or ten years time, the valuation will be lower but the share price will be notably higher.

**Risks**

In my view, the primary risks to HDFC Bank are what have held down the stock so far: valuation reduction and slower-than-expected growth. Merger-related growing pains could persist for longer than the management team and market participants expect, resulting in yet another period of stock price weakness.

In terms of nominal solvency, I view risks as being low. The bank’s loan book is highly diversified, asset quality is high overall, and the bank is large enough to be a key bellwether for India as a whole. India currently has household debt-to-GDP at 43% and non-financial corporate debt-to-GDP at 56%, according to the Bank for International Settlements. These are very reasonable numbers, indicating that while India does occasionally experience equity overvaluation and other economic challenges, debt is not really one of their worries at this time. I don’t see any macro-scale issues that could greatly disrupt HDFC Bank from that angle in the nominal sense.

For foreign investors, India’s currency is a big variable to monitor. HDFC Bank’s assets and liabilities are primarily denominated in India’s currency, and so any rapid devaluation of the currency relative to dollars or other major currencies would limit foreign shareholders’ gains when measured in dollars or their own currencies. The country has a workable government debt/GDP ratio of 81%, and the combined private and public sector has low dollar-denominated debt relative to GDP, unlike most developing countries. The biggest negative for the currency is the country’s structural trade deficit, which hasn’t been an acute issue so far but could become one if there is a prolonged energy supply crunch due to war or similar events.

![India Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-india-trade-balance.png)

Overall, I view nominal/crisis risk for the bank to be relatively low, but for purchasing power risk to be moderate for HDFC Bank. India is a country with a rising population and among the fastest-growing economies in the world, and owning its largest bank is a way to get broad-based exposure to that.

With the parent company merger behind them, the future looks bright, but the risk for delays, setbacks, and bumps along the way remain substantial.

## Final Thoughts: Dubai

I’m writing this report from Dubai in the United Arab Emirates, as my husband and I are visiting some close friends here. We’ll be heading back to Egypt in a few days.

Along with Egypt, the UAE is one of the new members of BRICS+, having joined in 2024. The economy here is of course a strong contrast to that of Egypt, however, with most indicators looking positive. They have decent GDP growth, low unemployment, low inflation, a trade surplus, and a government surplus:

![UAE Economy](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-22-uae-economy.png)

*Table Source: Trading Economics*

Along with Shanghai, the UAE has emerged as a strong gold trading hub for the BRICS+ and other similar nations, and as a large oil exporter is a key puzzle piece that helps BRICS+ fit together and complement each other well. Similar to Singapore, Dubai has emerged as a strong financial center and kind of “neutral ground” for a lot of different entities from around the world to operate in.

Back during 2008 on the eve of the global financial crisis, the UAE fell victim to the classic “Skyscraper Curse” where they built the tallest skyscrapers in the world and then fell into severe economic problems. During the bubble/boom period, UAE had a massive constructive wave, culminating in the Burj Khalifa that was built from 2004 through 2009 and still remains by far the tallest human structure in the world, at least until Jeddah Tower in Saudi Arabia is completed later this decade.

However, since the UAE had strong oil revenues (mainly Abu Dhabi) and good enough economic policies to continue to attract global capital, global visitors, and global businesses, they managed to continue to grow out of it, albeit at a reduced (and more sustainable) pace of GDP growth and credit formation:
