## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

In the current era of fiscal dominance (which I would generally categorize as beginning in 2019), liquidity has been a driving force for many asset prices even more-so than it has been historically.

Domestic liquidity has been flat-to-up since bottoming in late 2022 and early 2023.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Federal Reserve continues to run tight monetary policy, but the Treasury has offset that since late 2022 by running large deficits and shortening its average debt duration and helping to bring capital from reverse repos back into the financial system. As a result, the first three quarters of 2022 were particularly bad for liquidity, but the five quarters from late 2022 through 2023 have been decent for liquidity.

This following chart shows the rate-of-change of domestic liquidity, where higher means better liquidity and down means worse liquidity. Sometimes the Fed and Treasury are moving in a similar direction, but more often than not they are moving in opposite directions and it comes down to a difference in magnitudes between them. The most recent major trend changes (Fed in early 2022 and Treasury in late 2022) are highlighted in green:

![Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-liquidity.png)

That period during the first three quarters of 2022 was particularly brutal as both the Fed and Treasury were pointing downward for liquidity. Ever since the Treasury flipped the other way, it has been a more mixed situation with a mild upward bias.

Meanwhile, the U.S. broad money supply was decreasing until spring 2023, but then began turning flat and more recently, upward:

![M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-m2.png)

Looking globally, the dollar index matters quite a bit, and it has been moderately strengthening here in 2024. To summarize the past couple of years, it was rising in 2021 and 2022 until Q4 of 2022 coinciding with the bottom in liquidity, fell sharply in Q4 of 2022 as the Treasury’s operations became more positive for liquidity, and then has been flat throughout 2023 and into 2024.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-dollar-index.png)

The global broad money supply denominated in dollars is a broad and straightforward global liquidity measure, and it has been consolidating sideways for two years generally but is well off its late-2022 lows. The base effects from exactly 12 months ago have reset the year-over-year percentage change (orange line) to zero, even as the absolute figure (blue line) remains roughly sideways:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-global-m2.png)

Overall, I expect flat-to-upward liquidity in 2024, and rising liquidity over the next few years, but I continue to monitor it every two weeks both quantitatively and policy-wise to look for potential changes to that outlook.

**U.S. Economy**

Over the past two years, the U.S. economy has been in sharp divergence mode. Some cyclical sectors have been sharply decelerating and then ultimately contracting, like manufacturing. Commercial real estate was severely damaged by the combination of work-from-home trends and higher interest rates. But industries directly or indirectly on the receiving side of the large fiscal deficits have been doing relatively good.

A big challenge over the past several months has been to determine whether the cyclical aspects of the economy are still decelerating or if they are finding a floor and starting to stabilize. There are great analysts on both sides of that debate. Liquidity and PMIs are starting to stabilize and turn upward, while some other areas continue to point downward.

Although I was in the recession camp during 2022, by early 2023 I’ve instead been straddling that debate somewhat by saying that there are no immediate signs of recession due to improving liquidity conditions, and to the extent that one materializes, I expect it to be a mild recession or “emerging market-like” recession due to the huge fiscal deficits that continue to stimulate parts of the economy. This coincided with the period where the Treasury began offsetting the Fed’s liquidity drain, and we entered a more neutral or positive liquidity regime.

Overall, the trends in my view are pointing towards economic re-acceleration as a base case, but some of the downward areas can turn bad in a nonlinear way. Therefore, I don’t rule out the possibility of recession either, and still view us to be in this indeterminate state.

PMIs and some of their leading sub-components are pointing upward from a low level:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-pmi.png)

The Atlanta Fed’s current data points toward no recession here in Q1, although much of these inputs can be subject to revisions in hindsight:

![Atlanta Fed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-atlanta-fed.png)

The cyclical side of the labor market (e.g. temporary workers and overtime hours) still looks weak. A drop of this magnitude is normally associated with a recession and there’s no significant sign of stabilization yet:

![Cyclical Labor](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-cyclical-labor.png)

Temporary workers were basically flat in January and technically ever-so-slightly up, which gave them the first uptick in two years. There were some months that were flat with a tiny downtick, so it’s all within the margin of error.

Overall, the majority of indicators I track suggest some degree of upward re-acceleration, but it’s still fragile and subject to change, and not confirmed by all indicators. If it were to be confirmed by all indicators, it would likely already be in the middle of the uptrend by that point.

**Global Economy**

The copper-to-gold ratio is something I check regularly as a sort of “tiebreaker” on global economic trends. It correlates significantly with global PMIs, and so far has not shown a firm sign of bottoming yet:

![Copper-Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-copper-gold.png)

The domestic economies of China and Europe continue to be weak. This is partially due to the difference in fiscal policy between the United States and the rest of the world. The large fiscal deficits are stimulatory, and while they can cause considerable problems later, as long as there is considerable foreign demand for U.S. assets to keep the exchange rate of the currency strong, it allows the United States to outperform its developed country peers and expand its marketshare of global GDP.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has been consolidating between $40k and $45k for the past two months.

Most people were either calling for a massive near-term rally due to new bitcoin spot ETF inflows or a big “sell the news” event once the ETFs were out, and instead what the market got was sideways choppy price action. It turned out to be kind of boring, in other words. The market tends to do what is least expected, and boring was what was least expected in this case.

Long-term however, I think the ETFs are constructive for price, and it does make sense for any asset of this size and liquidity to have spot ETFs for it. Within the first few weeks, thousands of bitcoin came out of the Grayscale GBTC ETF and flowed into the other ETFs that offer lower fees, but that trend is starting to slow down, and net inflows seem to gradually be taking over.

**Thinking Globally**

This was my main take about the ETFs as they launched, which I posted on the open-source communication protocol called Nostr:

![Nostr Post](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-nostr-post.png)

What I was referring to there is that within the past couple of years there has been a significant emergence of bitcoin hubs, meaning towns where there is a dense organization of bitcoiners building circular economies and local custody.

One of the earliest and most notable examples of a bitcoin hub was “Bitcoin Beach”, which is the town of El Zonte in El Salvador. That experiment ended up leading to the country adopting bitcoin as legal tender nationwide, although El Zonte is still far ahead of the rest of the nation in terms of actually using it. Dozens of other hubs have followed in Bitcoin Beach’s wake in countries all around the world, especially in countries with weak currencies but vibrant tech scenes.

Meanwhile, Egypt is currently experiencing a currency crisis, and is likely to perform another official devaluation soon. The black market exchange rate for people in Egypt that actually want to get their hands on dollars is currently around twice what the official/artificial rate is, since the official rate is unsustainably pegged. Bloomberg’s chief emerging markets economist Ziad Daoud reports:

![USD EGP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-usd-egp.png)

Nigeria, which unlike Egypt has one of the highest bitcoin and stablecoin adoption rates in the world (and the highest peer-to-peer trading volumes in the world according to Chainalysis), has also been undergoing a currency crisis. They previously had a big gap between the black market rate and the official rate like Egypt currently has, and they’ve now let that official rate blow-out. This chart shows the dollar to naira exchange rate, via Bloomberg:

![USD NGN](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-usd-ngn.png)

These are the two largest economies in Africa, with a combined population of over 300 million people.

This type of thing happens regularly around the world, affecting billions of people. From Latin America to Africa to southeast Asia, it’s the same story over and over.

A key thing that bitcoin and stablecoins do is that they give people around the world more of a choice for their money. The world consists of 160+ different currency monopolies that control their financial borders rather tightly, and for decades it has been the case that if people are born in a badly performing one, then they don’t have much recourse for that unless they are wealthy enough to get access to foreign bank/brokerage accounts.

Bitcoin and stablecoins give people more access to saving technologies and cross-border payment technologies regardless of how bad their local currency and banking system is, as long as they come to know how they work and have some internet access, which is indeed the case for many people in these countries.

**Swan Mining**

[Swan Bitcoin](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/), the private company I’m on the board of, announced the existence of their mining division. The company has been building and operating this segment in stealth throughout much of 2023, and unveiled it publicly on January 25th.

Guillaume Girard, a bitcoin mining analyst at Galaxy Digital, [described](https://twitter.com/GuerillaV2/status/1750572475870589107) it as “possibly the fastest ramp up in hashrate for a US company ever (starting from zero)”.

With over 5 exahashes per second in mining capacity, Swan’s machines collectively perform nearly 1% of the mining on the Bitcoin network, and Swan has publicly disclosed that they expect to get the rest of their machines online by the end of March, which will bring them to more than 8 exahashes per second.

The current number of 5 exahashes per second puts Swan on par with a number of publicly-traded miners in terms of scale today, and there are only four public-traded miners with more than 8 exahashes per second.

Swan now offers numerous services including retail bitcoin purchases, a bitcoin IRA option, various services for HNW individuals and businesses, collaborative custody (multi-signature vault) services, industry media including various shows and the annual Pacific Bitcoin event, institutional services such as bitcoin-collateralized non-rehypothecated lending and specialized investment opportunities, and now a large mining component.

**A Macro and Bitcoin Blend**

I first covered bitcoin publicly [back in 2017](https://www.lynalden.com/cryptocurrencies/) with a neutral view. Two and a half years later in April 2020, during the pandemic price crash, is when I [turned bullish](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-4-12/) on the asset. The price had consolidated for two and a half years by that point, I had continued monitoring the industry throughout the 2018-2019 bear market, and by then had built up enough conviction to go long. And then after that I began getting involved in the startup and venture side of the industry.

Here in 2024, I continue to find my work in the industry to be very informative and rewarding.

-Witnessing and overseeing Swan grow from a small startup to a large and diversified company from the inside as an advisor in 2020 and director since 2021 has provided useful insights on the nuts and bolts of growing a company in this industry.

-My venture-focused work at [Ego Death Capital](https://egodeath.capital/) since 2022 has been very interesting as well, because it helps me see which broad industry development pipelines are gaining traction and which ones are still in early development.

Back before I focused full time on finance, I worked as the head engineer of an aviation simulation facility and ran the tech-related finances for it. The purpose of the facility was/is to provide customizable cockpit platforms and plenty of experienced pilots for scientists to test new technologies or procedures with, rather than to train pilots as many other aviation simulation facilities focus on.

I had previously begun my career there as an electrical engineer before moving up through the ranks over the years, and any given project had electrical engineers, mechanical engineers, software engineers, aerospace engineers, and technicians working on it, so by that point it was my role to tie that together technically and financially. I had to determine which trade-offs to pursue between the different disciplines for a given project or the facility as a whole, to procure the parts and services that the engineers needed, and to provide financial projections to upper management and secure funding from them.

When I analyze the macroeconomics of the global money system and publish research on it, I’m mainly doing it from a systems engineering perspective. The domestic and global monetary system is for the most part a human-designed system, and just like any other system it has inputs and outputs and control logic associated with it, as well as various failure modes and interventions.

I often find that my macro analysis is about *describing problems*. I’m looking at the established centralized and closed-source monetary system with its various imperfections and built-up imbalances, and seeing how those problems are spilling over into other areas to cause economic impacts or investable outcomes. The current iteration of the monetary system was designed during a different era, and in the present time it has multiple growing pains and points of failure.

So, what I find most rewarding about my work in this industry is that it’s about allocating capital to engineers that are trying to fix problems and develop better ways of doing things. Bitcoin and stablecoins and related technologies are open-source and cross-border. They’re fresh and new and provide novel angles to solve problems that prior generations of tech couldn’t solve.

And so any small part I can play to get capital into the hands of capable engineers and alternatively to steer away from destructive uses of capital, is of great interest to me. In other words, it’s about *solving problems* once they’ve been described.

## Bank Sector and Equal-Weight Market Analysis

The U.S. economy continues to experience sharp divergence between industries that are very sensitive to interest rates and industries that are not.

One of the stocks I cover that has worked out nicely along these lines is American Express (AXP). Most banks continue to surprise me to the downside but this one has been doing well.

Several months ago in late summer 2023, I wrote about American Express as a stock that I was re-emphasizing and getting back into as a long trade. I added it back to the newsletter portfolio and the top-12 stock list. These were my remarks from that time:

> I bought American Express (AXP) during March 2020 for the newsletter portfolio as it crashed in price. I then sold it in June 2021, as I considered it to have gotten a bit ahead of itself. It then spent the next 2+ years chopping sideways. This one ended up being traded well so far.
>
> I’m now rather bullish again, after its 2+ year period of consolidation. I don’t really know what it will do over the next six months but I’m bullish with a 3-5 year view. American Express continues to have a strong network effect, and has an emphasis toward the travel industry.
>
> ![AXP FASTGraph Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-20-axp-fastgraph-annotated.jpg)
>
> -August 20th 2023 premium report

Since then, the company has posted great fundamental results. The stock has also performed very well because the equity valuation was not very expensive:

![AXP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-axp-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

I think the stock is potentially getting a bit ahead of itself in the near-term, but I continue to like it with a 3-5 year view. Some of that view has now been pulled forward by these results.

Perhaps what is most remarkable is its divergence in performance from other bank stocks recently. For a while they were all relatively in-sync with each other, but as of this most recent quarter’s reported earnings, the differences were quickly sorted out:

![AXP vs Banks](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-axp-vs-banks.png)

The seven biggest U.S. banks in aggregate have little commercial real estate exposure. This includes the top four money-center banks (JPM, BAC, WFC, and C) as well as the next three super-regionals (TFC, USB, and PNC). Some of them, however, do face considerable duration risk exposure which is more manageable but puts pressure on their growth expectations. As you go down from there toward medium-sized regional banks, there tends to be a lot of commercial real estate exposure on the books, which is an obvious risk and needs to be analyzed on a bank-by-bank basis to see how bad it is for them.

In general, I think a lot of “quality” stocks in other sectors are overvalued. Many investment strategies rank stocks or sectors by various quality metrics and emphasize them, but that trade can get overdone for periods of time and I would argue that it is now. For example, Costco (COST) is a particularly excellent and high-quality company, but it’s trading at valuations that make it seem like it has cured cancer or invented AI:

![COST FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-cost-fastgraph.png)

That being said, although I think the “quality” factor is overdone in the broad market, within the financial sector specifically I think emphasizing quality still makes sense during this period of time. There’s no bubble in high-quality stocks within low-quality sectors, even though there’s a bubble in many high-quality stocks within high-quality sectors.

With huge pandemic stimulus, followed by a huge liquidity drain and sharp increase in interest rates to subdue the inflation that the stimulus caused, any sort of duration-mismatched balance sheet has been pushed around rather sharply, and stressed to its limits. Banks of course are among that group, with a combination of significantly mismatching durations between assets and liabilities, and high leverage to amplify that mismatch. And then many banks have been further pressured by changing office work trends.

The financial sector is probably under-owned in general and is outside of the “quality” bubble that the market seems to be in, which gives the sector space to perform well over time. But within the sector, a lot of risk management is necessary, so I think it’s best to focus on quality within a low-quality sector. There are a lot of landmines out there.

New York Community Bancorp (NYCB) recently ran into a major dividend cut and stock price decline:

![NYCB YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-nycb-ychart.png)

The company greatly missed analyst expectations when they reported earnings for the latest quarter, mainly due to building up loan loss reserves to offset a weak commercial lending portfolio. But another headwind was that after their latest acquisitions, they crossed over the $100 billion asset threshold which subjected them to greater regulations.

Similarly, Aozora Bank of Japan (AOZOY) took a major stock price cut as it revealed quarterly results. Even though it’s a Japanese bank, the source of its problems was ironically its U.S. commercial loan portfolio.

![AOZOY YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-aozoy-ychart.png)

While it’s certainly a sector that should be interesting to deep value investors, there are likely still several shoes to drop in the commercial real estate market and among lenders that are heavily exposed to that sector. Interest rates don’t need to increase any more to cause ongoing problems there; merely holding rates at roughly the same level can continue to cause problems as certain debts mature and need to be refinanced over time.

I don’t view the weakness in the commercial real estate sector as likely being big enough to tank the whole economy, but it’s certainly an ongoing headwind, and it greatly affects companies involved in that sector. Leveraged commercial real estate is a slow-motion train wreck for the equity holders of those properties and parts of the banking industry. Banks that are not too exposed to it can gradually absorb the shocks from that sector, and along the way individual small/medium banks with too much exposure can get knocked around more sharply and in some cases might need to be taken over.

Meanwhile, with the largest seven money-center and super-regional banks, mainly what is happening is a gradual absorption of the problem. By slowing down the dividend growth and retaining more earnings, they can strengthen their balance sheets against potential losses from either commercial real estate or exposure to long-duration securities. This can cause a stagnation in earnings for a period of time (and they are already priced for that scenario), but the situation should have fewer overall negative shocks than a 2008-style acute financial crisis in aggregate.

Big and diversified banks are taking some body shots rather than knockout strikes, in other words. It’s generally the regional banks that are more exposed to knockout strikes.

**Equal Weight Market Update**

The typical S&P 500 or Nasdaq 100 indices are weighted by market capitalization. The top several stocks in them can make up a large percentage of the total holdings, and there can be a positive feedback loop where better performance from the big companies can result in more passive inflows to them and consequently higher valuations.

But sometimes those market-weighted indices become overstretched in terms of valuation. This happened back in 2021, and they subsequently faced a very rough year for 2022.

Meanwhile, equal weight indices were less explosive to the upside in 2021, but then held up better during the rough year in 2022. A similar pattern may be setting up again. The market weight S&P 500 sharply outperformed in 2023, while the equal weight version has been consolidating and might be gearing up for a breakout ahead:

![RSP Equal Weight ETF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-rsp.png)

If we look at a ratio chart between the equal weight version and market weight version, we’re at a rather pivotal point:

![Equal vs Market Weight Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-equal-vs-market-performance.png)

In terms of sectors, which version outperforms is mostly a matter whether the technology sector outperforms or underperforms. The equal-weight version has less tech exposure and higher exposure to most other sectors, so in years where tech outperforms, the equal weight version underperforms. In years where tech struggles, the equal weight version tends to hold up better. These images are via State Street and Invesco based on their market weight (SPY) and equal weight (RSP) products respectively:

![Sector Breakdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-sector-breakdowns.png)

At current valuations, I personally like the equal weight version more. If the re-acceleration of PMIs and other cyclical indicators gathers some momentum, it should benefit the equal weight index as some of those stocks surprise to the upside and some of the over-owned top stocks in the market weight are trimmed.

## Final Thoughts: Watching China

China’s stock market has been incredibly weak. Starting in late 2020 at the top of their market, their government began cracking down on their tech companies to an unusual degree, which led many investors to be increasingly concerned regarding capital allocation to the country. Other companies were impaired by the various extended-duration lockdowns and real estate deleveraging.

Subsequently, foreign direct investment began to decline. And then in 2022, Russia invaded Ukraine and was sanctioned, meaning that western investors effectively got zeroed-out on Russian equities even if those companies were still performing fine.

With all of this occurring and with an eye toward China/Taiwan conflict risks, foreign investors began pulling out of China in both public markets and private markets. By 2023, China had their first negative foreign direct investment year in decades:

![China FDI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-china-fdi.jpg)

In recent weeks, however, China’s government has finally shown signs of concern and action toward their stock market. Stock prices have reached depths that have concerned the domestic public and is starting to elicit stimulus from the government, with more prioritization toward the stock market itself.

![MCHI Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-4-mchi.png)

There’s a good chance that this is becoming a tradeable bottom for Chinese equities. If I was a non-western investor, I’d be looking to potentially prioritize them significantly if various momentum or technical indicators turn up a bit and start making some minor higher highs and higher lows.

The challenge for investors globally but western investors particularly, however, is that they have to navigate the nonzero chance that within the next 5+ years or so, they could get zeroed out of the investments for any number of reasons, with conflict over Taiwan being the main one.

Therefore, as a small position I’m starting to find Chinese equities interesting again. But I mainly express it through broad emerging market ETFs, rather than major positions in individual stocks.
