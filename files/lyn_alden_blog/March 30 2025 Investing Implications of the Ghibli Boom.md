## Macro View

**Latest Economic Checkup**

In my prior reports, I commented about how Q1 2025 economic data is disturbed by an influx of import front-running.

In response to actual tariffs or in many cases just potential tariffs, many entities have decided to import things sooner rather than later, or otherwise repatriate things that they might not have otherwise repatriated any time soon.

My report from two weeks ago summarized the issue:

> The recent tariff uncertainty has resulted in an abnormally large spike of gold imports to the United States. A typical month might see $1-2 billion in gold imports, but in December it was $10 billion and in January it was $30 billion. These numbers are reported with a bit of a lag. Investors are likely protecting themselves around the margins in the event that gold they own overseas gets tariffed.
>
> This won’t have a large impact on GDP because most of that gold is not about economic activity. We can think of it more like a capital repatriation flow. But it does distort certain economic metrics that don’t separate it out from other types of imports.
>
> Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow continues to have a comical dislocation, with gold imports playing a big role in it. In my prior report, I warned that we can’t take this at face value even if it is notable:
>
> ![Atlanta Fed GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-gdp-now.png)
>
> The overall U.S. trade balance has a similar comical-looking drop to record lows, thanks partially to these gold movements which realistically are different than most other aspects of the trade balance:
>
> ![Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-trade-balance.png)
>
> Data aggregates aside, a more serious question is to ask, if some entities are front-running the risk of tariffs by importing gold, what *other things* might they be doing this with that *do* affect the economy in a business operation sense?
>
> For example, in response to U.S. tariff proposals, Europe threatened retaliatory 50% tariffs on U.S. whiskey, and in response President Trump threatened retaliatory-on-retaliatory [200% tariffs](https://apnews.com/article/trump-tariffs-wine-spirits-200-alcohol-dabead2d0e0fad64dc5e6250293ef016) on European wine and spirits. This is one of many ongoing uncertainties, and for example how many U.S. wine sellers and importers would go ahead and front-load some of their orders to protect against the probability of that going into effect? If some of them do, that would impact the trade balance within a given month or quarter, but it’s not really economic activity; it would just be pulling forward some demand as a protective measure which would mean fewer orders later, whether or not the tariffs go into effect since they would’ve already have stocked-up on inventory.
>
> This is likely to continue to be a noisy data period, both for investors and businesses. Even when the Q1 GDP report comes out, it won’t necessarily be high quality data.
>
> -March 16, 2025 premium report

## Investment Implications of The Ghibli Boom

During the past week, a new tech update initiated a meme trend of nearly unprecedented scale spread around the world, and reached all the way up to the White House.

Here’s an example of Mike Tyson with a 384,000 like post, as one tiny part of the trend:

![Tyson Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-tyson-tweet.jpg)

The context is that ChatGPT released a new capability that lets people upload a picture and ask the AI to redraw it in some other style. And it’s frighteningly accurate for two-dimensional art styles. The result was that millions of people around the world posted AI-generated pictures in Studio Ghibli’s art style.

![Altman Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-altman-tweet.png)

You can make a family photo where all of you look like you’re drawn by the Simpsons creators. You could take a popular meme, and ask it to re-draw it in the One Piece art style. There are endless combinations.

In this detailed section, I’ll cover two background points, then describe the trend for those that aren’t familiar or who might be lacking context, then I’ll talk about some specific nuts and bolts of it, and conclude with some investment implications.

**Background Point One: AI Art Generation 101**

I have personally been experimenting with AI art generation for about two years now. And not just a few hours of dabbling; it’s been quite a hobby of mine that I lose weekends into sometimes. So, I’ve been able to see some of the step-by-step progressions of it first-hand rather than just hearing about it. I’ve also experienced many of its frustrations and shortcomings.

Other than occasionally using it for a piece of article header art, I haven’t used it commercially. Even for most article header art, I prefer using existing public domain photographs for that purpose.

Instead, I primarily use AI art generation for creating images that I can use when playing tabletop games with my husband and friends. Examples include custom character illustrations or landscapes, meant for private entertainment. Or for my own amusement or side projects.

After one particular weekend of playing around with MidJourney back in December 2023, I put together a public art piece called “[The Four Monies](https://www.lynalden.com/the-four-monies/)” as a baseline test of where we were at with the technology:

![Four Monies](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-four-monies.jpg)

And coincidentally, it was just used in a presentation on the other side of the world in India this morning:

![Art India](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-art-india.jpg)

A more recent purpose is that I’ve been using AI for mock-ups, when I want to conceptualize an idea for a more professional illustration of some sort.

Rather than taking business away from artists, I have two artists contracted for a rather expensive visual project later this year, paying for a full month or more of each of their work, for which my AI mockups are helpful to provide them as part of the collaboration process. In addition to ethical reasons why I would hire them, a key practical reason is simply that none of the AI tools can yet create a custom piece at the precision and quality and unique style the project requires, and that those two artists are capable of.

A challenge I’ve run into for two years, including here in the present day, is getting the AI to follow directions. The tools keep getting better, but getting the AI to actually listen to you for precise instructions after even a dozen increasingly refined requests is still extremely hard.

Even once you get close, you rarely get it perfect, because there is a “last mile” problem. At a certain point, instructions stop getting followed. The reason has to do with the fact that the AI doesn’t actually understand the underlying logic enough.

For example, if I want a scar on someone’s face to look a certain way in a piece of art, I can describe to a human artist how the scar happened and how it should look, with some sketches or sample pictures. Their first or second pro sketch might not be perfect, but with some input by me, after a relatively small number of attempts, we’ll get to a result that almost 100% matches my vision. They’ll potentially even exceed my vision of it.

With AI, it doesn’t grasp the underlying logic of say, what a knife cut or a bullet impact would do on a given area, and instead it is mainly just pattern-matching the visual concept of a scar on a surface without appreciating the underlying logic. It has trouble following my directions. Depending on the complexity, it might get 90% there, or might literally not be able to produce an acceptable outcome of what I’m asking it do even after 20 prompts and refinements.

So, we’re still early, but my conclusion is that I’ve found AI art to be great fun for casual aspects and professional mock-ups, but less-so for final professional works unless they’re simple enough. Even when AI reaches the level of being able to create professional works with well-established styles, the next concern will be that it can’t yet forge ahead on truly new styles itself, and instead is mainly a great remixer tool.

**Background Point Two: Studio Ghibli**

Japan has long-since punched above its weight in terms of visual media, with a heavy concentration in animation. Compared to other countries of similar or larger population and GDP, Japanese visual media spreads to North American, European, and global viewers to a shocking degree.

-In the West, for a long time animations or “cartoons” were generally written for children or mild Sunday newspaper amusement, or for a political point.

-In Japan, animation styles of various types have long since been associated with children’s programming, or for teenagers, or for adults. Written content of any age level or genre could be turned into manga or anime. This trend eventually spread elsewhere to some degree, with certain examples of western animation aimed at late teen and adult audiences with very positive reception (e.g. “Arcane” on Netflix or “Invincible” on Amazon).

Among all Japanese animation studios, there is probably none more storied than Studio Ghibli. They’re often called the “Disney of Japan” to give a one-sentence description, but that sells them short. They produce some of the most acclaimed and watched animated content in the world.

Founded in 1985, Studio Ghibli primarily produces animated movies, and they reach a global audience with commercial and critical acclaim. While they do focus on younger audiences like Walt Disney Animation Studios, they also have an edgier/darker aspect, and yet simultaneously with an extremely wholesome aspect in terms of ethical themes and the lived-in community/nature details of the world that they depict. That’s the combination that makes them unique.

Over the past four decades, they’ve had entries all along the top end of Japan’s top-grossing international films list, with *Spirited Away* being their most successful film financially. Their primary creator, Hayao Miyazaki, is internationally viewed as a genius and among the top tier filmmakers of the world, but like Walt Disney Animation Studios at their peak, there has also been a strong depth of studio talent around Miyazaki that can operate independently but within the same group.

I personally grew up watching several of their films, with 1997’s Princess Mononoke and 2001’s Spirited Away being among my favorites. (Contrary to what the name might imply, Princess Mononoke was one of their more violent ones; with major themes centered around industrialization vs nature).

![Ghibli Posters](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-ghibli-posters.jpg)

Their darkest film ever was probably 1988’s Grave of the Fireflies, which depicted two orphaned and starving children trying to survive the final months of Japan losing World War II.

Spirited Away hit the studio’s global sweet spot in terms of audience and accolades, because it stars a young child navigating a strange spiritual world, and it was suitable for children while having a thematic depth of level of storytelling that is equally relevant for children and adults as a literary tale. Most of their stories aim somewhere pretty wide along that spectrum, and that one hit the entire spectrum.

**1 + 1 = Millions**

Solving part of my previously-mentioned “last mile” problem is hard, and it’s still nowhere near solved for unique prompts.

But what triggered this recent massive wave of viral usage, was a new ChatGPT upgrade which enabled users to give the AI a specific picture, and it’ll draw your picture in any art style you want, and follow some basic instructions like if you want to eliminate something from the background.

So while it can’t just automatically replace any artist (as some fear-oriented responses say), it opens up all sorts of customization options for billions of people.

There was an initial spark of people posting Studio Ghibli style posts of things, and that caught on as one of the world’s most recognizable art styles, and so almost everyone trended toward that.

The initial online use-case for turning pictures into Ghibli-style is basically memes, and recreating the entire backlog of popular memes in other formats.

But the quieter and yet potentially broader use-case is, for example, recreating family pictures. A humorous Twitter/X post summarized it:

![Alpha Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-alpha-tweet.jpg)

He then continued with a [whole thread](https://x.com/GrantSlatton/status/1904649625749119437) sharing examples of people animating their most cherished family pics of their spouses, children, pets, etc.

I initially tested it by feeding it some of my public pictures. I avoided copyrighted stuff or family/friends so I could share them publicly, as modifications of already public pictures:

![Lyn Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-lyn-tweet.jpg)

Never before have I seen a tool that could so reliably recreate existing pictures in other styles on the first try, or at all. And I tested it in multiple different styles.

And then my post inevitably spawned further examples of family pictures:

![Niece Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-niece.jpg)

In the subsequent days, I received plenty of example pics from friends of their families that are not shared publicly, and also created some family pics myself and sent them to those involved. That’s the iceberg under the surface. People are creating all sorts of content privately with their friends and family.

**Limitations and Advancements**

-Currently, the technology is advanced enough that it can recreate two-dimensional animation styles with a frightening degree of identifiability. Someone might see pictures and just see “anime” but there are myriad individual styles of anime (e.g. Studio Ghibli vs One Piece), as well as Western styles of animation (e.g. the Simpsons vs DC Comics) and the tool can create *specific* ones very accurately, with Studio Ghibli being the main trend upon the new tool’s launch.

-It can create somewhat interesting 3D fixed broader art styles (e.g. van Gogh or Monet), but unlike those 2D styles, you’ll be able to tell right away they’re kind of “off”. The technology needs to progress to recreate those more accurately.

-2D animated motion pictures is increasingly doable. It’s already being flirted with; PJ Ace said he spent $250 and 9 hours to [recreate](https://x.com/PJaccetturo/status/1905151192948478055) the Lord of the Rings trailer in this Studio Ghibli style. It doesn’t recreate the audio, and so the mouths don’t move with the voices well, but it’s a serious mix of animated scenes that one-by-one match the live action trailer. We can at least see a glimpse into the future of how this will be possible.

-3D animated motion pictures will naturally take more time for these tools to recreate than that. But we can extrapolate out to see it on the horizon.

Here we can also check back to my prior point about AI art generation tools not really understanding logic.

On March 26th, I recorded a podcast with Mark Jeffery. As he was editing it that day, and while this Ghibli boom was occurring, he posted a funny screenshot from the show where he looked like he had a lot of emotion, and fed it into an older AI model (rather than ChatGPT with this new feature). That model provided rather poor results that didn’t capture his facial hair properly, didn’t capture my hair properly, didn’t understand the background at all, and had more errors with limbs and such.

I quickly put that same screenshot into ChatGPT and got a more representative (and still funny) version:

![Mark Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-mark-tweet.jpg)

But my favorite thing about that exercise was seeing a glimpse of how the model “thinks”.

Since it was a podcast, the input was a split-screen image. On my wall, I have pictures of cherry blossoms and butterflies. On Mark’s wall, he has various pictures of framed content. His pieces are smaller, and generally don’t show up well in the low-resolution screenshot he captured.

However, AI is primarily an *extrapolation* machine, whether for language or images. And so, Mark had one picture on his wall that kind of has a butterfly-shape when analyzed in low resolution, even though it’s not a butterfly. Chat GPT’s AI model did not understand the split screen aspect of the image, and it assumed that because my image is a blue butterfly, that Mark’s unclear image right next to it (despite being on the other side of the split screen) might also be a blue butterfly. It was a semi-logical (albeit incorrect) extrapolation from pattern recognition rather than having enough logic or awareness to truly understand the context of the image:

![How ChatGPT Works](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-how-chat-gpt-works.jpg)

I found that interesting. That’s the type of reason for why, going back to my prior example of asking AI to get a scar logically correct, sometimes no amount of tries can fix an issue to perfection. It’s extrapolating from existing work and patterns, not understanding your prompt from first principles.

That might change in the future, but it’s been a challenge for years, it’s a fundamental part of how these models work currently, and it’s likely to take a long time to solve even as the overall models keep rapidly improving.

**Ethical and Legal Concerns**

Back in 2016, Hayao Miyazaki, the mastermind behind Studio Ghibli (who was in his 70s then, and his 80s now) was shown an AI video, and he [said](https://x.com/nuberodesign/status/1904954270119588033) (translated from Japanese):

> I am utterly disgusted. If you want to go ahead and make creepy stuff, go ahead and do it. I would never wish to incorporate this technology into my work at all. I strongly feel that this is an insult to life itself.

The younger team that showed him explained that it was just an experiment. He then asked what their goal was. One of them said to build a machine that can draw like humans do. He rhetorically asked, “would you?” in a negative tone. In a later part of the edit, outside of that scene, he said “I feel like we are nearing to the end of times. We humans are losing faith in ourselves.”

Some people take this today to say that Miyazaki would hate this trend of people Ghibli-izing their pictures. He might end up commenting, and indeed he probably wouldn’t love it, but it’s not the same thing as the original context. His response to a 2016 terrible-looking animation is not necessarily applicable to the world recreating all forms of content in Studio Ghibli style. And by extension, this trend was the biggest-ever global marketing event for Studio Ghibli.

AI generation doesn’t create truly new things that fill us with wonder. It remixes humanity’s greatest hits. It lets people apply the wonder of Studio Ghibli to their own life, which is in itself kind of a minor miracle. Of course Studio Ghibli should keep focusing on hand-drawn art; that’s part of what makes the result so warm and unique and why so many people enjoyed porting it over to their own photos.

Studio Ghibli does incorporate some new technologies over time (i.e. a 2020s Ghibli film is visibly better than a 1980s one), so AI might play a role in there at some point, but at the end of the day they’re humans creating beautiful art, so it makes sense that Miyazaki would be disgusted by seeing a 2016 AI video and said he wouldn’t incorporate it into his work. His studio’s themes for decades have also heavily analyzed nature vs industrialization.

So, Miyazaki is not realistically making these comments out of fear, as though every Ghibli recreation of a meme or someone’s family photo takes away from him and his team as artists. Neither he or anyone at the top of their art thinks this technology is anywhere near capable of producing a film that seems like it was written and animated by Studio Ghibli, with all of the heart and soul of those films, that could commercially or critically compete with them.

The scene-by-scene nuances they create that somehow, every time, capture human life, family life, the glory of nature, and then various corruptions against that ideal (and yet are often from villains that themselves are relatable, and struggling themselves as well). They spend years on a film, scene by scene, line by line, channeling some of the deepest human experiences.

Critics of this phenomenon are taking his response to a soulless-looking AI video in 2016, about whether he’d want to incorporate it into his own art, his own studio, and applying it nearly a decade later to a world that can recreate all sorts of images in his studio’s warm style, which is an activity that has given millions of people and families bursts of joy. It expands the warmth that his studio has created, rather than compete with it or enter into it. It’s a 180-degree different context.

It’s kind of like when Martin Scorsese offhandedly said in an interview that Marvel movies were more like theme parks than movies, and don’t count as cinema. However, once that reached media broad attention and he got pushback about it, Scorsese quickly wrote a more detailed [opinion piece](https://www.nytimes.com/2019/11/04/opinion/martin-scorsese-marvel.html) in the NYT where he softened and clarified his stance given more room and context. He basically said that they’re not for him, and that he doesn’t view them as high art. To interject an example, he’s basically saying *Captain America* and *The Godfather* aren’t really comparable pieces of work. Which is true.

People recreating their family photos Ghibli-style is generally an act of love and warmth. Someone trying to recreate Ghibli art for *commercial* purposes using these tools is 1) unlikely to recreate their magic and 2) generally unethical or at least somewhat soulless in their approach even if they manage to get a quick buck.

So inevitably, when a trend of this scale takes off, people talk about the legal ramifications.

-AI models freely learning from multiple forms of art to build a dataset and then collecting revenue from subscribers to remix some of that art is indeed a big deal. It has ethical, legal, social, political, and economic implications. But not always the implications we think.

-When filesharing took off, people spoke about the death of the music industry. Instead, what happened over time was that cheaper/free music helped to serve as marketing for bands, and they increasingly made their money from live performances and merchandise from a userbase that found it easier to access their music and get to know them. And people do pay for premium streaming services, with a big percentage going to artists. Today, bands generally post their content for free on YouTube and such. Simply hearing their music is free. *Experiencing it* is where they make money.

-Many well-known authors have often found that when their books are pirated, their paid sales unintuitively go up. If people are trying to pirate your book, that’s actually a good sign. The vast majority of authors face the opposite problem: they could literally offer it for free and yet nobody wants it. There are an infinite number of books out there. When a book is pirated, it means people are talking about it, and some percentage of those hearing about it will buy it. Saifedean Ammous, a best-selling author, famously links to pirate sites for his books. Sometimes when the link goes dead as pirate sites sometimes do, he asks people to help him find new links to pirate sites.

![Saifedean Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-saifedean-tweet.jpg)

-Many famous authors, like Brandon Sanderson, have sold $250 version of their books in leather-bound formats to super-fans who love the work and want to eternalize the literature on their shelf and flex to their friends. My good friend Alex Gladstein, the chief strategy officer of the Human Rights Foundation, is selling a leather-bound collected edition of his works, and there are 100 uniquely signed and numbered copies. On the auction site [Scarce.city](https://scarce.city/sales/a-trojan-horse-for-freedom-collected-works-by-alex-gladstein), they’re currently going for about $800, with three left. All of the proceeds go to open source Bitcoin development. I posted pictures on Twitter/X of my copy to highlight its quality. People can find Sanderson’s or Gladstein’s works in basic formats easily, and free if they try hard enough. And yet these custom products sell.

![Golden Book](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-golden-book.jpg)

-For my own book, *Broken Money*, I don’t go after any pirate sites. People who like me or my brand, and want to buy the best formats, pay money for the book. But *Broken Money* is also written for people around the world, often in developing countries where money is the most broken, and the cost of a book is substantial for many of them. I’d rather them read it for free than not read it at all. So, I never contest piracy at that scale. I would only take issue if some major corporation decided to pirate it in the United States or other big developed markets for it, which is not really a thing that happens in the industry. So I reserve rights, but view most attempts at piracy as a compliment rather than a threat, which is substantiated by broad industry trends.

-Another issue is with text-based content theft. Google looks through websites all around the world, and used to primarily send searchers to the right website for their needs. However, they now increasingly just put an AI answer at the top of the search results, which they generated by looking through everyone’s content. They link to sources, but searchers often don’t go to those websites because Google answered the question right within the search screen.

Bill Gross (the one famous from Idea Lab, not Pimco) recently founded a company called [ProRata](https://www.prorata.ai/) that provides solutions for AI to attribute and give back to those whose content was used. I have no affiliation with it, but from speaking with him, he put it on my radar, and it’s something I find very interesting. I like how he approaches things and am glad to see people are working on approaches to the problem.

> Current AI answer engines rely on shoplifted, plagiarized content. This creates an environment where creators get nothing, and disinformation thrives. ProRata is pro-author, pro-artist and pro-consumer. Our technology allows creators to get credited and compensated while consumers get attributed, accurate answers.
>
> -Bill Gross

![ProRata](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-prorata.png)

I don’t know what the ultimate resolution will look like, but much like the music industry or the book publishing industry, I do think the economics of visual creatorship and website content will change over time. There could be certain landmark court outcomes that affect the impact of the biggest centralized aggregators. There could be adjustments by creators themselves, and the consumers of their content. And it still remains to be seen how capable AI will be of creating new art at high levels, rather than just remixing existing art.

In other words, there’s no doubt that AI can help people create advertisements, illustrations, mock-ups, text, and so forth. And much of that draws upon the existing state of the art.

However, it also makes some of those services cheaper, and thus expands those capabilities to new people that couldn’t previously afford them at all. More people can dabble in custom graphics than they could a few years ago, thanks to AI. But at high levels, if someone wants to create something special and new, AI isn’t really capable of that yet.

Plus, artists themselves can use AI as a tool. For example, graphic designers themselves can use AI for mockups to figure out their final work. This can save time and enable them to do more projects for clients.

I’m bearish on the idea that people will just use AI to recreate high quality art, at least for a while. We’ll see how good these tools get.

Instead, I’m bullish on the idea that some of these tools will expand the pie for who can access this type of work, and increase the productivity of those who produce this work, especially at a high level.

It will also have a confusing period of shuffling around the economics of certain industries, which can make it look bullish or bearish at various times as it swings around and adjusts, much like what happened to the music industry and the filesharing/streaming industry.

**What the Next Frontier Means**

When extrapolating this to the future, I have to first talk about an example from the past.

From 2005 through 2008, Nickelodeon ran one of their most popular animated series ever: *Avatar: the Last Airbender*. Somehow, they managed to make a children’s/teenager’s show whose premise was about literal genocide, while also being warm and heartfelt, and it was both commercially and critically popular. The protagonists searched for meaning and growth and redemption and peace with a backdrop of (semi-filtered, Disney-style) wide-scale war and loss.

In addition to a good story and character writing, part of what made *Avatar: The Last Airbender* successful was that they took visual choreography very seriously. There were four different types of elemental magic users, and they would do various forms of Kung-Fu which would generate enhanced fire/air/water/earth magical effects. Each style was distinct, and the elemental outcomes closely matched the physical moves of the fighters doing them, which made for a very logical and aesthetically pleasing outcome. The creators worked with Sifu Kisu as their key expert choreographer, and then partnered with him again on their 2012-2014 sequel series for teenagers and young adults, *The Legend of Korra*, which had similar levels of quality.

In 2010, M. Night Shyamalan adapted the first season of the popular show into a live-action movie. If successful, it could have begun a massive live-action movie franchise, potentially on the scale of something like Harry Potter.

Instead, the first movie didn’t fully make its money back and was panned by critics and audiences. As of this writing, it has a 5% “fresh” rating by critics and a 30% “fresh” rating by audiences on Rotten Tomatoes. The sequels were cancelled.

There were multiple reasons why it didn’t work out that are beyond the scope of this article, including the writing of course, but one of the key ones for the purpose of this piece was that they did not use the existing choreographer. The resulting choreography was publicly mocked for being of very low quality.

Instead of each fighter’s punch/kick/movement being closely matched by an elemental outcome, there would be weird scenes of someone throwing multiple punches and kicks and then afterward a single rock slowly floats toward an enemy to hit them, uncorrelated to the movements of the one doing it. This was a $150 million film in 2010 (basically double that today), so the budget wasn’t the limiting factor. The creators just didn’t have their pulse on it, didn’t have the attention to detail, that made the original so good. And then the original team went and created *The Legend of Korra* and showed they could do it again after Shyamalan’s team stumbled.

In the handful of years that followed the 2010 Shyamalan/Airbender flop, as YouTube rose in popularity as a platform, multiple individuals who were fans of the series made CGI-enhanced videos of themselves performing the choreography associated with the series.

A particularly good one, early on, involved a young man performing various martial arts moves, and various earth magic “earthbending” effects would happen, like boulders being ripped out of the ground and flying in the direction of his strikes, quite well aligned with the moves as the cartoon did. It looked great for such a shoestring budget in the 2010s, to the point where Shyamalan’s $150 million production should have hired that amateur to do 10x better than they did. A wave of such videos followed, as proof that the capability was there, for those that paid attention to details and cared about the subject material.

That was kind of a “first glimpse” into high-budget replications, or a glimpse into the decentralization of creatorship in what was historically only a high-budget blockbuster type of medium. Individual fans or small groups of friends with some pocket money could literally (and by a wide margin) outperform a $150 million bad movie in terms of CGI-enhanced magical Kung Fu. They couldn’t have made those videos a decade earlier.

In 2024, Netflix released an 8-episode live action season of the show to try again. While it didn’t capture the success of the original, it held up better than the 2010 film and sequel seasons are moving forward. It costs $15 million per episode.

Back to the present, as a decade of creator tools have improved since then, the barriers to entry are falling. What was first the exception, is now becoming more commonplace. Those with more money (combined with skill and taste) can of course create better visuals than low-budget shops, but what was once \*only\* possible by major studios can now be explored to some serious degree by low-budget shops.

-There was a period where being a radio or television talk show was a financially challenging activity, and then podcasting technology made it ubiquitous and accessible. The industry went through growing pains and still is, but over time, a variety of quality shows emerged, reached major scales, became polished, and even big-name news and radio people moved toward podcasting.

-There was a period where it was hard to publish a book without a big publisher, and the publishers were mostly concentrated around New York. Various technologies made it easier to self-publish, but of course the vast majority of early self-published books were terrible, since they had almost no gatekeeper. But every once in a while there was an outlier success, including among those that gatekeepers otherwise rejected or would have altered negatively. Over time, the quality in the space became more serious, at least for readers who know how to filter to the top 1% of that long tail. And a whole industry of professional editors and cover designers sprung up around that new space to further enhance its quality and skate where the puck was going.

-Movies and shows are kind of the final frontier here. It’s inherently more expensive to make a moving visual medium than to make radio or text. We needed time to let technology progress for a while. But we will likely reach a point soon where individual creators or small groups can create rather compelling visual movies and shows if they have the right blend of skill and taste. Of course, like any other medium, the long tail of them will be weak, but there will be gems.

-A hybrid model is likely to reach product-market fit. For example, imagine being able to use AI to help visualize certain characters or settings, and then hire graphic designers to help bring them to life with *new and unique aspects* *in their style*, and then use AI to *animate* those uniquely-designed characters, thus transforming them from a static image to a moving one. Of course, for that to work properly, the graphic designer would likely need to be hired to provide multiple images at different angles. But that’s still more efficient than hand-drawing each one, which might work for a major studio but is cost prohibitive to individual creators or small teams. This blend can reduce the cost of making many types of motion pictures, and yet there are humans in the loop injecting fresh ideas and fresh content. The result could be that more content is created, not less.

**Tying This Into Investments**

Some investors think that Alphabet (GOOGL) will be disrupted by AI. And it’s a realistic concern. They also have antitrust issues as a perpetual background headache.

Specifically, some investors are rightfully concerned that AI searches via ChatGPT and others will eat into their search market share, and already are. Alternatively, the concern is that Google will have to increasingly incorporate AI into their search results (as they already have), and that this will eat into their ad revenue. That’s a realistic concern as well.

But, for context, we have to separate searches into two main types.

-The first type is knowledge-based. If I search to find out the name of Napoleon Bonaparte’s mother, it doesn’t really matter who tells me the answer because they’re not going to make money off that search anyway. It could be Google organically pointing me toward Wikipedia, or any other site. It could be Google giving me the AI result upfront with some links for reference which I won’t have a reason to click through. This is free information. Hardly anyone cares about those searches being disrupted.

-The second type is commercial-based. If I search “plumbers near me” or “electricians near me” then that is a super valuable search for advertisers and platforms that earn ad revenue. Every plumbing company or electrician company in my zip code wants a chance at me calling them for business. And then more broadly “best cloud storage” is a big monetizable result. Or even “How do VPNs work?” (knowing that VPNs are a profitable marketing niche).

AI has not taken meaningful commercial search market share from Google yet. And if they start to do so, then there’s a balance there. AI companies like OpenAI (parent of ChatGPT) are *unprofitable*. Over time, they either have to charge you more, reduce the quality of their service, or find alternative revenue sources. If they suddenly start to find that people keeping asking them “which plumber near me is best?” then they’re likely going to monetize that information. Which means Google can compete with them to keep it monetized on their platform.

More relevant for this article is YouTube, which is owned by Alphabet. It’s by far the most dominant platform for user-hosted videos, and is the biggest video content search engine in the world. As a social media website, YouTube has a tremendous network effect. The only rival at that sort of scale is TikTok, and that’s mainly for short clips.

For a long time, there has been a small trend of independent publishers making short horror films on YouTube. That’s historically a low-budget niche, which means it’s accessible to low-budget creators. Imagine what will happen if technology improves so that individual users can create short films, episodic series, or feature films for *all* genres at 1/10th of the cost that it currently takes, thanks to AI tools. How much more valuable could the world’s biggest video social media network and search engine become?

Here are Alphabet’s revenue numbers for 2023/2024 from their 10-k:

![Alphabet Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-alphabet-revenue.png)

Everyone is of course worried about Google Search revenue getting disrupted (even though it’s still growing, and given my caveat about the difference between knowledge searches and commercial searches).

But I see far fewer people focusing on the possibility of YouTube’s size doubling, then doubling again. It’s about a tenth of the company’s revenue. What if it becomes a fifth, a third, or half?

I’m not saying I’m super bullish on Alphabet, but I do continue to hold it as a small slice in my newsletter portfolio for this reason, and I’m looking to start a new position in my other holdings if it starts to show signs of a bottom. Especially because they’re trading at under 20x earnings with a fortress balance sheet, and have plenty of other bets.

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-googl-fastgraph.png)

The company has initiated a dividend, and is transitioning more towards being a tech value stock than a growth stock. They’re returning capital to shareholders while still growing. I’m not looking to catch a falling knife for the moment (along with the rest of the market in aggregate), but they’ve got my attention.

Additionally, I do think GPUs will continue to sell at massive scale, likely after a period of consolidation. The better that AI gets, including art/video AI (which is particularly GPU intensive), the more we’ll need GPUs. And I think AMD has room to participate for the long run.

While I’m not ready to pull the trigger quite yet, I do keep watching AMD at these levels:

![AMD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-30-amd-fastgraph.png)

I remain bullish on a number of non-tech value stocks and global rotation toward other parts of the world, but I continue to view it as prudent to monitor major tech trends and to conceptualize which areas might be more bullish than consensus expects, or more bearish than consensus expects, including for large-cap U.S. technology stocks.

Overall, I think AI-generated art is one small piece of a much broader AI growth trend, and that small piece just got a major catalyst for acceleration this week, so hopefully this report helped break down some of its nuances and separate hype from longer-run implications.
