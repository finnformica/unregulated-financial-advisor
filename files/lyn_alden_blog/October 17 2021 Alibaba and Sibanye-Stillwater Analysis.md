**The US Business Cycle**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than cherry pick good or bad data points to fit a narrative.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 has shown decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation even as year-ago base effects wore off:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

While I expected some dollar strength or consolidation in the first half of 2021 within the broader context of an ongoing dollar bear market (and wrote/spoke about that late last year and early this year), the duration of the move and the height of the move have so far extended a bit more than my base case was. However, my base case continues to be structurally bearish on the dollar, mainly due to current account deficits and larger fiscal deficits as a percentage of GDP than peer nations. However, it’s important to let the market speak. So at the moment, I remain tactically neutral on the dollar, and continue to watch for more information. The larger and more important theme is the devaluation of all major fiat currencies, but the level of the dollar relative to other currencies can affect the performance of various assets, especially commodities and emerging markets.

## Energy Note

Global coal prices have soared to a record $245/ton, which is particularly hurting China and other Asian countries that heavily rely on coal. The reason they heavily rely on coal is because they have a lot of it in their ground, whereas oil and gas they need to import, and building coal plans has a fast turnaround time and energy payback period.

![Coal Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-coal-price.png)

China was trying to limit coal production in order to reduce CO2 emissions and lower air pollution, and also cut its coal imports from Australia due to a set of geopolitical arguments between the two countries. As a result, it had a supply/demand mismatch and now it’s facing acute coal shortages, resulting in rolling blackouts in some provinces.

Meanwhile, Europe decommissioned some of its nuclear power plants and stopped building them over the past decade. As a result, they kept relying on more coal that they otherwise could have phased out, and they’re extremely reliant on Russian natural gas.

Natural gas is more expensive to transport than oil (requiring super-cool liquified gas if it’s going to anywhere where a pipeline doesn’t go), and so its pricing tends to vary a lot more than oil between locations. Natural gas prices in the US are currently elevated, at multi-year highs, but gas prices in Europe have absolutely skyrocketed to a much larger problem.

Here are Dutch TTF gas futures, which serve as the benchmark for Europe:

![Dutch Gas Futures](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-dutch-gas-futures.png)

*Chart Source: [Barchart.com](https://www.barchart.com/futures/quotes/TGV21/interactive-chart)*

Unlike global coal and European natural gas which have gone to parabolic records, the WTI crude oil price is not at all-time highs, but is now back over $80:

![Oil Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-oil.png)

I wrote about the prospect for and oil and gas bull market based on lack of investment back in a [June 2021 public article](https://www.lynalden.com/oil-and-gas/) (and prior to that, in some 2020 premium reports) and it has played out faster and more aggressively than I would have guessed.

Some European and Chinese businesses are temporarily shutting down due to high electricity costs, which takes away some of the demand and helps prices stabilize, but causes economic harm and worsens the supply chain issues in other areas. I would fade parabolic price spikes for that reason, but I remain focused on the fact that energy is structurally under-invested in. Politicians have pushed to phase out oil and gas and coal and even nuclear, without proper plans for their replacements.

I am structurally bullish on oil and gas producers going forward in this decade for many years ahead, as well as long-dated oil futures, but would caution against taking excess risk whenever it becomes rather consensus, as it’s kind of becoming at the moment. Many producers and midstream transporters continue to have attractive valuations, and are investments that I’m willing to buy and put away for years as dividend-paying compounders. I think the 2020s will be very different than the 2010s for oil prices and oil producers.

Uranium had a price spike in prior months, largely due to Sprott buying up a lot of it. Eventually that trade faded and uranium prices fell. However, the spike in natural gas and coal prices has renewed public and politician interest in nuclear power over the past month, which played a role in giving uranium prices another bump up:

![Uranium Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-uranium.png)

I wrote the bullish fundamental case for uranium back in an [October 2020 premium report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-10-25/), and this is also one of those things that caught on faster than I would have guessed. The global Realpolitik is shifting towards nuclear energy after a lengthy period of relative opposition, mainly due to cold hard math and lack of better alternatives. Despite risks from 1970s-era tech, nuclear energy is one of the few sources of energy that is both dense and relatively clean and has a better safety record than almost anything else, which can be further improved with 21st-century tech.

After a year of bullishness on uranium from October 2020 to October 2021, I am more tactically neutral at current prices, but continue to like it as part of a diversified portfolio.

I personally don’t rely on any particular bitcoin price estimation method in the pure sense. Notably, as far as I can tell and depending on what exchange you look at for your reference, it already briefly touched new all-time intraday highs as denominated in euros and yen, but not yet dollars.

Meanwhile, the SEC has allowed a bitcoin futures ETF, which is [set to likely begin trading next week](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/10/15/the-first-bitcoin-futures-etf-in-the-us-is-set-to-begin-trading-next-week.html).

Some other countries already have spot bitcoin ETFs, meaning they hold bitcoin in cold storage. In addition, the over-the-counter Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) also holds spot bitcoin in cold storage. However, the US has been slow to approve any spot bitcoin ETFs.

Futures-based commodity ETFs roll futures contracts to synthetically provide exposure to the price of the underlying commodity, without directly holding the underlying commodity. For agriculture and energy ETFs, this has been a problem, as rolling futures have resulted in significant loss of capital compared to if the underlying commodity was held.

This chart shows the performance of WTI crude relative to the United States Oil Fund (USO), which uses futures to track the price of oil:

![WTI vs USO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-wti-vs-uso.png)

Imagine losing 89% of your capital in the USO oil ETF over the long run, when the oil price itself went up 9% during that time. This massive underperformance was due to “contango bleed”, which is a fancy way of saying that the product continually lost money as it kept rolling futures into further-out futures when the futures price was above the spot price, especially during bear markets.

The same thing happened to the Invesco DB Agriculture Fund (DBA). It also deeply underperformed its underlying basket of agricultural commodities over more than a decade.

These aren’t fringe products. These are among the benchmark high-volume and high-AUM ETFs for their respective commodities. They’re useful for short-term traders but not good for long-term holders. They’re not intended to be bought and held for years as a passive position.

Now, due to differing storage costs, not all futures-based commodity ETFs would do quite that poorly. And this problem is most severe for commodities in bear markets, like oil had for the past decade.

In other words, a futures-based bitcoin ETF won’t necessarily track the bitcoin price well, and could underperform over long periods of time. It likely won’t perform as poorly as the oil or agriculture ETFs, but it’s not well-designed for long-term holders.

I prefer holding cold storage bitcoin when possible. In a retirement account, I am fine with the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) as a proxy. I also like some of the miners and MicroStrategy (MSTR) as corporate equities with a lot of bitcoin price exposure.

However, the ETF is a relatively big deal. It signals acceptance of bitcoin by the SEC, and will be the first security in the US that tries to track the price of bitcoin on a major exchange, rather than over-the-counter, or through a proxy like MicroStrategy (MSTR) which is a software corporation that smartly adopted a bitcoin standard in 2020 and has been used as a bitcoin proxy investment by some equity investors and bond investors, including me.

Ironically, I still prefer the Grayscales and the MicroStrategies of this market over futures-based ETFs (and more so, simply holding bitcoin in cold storage), but it nonetheless signals regulatory clarity to some extent, which will encourage some conservative institutions and RIAs that have so far missed out, consider an allocation.

Meanwhile, the Lightning Network reached over 3,000 BTC in capacity. Developers and users continue to expand this second-layer ecosystem of bitcoin for fast payments:

![Lightning Capacity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-lightning-capacity.png)

I remain structurally bullish on bitcoin, and its lightning layer as well. I do have some concerns that an ETF launch could be a temporary “sell the news event” for bitcoin, especially since sentiment is pretty bullish at the moment, but my main view is to hold bitcoin as a portion of a portfolio with a multi-year view regardless of intermediate-term bullish or bearish issues, and consider dollar-cost averaging into it.

## Alibaba Update

For this premium report’s stock analysis, I’m going to split the focus between some Chinese stocks and a look into the platinum miner space. These two areas have been very punished in recent months, but are showing some initial signs of bottoming and are worth getting back on our radar.

I consider them to be contrarian highly-volatile bottom-fishing areas, rather than sound set-and-forget investments.

Chinese stocks have been among the weakest assets globally year-to-date, after a particularly strong 2020 performance period for them. China’s credit cycle tends to contract every three years approximately, as part of a managed cycle, and the country’s biggest area of financial concern has been its high real estate leverage. So, I avoided/minimized Chinese real estate exposure by emphasizing other emerging markets like Russia and India, and within China, stuck to internet stocks with strong balance sheets where possible to avoid the real estate side.

However, China’s President Xi Jinping threw a wrench in that strategy starting in November 2020 as he personally cracked down on the internet sector. In other words, the jurisdictional tail risk for these stocks became the defining factor, rather than their own fundamentals. Russian and Indian equities continued to do well but Chinese stocks, including tech stocks, took a beating.

Jinping’s CCP stopped Alibaba’s (BABA) IPO of its payment business and forced it to restructure. They then went after monopolistic practices of e-commerce companies including Alibaba and to a lesser extent, JD (JD) and others. They then clamped down on video games among Chinese youth, using strict authoritarian measures to control video game time, which put a dent in Tencent (TCEHY), and they also went after Tencent’s dominant market share in the music industry. As a capstone, the large internet companies including Alibaba and Tencent were forced to invest billions of dollars (technically in yuan, but equivalent to billions of dollars) into China’s current “Common Prosperity” theme between now and 2025. The CCP also went even harder after other sectors like private education that I’m fortunately not invested in at all.

This was further compounded by the Chinese real estate sector starting to implode, with Evergrande. China has this new “Common Prosperity” theme that goes against the interest of real estate speculation, which has been a multi-year problem in China. The CCP is basically doing a controlled demolition of Evergrande as the figurehead of Chinese real estate speculation, and more broadly, the whole sector is likely to struggle for a while. I’m not bullish on Chinese real estate over the long run from current levels.

For the past month, bearish news on China and particularly Evergrande became a key theme across financial media. Sentiment on the country is rock bottom. Larry Fink, CEO of BlackRock (BLK) was grilled on CNBC about his firm’s investments in China. The consensus is, “why own anything in China?”, and it’s a rational question to ask.

However, investors often don’t take the question a step further, and ask about all of the major S&P 500 companies who have significant presence in China, and thus are subject to similar jurisdictional tail risks. The 800-pound gorilla for that question is Apple (AAPL), but there are dozens of other famous large-cap US companies with considerable ties to China as a market for production and/or consumption of their products. Owning some Chinese stocks is just the tip of the spear if US and China have an economic decoupling.

As of the past two months, Chinese internet stocks are ironically showing initial signs of bottoming. Here is the KWEB ETF, which is a popular ETF that holds Chinese internet stocks:

Alibaba (BABA) is closer to the heart of the regulatory storm, and unlike those prior two, it continued to experience weakness and made a new low in early October, but has since had a sharp move up as well.

Now, that doesn’t mean this can’t be a false bottom where they eventually shoot lower, but it’s clearly a point worth watching, and potentially using as a trading stop to express a bullish view on a rebound.

For those who aren’t familiar, Alibaba is a large e-commerce platform, as well as a cloud computing, media, and mobile payments platform. Tencent is a large set of social networks, a mobile payment network, and a gaming conglomerate that holds assets both within China and around the world. Both of them, especially Tencent, also make many investments in various startups to further diversify themselves.

My view for the past few months has been that the core of the pain is likely over at this point for these Chinese internet names, and now it’s a matter of investor sentiment. Foreign investors have little incentive to hold Chinese stocks when Xi Jinping is going after whatever sectors he feels don’t contribute to long-term Chinese prosperity in their current form, especially when such investors also have little assurance about their legal claims to the assets they hold (ie Cayman-based companies as middlemen).

However, Charlie Munger may have shifted that public sentiment, after fundamentals already showed signs of bottoming out with blood metaphorically in the streets. The 97-year old billionaire vice chairman of Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B) is a long-term China bull, and even defended some CCP actions against Alibaba’s founder Jack Ma this year (which on a humanitarian basis is something I strongly disagree with Munger on, at least in the specific way that the CCP handled it).

In addition to his longtime vice chairman role at Berkshire, Munger is also the chairman of Daily Journal (DJCO) and overseas their investments, which constitute a large percentage of the market capitalization of the company. In terms of professional experience, in addition to overseeing conglomerates, Munger is specifically a banking expert. That’s a key niche of his.

The point of this preamble is that in Daily Journal’s SEC filings, they recently disclosed a massive increase in their position on Alibaba in the latest quarter. This is notable for a couple reasons. The first reason is simply the scale of Munger’s long-term investment prowess; his brand is now firmly behind Alibaba. The second is the fact that the Daily Journal only holds five stocks, of which three stocks account for 96% of the portfolio, and they very rarely buy or sell any of stocks. So, this isn’t a token position. In their portfolio, 43% consists of Bank of America (BAC) stock, 33% consists of Wells Fargo (WFC) stock, 20% consists of Alibaba (BABA) stock, and the remaining 4% consists of US Bancorp (USB) and a tiny stake in South Korean steelmaking firm Posco (PKX).

Well, in a rare move for this recent quarter, Munger increased their Alibaba position by over 80%, which is a near-doubling of their prior position to that 20% level in the portfolio. He doubled-down on the stock and added to the position when sentiment was utterly bearish on it, with record-low valuations and a record-low relative strength index. From a man who rarely buys anything in that portfolio.

Maybe he’s wrong. He’s 97 years old. He hates digital assets like bitcoin. He’s committed to the establishment system. He defended CCP actions against Alibaba founder Jack Ma. He’s heavily invested in large-cap banks, including Wells Fargo that had a series of management/corruption issues. Maybe his opinion is not extremely relevant anymore?

However, maybe he’s right. Despite being 97, he’s as mentally sharp as always judging by his interviews, including ones I disagree with. From his history and his financial scale, he probably has good Chinese connections as it relates to their domestic political intentions. I separately have been highlighting the consensus oversold sentiment of Chinese stocks, and Munger seems to have put big capital into that contrarian trade. Alibaba’s stock price jumped on the news of Daily Journal’s disclosure of the large purchase.

Some investors may not consider Chinese stocks to be truly investable. Perhaps then it is better to consider them as a position trade then, to be held for a year or two.

My base case, until further evidence suggests otherwise, is that Chinese internet stocks have bottomed. It’s a relatively low-risk thesis at this point, considering low valuations and deeply negative investor sentiment, assuming that position sizing is rational. We can draw a line at the prior low, which isn’t much lower than the current price, and invalidate that thesis if it firmly closes below that level. For traders, a breach of that prior low would likely mean triggering a stop loss and selling the position; for investors it would mean re-examining the thesis and making a new buy/sell decision based on current information at the time.

The market often works in ironic ways. It teases and trolls the most people, doing exactly what they don’t expect. As Evergrande reaches global news and the Chinese real estate sector clearly has a lot of pain to go through, when absolutely nobody other than a few investors like Munger in the world want to own Chinese stocks, and when BlackRock’s CEO awkwardly has to defend why his firm even owns Chinese stocks at this point, is when Chinese stocks are likely to bottom and move back up. They don’t have to, but that would be the typical irony of the market.

As a long-term position trade, I’m bullish on Alibaba (BABA), Tencent (TCEHY), and JD (JD) from here unless they breach lower lows. The thesis is that this was the bottom of the drawdown, unless proven otherwise. Alibaba’s drawdown was its lowest in history while Tencent and JD had their second lowest drawdown in history:

![Chinese Drawdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-chinese-drawdowns.png)

For investors that manage their position sizes and respect prior low invalidation points, this is potentially a good money-making opportunity on a risk-adjusted basis. As a trade, downside is somewhat limited except for extreme scenarios if you have a stop loss at prior lows, and upside is significant at 50%+ if these stocks rebound. Use the trade setup as you will, or skip it.

The key risk, of course, would be that the regulatory crackdown is not over. Xi Jinping could continue to go after the Chinese tech sector, and negative investor sentiment could deepen further as the real estate sector in the country continues to be weak (and I expect it to continue to be weak). In an extreme case, Americans could be barred by the US government from owning Chinese assets (even as Chinese firms own a lot of US assets) and thus forced to liquidate their positions at prices they would prefer to buy at.

More mildly, Alibaba and other Chinese stocks could be delisted from major US exchanges due to a determination by the SEC that they no longer meet US accounting standards. This would be a shock news event but not a major issue in and of itself; Tencent for example already does not trade on a major US exchange anyway. This wouldn’t affect their ability to be held in popular international ETFs, unless separate legislation went after that as well.

## Platinum and Sibanye-Stillwater Analysis

Most commodities have rallied in 2021, but precious metals including gold, silver, and platinum have been weak.

Those metals are not trading in unison, though. Gold and silver hit local highs in August 2020, and have been consolidating ever since then. Platinum topped much later in February 2021, and corrected for very different reasons. It is showing signs of stabilization over the past month, though:

![Platinum Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-platinum.png)

The key fundamental catalyst for why platinum and other platinum-group metals corrected sharply since the spring of this year, is that the global semiconductor shortage has resulted in global automakers cutting their production of new vehicles, since they can’t get enough semiconductors for those vehicles. Platinum group metals are heavily used in new vehicle production, so ironically this period of inflation and shortages in other areas has reduced the demand of these metals, and hurt their prices.

However, this semiconductor shortage and auto production slowdown is a massive problem and a lot of capital is being thrown at correcting it. The supply side and long-term bullish thesis for platinum remains attractive, despite this big temporary issue in the market.

I like platinum from current levels as a renewed thesis, despite it having been a weak-performing position over the past couple years.

The platinum and platinum-group metal miners look interesting here as well. They were absolutely crushed in prior years due to low platinum prices and other issues, but in 2019, 2020, and 2021 entered a renewed bull market with serious earnings and stock price appreciation.

Most of the big ones are South African companies, although they have USD-denominated ADRS as well. Sibanye-Stillwater (SBSW) and Impala Platinum (IMPUY) are some of the largest stocks in iShares MSCI South Africa ETF index, at about 4% each at the moment. Sibanye’s ADR trades on the NYSE, while Impala’s ADR trades OTC. They each own significant mining assets in South Africa, as well as significant mining assets in the United States and Canada.

Here’s SBSW’s F.A.S.T. Graph:

![SBSW FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-sbsw-fastgraph.png)

And here’s IMPUY’s, which goes back longer, which puts it into perspective relative to the previous commodities bull market from years ago:

![IMPUY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-impuy-fastgraph.png)

When they go through a period of not having positive earnings like that, they instead have to issue shares to fund themselves, which dilutes the value of individual shares. Both stocks suffered tremendously during the commodities bear market of the 2010s, which I view as likely being over. We’ll have ups and downs but I consider most commodities to be in a bullish cycle in the 2020s.

The stocks did very well in the past few years. They generated great free cash flows, and used those cash flows to strengthen their balance sheets and pay fat dividends, after a number of years of not paying dividends.

Here’s SBSW’s net debt position:

![SBSW YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-sbsw-balance-sheet.png)

Cash levels are up, debt levels are down, and so net debt is way down. After building up a lot of debt a few years ago, the company used its 2019-2021 windfall from the rising price of platinum group metals (especially palladium and rhodium) to pay down debt and raise cash. It now has more cash than debt, meaning its net debt position is negative (a great thing).

Impala’s balance sheet looks similar, and better. No debt and tons of cash:

![IMPUY Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-impuy-balance-sheet.png)

Despite now having pristine balance sheets, however, they are still considered somewhat junk by credit markets. This is because the combination of operational risk, jurisdictional risk, and commodity price can evaporate their earnings. An investor can wake up to a headline that dramatically reduces the value of the company.

With these two stocks, an investor is buying a leveraged play on platinum group metals, mainly in the form of operational leverage rather than financial leverage at this point, considering their clean balance sheets. Both stocks trade at a low-single-digit price/earnings ratio, although that’s mainly because the market expects earnings to fade from 2021 peak levels going forward, with weaker rhodium prices and somewhat weaker palladium and platinum prices.

SBSW’s dividend policy is to pay out 25-35% of its normalized earnings to shareholders, and Impala’s payout ratio is expected to be higher going forward. Now that their balance sheets are already rock-solid, most cash can go towards dividends and some acquisitions. So, even if earnings get cut in half, and the stocks at current prices go from a low-single-digit price/earnings ratio to a high-single-digit price/earnings ratio, that’s pretty attractive.

The key risk for both of these stocks is that if auto demand remains weak due to multi-year supply chain problems, then rhodium, palladium, and platinum could go lower and badly hurt these companies’ earnings (ie their earnings could be more than cut in half, or even outright go negative if it’s bad enough). Other key risks include operational risk at major mines, and jurisdictional risk from South Africa which has considerable political instability.

The conservative way to play this space is to simply hold some platinum as a diversifier, either physically or with an ETF like the Aberdeen Platinum ETF (PPLT). It’s a precious metal that doubles as an industrial metal for various purposes, including catalytic converters and hydrogen cells. That leaves out operational mining risk and jurisdictional risk.

The aggressive way to play this space is to buy SBSW or IMPUY or both, although probably as small positions given the risk. Or as a larger trading position with a tight stop at recent lows.

Both SBSW and IMPUY rallied tremendously into February 2021, and then corrected by over 40% into September 2021. They both then put in sharp bottoms during September 2021:

![SBSW Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-sbsw-chart.png)

My base case is bullish on both stocks from current levels, although I must stress that these are very risky stocks. They can compliment a commodities portfolio.

Sibanye has been diversifying by acquiring “green metal” projects, to gain exposure to lithium, nickel, cobalt, and others that are heavily used in EV and other battery applications. This comes with risk but also hedges their platinum group metal exposure if they continue to build up that side of the asset portfolio. Impala on the other hand has a stronger balance sheet and is expected by analysts to pay a larger dividend than Sibanye going forward.

## Final Thoughts: Central Bank Tightening

The core of my portfolio continues to consist of dividend growth stocks, with targeted tech stocks and commodities plays as well. I remain structurally bullish on bitcoin as a core position, which is up about 8.5x since I first recommended it in April 2020, but it continues to have long-term growth potential in my view.

Short-term TIPS are [imperfect but useful](https://www.lynalden.com/tips/) for some capital also, to buffer against the possibility of sizable corrections in risk assets that may occur ahead as central banks tilt from hyper dovish to slightly less dovish.

Many emerging markets central banks in Latin America and Eastern Europe have been raising rates for months, since they have less fiscal and monetary space than developed markets in this sort of inflationary environment, if they want to protect their currencies. Brazil raised their key interest rate from 2% to 6% during 2021. Mexico raised theirs from 4% to 4.75%.  Chile raised theirs from 0.5% to 2.75%. Russia raised theirs from 4.25% to 6.75%. Poland raised theirs from 0.1% to 0.5%.

Asian emerging market interest rates have been flat at positive levels in terms of rates this year. India’s key rate for example has been held flat at 4%. China, Malaysia, Thailand, and Indonesia are flat at lower but positive levels.

The Fed isn’t anywhere close to raising rates off zero yet, but they’re likely going to start trimming their rate of asset purchases in late 2021 or early 2022. Overall, with fiscal stimulus on hold for now and central banks getting less dovish (some of them like Bank of Russia are outright hawkish, while others like the Fed and ECB and BOJ are just turning less hyper-dovish than before), there is room for caution.

This chart shows the percentage of US commercial bank assets that consist of cash in blue. It’s at record highs, fueled by the Fed’s QE. The red line on the right axis is the volume of reverse repo activity, which is a sign of excess liquidity. The last time reverse repos spiked was towards the end of the Fed’s “QE3” program, which is the only other time that US banks were stuffed with this much cash.

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-bank-cash.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This chart is similar. It shows the Fed’s balance sheet in blue, and shows the volume of reverse repo activity in red. Last time, the spike in reverse repo activity was the signal for the Fed to stop doing QE, meaning stop expanding their balance sheet. They then tightened monetary policy for five years, meaning they held their balance sheet flat for a while, and then temporarily reduced their balance sheet while raising rates, until they were forced to reverse course and loosen again.

![Fed Taper](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-fed-taper.png)*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

I don’t think the Fed will do five years of tightening this time, but I do think they are going to do some tightening. They’ll likely reduce their rate of asset purchases. Maybe down the line they will raise interest rates slightly, but I’m not sure they’ll be able to get that far.

With housing-based CPI heading up in the US, along with ongoing global supply chain issues, inflation is likely to remain hot into year-end, and central banks are increasingly responding to it. Nonetheless, developed market inflation-adjusted interest rates remain near record lows, as their monetary response is deliberately slow to let inflation run hot, due to the constraint of how much debt is in the system.

![Real Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-17-real-rates.png)

One way to think about it is that emerging markets don’t have the liberty to hold rates low like developed markets have done in this inflationary environment, while developed markets don’t have the liberty to easily raise rates like emerging markets can.

For emerging markets, weaker financial systems, foreign-denominated debt, and lack of external demand for their currencies are the key constraints that force them to tighten monetary policy. For developed markets, extremely high levels of debt relative to GDP (denominated in their own currencies) along with slow real GDP growth are the key constraints that prevent them from tightening monetary policy too much.

I continue to prefer hard assets over paper assets in this sort of market, but with an expectation and acceptance of volatility. That means an emphasis on strong balance sheets, a tendency to fade periods of localized euphoria, and a careful analysis of valuations, while maintaining exposure to assets that benefit from ongoing inflation where possible.
