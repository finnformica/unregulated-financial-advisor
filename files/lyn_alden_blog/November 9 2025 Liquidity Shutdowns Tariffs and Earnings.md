## Macro View

**Tariff Updates**

In my prior report, I brought up the then-upcoming tariff hearing:

> In parallel, the US Supreme Court is set to hear the case about tariffs starting on November 5th. President Trump has relied on emergency powers to impose the tariffs, and two court levels have ruled against him on that so far as it escalated its way through the court system (i.e. arguing that the breadth and length of the tariffs he has imposed falls outside of the scope of emergency powers and executive orders, and thus should be set by Congress). It’ll be up to the Supreme Court to decide the final outcome. Polymarket betting participants current place 41% odds of the Supreme Court ruling in Trump’s favor, but the volume is pretty low to draw any major conclusions from.
>
> -October 26, 2025 premium report

As an update, the Supreme Court did indeed have that hearing on November 5th. And based on the questions and remarks by the justices, market odds of the court ruling in Trump’s favor had a sudden drop from ~40% to ~20%.

![Supreme Court Tariffs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-supreme-court-tariffs.png)

The nine-member court currently consists of six conservative justices and three liberal justices. All three liberal justices appeared skeptical of the President’s use of emergency powers for tariffs, but notably, several of the conservative justices did as well. Assuming the three liberal justices vote against the emergency justification for tariffs, it would require two of the six conservatives to side with them to strike the extra tariffs down. Historically speaking, a significant subset of political conservatives are opposed to new forms of taxation that do not go through Congress, so this is indeed a case that isn’t necessarily expected to go along party lines.

The ruling may take as long as June 2026 to be announced. However, based on remarks by the Supreme Court, many professional court-watchers expect a decision to be made later this year or early next year, on an expedited basis.

It’s not necessarily a binary outcome. The Supreme Court may limit the power without removing it entirely. And there are complexities regarding whether or how the US federal government would pay back the tariffs already taken if they are ruled to have been illegal.

The tariffs themselves are paid by the importers on US soil. However, who *ultimately* pays the tariffs (i.e. who bears the cost) is a matter of debate and analysis, and can change over time.

-If foreign exporters lower prices to offset the tariffs that American importers pay, then those foreigners are basically paying them instead. However, import price indices show that’s not happening in aggregate so far.

-If importers and other middle-men pass on the costs in the form of higher prices, then American consumers are basically paying them. Overall consumer price index changes have been mild thus far, indicating that some of it is trickling out but not most of it yet.

-If importers don’t pass on prices aggressively, and exporters don’t cut their prices aggressively, then those importers and other middle-men are basically (and literally) paying them in the form of lower margins and the actual tariff payments themselves, which by extension can also negatively impact employment and capex decisions in those industries.

Goldman Sachs released a report in October, using data up through August, that determined US businesses were absorbing half of the cost, with American consumers absorbing about a third, and foreigners paying the smallest share. However, by the end of the year, they expect the majority of the burden to shift toward American consumers.

Fox Business [reports](https://www.foxbusiness.com/economy/us-businesses-consumers-shoulder-bulk-tariff-cost-burden-goldman-sachs-finds?utm_source=chatgpt.com) (Oct 21, 2025):

> Goldman Sachs economists estimated that as of August, U.S. businesses were absorbing a net 51% of tariff costs, while American consumers were shouldering 37% of the burden. They also estimated that 9% of the cost was paid by foreign exporters, and about 3% was attributed to potential tariff evasion.
>
> […]
>
> Goldman’s economists assessed that by the end of 2025, U.S. consumers will be absorbing 55% of tariff costs, while 22% will fall on U.S. businesses, 18% on foreign exporters and 5% on potential tariff evasion.

From a macro perspective, the Supreme Court decision here basically represents a $300+ billion swing in annual fiscal policy. Striking down most of the emergency-power tariffs would basically be a mild stimulus from the current baseline, or a roughly 15% expansion in the fiscal deficit, or about 1% of nominal GDP. Allowing most of the tariffs would keep the current status quo in effect, and potentially enable the President to get more aggressive with the use of emergency powers for the tariffs.

In addition to the tariff revenue itself, there is also a harder-to-quantify uncertainty factor involved. Many businesses have delayed capex decisions until they have more clarity on the legality of the tariffs.

Suppose two companies A and B import widgets, and those widgets are now tariffed by 30%, and that could go up or down at the speed of a tweet during the upcoming months or years. They could spend a lot of money to make those widgets domestically, but they estimate that the cost will be 25-50% higher to do so, and take years to pay back the costs of construction and changing supply chains. If Company A decides to invest in on-shore production while Company B does nothing and waits to see what happens, and then the tariffs are mostly struck down, then Company A just lost, since they invested in a new direction that will cost perpetually more to produce than Company B. If the tariffs are upheld, then Company B is a bit of a laggard on reshoring, but on a multi-year time horizon that’s not the end of the world. Thus for many companies, waiting to see what happens is the optimal choice. This weighs on their employment decisions and investment decisions in the meantime.

And we see this somewhat in the data:

> Some of the anomalies show up in purchasing manager’s indices. Let’s look at manufacturing, for example, both the activity itself and the price portion of it:
>
> ![Manufacturing PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-manufacturing.png)
>
> We are in a period of prolonged slump in manufacturing PMI, where it hasn’t totally collapsed as it does in a recession, but it has spent years chopping mostly below 50, which means mild contraction in manufacturing activity. Here in 2025, the “prices paid” portion spiked despite the PMI remaining low, which is a stagflationary aspect associated with tariffs.
>
> Services are typically less cyclical than manufacturing, but service companies have to buy goods as well. Industries like homebuilders and utilities are services, for example, but they have to buy a lot of physical goods, and many of those are tariffed. As a result, there is a rare spike in prices paid by services companies despite weakening service activity:
>
> ![PMI Services](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-services-annotated.png)
>
> So far, most of the tariff costs have been absorbed by the companies as they compete to retain market share, but in the coming months my base case is that those costs will start to trickle out more in the form of mild price increases.
>
> -October 26, 2025 premium report

Here on Sunday, Trump once again mentioned the intention to give a consumer stimulus from the tariff revenue. He has brought this up a number of times in recent months, though it generally takes an act of Congress to perform that type of spending. If enacted one way or another, it would basically eliminate the tariff impact on most consumers, but at a cost of $300+ billion that would approximately use up the first full year of tariff revenue.

**US Business Cycle**

Due to the government shutdown, a significant percentage of economic data is not being released. However, there are still alternative data providers.

Most of what’s available shows ongoing economic deceleration. For example, [Revelio Labs](https://www.reveliolabs.com/public-labor-statistics/employment/) released data on November 6th for the month of October that showed an aggregate decline in total jobs from an already sluggish baseline:

![Revelio Employment Data](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-revelio-employment.png)

The University of Michigan released their [November data](https://www.sca.isr.umich.edu/) for national consumer sentiment, and it came in at the second-lowest reading in the nearly 50-year dataset that stretches back to the late 1970s. Among political independents, it sunk below the prior lowest point for a new record low:

![Consumer Sentiment](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-consumer-sentiment.png)

The overall economy continues to act with two speeds. Some industries, particularly those fueled directly or indirectly by fiscal deficits and AI spending, continue to do well. Other areas, particularly those impacted by semi-tight monetary policy and overall economic malaise, continue to struggle.

**Government Shutdown Updates**

The current government shutdown recently became the longest in US history, surpassing the 2018 shutdown which lasted 35 days.

Betters on Polymarket, with rather high volumes, currently have November 14th as the weighted estimated date for it to end:

![Polymarket Shutdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-shutdown.png)

As it reaches this unprecedented length, it’s starting to show up in more material ways than the first month.

Initially, it looked like it was going to result in a reduction in food stamp payments here in November. However, a judge blocked that from happening, and the result is that the government is paying out the full food stamp amounts while they appeal that decision to a higher court.

Next up, it’s hitting flights. Air traffic controllers are currently not getting paid, resulting in some of them not coming in to work and instead working other jobs, and that is reducing the National Airspace System’s ability to handle flights. Transportation Secretary Sean Duffy issued orders to airlines to reduce traffic for safety reasons, and [said](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/faa-disruptions-could-rise-to-20-if-shutdown-continues-6caf2de9) that up to 15-20% of flights could be cancelled in the coming weeks. Initial plans are to gradually cancel upwards of 10% of flights during this upcoming week.

It also is impacting liquidity, as described in the next section.

**Liquidity**

There continues to be plenty of liquidity news to report, and we’re likely nearing a bottom in domestic base liquidity.

For starters before getting into new stuff, it’s useful to refresh the liquidity news from the prior report, since there was a lot going on:

> There is some pretty big liquidity news to report, but it’s not the sensationalist stuff that has been posted around social media lately.
>
> The Fed has been shrinking its balance sheet from excess levels since mid-2022. Occasionally it would cause some issues, but the Treasury Department would offset that with more T-bill issuance to pull excess liquidity from the reverse repo facility instead, and put that back into the financial system. Between the Fed and the Treasury, they kept domestic liquidity almost completely flat.
>
> Now, the reverse repo facility is drained, and the Fed’s balance sheet is nearing levels that are tight enough to cause some issues. Mostly these issues briefly pop up at the end of a quarter, because financial institutions perform “window dressing” to temporarily make their balance sheet look ideal in a snapshot for their quarterly report.
>
> The Fed has a standing repo facility now, so that if financial institutions need cash in exchange for pledging safe collateral, it’s available to them. That facility has been used a few times during these quarter-end window-dressing periods.
>
> However, since mid-September and continuing into late October, the Fed’s repo facility has been repeatedly used even without it just being the end of a quarter:
>
> ![Repo Activity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-repo.png)
>
> Additionally, the secured overnight financing rate (aka SOFR) has mildly spiked as well, relative to the rate of interest paid by the Fed on bank reserves. This indicates mild liquidity shortages in the private collateralized borrowing market between financial institutions:
>
> ![SOFR and IORB](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-sofr-and-iorb.png)
>
> ![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-sofr-iorb.png)
>
> All of this is to say that the Fed’s quantitative tightening is starting to reach a persistent level of liquidity shortages in the domestic banking system.
>
> Then, in mid-October, Fed Chairman Powell acknowledged these signs of liquidity strain and said the Fed may soon end its balance sheet reduction.
>
> If we look at bank cash as a percentage of total bank assets, it currently stands at around $3 trillion, or around 12.6% of total bank assets:
>
> ![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-cash.png)
>
> ![Bank Cash Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-cash-share.png)
>
> Years ago, the New York Fed put out a [research report](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1019.pdf) concluding that within the current regulatory framework, 8-10% bank cash as a percentage of total bank assets is the transition point between “scarce reserves” and “ample reserves”, and 12-13% bank cash as a percentage of total bank assets is the transition point between “ample reserves” and “abundant reserves”. The Fed is aiming to get down from an abundant reserve environment to an ample reserve environment, and so that 12-13% level is their approximate target which they’ve now reached. Back in 2019 they went all the way down to the scarce reserves level and triggered a big repo spike, and they’re aiming for a smoother landing than that this time.
>
> […]
>
> Now we are finally getting to the point where *overall* reserves are reaching “ample” rather than “abundant” levels, and so liquidity strains are popping up more persistently and the Fed is discussing an end to its balance sheet reduction.
>
> Back in 2024, I expected us to reach this level by 2025. However, the Fed instead slowed their rate of balance sheet reduction (quantitative tightening), which let them extend the time of performing their balance sheet reduction without actually changing the level that they’d ultimately get it to.
>
> Once they slowed it, I’ve been pointing toward late 2025 or 2026 as the time for liquidity strains and the end of the Fed’s balance sheet reduction, and we’re right on track.
>
> So, all of this is playing out as expected. The financial system is indeed running into tight liquidity conditions here in Q4 2025, the Fed will end its balance sheet reduction in the coming months, and then we’ll see their timeline toward gradually expanding it in the months that follow from there.
>
> It won’t be a large stimulus, but rather will be a mild pro-liquidity event that will mark a multi-year pivot in the balance sheet from shrinking to neutral and then back toward gradually expanding.
>
> ![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-fed-balance-sheet.png)
>
> -October 26, 2025 premium report

Now, here’s the news since then.

On October 29th, the Fed announced at their FOMC meeting that they will end their balance sheet reduction at the start of December. Specifically, they will continue to let mortgage-backed securities mature off their balance sheet slowly, but they will start reinvesting those proceeds into T-bills, which will keep the aggregate balance sheet flat from that point. They also cut interest rates by 0.25%.

I expect that within the first half of 2026, the Fed will shift toward gradual but persistent balance sheet increases again. This will not be for the purpose of economic stimulation and thus they won’t call it quantitative easing, but instead will be described as them maintaining ample reserves in the banking system. It allows banks to continue increasing aggregate loan volumes without running into liquidity constraints.

The repo and SOFR stress (strains in liquidity) that I highlighted in my prior report appears to be done for now, but will likely come back from time to time until the Fed reaches that point of balance sheet expansion.

Interestingly, the government shutdown seems to have mildly impacted those repo and SOFR liquidity strains. After the debt ceiling was resolved this summer, the Treasury Department was intending to refill its cash account to $850 billion (which sucks liquidity out of the banking system as they do it). However, they actually *overfilled* it to $958 billion at its peak during late October, seemingly due to a halt in certain of the government’s expenditures. This put pressure on an already-tight system by sucking an extra $108 billion out.

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-tga.png)

The good news is that the repo activity and SOFR-IORB spreads have died down for now. The Fed’s standing repo facility is designed to put out little liquidity fires like this until the Fed can come to more permanent solutions for them (like halting balance sheet reduction which they’ve announced, and eventually resuming balance sheet growth).

![Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-repo.png)

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-sofr-iorb.png)

What to watch:

-If the Treasury fills up its cash account even higher, then some of these liquidity strains will likely return, and the Fed’s repo facility will help put them out.

-The end-of-year window dressing period (final days of December) has a decent chance of causing a temporary spike in these liquidity strains.

-Within the first half of 2026, there’s a decent probability that the liquidity strains will return, and the Fed will shift from a flat balance sheet toward gradual but persistent balance sheet expansion, roughly in line with nominal GDP growth.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin and the broader cryptocurrency space continued to face bear market pressures in recent weeks. Social media sentiment is currently very bearish on it, which is often a contraindicator.

On one hand, bitcoin touched new all-time highs to over $125k as recently as October 2025. On the other hand, bitcoin has basically been flat in a choppy range for a year since November 2024 when it first reached $100k, having oscillated between about $75k and $125k since then:

![Bitcoin Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-btc-price.png)

The primary driver of demand over the past year has been treasury companies, ETFs, and their investors. The primary source of supply during that time has been long-term holders who are up big selling into the strength, which happens in every bull cycle.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-hodl-wave.png)

Long-term holders sell for all sorts of reasons. Many of them are up 5x or 10x or 20x or far more, depending on how long they’ve held. For some, bitcoin could be 90% of their net worth or more due to all that appreciation, and so they finally rebalance. Others have started families and have more expenses compared to 5-10 years ago, or otherwise just want to increase their baseline consumption and sell some of their appreciated bitcoin to do so.

Now that bitcoin is a highly-liquid asset with a market capitalization of over $2 trillion and significant integration with existing capital markets, we should expect less extreme price swings from it. Both to the upside and the downside, I expect somewhat more muted behavior compared to prior cycles. However, that’s relative to rather extreme volatility, and I still expect considerable volatility in the years ahead.

Many bitcoin bulls have it in their minds that bitcoin price action must occur in four-year cycles. This is because the supply halving cycle occurs every 210,000 blocks, which is approximately four years. The prior three bitcoin cycle tops occurred in Q4 2013, Q4 2017, and Q4 2021. And so, many of them fear that here in Q4 2025 we have seen the top for the next couple of years.

![BTC Halvings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-btc-halvings.png)

However, the supply of new bitcoin from miners now represents only 3.125 bitcoins per block, or about 165,000 bitcoins per year. That’s approximately 0.8% annual supply growth. A reduction in new supply from 1.6% per year to 0.8% per year to 0.4% per year matters a lot less than it did back in the early cycles.

Here are charts from [*Broken Money*](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR) on the past and future supply growth of bitcoin:

![Bitcoin Supply Log](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-21-B.png)

![Bitcoin Supply Linear](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-21-C.png)

In those prior cycles, there were fewer coins in circulation, and there were more coins produced every 10 minutes, and so the percentage growth rate was much larger, and the “halving” event was therefore much more meaningful on the overall supply of coins. Now it has a much smaller impact.

A much bigger impact is whether bullish price action convinces 5-10% of existing long-term coinholders to trim their positions or not. That dwarfs the 0.8% annual new coin supply from mining, whereas it did not dwarf the new coin supply from mining two or three cycles ago.

Going forward, I think more dovish policy from the Fed will be mildly beneficial for assets such as bitcoin. Over the next 6-12 months, we’ll likely see mildly lower interest rates and a resumption of balance sheet growth, which should improve domestic liquidity indicators and thus help on the demand side.

For the supply side, I expect that as bitcoin chops along, the selling pressure from existing holders will diminish, and that’ll start setting the floor for the next move up.

In terms of timing, what I’m waiting to see before pounding the table on the asset again is for the weekly MACD to shift positive, which hasn’t happened yet:

![Bitcoin Weekly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-btc-weekly.png)

The last time that momentum shift occurred, I highlighted the key timing, and it rather quickly jumped from $93k to $125k:

## PayPal vs Fiserv: A Tale of Balance Sheets

In my report from a month ago, I highlighted PayPal (PYPL) and Accenture (ACN) as new investment ideas.

I also put Fiserv (FI) on the watch list. I’ve already got a 1.2% Fiserv position in the newsletter portfolio, and its price was recently on the descent after a prior big gain. I was becoming re-interested in it, but waited to see a technical bottom first rather than loading up more on it:

> Approximately three and a half years ago (long enough that FAST Graphs looked different), I dedicated the entirety of my May 1, 2022 report’s investment analysis section to the payments company Fiserv (FI, previously FISV), analyzing its recent acquisition, growth prospects, balance sheet, and risks. The conclusion was bullish.
>
> *Overall, due to two years of underperformance, Fiserv is trading at historically low valuations for the company. Their estimated forward price/earnings ratio is about 15x.*
>
> *![FISV FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-1-fisv-fastgraph.png)*
>
> *Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*
>
> *I’m fairly bullish on Fiserv as part of a diversified portfolio. Their operations are rather resistant to supply chain problems or economic slowdowns, and their valuation is very reasonable.*
>
> *-May 1, 2022 premium report*
>
> Fiserv bottomed six weeks later in mid-June 2022, and then more than doubled two and a half years later by late 2024. However, it then sold off sharply here in 2025, due to pressures from competitors and some delayed company initiatives.
>
> ![FI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-fi-fastgraph.png)
>
> I think it’s a classic case of the stock getting emotionally ahead of itself, and then getting emotionally oversold. Fundamentals haven’t varied too wildly and have just grown at a relatively smooth rate.
>
> Unlike Accenture and PayPal, I’m just in watch mode for Fiserv at the moment. I already have longs at a pretty low cost basis, and want it to put in some semblance of a technical bottom before actively adding to the position. If/when we reach that point, I’ll likely dedicate more analysis to it and potentially make some trades around it.
>
> -October 12, 2025 premium report

I’m glad I waited rather than loading up last month, because Fiserv got absolutely slammed when they released earnings on October 29th. They reported a rare triple-miss: underperformance on revenue, underperformance on margins, and reduced forward guidance.

It was so bad that analysts reduced their earnings expectations to basically zero growth for the next three years which you can see in this updated next FAST Graph, and the stock price crashed:

![FI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-fi-fastgraph.png)

![Batman Slap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-batman-slap-meme.jpg)

The most interesting thing in my view is to see why the magnitude of the change was so large for such an established company. They’ve never reported something this extreme in decades of operation.

Fiserv got a new CEO in May of this year. The prior CEO, Frank Bisignano, was appointed to head the Social Security Administration. As part of that transition to government work, he had to sell his Fiserv shares, which totaled over $500 million worth, and he did so prior to this earnings announcement. So, he got out while the price was good.

The new CEO basically said in the earnings call that prior guidance was overly rosy, and that recent growth was heavily reliant on Argentina. The company under the prior CEO seems like it was making a lot of decisions to optimize for the short-term and pad the numbers, at the cost of building up some issues for the longer run. And in this quarter, the new CEO let it all out.

On its own, that would have been bad. But what exacerbated the issue was that Fiserv has a lot of debt. That’s a well-known fact, but the incredible stickiness of their business and reliability of their cash flows enabled that debt to be maintained, and overall interest expense was quite manageable. However, when earnings expectations drop a ton, it hurts a leveraged company *way* more than an unleveraged company.

Specifically, Fiserv has $29 billion in net debt (with a big chunk of it coming from their 2019 acquisition) and about $3.6 billion in annual income. This gives it net debt equal to about 8x their annual income:

![Fiserv Debt to Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-fiserv-debt-vs-income.png)

If the company can double income every 5-7 years while the debt is relatively fixed and locked in at low rates, then that is manageable. It’s like someone who has a big mortgage, but their job is very steady and the house it’s attached to is in a good part of town and appreciating in price.

However, if earnings forecasts get slashed to zero growth, then a net debt load of 8x annual earnings is a lot, and puts the overall value of the equity in question. The forward price/earnings ratio is in the single digits now, which is quite cheap. However, the company needs to show signs of stabilization and growth to become an attractive risk/reward proposition.

Therefore, I will continue to hold the small position but will not be adding to it until I see evidence that it’s a value opportunity rather than a value trap. I do expect that the company will solidify things and expand from there, but let’s see some quarters of that starting to happen.

PayPal, which I did initiate a position in, also reported earnings the same week and fortunately they had solid results. They mildly boosted their forward expectations, they announced a partnership with OpenAI to embed its digital wallet into ChatGPT, and they also initiated a dividend for the first time. The company’s fundamentals continue to look attractive with a very reasonable valuation.

![PYPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-pypl-fastgraph.png)

Importantly, PayPal has a great balance sheet. While they have a decent amount of debt, they also have a ton of cash, and so their net debt position (i.e. debt minus cash) is very low. They only have about $1.4 billion in net debt, and they have $4.9 billion in annual net income, and so their debt/income ratio is about 0.3x (which compares favorably to Fiserv’s 8x).

![PayPal Debt to Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-pypl-debt-vs-income.png)

In other words, by using just four months worth of income, PayPal could bring their net debt to zero. The company trades at under 13x earnings, and almost all of the company consists of equity value rather than debt.

Risks certainly remain for PayPal. They’re undergoing a transformation to revive growth. I expect it to be successful, but there’s no guarantee it will be. However, by having a low earnings multiple, and just as importantly by having low debt, the pressure is mostly off. They’re already priced for little growth, and their lack of net debt makes it so that moderate delays or disappointments in their growth initiatives wouldn’t greatly threaten the equity value.

And so, I continue to be bullish on PayPal, while I adopt a wait-and-see approach to see if Fiserv can right itself.

## Final Thoughts: Gold Consolidation

> Having doubled in less than two years, it now has the most overbought monthly RSI in history:
>
> ![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-gold-chart.png)
>
> In the tactical sense, the current levels concern me, and I think risk management is in order. It’s not a bad time to check your allocations and considering rebalancing. We’re at levels for which a 6-month, 12-month, or longer correction/consolidation would not surprise me at all.
>
> In a structural strategic/sense, however, I still don’t view gold as being fundamentally overvalued. The world is becoming more multi-polar, with gold as the largest neutral reserve asset and price-settling mechanism available. As major global shifts like this occur, it’s natural for things to get quite extreme, and the entire gold market cap is still a rather small share of global assets.
>
> Thus, my current approach for precious metals is one of caution rather than one of outright avoidance.
>
> -October 26, 2025

Since then, gold has consolidated, and is mildly lower:

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-gold.png)

Even as a long term bull on it, I think it’s very healthy for gold’s price to chill out for a bit after this much of a run, letting off some steam and some headlines. It’ll be worth revisiting once it rebuilds a new foundation to launch up from.

I still think we’ll see lower-lows in the S&P 500 to gold ratio for this cycle, and the structural momentum indicators generally point in that direction. However, that’s an approximately five-year view or more, and so I’d expect it to zig and zag around during that timeframe.
