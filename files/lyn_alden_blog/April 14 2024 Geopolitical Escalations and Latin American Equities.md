# Geopolitical Escalations and Latin American Equities

*April 14, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report focuses on geopolitical escalation, liquidity conditions, the economy, and inflation.

The digital asset section discusses bitcoin price action, the upcoming supply halving, and ETF flows.

The investment analysis section looks at Latin American equities for some high-risk value plays.

## Macro View

**Geopolitical Escalation**

On Saturday, Iran launched a large drone and missile strike at Israel. It was partially telegraphed ahead of time, and much of the attack was intercepted. Iran then followed up with a clarification statement that it was in response to Israel’s strike on their consulate in Syria, that they aimed for military targets, and that they consider the matter concluded unless Israel retaliates.

Most financial markets were closed for the weekend, but 24/7 cryptocurrency markets reacted with high volatility on the headlines, which was exacerbated by liquidations of leveraged traders. They began to partially recover within about an hour as Iran made their clarification statements, which were seen as a path toward containment and de-escalation. As of Sunday, Israel’s prime minister reportedly is declining to retaliate after talking with the U.S. president.

Much of this comes off as performative at this point, which is a good thing in this context in the sense that it achieves the need for the governments involved to save face while also avoiding further escalation.

However, this is still a developing story and goes well beyond financial markets, as it represents a new level of kinetic engagement between well-armed powers that have historically fought via proxy rather than via direct attacks from one to the other. I think this reiterates why having some energy exposure in a portfolio is prudent. Even without geopolitical escalation in the Middle East there is a good case to own oil and gas stocks as a diversifier, and the potential for war-related escalation only strengthens that case.

**Liquidity Indicators**

Global liquidity has been flat for the past month, which all else being equal tends to result in lackluster results for asset prices. Assets ranging from the S&P 500 to bitcoin have also chopped along over the past month.

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-global-m2.png)

The CPI report for March (posted on April 10th) came in hot. Consensus economists were expecting 0.3% increases in headline and core inflation, but they came in closer to 0.4% instead. This resulted in the market reducing its expectations of future Fed cuts and thus increasing the strength of the dollar index, which weighed down liquidity in recent days.

Year-over-year CPI is now up to 3.48%, which represents a mild re-acceleration in inflation. At the very least, it is “sticky” inflation compared to the Fed’s target of 2%.

![CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-cpi.png)

Headline inflation (the purple line in the charts above) includes most things, while core inflation (the orange line in the chart above) excludes food and energy since those tend to be volatile and it can be useful to know some of the less-volatile price changes as well. So-called “supercore” inflation focuses on services ex-housing, and that has been hot too:

![Inflation Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-inflation-tweets.png)

In response to the CPI report, 2-year Treasury yields rose, and equities, gold, and bitcoin ran into a period of volatility.

![2-Year Treasury Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-2yr.png)

The market has priced in a less dovish Fed going forward. However, when looking at it through the lens of fiscal dominance, a less dovish Fed means even larger fiscal deficits, which is ironically a significant inflationary contributor.

One of the major themes I have been presenting is that when the public debt/GDP ratio is over 100% and annual fiscal deficits are becoming structurally larger than the combined sum of annual net bank loan creation and corporate bond issuance, interest rates start to work differently when it comes to controlling inflation.

When public debt and deficits are that high, interest rates become less effective at suppressing inflation, and in some cases can even exacerbate inflation, which is unintuitive. This is because the economy is in “fiscal dominance” mode rather than “monetary dominance” mode, and it’s unlike anything that most developed markets have experienced in the past 40 years. Most developed markets experienced it back in the 1940s, and many emerging markets experience it regularly, but those are not commonly studied by investors in developed markets today. And so it is has been an unintuitive and out-of-consensus view for a while but is starting to be more widely understood.

![Inflation Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-inflation-meme.png)

Back in early 2022, my written view was that the hawkish Fed would likely lead to a U.S. recession. I was in the consensus in other words, and throughout 2022, most economic indicators rapidly decelerated, the yield curve inverted, most asset prices performed poorly, and so forth. I knew that high interest rates would be expensive for the government and unsustainable in the long run and had written about structural fiscal dominance back in 2019 and 2020, but I assumed that the higher rates would weigh on the private sector in 2022, and that was what was happening.

But by early 2023, the data started to suggest otherwise, and the bank liquidity bailouts in March 2023 really sealed the deal. The federal government’s debt is shorter term than a lot of the private market’s debt and gets refinanced at higher rates more rapidly, and so higher rates are becoming somewhat stimulatory for some sectors of the economy that are directly or indirectly on the receiving side of those deficits. And so I pivoted my view based on the data in a direction that deviated from consensus, and the data continue to suggest that this is what is happening.

Specifically, if the government runs very large deficits and has high debt/GDP, and the central bank operates at high interest rates, but then also the government and central bank shield the private sector from much of the harm of those policies (for example, by making sure uninsured depositors don’t lose money from bank failures like in March 2023), then those high rates have a stimulatory effect for many parts of the economy that equals or possibly outweighs their slowdown effect. This is because there are many entities in the private sector that are on the receiving side of those fiscal deficits and interest expense (which is interest income for those entities). The exceptions to this are the sectors that have weak fundamentals and a lot of short-duration debt with high leverage ratios, such as office commercial real estate. Those sectors don’t really benefit from fiscal dominance.

However, if the Fed does cut rates, then there would be a risk of materially higher oil prices, which are also inflationary. So I lean toward an inflationary outlook either way, which is a hallmark of fiscal dominance.

Due to how unintuitive this concept is, here’s a repeat snippet and charts from my [February 2024 public newsletter issue](https://www.lynalden.com/february-2024-newsletter/) focused on this topic of fiscal dominance:

And I’ll use a nearly 70-year period from 1955 to the present, representing the majority of the post-war era.
>

The yellow areas indicate periods where the red line is higher than the blue line, meaning that public deficit spending is bigger than end-user private debt creation in absolute dollar terms. The vertical gray bars represent recessions:
>
> ![Public vs Private Debt 1](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/february-2024-newsletter-1955-1990.png)
>
> And this chart shows the period from 1990 to the present, representing (almost) another three-and-a-half decades:
>
> ![Public vs Private Debt 2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/february-2024-newsletter-1990-2023.png)
>
> The first observable trend is a cyclical one. During economic expansions, cyclical main street private sector debt (blue lines) tends to grow considerably. And then it tends to shrink a lot during and shortly after recessions, as some borrowers default and as lenders are more reticent to make new loans or buy new bonds. Acting somewhat countercyclically to that, fiscal deficits (red lines) tend to be lower during economic expansions, but tend to shoot up in the aftermath of a recession. And to be clear, the Fed’s hawkish positioning did slow down private sector credit creation as intended; it’s just that this downward force was overridden by the fiscal side starting in 2023, and that same hawkish positioning that slowed down private credit ironically contributed to the fiscal side growing bigger. And by most indicators, that slowdown in private credit has potentially already reached its weakest point or nearly so.

Senior loan officer surveys suggest that the peak rate of tightening credit standards from banks occurred in Q3 2023 and since then has been stabilizing:

![SLOOS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-sloos.png)

Bank loans are still growing at a slow rate, and are showing early signs of stabilization in their year-over-year falling rate of growth:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-bank-loans.png)

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The U.S. manufacturing sector continues to show signs of re-acceleration, with for example the manufacturing purchasing managers’ index back above 50 into mild expansion territory:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-ism-pmi.png)

I do have some concerns about U.S. credit spreads (e.g. the spread in yields between high-yield corporate debt and Treasuries). They are very inversely correlated to PMIs, but they are historically low compared to where PMIs are. If PMIs continue upward over the next 18 months, we should normally see falling credit spreads, but credit spreads are already near the low end of their historical range:

![Credit Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-credit-spreads.png)

This compression in spreads is partially because U.S. Treasury yields are higher than normal, and they’ve been acting unusually for the cycle. Usually when PMIs are falling, Treasury yields are also falling, which contributes to wider credit spreads. However in this cycle, as PMIs fell, U.S. Treasury yields *rose*, likely due to a combination of high inflation and the sheer amount of Treasuries that have been coming to market.

As we transition from monetary dominance to fiscal dominance (and I think we’ve been in that transition periodically since 2018 or 2019), we will likely continue to see more of these cyclical anomalies. Outside of a few trouble spots like commercial real estate, in this cycle the U.S. government has had a bigger “credit” problem than most of the private sector. As I described previously, the U.S. federal government has rather short duration on its debt compared to most large investment grade corporations, and so as interest rates rose, it ironically hit the government first and hardest. And in response to liquidity problems, the Treasury Department has been doing things like issuing most of their debt at even shorter durations, which is directionally similar to how emerging markets deal with  fiscal crises.

My expectation in the following 18 months is to see a re-steepening of the U.S. Treasury yield curve, perhaps from a combination of moderate front-end cuts and moderate long-end increases. That’s probably the most optimal path for the Federal Reserve to pursue at this time in terms of 1) trying to keep downward pressure on private sector lending and 2) preventing U.S. federal interest expense from continuing to accelerate. Putting upward pressure on long-end yields may keep downward pressure on private sector lending and refinancing (to keep inflation relatively contained), while trimming short-end rates can moderate federal interest expense due to how front-loaded the federal government is on its interest burden.

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-yield-curve.png)

If that happens, I would expect to see a bit of a rotation in equity performance. The big winners in 2023 are rather expensive and crowded trades, and a higher 10-year Treasury could pressure their valuations even as their fundamentals continue to do well. Meanwhile, this could benefit companies that do well from a rising manufacturing sector such as energy producers, or certain financial companies that benefit from a steeper yield curve.

**China Business Cycle**

China’s credit impulse has been rather unusual lately as well. Rather than big swings up and swings down, they’ve been maintaining a flatter approach.

![China Credit Impulse](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-china-credit-impulse.png)

As a result, inflation in China continues to be low, and they continue to be somewhat of a weight on global commodity prices. In many aspects, China has been running the opposite playbook as the United States, with tighter fiscal policy but looser monetary policy.

## Digital Assets Note

**Headline News**

Bitcoin has been trading in a relatively tight range above and below $70,000 for about a month. Most assets had weak price action on Friday, which was partly attributed to headlines around Iran’s likelihood of an attack on Israel, and bitcoin traded down to $66,000.

On Saturday, bitcoin immediately dropped from $66k to $61k on the headlines that Iran had launched drones and missiles at Israel. With most financial markets closed around the world, cryptocurrency markets are among the only place with liquidity for financial markets to take action, express a view on what will occur, offset other positions, and so forth. Most other cryptocurrencies fell more than bitcoin. However, Paxos Gold (PAXG), the most liquid gold-redeemable stablecoin, had a spike of volume and briefly traded from under $2,400 to $3,000 (representing one ounce of gold). But it quickly fell from there back to $2,400-$2,500 and began chopping from there. It’s much less liquid than larger gold markets that are not open on weekends, and so it was an interesting but imperfect signal at the time for what larger gold markets might do when they open.

An hour later, when Iran announced that the attack was in response to Israel’s attack on Iran’s consulate in Syria and that they consider the matter concluded unless Israel retaliates, the various crypto markets began to normalize. Iran signaling that it was basically a telegraphed tit-for-tat attack rather than something more destructive up front, market participants returned partially to the pre-attack baseline to see what if any escalation will occur.

**ETF Flows**

For the past month, total ETF holdings of bitcoin stalled because GBTC outflows re-accelerated, which offset the inflows to the other ETFs. This is partially because bankruptcy estates like Genesis were selling some of their GBTC shares, which is a leftover byproduct of the leveraged implosions that happened in 2022 rather than a reflection of current market forces.

![Bitcoin ETF Flows](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-bitcoin-flows.png)

My expectation is to see a pickup in total ETF inflows. It might not be as strong as it was through February and into early March, but I suspect that they’ll be better than the current period of mid-March through mid-April. And the overall rotation out of GBTC and into the other ETFs should continue due to the large difference in fees.

Meanwhile, the majority of indicators suggest that the bitcoin bull market is likely in the middle of its progress. It has certainly heated up, but in my view there is likely still more to go within the next 18 months. Large corrections of 20-30% or more routinely occur to bitcoin in bull markets, although larger geopolitical events could potentially play a role as well. For most people that want some bitcoin, I think [dollar-cost averaging into it](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) makes sense, so that its various ups and downs are less impactful.

![Bitcoin vs Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-bitcoin-vs-global-m2.png)

The ETHBTC ratio touched new cycle lows of under 0.047. My base case is that this ratio will continue to be lackluster going forward. Ethereum’s price is partially supported by its low issuance rate, but the path to an Ethereum ETF is currently murky, and Ethereum as a network faces a lot of competition from other proof-of-stake programmable blockchains.

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-ethbtc.png)

In general, as the price of bitcoin has corrected from over $73k to $61k and back up a bit, it has washed out the prices of other cryptocurrencies even more, where a lot of speculative froth had been building in recent months. As a result, the measure of Bitcoin dominance, referring to Bitcoin’s market capitalization compared to the combined capitalization of all cryptocurrencies, climbed back over 56%, which is a new high for this cycle.

**The Fourth Bitcoin Halving**

Every 210,000 blocks (which is approximately four years), the number of new bitcoins issued per block gets cut in half.

Within the next week, the Bitcoin network will undergo its fourth ever halving event after reaching 840,000 total blocks of transactions.

Miners make money in two main ways from adding a new block to the blockchain: 1) they earn newly-generated bitcoins and 2) they earn fees from users who paid fees to prioritize getting their transactions into the next block. Each block can hold a few thousand transactions, with the precise number depending on the complexity of the transactions.

This means two primary things. The first is that the Bitcoin network’s annual supply inflation rate will decrease from 1.7% to 0.85%. In other words, the number of new bitcoins generated per day will decrease from about 900 to about 450. At an average price of $70,000 per bitcoin, that would be $31.5 million worth of daily bitcoin creation. In comparison, gold historically has an annual supply growth rate of about 1.5%, and at current prices and production rates about $670 million worth of new gold supply hits the market daily and needs to be absorbed by global market demand.

The second thing is that the weakest miners are likely to struggle, and some may go offline, since their revenue will be impacted for a period of time. The miners with the highest electricity costs, the most debt, or other problems are the ones that will face most of the pressure. The miners with the lowest electricity costs, strongest balance sheets, and other strong points will have less of an issue. Regardless of how many miners leave the network or join the network, the automatic difficulty adjustment that occurs every two weeks will help ensure that new blocks of transactions keep being produced every ten minutes on average.

Casey Rodarmor, who popularized Ordinals Theory and the proliferation of Inscriptions (NFTs on the Bitcoin network), is planning to release a new protocol called Runes around the time of the halving. There is currently a protocol called BRC-20 that enables people to make various tokens on the Bitcoin network. Runes is intended to be a more efficient version of that type of token protocol, because it results in less network bloat.

It’s likely that the initial burst of usage of this new protocol will result in a period of network congestion and higher transaction fees. If that occurs, it would be temporarily beneficial for miners due to them receiving higher fees (thereby potentially mitigating the impact of the halving event), but temporarily harmful for users because they would be the ones paying higher fees amid the network congestion.

## Latin American Equities

Latin America as a region is among the most physically and politically distanced regions from the various geopolitical conflicts and outright wars in Eastern Europe and the Middle East.

And after Latin America as a whole went through severe economic turmoil from 2014 through 2020 (largely due to a strong dollar and weak commodity prices), several of the countries in the region have been gradually recovering, and their assets are quite little-owned by foreign investors.

However, there are still a lot of economic and political risks in the region, which makes it important to size positions accordingly in order to manage risk.

Since I am generally bullish on energy and commodities in general, I often look for alternative ways to get exposure to that thesis. Latin American equities are among the cheapest and riskiest ways to get exposure, but they can also act as a bit of a portfolio diversifier.

Brazil’s economic cycle, being a commodity-based emerging market that relies on dollar financing, is very much tied to the dollar index and global commodity prices. During periods characterized by a strong dollar and falling commodity prices (mid-1980s, late-1990s, and 2014-2020), Brazil has historically gone into outright economic depression. Conversely, during periods of a weaker dollar and stronger commodity prices, Brazil’s economy tends to enjoy a massive boom.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-dxy.png)

![Brazil GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-brazil-gdp-2.png)

In the 2014-2020 cycle, Brazil’s currency held up better than prior strong-dollar weak-commodity cycles because they had accumulated a reasonable amount of reserve assets and kept overall external debt levels relatively in-check. So, although they ran into significant economic pain, they have avoided the catastrophic currency problems of Argentina, Turkey, Lebanon, and many African countries that were hit the hardest in this cycle.

![Emerging Market Debt and Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-emerging-market-debt-vs-reserves.png)

Brazil’s central bank was one of the most hawkish in the world this cycle; they raised rates before the Fed, reached among the highest real rates in the world, and are now in a gradual cutting cycle since Q3 2023. Decent commodity prices and a global central bank cutting cycle should benefit them.

![Brazil Interest Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-brazil-interest-rate.png)

Colombia is in a similar position. They export a lot of oil, so when oil prices fell in 2014 from the rise of U.S. shale oil, it hurt their exports quite a bit:

![Colombia Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-colombia-exports.png)

And as a result, their exchange rate sharply fell vs the dollar in 2014 and has stayed down since:

![Colombian Peso](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-colombian-peso.png)

But much like Brazil, I think Colombia is likely at a late stage in its economic depression, and in my view has more upside potential over the next 5+ years than downside potential (although it has plenty of both).

![Colombia GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-colombia-gdp.png)

**Bancolombia Snapshot**

Colombia’s leading bank, Bancolombia, traces its routes back to 1875, and its ADR has been trading on the New York Stock Exchange since 1995 under the ticker “CIB”.

In addition to Colombia, the bank operates with strong marketshare in El Salvador, Panama, and Guatemala. Their lending is focused on two-thirds on the commercial side and one-third on the consumer side:

![Bancolombia Loans and Funding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-bancolombia-loans-funding.png)

*Chart Source: [Bancolombia Corporate Presentation](https://www.grupobancolombia.com/investor-relations/financial-information/presentations)*

The stock currently trades at a lower multiple of its earnings and book value than average, and pays a dividend yield of over 8%. It is considered quite risky, however, with a BB+ credit rating (i.e. just below investment grade). The chart below shows nearly the past three decades of the bank’s price/book ratio, indicating that the current ratio is back near where it was during the early 2000s strong dollar environment:

![CIB Price/Book Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-cib-pb-ratio.png)

The stock has provided decent returns in dollar terms, but most of them have come from dividends. The difference on the following YChart between total returns and price returns is due to cumulative dividends:

![CIB Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-cib-performance.png)

The stock price for a lot of Latin American banks including Bancolombia had four major lows in recent years. The first was during the global financial crisis in 2008/2009. The second was in late 2015 after the initial collapse in the oil price and the sharp move up in the dollar index. The third was the COVID-crash low of early 2020. The fourth was the retracement back down to that low in late 2022 as many central banks in Latin America raised interest rates up to the double digits to try to quell inflation.

My expectation is that their prices have room to run positively in the upcoming years, having gone through so many liquidity crunches, FX swings, and other issues. They have endured this strong dollar and weak oil cycle for a while, and I think having a small position in the stock can provide an asymmetrical risk/reward outcome if we enter a regime of stronger oil and a weaker dollar.

![CIB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-cib-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a common measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Overall, I think it’s reasonable to have a small slice of Latin American equity exposure at this time. The majority of the equity indices in these countries consist of financials, energy, materials, and other value-oriented sectors, but they have extra reward and risk compared to U.S. value stocks. Therefore, a small position can go a long way in terms of exposure.

## Final Thoughts: Gold’s Breakout

Several of my concluding sections in reports over the past several months have been about gold.

This is because the asset was in a pretty tight rangebound structure, akin to a “cup and handle” chart, and my expectation has been that whenever it firmly breaks out above its $2k resistance, it would likely have a lot of room to run.

> I still think gold is worth watching pretty closely at these levels. It has been banging its head against the approximately $2,000 level, and is currently near the high end of its recent three-year price range.
>
> ![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-11-26-gold.png)
>
> I expect the yellow metal to run pretty significantly if it firmly breaks above this recent zone. Historically, gold generally does well after the Federal Reserve stops raising rates, so it’s worth monitoring.
>
> I continue to use gold as a small portfolio diversifier position. This paid off very well in the 2018-2020 period, has stalled in the 2020-2023 period, and I suspect the 2024-2026 period will be better again for that portfolio slice. We’ll see.
>
> -November 26, 2023 report

As of this writing, gold has broken out, is up to over $2,300 USD per ounce, and has outperformed the S&P 500 year-to-date.

![Gold Since 1990](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-gold-since-1990.png)

In fact, gold has cumulatively outperformed the S&P 500 since late 2021. In other words, the S&P 500 to gold ratio peaked in late 2021 and has been gradually rolling over:

![SPX vs Gold Since 1990](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-14-spx-gold-since-1990.png)

Notably, most of the demand for gold is not coming from retail investors, and especially not from U.S. ETFs. Gold tonnage in U.S. ETFs is down despite the bullish price action. Instead, a lot of the demand is coming from central banks and other foreign investors.

From the 1980s through the 2000s as the world was becoming more and more unipolar, central banks were more interested in buying Treasuries than gold. However, the global financial crisis in 2009 marked a pivotal change; central bank holdings of gold bottomed that year and have been gradually rising ever since. The 2022 freezing of Russian reserves by the United States and Europe due to their invasion of Ukraine has been another pivotal point, although that one is still relatively recent. Now we have a world that is shifting toward being more multipolar.

Treasuries are growing in annual supply at a much faster rate than the gold supply is, and can be frozen by the issuer unlike gold. In an increasingly multipolar world and one where the United States is running larger than normal structural fiscal deficits, gold’s relative attractiveness compared to Treasuries as a reserve asset is elevated.

I expect gold to have various sell-offs as it climbs the “wall of worry”. Investors are already talking about it being overbought, which might not be wrong in the tactical sense, but when zooming out, it has broken out of a 3-year consolidation and is not all that much higher than it was back in 2011. My base expectation is that it has a lot more room to run in the years ahead.
