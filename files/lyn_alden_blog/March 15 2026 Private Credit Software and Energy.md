## Macro View

**Liquidity Conditions**

During crises, investors tend to flock to safe-haven assets, which among fiat currencies often (but not always) means the dollar.

Additionally, the majority of cross-border liabilities are denominated in dollars. Overall cross-border liabilities outstanding are collectively much larger than annual cross-border cashflows, and so whenever those cashflows are disrupted, those liabilities start becoming a problem quickly. Importantly, the majority of those dollar liabilities are not owed to entities in the United States, but nonetheless, it means there exists inflexible demand for dollars to meet existing liabilities. During crises, there can be a scramble for dollars out of necessity, not choice.

As a result, the dollar index (which compares the dollar to a basket of other major currencies, especially the euro) has inched up to over 100. This puts pressure on global liquidity. It isn’t a big deal yet by any stretch, but it’s a bit of a local breakout:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-dxy.png)

In addition, when individuals and companies have to pay more for energy and other basic materials, it means they have less purchasing power to spend elsewhere. The circulating flow of dollars outside of the energy sector risks stagnating. Overall, global liquidity isn’t great at the moment.

Domestic liquidity in the US financial system is still fine. It’s not without issues, but it remains much better than Q4 2025:

![SOFR IORB Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-sofr-iorb-annotated.png)

The Fed continues to expand its balance sheet for now (the “gradual print” as I’ve been calling it), which helps keep a lid on liquidity problems. In addition, the Fed has standing repo facilities for domestic institutions and standing swap lines with five foreign central banks, which together serve as the first lines of defense against most types of liquidity problems. They give the Fed time to respond more directly if there is an issue that those standing facilities cannot assist with.

**US Economic Deceleration**

GDP is reported in phases, since it takes a long time to compile the results. The initial report is an estimate, and then two further reports come out that refine the number.

Q4 2025 had weak headline growth, and the recent revision reported last week further dropped it. As of the latest estimate, Q4 2025 had just 0.7% annualized real GDP growth during the quarter. Importantly, this came along with net job creations that were revised downward for the year 2025 as well.

Meanwhile, the Atlanta Fed’s GDP estimate for Q1 2026, which isn’t perfect but takes into account monthly data as it rolls in, projects mediocre numbers:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-gdpnow.png)

According to the most recent official numbers, total jobs in the US have been completely flat since April 2025. This is partially due to difficulty graduates are having when looking for jobs, partially due to aging and slowing demographics, partly due to changes in net migration, and partly due to AI.

![Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-payems.png)

Overall, the US economy continues to operate at two speeds. Individuals, corporations, and industries that are on the receiving side of large US fiscal deficits or AI capex spending tend to be doing well. Meanwhile, those who are lower on the income spectrum and/or pressured by inflation and/or semi-tight monetary policy, tend to be having a rougher time. And it’s a rough time to be a white collar professional seeking employment.

The overall result continues to head up nominally (which is a hallmark of fiscal dominance) but is at stall speed by several tangible metrics. The economic shields are down, in other words, and a prolonged oil price spike is now a rising threat to that vulnerable situation.

**The Strait of Hormuz**

The war against Iran has now been going on for more than two weeks.

Crossings through the narrow Strait of Hormuz have been heavily impaired during this whole time, and many sources of energy production have been temporarily taken offline.

So far, it’s shaping up to be notably worse than the base case described in my prior report. Here’s a recap:

> By far the leading news this weekend is that Israel and the United States have struck Iran in a major operation, with a specific intention toward regime change and killing Iran’s supreme leader.
>
> Iran is a significant oil and liquid fuels producer, accounting for 3-5% of global production depending on what moment in time the figure is being estimated.
>
> ![Iran Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-iran-oil-production.png)
>
> However, the total potential impact from war in the region is much larger than that. The exact numbers vary by time and whether one focuses on oil specifically or the combo of oil and other hydrocarbon liquids, but somewhere in the ballpark of 20% of global production goes through the Strait of Hormuz. The vast majority of it heads east toward Asian markets.
>
> Iran has announced the closing of the Strait, but GPS monitoring on Saturday indicated that ships were still passing through, albeit at reduced volumes. Beyond that, there’s an issue of insurers cancelling policies on ships that pass through the Strait, which can turn the transit into an uneconomical decision regardless of whether the Strait is technically closed or not.
>
> As of this writing on Sunday, [three ships have been struck](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/three-tankers-damaged-gulf-us-iran-conflict-escalates-2026-03-01/), and over 200 are holding at anchor instead of passing through. The shipping company Maersk has an announced a suspension of its ships passing through.
>
> Additionally, Iran can strike major production assets throughout the region, taking them offline for weeks or months depending on the severity of the hit. Many of those attacks can be intercepted, especially if there are major naval assets in the region prepared to do exactly that, but not necessarily all of them. And Iran’s initial counterattacks [depleted](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/u-s-races-to-accomplish-iran-mission-before-munitions-run-out-c014acbc) a significant percentage of US stockpiles of interceptors, since our military weakness is the limited size of our industrial base and the finite speed with which we can produce them.
>
> As a base case, I don’t expect the Strait to remain closed or severely limited for very long, nor do I expect catastrophic losses in energy production in the region, but assessing that is not my expertise. As a general rule, Iran has been unable or willing to hit as hard as they’ve often threatened, and significant US military assets were concentrated in the region by commanders that have significant intelligence on Iran’s capabilities. With a fractured leadership structure, Iran’s capabilities are also impaired the longer the operations continue. The military experts I’m in contact with hold those views as well.
>
> But overall, this is part of why I have a significant amount of energy producers in the model portfolios. There are many tail risk scenarios, including primarily this one, that can drive energy prices higher. Beyond that, energy prices and energy producers are often uncorrelated with broader equities and bonds, because a shortage in energy can create a stagflationary situation that is good for energy producers (or at least those who are largely unaffected by whatever crisis caused the shortage) while being bad for most other assets.
>
> Outside of potential oil impacts, this is mostly a humanitarian and geopolitical topic. It potentially impacts US midterm outcomes (this administration explicitly ran against war), US/China relations, the quality of life for Iranian citizens, civilian casualties in the war, and many other topics that are outside of the scope of a financial/tech analyst.
>
> -March 1, 2026 premium report

Although some energy producing assets have been damaged, so far it hasn’t been too severe (at least, outside of Iran). Significant energy assets were taken offline by their operators to protect them and because they can’t get most of the products transported anyway, rather than due to outright damage. And it has only been a little over two weeks, so we’ve still not entered “very long” yet.

However, the region is in a pretty terrible place after the two-week mark. There is no clear path of de-escalation or clear timeframe for when the Strait might be safe to cross (and insurable for ships to do so) and there are contradictory statements from US officials (including President Trump and Secretary Hegseth) about what outcomes might result in an end to the war. Even when the primary portion of the war ends, it’s unclear how quickly the Strait might return to a safety level that approximates the pre-war period. High-signal military experts that I use as sources are considerably less optimistic than they were two weeks ago.

Additionally, multiple nations situated on the Persian Gulf who are allied with the United States are in rough shape, since their economies depend on flowing energy and/or luxury tourism, which is all impaired. This potentially weakens the United States’ long-term influence in the region, but it’s too early to tell.

Meanwhile, Iran continues to generate substantial counterattacks even after the initial fracturing of their leadership structure. Their one-way drones ([Shahed 136](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HESA_Shahed_136)) have been quite effective, due to how inexpensive they are versus the high expense of the interceptors that can shoot them down.

In recent years, during the Russian war on Ukraine, Iran provided this one-way drone technology to Russia (which they call Geran-2), and now the US entered this war with their own variant of the one-way drones called LUCAS. The US drones have reportedly been effective but haven’t been ramped up to massive manufacturing scale yet. Foreign Affairs has [an interesting article](https://www.foreignaffairs.com/iran/irans-drone-advantage) about how the US often captures enemy technology to learn to defeat it, and very rarely chooses to deploy it itself, but this was an exceptional case where they have done so.

## Updates on Banks and Software

**Banks and Private Credit**

The problems in private credit and private equity (and more broadly non-deposit financial institutions or “NDFI” aka “shadow banks”) continue to attract bearish attention.

In short, various regulations made it harder for banks to perform certain types of lending. And so NDFIs which do that type of lending became much larger, and actual banks can lend to those NDFIs.

The reason NDFIs can do it more easily, is because they don’t take liquid deposits as liabilities. They aren’t operating on fractional reserve, in other words. They’re not betting someone’s checking account on these types of loans. Instead, they’re taking longer-term investor capital from high net worth investors and institutions, and allocating it via equity or credit. That means they have less regulatory scrutiny.

Banks can lend to NDFIs because they have safeguards against the risk coming back to hit them. The first line of losses goes to the various investors in NDFIs, such as private credit and private equity funds. Losses have to become pretty severe before those NDFIs start defaulting on banks that lent them money. So, banks are involved, but in a sharply risk-reduced way.

People have been searching for “private credit” a lot lately, because more private credit funds are slowing down investor redemptions and running into issues. Here’s the latest from Google Trends:

![Private Credit Searches](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-private-credit-searches.png)

And it’s indeed a problem for NDFI investors. There really are issues in private credit and private equity. And by extension, there are problems in some life insurers that bet heavily on them (in part because those insurers are themselves partially controlled by NDFIs).

But the multi-trillion dollar question that investors are increasingly asking, is whether there is contagion risk to the broader banking system, like subprime mortgages in 2008.

While anything is technically possible if enough things are messed up in a row, my answer continues to be that contagion risks from NDFIs to the broader banking system are not that high. In other words, some *individual banks* might indeed get into trouble from lending to a private credit fund or similar activity (and probably will), but there’s little evidence suggesting that the problem is big enough to cause a 2008-style crisis to the US banking system *in aggregate*.

Here was my overview on that topic from back in October:

> The latest talking point about banks is their high rate of lending to non-deposit financial institutions or NDFIs. Broadly speaking, a NDFI is any financial institution that does not accept deposits and thus is not regulated like a bank. This can include hedge funds, pension funds, insurance companies, private credit, private equity, and various credit intermediaries (entities that borrow from non-deposit sources and make loans with that capital).
>
> The total amount of loans by US banks to NDFIs has reached $1.7 trillion:
>
> ![Loans to NDFIs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-loans-to-ndfi.png)
>
> To put that in context, total bank assets are around $24.5 trillion, and so loans to NDFIs make up about 7% of total bank assets:
>
> ![Loans to NDFIs Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-loans-to-ndfi-share.png)
>
> As those chart show, there was a big $250 billion jump right at the start of 2025. This is because US federal bank regulators finalized [a new set of reporting requirements](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2024/06/us-banking-regulators-finalize-nonbank-lending-reporting-requirements) for US bank about loans to NDFIs, which went into effect at the start of 2025. These requirements also expanded the definition of NDFIs. So, that vertical jump wasn’t a bunch of new NDFI loans being created; it was previously unreported ones being reported. Nonetheless, even without that jump, the growth has been significant.
>
> The growth in bank loans to NDFIs is partially due to a regulatory mismatch. Banks are the most regulated of financial entities, especially ever since the global financial crisis of 2008, whereas non-bank lenders or “shadow banks” have less stringent regulations. Thus, banks have played it safe with conservative lending standards, and in part have lent to NDFIs who can then lend a bit more flexibly, providing a buffer between the bank and the end-borrower.
>
> Now, when we talk about NDFIs or concerns about them, most people aren’t talking about insurance companies, pensions, or hedge funds in this context. They’re talking about the lenders. Primarily they refer to private credit, and this can also be expanded to include private equity.
>
> People often think of “shadow banks” or “private credit” as an inherently spooky thing, but they’re not really. In many ways, they are less extreme than a normal bank in theory. A normal bank operates with massive leverage, and uses liquid liabilities (deposits) to make illiquid loans, which can be quite dangerous. They take your liquid savings, or your payroll account if you’re a business, and lend it out to various borrowers. Private credit, however, uses investor money to make loans, and generally the private credit fund tells the investors up front that their funds may not be very liquid during times of stress. In other words, private credit uses illiquid liabilities (investor/institutional savings and bank loans) to make illiquid loans, which is quite fair in and of itself. And when a private credit fund blows up, it’s way less harmful than when a bank blows up, since it’s generally not Mom-and-Pop deposits in peril, or the payroll checking account of a startup.
>
> So, the theoretical basis for private credit is sound. In practice, however, there are indeed some issues. Since private credit funds are often filling a role that banks can’t or won’t do, private credit lending activities tend to be higher on the risk spectrum, although they will vary wildly in terms of the types of risk they take on. Additionally, the whole private credit market is less transparent than the regulated banking environment. This combination of higher risk and opaqueness means that it’s not too hard for a micro-bubble to form and pop, causing some damage without a lot of warning.
>
> The reason I say “micro-bubble” is because the raw numbers are pretty constrained. [According to Moody’s](https://www.moodys.com/web/en/us/insights/data-stories/breakdown-of-banks-annual-reporting-on-private-credit.html), US banks have lent about $300 billion to private credit funds. They also lent $285 billion to private equity, and have an additional $340 billion in credit facilities that are available to those borrowers but that haven’t been tapped yet, and thus haven’t been put at risk yet.
>
> In context, US banks have $24.5 trillion in assets and $2.5 trillion in equity capital. Even in an extreme event where 50% of all loans to private credit funds default, that’s a $150 billion hit to banks.
>
> There are cracks in private equity and credit. That much is true. And the weakening lower-income consumer segment is contributing to that. But the majority of the pain is or will be felt by the investors in those types of funds that face problems. It takes quite a lot of damage in a private credit fund for them to not only fail their investors but also outright default on their loans to the bank. And then as shown by the numbers above, even a massive default wave by private equity and credit is absorbable by banks in aggregate (although surely some over-exposed banks could be in trouble).
>
> When people see big numbers in lending, and warnings around excess, they often think about the global financial crisis. However, the global financial crisis, aka the subprime mortgage crisis, was focused on residential real estate lending, which is the biggest bank lending category of all. Total mortgages outstanding were $10.8 trillion in 2008, and total bank assets were only $12.2 trillion. Those mortgage liabilities were held partially by banks, and partially by NDFIs. A major default cycle in residential real estate mortgages was a true kill shot for the US banking system, which was why it was such a crisis at the time.
>
> The global financial crisis was the absolute peak in the private sector debt bubble. Banks only had 3% of their assets in cash. Since then, the debt has rotated from the private sector to the public sector, especially as a share of GDP. Banks have much higher ratios of cash and Treasuries now than they did going into 2008.
>
> ![Debt to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-debt-to-gdp.png)
>
> To the extent that banks have problem areas today, they are mainly micro-issues. In other words, some industries are indeed hurting (commercial real estate, certain types of private equity and private credit, etc), but that is mostly a problem for them and their investors, and not a large enough issue to cripple the US banking system as a whole.
>
> For example, we can take this tweet:
>
> ![Private Credit Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-loans-to-private-credit.png)
>
> Seems like a problem, right?
>
> Let’s start with the biggest: Wells Fargo. They have $1.74 trillion in assets, of which their $59.7 billion in private credit exposure represents 3.4% of their total assets. If half of all of that defaulted on them tomorrow, it’d be a ~$30 billion hit, which would represent a 1.7% loss of their total assets, and a moderate dent in their total equity capital.
>
> Next we can look at Truist, since I’m an investor in that one. Truist has $535 billion in assets, of which $19.5 billion is made up of loans to private credit. That represents 3.6% of their total assets, and if half of it were to all default tomorrow, it wouldn’t feel great but it would represent only a 1.8% reduction in their assets and a moderate dent to their total equity capital.
>
> The list goes on.
>
> At this point, a bear might acknowledge that any individual problem area is pretty small, but all of the problem areas together are pretty big. If there would be a massive crash in commercial real estate, combined with a massive crash in private equity and private credit, then that could damage banks. And of course, that’s true.
>
> But even that is containable. The first line of damage hits investors in those types of funds. The equity holders in commercial real estate, the equity holders in private equity, the investors in various funds that focus on these areas, are who would feel the pain first as their investments are marked down and in some cases zeroed out. Many loans by banks are highly collateralized, meaning it takes a massive loss of value to even partially breach the amount they’ve lent.
>
> The second line of damage if it gets bad enough hits the banks via defaults on the loans they’ve made, but that’s where these ratios matter. Their exposure to these troubled areas collectively represents a rather manageable share of their total assets and total equity capital.
>
> Here’s a chart of cash and Treasuries held by banks as a percentage their total assets:
>
> ![Bank Safe Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-safe-assets.png)
>
> In the run-up to the global financial crisis, this figure dropped to just 13%. This meant that only 13% of bank assets were held in nominally risk-free assets, and the other 87% was exposed to things that had a realistic chance of default.
>
> Fast-forward to today, banks have 32% of their assets in nominally risk-free assets, nearly a record high, and only 68% in assets that have a realistic chance of default. And those 68% of assets are made with pretty tight lending standards today.
>
> As the regional bank crisis of March 2023 showed, banks can indeed choke even on Treasuries in extreme scenarios, but that only applied to banks with very unique circumstances. They had huge deposit growth, mainly among flighty depositors above the $250k FDIC insurance limit, and heavily invested in long-duration Treasuries. Facing the most rapid rate hiking cycle in history, a handful of those banks like Silicon Valley Bank had their deposits rug-pulled and thus ironically faced insolvency even as they held default-free assets. The rest of the banking system was fine.
>
> Most well-diversified banks in the US are sound. They hold considerable cash and Treasuries, already survived the rapid rate-hiking cycle, and they hold a diverse array of loans that for the most part have pretty low default rates even in adverse scenarios.
>
> So many people are looking for the next private sector bubble, and while small-to-medium ones will indeed pop up from time to time, the biggest bubble is up at the sovereign level: the federal debts and deficits. And that’s a bubble that ends in fire (debasement and inflation), not ice (defaults).
>
> -October 26, 2025 premium report

The big four national G-SIB banks (JP Morgan Chase, Wells Fargo, Bank of America, and Citigroup) have 4% to 10% of their assets invested in NDFIs. That’s very manageable.

The three super-regionals (PNC Financial, US Bancorp, and Truist Financial) have 6-12% exposure. That’s also very manageable.

Most of the regionals that make up the rest of the chart are in that range as well. The outlier is First Citizens BancShares with 16% exposure, but that’s not a large bank.

Massive NDFI losses would have to occur to private equity investors and private credit investors to start hitting most of these banks. And then even if that were to occur and start hitting these banks, most of them could lose 50% of their entire NDFI book of loans and still be solvent.

Overall, US banks have $1.9 trillion in lending exposure to NDFIs, which is up from $1.7 trillion when I discussed the issue back in October.

![NDFI Exposure](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-ndfi-exposure-annotated.png)

I do think the trend is concerning in and of itself. Investors in private credit and private equity should be on alert for risk exposures. Bank investors should check to see how much exposure their favorite banks have to the space.

However, it’s still at a level that is quite containable in the aggregate sense. It’s an area I’ll continue to watch with interest, but not yet with alarm.

Thus, I continue to be bullish on select regional and super-regional banks, and this recent selloff has alleviated the near-term overbought aspect that some of their stocks had.

**AI-Disrupted SaaS Companies**

The market continues to be spooked about the prospects of AI disrupting various software companies, but we’ve got another data point.

I’m currently watching Adobe (ADBE) as a key bellwether, along with some others that I highlighted in the March 1st report. They announced earnings last week, which were pretty good, but the stock sold off anyway. Their 62-year old long-time CEO announced that he’ll be shifting toward being the executive chairman after his CEO successor is named, which spooked already-bearish investors.

![ADBE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-adbe-fastgraph.png)

Adobe has been effectively shifting toward a freemium strategy (a bunch of free software offerings to attract basic users, with paid paths toward more advanced features). Their creative freemium users crossed 80 million monthly active users during this prior quarter, which is a 50% increase year-over-year and includes browser-based and mobile versions of their design software.

Additionally, their AI offering, Firefly, saw a massive 45% quarter-over-quarter increase in generative credits consumed by users. Users of Adobe design software are increasingly using Adobe’s add-in AI features, which Adobe charges for based on consumption levels.

The key metric that investors look at for SaaS companies is annualized recurring revenue. Fremium stuff is great for maintaining a funnel of new users, but ultimately it has to translate into revenue. Adobe reported 10.9% year-over-year ARR growth, and expects 10.2% ARR growth over the next year. Investors don’t seem to love that deceleration, but the absolute growth levels are solid, and I’d argue it’s already more than priced in. The company is trading at around 11x forward 2026 earnings, and with a fortress balance sheet.

I continue to be bullish fundamentally and view it as a stock to gradually add to, but am waiting to pound the table on it until more of a technical bottom forms. And I would encourage proper position sizing since there are still some key questions here.

Overall, I think several SaaS companies will make for great contrarian investments, but we’re still a bit early on them so I’m only dabbling for now.

## Final Thoughts: Sci Fi Becoming Real

We live in such a fast-moving time.

That’s partially why I wrote my new science fiction book now, rather than keep waiting. The story has been in my head for well over a decade, and I figured I might write it whenever I retired from finance, but technologies that were far out back when I thought of it are closer to becoming reality today.

If I didn’t prioritize it, portions of it risked becoming historical fiction rather than science fiction.

While much of the physical world changes pretty slowly, the technology we interact with changes rapidly, and then by extension, our behavior patterns change rapidly as well.

Take dating for example. In 1994, US couples met each other primarily through friends, work, family, bars, and school/college. Only about 1% met online. Just 30 years later, or a single generation of time, that online figure jumped to 61%, dwarfing all other categories combined:

![1994](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-1994.png)

![2024](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-15-2024.png)

Imagine how much that one key change can trickle into so many other social dynamics, economic outcomes, etc. And then multiply it for every other category where technology has radically changed our lives, such as how we consume news or how much time we spend looking at screens.

The adoption of the internet changed countless things, and then as bandwidth improved, the extra layer of social media changed many things more. More recently, the creators of AI chatbots and AI agents are building on top of that already-rapidly-changing foundation.

Trading is about predicting the near-term, investing is about predicting the intermediate-term, and that largely leaves the long-term to the imagination. But by having an idea of what that last category might look like (i.e. decades or centuries in the future), it helps us frame the shorter time periods as well (i.e. what has to happen in the next 5-10 years as a milestone, if the future is going to look a certain way in 50 years?)

There are a lot of things to monitor these days, such as the geopolitics around the Strait of Hormuz, the problems festering in private credit, and the socioeconomic impacts of rapidly-advancing AI technologies. The former two are more short-term or intermediate-term (although they can have longer-range effects), while AI is mainly about the intermediate-term and long-term.

Spending a lot of my spare time during the past year and a half writing science fiction forced me to think deeply about technologies like AI, virtual reality, robotics, batteries, social media, gene editing, and more. This includes their various rates of adoption and improvement and their potential bottlenecks and effective ceilings or stagnation points.

But ultimately, it’s a human story, so I always circle back to how changes in these things affect *people*.
