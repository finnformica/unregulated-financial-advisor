# Amgen (AMGN) Deep Dive Analysis

*December 6, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report provides macro updates on the U.S. economic situation and global PMI indicators. It then dives into an update on Amgen, one of our previous stock picks. At a time when many of our other ideas have rocketed off, Amgen keeps plodding along and still remains relatively cheap.

As a housekeeping note, I published a new public research piece this week:

* [The Fraying of the US Global Currency Reserve System](https://www.lynalden.com/fraying-petrodollar-system/)

That longform article provides context for how the global monetary system works, why it hasn’t been functioning very well over the past decade, and in what ways it’s beginning to change around the margins.

## Macro Update

The market has been dealing with short-term bad news and long-term good news over the past month.

In the short-term, a rebound in virus hospitalizations, shifting consumer behavior around that issue, and renewed partial shutdowns from various governments, have weighed on economic growth in some areas.

In the long-term, markets appear optimistic about vaccines and overall 2021 global recovery potential, and the prospect for a “skinny” fiscal-stimulus is firmly back on the table in this post-election environment, but may be overlooking some of the near-term headwinds.

**Coronavirus Hospitalizations**

The latest pandemic wave has lead to various partial shutdowns in Europe and the United States, which has particularly impacted small businesses. As parts of the economy continue to rebound, other parts continue to sag.

The United States has a record number of people currently hospitalized for the virus, and this number is more than a 50% increase from the previous two waves:

![Coronavirus Hospitalizations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-coronavirus-hospitalizations.png)

The United States currently has the fifth-highest death count per capita from the virus among highly-developed countries, and when that’s adjusted for differences in median age, we’re the second or third highest.

I focus on hospitalizations and deaths rather than cases, because cases are highly dependent on test counts and thus not the most useful metric. Additionally, when a hospital system gets close to reaching capacity, that tends to be the catalyst for when local and state governments enforce partial shutdowns to try to bend the growth curve.

**US Jobs Report**

The jobs report last week was rather bad. One of the worst data points out there.

I have proposed since April that we would likely see a “backward square root sign” recovery in US jobs, meaning a partial-V sharp bounce-back after the March/April forced closures, but then a long flat grind to get the rest of the jobs back as the full credit cycle plays out. And that hypothetical chart looks like a backward square root sign.

Dreadfully, month after month have continued to reinforce that view, with last week’s report being the worst with only 245,000 net jobs added:

![US Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As of November 2020, the US currently has 10 million fewer jobs than the peak in February 2020. It would take 420,000 net new jobs per month from this point to reach new highs in two years by November 2022.

The US currently has a lower labor participation rate than Japan, even though Japan’s median age is 10 years older.

**The US Business Cycle Heatmap**

If we take a broader view of the US economy and break it down part by part, we see some divergences. The economic bottom was in Q2 2020, but from there, some areas have bounced back more than others. Retail sales, capital spending, and construction spending, have been the strong areas. Jobs have been the weakest area.

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than cherry pick good or bad data points to fit a narrative:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

Official consumer price inflation measures for November aren’t out for a few more days so the heatmap column for CPI ends in October, but we have the Treasury market’s inflation expectations in real time through early December.

There was a dip in October for both official CPI (blue) and inflation expectations (red), but since then, inflation expectations have rebounded to 12+ month highs:

![Inflation Expectations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-inflation.png)

We’ll see if official November CPI data confirms or refutes what the Treasury market is pricing in. Meanwhile, commodities, equities, and nominal Treasury yields are currently corroborating the higher-inflation theme over the past month.

**Dollar Update**

Over the past month, the dollar broke below the lower-bound support levels that it established this summer, and reached multi-year lows in reference to a basket of other major currencies. Here’s the dollar index chart:

![USD Weekly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-usd-weekly.png)

My [research piece on the global reserve system](https://www.lynalden.com/fraying-petrodollar-system/) goes into detail on the US dollar. And as I explain in that article, I have no strong opinion about the next 3-6 months for the dollar (economic shutdowns and fiscal stimulus levels both factor heavily into that period), but I remain longer-term dollar bearish.

The next support level for the dollar index is just below 89. This was the low from early 2018. Similarly, the EUR/USD pair (and the euro is the biggest component of the dollar index comparison basket) has a resistance level at $1.25 dollars to per euro. If the dollar index breaks firmly below 89, and/or the USD/EUR pair breaks firmly above 1.25, then the floor is pretty much open for the dollar to fall further.

However, I’d expect some support at those levels, especially given that there’s already plenty of short interest in the dollar, so it’s becoming a rather consensus trade. Longer-term, I think the dollar can break those levels and get weaker.

## Bitcoin Update

In my previous report, on November 22, I updated readers about Bitcoin’s recent multi-month parabolic move upward, and cautioned about the prospect for a correction or consolidation in its price, while re-iterating my bullish outlook through next year. I showed various charts indicating near-term overbought levels, but not cycle peak major overbought levels. Here was my summary point:

> My base case is to expect some resistance and corrections/consolidations around this current level through $20,000 or so, because it’s a retest of previous all-time highs set in 2017, and so some selling pressure is natural. Therefore, it’s important to manage expectations around this level. I remain bullish into 2021 with the caveat about this potential near-term resistance.

Bitcoin indeed had a big correction during American Thanksgiving and the day after, on Nov 24, when many of the big institutional buyers were likely absent. Bitcoin’s price fell from over $19,000 to under $17,000, and then quickly recovered. Since then, it briefly touched new highs but mostly has been trading in a $18,500-$19,500 price range right near all-time highs, with just under $20,000 serving as a resistance level.

The Bitcoin protocol generates a certain number of new coins every time a new block on the blockchain is generated, which happens every ten minutes. From inception of the protocol in 2009, the number of new coins generated per block is pre-programmed to get cut in half every 210,000 blocks, which is approximately every 4 years. Here’s the long-term log chart of Bitcoin’s price, with supply halving events and cycle-highs annotated:

![Bitcoin Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-bitcoin-price.png)

My base case is to see the price continue the pattern and eventually punch through $20,000 to the upside, but I don’t have a firm view about when; it could happen tomorrow or it could happen three months from now, and neither of those would be particularly surprising to me. My public article on Bitcoin is [here](https://www.lynalden.com/invest-in-bitcoin/).

For now, it remains in a somewhat near-term risky position under its resistance level, and in my view would be somewhat “de-risked” as a trade if/when it does firmly punch through $20,000.

For folks that are interested in having a nonzero position in Bitcoin but feel they missed out on this year’s rally and don’t want to risk buying a top, then dollar-cost averaging into it is the simplest approach. For the next simplest approach, here’s a straightforward game plan to consider:

* Buy any big dips, if they occur, to create an initial position.
* If no dip, buy if it decisively breaks over $20,000.

## Chinese Stock Potential De-Listing

There appears to be [bipartisan support](https://www.investors.com/news/china-stocks-delisting-threat-grows-for-alibaba-nio-house-vote-kandi-fraud-allegations/) to potentially de-list some Chinese stocks from US stock exchanges. Some research members have asked me about it, since we do own a couple of them as small positions in portfolios.

On the surface this is about auditing differences, but at its heart, it’s about growing tension and a gradual economic cold war between US and China.

Large Chinese companies often have listings on stock exchanges outside of Mainland China, including in Hong Kong and the United States. The new US viewpoint is that Chinese stocks on US exchanges should have to conform to the same auditing standards as US companies. China, however, doesn’t want American regulatory bodies looking through their companies’ books.

Specifically, Chinese stocks have independent auditors like other companies, but the US-based Public Company Accounting Oversight Board (PCAOB) isn’t granted access to the books.

Historically, the independent auditor for Alibaba (BABA), Tencent (TCEHY), and JD (JD) has been PwC, one of the Big Four global accounting firms. In 2019, JD switched to Deloitte, another Big Four global accounting firm.

**Why I’m Taking No Action**

I’m not selling BABA, JD, or TCEHY from any portfolios, for two reasons.

First of all, these three large-cap and mega-cap companies don’t require American capital. It’s nice, and they like it, but they don’t need it. They’re profitable and self-sufficient at this point. Being delisted from American exchanges wouldn’t significantly affect their fundamental operations. Any sharp sell-off in their stock prices related to a de-listing would likely be buying opportunities. Delisting is not a huge blow to the companies themselves; it’s about convenience of Americans being able to access them for investment.

Secondly, the bill is not about immediate de-listing. It sets a long-term *potential* for delisting in the year or two ahead. If it’s passed, we’ll see how China and the companies respond, and how Yellen-elect’s Treasury Department interprets it. The worst case scenario is that we won’t be able to hold JD and BABA in the M1 Newsletter portfolio at some point in the future, since M1 only grants access to major US exchanges. It’s not a near-term problem.

For Tencent in particular, there’s a third reason. Tencent doesn’t trade on major US exchanges anyway. For Americans, it’s available over-the-counter or through Hong Kong. That’s why I have it in my personal holdings and in the No Limits portfolio, but not in any of the M1 portfolios. BABA trades on the NYSE, and JD trades on the NASDAQ, so we have those in the M1 newsletter portfolio.

For risk management, I maintain limited position sizes in any one Chinese company. Small Chinese companies often do have fraud, so I avoid them, and focus on large caps for Chinese equity exposure. Overall, in terms of “trust” for Chinese companies, I rank TCEHY and JD as being rather high, and BABA a bit less. However, BABA comes with a lower valuation, so I view it as having similar risk when the position size is managed appropriately.

## Amgen (AMGN) Deep Dive Analysis

I previously covered Amgen (AMGN) in my [February 16th deep dive report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-2-16/) so I will keep this one more concise, but among stocks I cover, I still like its risk/reward ratio quite a bit.

In the several months after my deep dive report, Amgen outperformed the market by a hefty margin, but in recent months it has been lagging:

![AMGN Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-amgn-performance.png)

The underperformance began in October when Amgen [announced results](https://www.amgen.com/media/news-releases/2020/10/amgen-cytokinetics-and-servier-announce-topline-results-from-galactichf-a-phase-3-trial-of-omecamtiv-mecarbil-in-patients-with-heart-failure/) for a phase 3 trial for omecamtiv mecarbil that came in below expectations. Amgen had a quick correction, and then traded in a choppy sideways pattern as the broader market continued to rise. In November, Amgen [stepped away](https://www.amgen.com/media/news-releases/2020/11/amgen-to-transition-development-and-commercial-rights-for-omecamtiv-mecarbil-and-amg-594-to-cytokinetics/) from the project.

Amgen’s strength is that it has a diversified set of products and plenty of capacity for both in-house product development and acquisitions, but a setback in key trials still does quickly translate into a reset of stock prices in response to mildly lower growth expectations. One of their *potential* growth vectors is off the table.

On the other hand, Amgen continues to expand its presence around the world. Over the past decade, they expanded their market access from 50 to 100 countries. This year, they’ve emphasized getting a better foothold in Japan.

I continue to watch Amgen’s product pipeline overall, since failing to introduce new blockbuster products is their biggest long-term risk. They still have plenty of projects in their pipeline, including new drugs and new uses for existing drugs, although I would like to see them strengthen it in the years ahead. However, considering the risks of all of the other equities I hold, I like how Amgen affects the overall portfolio balance, especially considering Amgen’s low valuation.

The next 3-6 months are a key test for the global reflation narrative. As previously shown, PMIs rolled over in some countries and the American job market is a mess. Millions of Americans who can’t pay rent become vulnerable to eviction from their rented homes at the end of December when this year’s eviction mortarium expires. A correction (and buying opportunity) is potentially in the cards for some economically-sensitive stocks that have sharply rallied over the past month, if this economic situation rolls over into a hard landing.

In this environment of uncertainty, healthcare stocks remain a good opportunity in my view. A divided or centrist government is unlikely to impose major changes to the US healthcare system, and healthcare stocks are not very vulnerable to weak economic data, since their products are used whether there’s an economic recession or expansion. A basket of blue-chip healthcare names remains attractive as a portfolio ballast.

**Management Quality and Balance Sheet**

Over the past ten years, Amgen has outperformed the S&P 500 and the biotech sector. The past five years have been more corrective for biotech in general, but Amgen has done well enough.

Amgen has a long history of strong returns on invested capital that are well above their weighted average cost of capital:

![AMGN ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-amgen-roic.png)

At the start of 2018, Amgen had over $40 billion in cash-equivalents and short-term investments on their balance sheet.

With interest rates going down, they took $30 billion of that cash and plowed it into share buybacks, which increased the ownership stake of each remaining share, and thus increased per-share earnings and per-share dividends, etc. However, this brought the balance sheet from being “pristine” to “good”. This occurred during 2018 and 2019.

In 2020, AMGN has mildly strengthened their balance sheet again. Cash-equivalents and short-term investments have grown faster than debt year-to-date for them:

![AMGN Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-amgn-2020-balance-sheet.png)

With $34 billion in long-term debt, and $12 billion in cash, AMGN has $22 billion in net long-term debt. That’s about 3x their annual net income, and 2x their annual free cash flow, and thus represents a rather strong balance sheet. Their S&P credit rating is A+.

**Valuation Analysis**

After its recent consolidation, Amgen’s valuation is just below its 5-year average range. This chart shows its cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio in purple, and price/sales ratio in orange:

![AMGN Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-amgn-valuation.png)

Their F.A.S.T. Graph also remains quite attractive, with a blended price/earnings ratio of less than 15x and an attractive dividend-adjusted PEG ratio:

![AMGN FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-amgn-fastgraph.png)

For context, it makes sense to compare it to the S&P 500.

While Amgen trades in-line with its earnings, the S&P 500 has decoupled from 2020 earnings and trades at historically fairly-valued levels for 2022 or 2023 earnings:

![SPX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-12-6-spx-fastgraph.png)

So, between the two, I’d rather go with Amgen. Or, more specifically, I’d rather go with a diverse basket of companies like Amgen, to get broad exposure to many appropriately-valued companies without taking on any significant single-company risk. Therefore, Amgen continues to fit well within the scheme of our portfolios and stock-selection process.

**Company Risks**

Amgen operates in an industry that is not very cyclical, but that does have two substantial risks.

*R&D Pipeline Risks*

The company must continually reinvent itself with successful R&D for new products to replace existing products that either reach patent expiration or face new competition. The October announcement about a disappointing trial underscores this.

It is challenging to forecast in advance which products will be duds (fail clinical trials, or quickly become irrelevant despite working as intended due to a better competing product), marginally profitable (plenty of R&D spending followed by only modest sales to recoup the initial expenses), or wildly successful (where the product becomes the leader in its category, and brings in billions and billions of free cash flow and pays for itself many times over). The big profitable drugs help pay for all of the smaller drugs that are important for patients but that don’t fully pay for their own R&D efforts.

The company is spending sizable amounts on R&D, has decent existing patent protection for many of their products, and can use acquisitions to further diversify and grow as needed.

*U.S. Political Risk*

With 3/4ths of the company’s sales coming from the United States, Amgen is vulnerable to U.S. healthcare reform. Amgen’s 10-k risk assessment explains it well:

> Sales of our principal products are dependent on the availability and extent of coverage and reimbursement from third-party payers. In many markets around the world, these payers, including government health systems, private health insurers and other organizations, remain focused on reducing the cost of healthcare, and their efforts have intensified as a result of rising healthcare costs and economic challenges. Drugs remain heavily scrutinized for cost containment. As a result, payers are becoming more restrictive regarding the use of biopharmaceutical products and scrutinizing the prices of these products while requiring a higher level of clinical evidence to support the benefits such products bring to patients and the broader healthcare system. These pressures are intensified where our products are subject to competition, including from biosimilars.

Medical costs have increased at a much faster rate than broad consumer inflation over the past few decades, and people in the United States pay far higher per-capita healthcare costs than any other country in the world. Many U.S. political candidates are in favor of significant healthcare reform. The current regime is very favorable to healthcare companies, but future programs may be less beneficial to them.

Unlike health insurance companies that could potentially face existential risk if something like Medicare for All is passed into law, healthcare product companies like Amgen mainly would face margin pressure in certain reform scenarios. So, the risk is notable, but less severe for companies like Amgen.

In addition, it helps to keep in mind the process in which healthcare reform would take place. First, a candidate that favors substantial healthcare reform would need to win the U.S. presidency. Then, they would likely also require a majority in the US House of Representatives, and enough dominance in the US Senate to either overcome a filibuster or eliminate the filibuster. This isn’t the case for the next 2-4 years at least.

So, the potential for healthcare reform needs to be on the radar of healthcare stock investors, but proper diversification, position-sizing, and a focus on companies that make important products, can mitigate the risk.

## Summary

The next 3-6 months remain highly uncertain. I’m curious to see what happens with fiscal stimulus or lack thereof, the US housing situation after the eviction moratorium expires, and the overall US jobs situation. I will continue to report on these items.

We still have structural disinflation forces in many parts of the world, including the US, but also have a bounce-back effect and a large degree of monetary stimulus. As is generally the case at the ends of [long-term debt cycles](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/), I expect the inflationary forces to eventually win out, but it can be a very bumpy ride during that transition process.

I remain long-term bullish on commodities and decent-quality value stocks, but we’ve come very far very fast this past month, and I would prefer to see a pullback in some of those names to rebalance some capital into.

Consequently, healthcare stocks remain a boring but relatively attractive investment area at this time. They are neither overvalued, like some tech stocks are, nor are they vulnerable to an economic shock over the next 3-6 months, and thus are kind of in that risk/reward sweet spot. I prefer emphasizing several healthcare stocks rather than betting too heavily on any one in particular, due to pipeline risk, or litigation risk, etc.

In addition to equities, I continue to like allocations to bitcoin, gold, silver, and platinum in my portfolios. Bitcoin remains my high-volatility preference over the next year, while platinum’s recent break-out has been a moderate-volatility opportunity that I’ve rotated some of my portfolios slightly into, as described in the previous report’s portfolio update section.
