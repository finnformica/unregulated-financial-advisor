## Macro View

Financial markets are dealing with two somewhat opposing forces right now: liquidity conditions (+) and the economic cycle (-).

**Liquidity conditions**

Throughout much of 2022, various measures of domestic U.S. liquidity were declining, primarily due to the Fed’s balance sheet reduction. However, starting at the the beginning of Q4 2022, domestic liquidity conditions instead began going sideways, because the Treasury’s drawdown of their cash account began offsetting the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet reduction.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-domestic-liquidity-annotated.png)

This pivot in liquidity also marked the top in the dollar index (which is good for global liquidity), the re-liquification of the highly-illiquid Treasury bond market, and the bottom in both the S&P 500 and the gold price. With somewhat of a lag due to the FTX/Alameda industry-specific fraud and blow-up, bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies began rallying off of that bottom as well.

[![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-dxy.png)](https://ycharts.com/store/start_trial_register?utm_source=lyn+alden)

The problem is that this improvement in liquidity is contributing to a mini-resurgence in inflation as well. A number of recent inflation reports came in above expectations (despite still being on a downtrend in year-over-year percentage terms), and for example the [Manheim used car price index](https://publish.manheim.com/en/services/consulting/used-vehicle-value-index.html) had a big bounce.

![Used Car Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-used-car-index.png)

Readers will recall that my big theme throughout 2022 is that the Fed and other policymakers can suppress inflation temporarily by going all-out on tightening, and thus likely causing a recession in the process. As soon as they allow another growth cycle, then those inflationary pressures are likely to return, since the fundamental inflationary issues (big fiscal deficits and resource constraints, among other things) remain mostly unaddressed.

In that sense, the current monetary policy towards inflation is like holding a volleyball underwater; it’s ready to pop back up once the holder lets go. This past few months has been an example of slightly letting it go.

This recent letting-go and its associated results will likely reignite some hawkishness from Fed Chairman Jerome Powell, and interest rates markets are already pricing that in with a re-upping of forward expectations of interest rates. As long as nothing is outright breaking and labor markets are still strong, Powell can maintain his hawkish approach. He’s going to try to push that volleyball back down, hard. Jim Bianco posted some good charts on the market’s re-pricing of forward Fed rate levels:

![Bianco Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-bianco-tweet.png)

What that chart shows is that market expectations regarding the top in interest rates jumped from 4.88% on February 2nd to 5.30% on February 17th, and pushed back the expected top in rates from June 2023 to August 2023. Higher for longer.

Most analysis on inflation seems to be about whether and how quickly the Fed can get back to 2%. While that’s important for tactical trading this year, I think that’s the less interesting question. The more interesting question to me is can they get to 2% inflation without a recession, and then can they keep it at 2% in the next growth cycle. My base case answer is that no, they can’t.

The problem in the longer-run, in my view, is that the Fed is fighting 1940s-style fiscal-driven inflation with 1970s-style monetary policy (which is primarily used to combat lending-driven inflation, and thus not likely the right tool). If they keep interest rates up long enough and high enough, this could backfire by actually exacerbating inflation, not in the near term but in the longer-term, by *increasing* fiscal budget deficits and thus fiscal-driven inflation due to larger interest expenditure. Most of the increase in money supply and associated price increases in recent years came not from bank lending but rather from fiscal deficits, and yet the Fed is mainly targeting bank lending and asset prices. Powell directly and publicly called for more fiscal stimulus in 2020 (which is rather unusual for a Fed chair), and so he can’t realistically blame fiscal stimulus for inflation now, which politicizes the matter.

But that risk of positive rate and inflation correlation is more of a mid-2020s story, rather than the key story right now. It’s also Monday morning quarterbacking; Powell is going to do what Powell is going to do and investors should invest accordingly. I’ll have some articles about that specific problem in the months ahead. Right now, monetary tightening is the name of the game, and it can likely suppress private sector lending faster than it exacerbates fiscal deficits, for now.

Treasury Secretary Yellen estimated that the federal government can fund itself despite the debt ceiling, using extraordinary measures, at least through June. It would risk a default sometime between July and September if the debt ceiling is not raised, and the estimate is loose because it depends on details of tax revenue, timing of expenditures, and other factors. The drawdown of the Treasury cash account to nearly zero is one of the extraordinary measures, and is actually good for liquidity when it happens.

So, unless I see evidence to the contrary, my base case is for neutral domestic liquidity conditions through June 2023. The Fed will be doing QT, the Treasury will likely be offsetting that (not necessarily on a week-by-week basis but on a month-by-month basis), and reverse repos will be flat-to-down. After that, we’ll see.

How this will affect global liquidity will primarily depend on dollar strength. I admittedly don’t have a firm view on the dollar index at this time. China re-opening and Europe getting out of its dangerous winter months and leaving behind the risk of acute natural gas shortages (thankfully they had an unusually warm winter, and avoided a heating crisis) are somewhat beneficial for their currencies. Japan is set to have a new central bank governor, Kazuo Ueda, who may be slightly more hawkish than his ultra-dovish long-serving predecessor Haruhiko Kuroda. On the other hand, Jerome Powell’s likely higher-for-longer approach after seeing a resurgence in financial conditions and inflation and meme stocks and crypto is likely to be somewhat dollar bullish as he tries to smash some of that back down.

Michael Howell of Crossborder Capital (a specialist in global liquidity) has [articulated](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=58tBuswnOs4) a positive view on global liquidity going forward. Here’s one of his charts, presented by Jack Farley:

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The U.S. business cycle continues to point weak and downward, with recession risk on the horizon for 2023/2024 but with nothing snapping yet.

Large cash balances, large ongoing fiscal deficits, labor shortages, the downturn in energy prices, and the recent uptick in global liquidity, are all helping to keep the economy reasonably afloat. In contrast, weakness in housing, tech, manufacturing, and other industries sensitive to the sharp rise in interest rates are all contributing to keep things rather weak.

Leading economic indicators, such as by the [Conference Board](https://www.conference-board.org/topics/us-leading-indicators) and others, are pointing negative. However, some of them had a bit of a bounce in recent months (at still negative levels) in this “less bad” liquidity environment:

![Leading Indicators](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-leading-indicators.png)

This minor rebound could be somewhat neutral-to-positive into the middle of 2023. The Treasury is likely to offset the Fed’s liquidity drain up through that point, unless the debt ceiling impasse is resolved early, which seems unlikely. Meanwhile, with China re-opening and other emerging markets doing okay, it can be beneficial for the U.S. economy via an uptick in global trade.

Once the debt ceiling impasse is resolved one way or another in around Q3 2023, and the Treasury refills its cash account with more debt issuance, it would cease offsetting Fed balance sheet reductions. Meanwhile, with Jerome Powell looking to quell inflation at all costs for the sake of his legacy, using 1970s-style policy to deal with 1940s-style fiscal-driven inflation, we should assume until further notice that most rebounds are to be met by monetary hammers. I think some of those hammers will ironically contribute to long-term inflation, but they are impactful against short-term inflation, and he’s most likely ready to use them.

Since liquidity conditions can influence economic performance, especially globally, I’m hesitant to make predictions about the second half of 2023 or into 2024 until we see more clarity about the U.S. federal debt ceiling impasse and the interplay between the Fed and the Treasury from that point onward. That’s more of a tactical question that we’ll get to in time.

In multiple articles, newsletters, and reports, I suggested fading any parabolic commodity spikes, whether it’s American lumber prices or European gas prices or things like that, while keeping an eye towards structural commodity price inflation in the 2020s decade. **Charlie Munger on Banning Cryptocurrency**

Charlie Munger, one of the smartest investment polymaths in modern history, has a famous quote that says “I never allow myself to hold an opinion on anything that I don’t know the other side’s argument better than they do.” I wholeheartedly agree with that approach and is how I try to frame my thinking on any given topic.

And yet, Munger (aged 99) has advocated the outright banning of bitcoin and cryptocurrency in the United States (despite it merely being information; the memorization of numbers and the use of open-source code to exchange those numbers) and praised the Communist Party of China for banning it (which was somewhat ineffective due to that aforementioned openness; ~20% of bitcoin mining still occurs in China and millions of bitcoin users exist in China despite a crackdown by one of the most authoritarian governments in the world) and lamented that the United States did not follow China’s direction on that matter.

> What should the U.S. do after a ban of cryptocurrencies is in place? Well, one more action might make sense: Thank the Chinese communist leader for his splendid example of uncommon sense.
>
> –[Charlie Munger, February 2023, WSJ](https://www.wsj.com/articles/why-america-should-ban-crypto-regulation-economy-finance-china-england-trading-currency-securities-commodity-gamble-11675287477)

Ironically, bitcoin and cryptocurrencies allow capital to flow out of China (where it generally prefers to escape from due to authoritarian rule) towards freer markets that it desires to be in (the United States, Canada, Australia, southeastern Asian countries, and so forth), and yet Munger praises the reasoning of Chinese authoritarians’ banning of the technology, despite its limited effect. The kneejerk idea to ban a whole class of new technology has historically not been wise.

Recently on CNBC, the anchor reminded Munger of that quote about knowing the other side’s argument better than they do before having an opinion, and asked him to present it for cryptocurrency. His response was to fail to do so, and instead say there are no good arguments against his position, say the people who oppose his position are idiots, and display no ability to understand any aspect of the technology.

As a long-term fan of Charlie Munger from an investor standpoint, I find his lack of research and reasoning to be disappointing here.

Is Munger able to discuss the implications of bitcoin’s utility to move capital around globally by remembering twelve words? What are his views on Segwit and Taproot soft-fork updates to Bitcoin? Has he heard of them? Does he have an opinion on appropriate block size size and the associated details around decentralization, or whether the base layer should be Turing-complete? Can he articulate key technical differences between BTC, ETH, SOL, AVAX, DOGE, and others? Probably not. Does he know that bitcoin miners and their curtailment agreements are competing with his company’s proposed natural gas peaking plants in Texas as a solution for grid instability, and could that be influencing his opinion? It’s not clear.

If the United States were to try to ban bitcoin, can he articulate how such a ruling would relate to the precedents on the 1st Amendment regarding code being speech that came up in the 1990s with the widespread use of encryption, and/or could he provide details on the methods in which such enforcement would be done, since the bitcoin client software can be run on devices as simple as aged laptops or Raspberry Pi single-circuit computers? Most likely no.

Is he familiar with the usage of bitcoin, stablecoins, and related assets by people in war-torn, authoritarian, and/or persistently inflationary developing countries to preserve and/or move some of their purchasing power? It’s hard to say, but he hasn’t commented on it. Is Munger aware that there is still considerable cryptocurrency usage and even mining in China after the ban, and that due to its nature, decentralized technology is not able to be eliminated as easily as Twitter or Google can be from within their borders by the stroke of a pen? It seems as though he may not be.

Munger has an inability to articulate his opponents’ arguments on this matter, nor can he even list the variables that his opponents’ arguments draw from. He is multiple layers away from being able to present a set of coherent arguments for or against the open-source technology that he recommends banning wholesale, and yet has praised the leader of China’s communist party for attempting-but-somewhat-failing to ban it in his own authoritarian country.

Munger is, however, quite right about how *most* of the cryptocurrency industry operates, and is why I separate the concept of bitcoin from the concept of the broad cryptocurrency industry in general. Munger generally lumps bitcoin in with every other cryptocurrency despite fundamentally different origins and details. In his WSJ op-ed, Munger wrote:

> In the U.S. in recent years, privately owned companies have issued thousands of new cryptocurrencies, large and small. These have later become publicly traded without any governmental pre-approval of disclosures.
>
> In some cases, a big block of cryptocurrency has been sold to a promoter for almost nothing, after which the public buys in at much higher prices without fully understanding the pre-dilution in favor of the promoter.
>
> –[Charlie Munger, February 2023, WSJ](https://www.wsj.com/articles/why-america-should-ban-crypto-regulation-economy-finance-china-england-trading-currency-securities-commodity-gamble-11675287477)

I’ve covered this problem of grift and bad incentives in a number of articles:

* [Digital Alchemy](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/)
* [The Problems with DeFi and Crypto](https://www.lynalden.com/defi-problems/)
* [The World’s Money Problem](https://www.lynalden.com/december-2022-newsletter/)

Munger is correct that the majority of the cryptocurrency industry is grift and makes use of regulatory arbitrage to sell unregistered securities to the retail public, and thus get fast exit liquidity for early developers, investors, and promoters whether or not they actually build a sustainable project. I expect existing securities laws to be increasingly asserted on most of them by several jurisdictions. And I don’t think Munger is wrong to want uniform securities laws enforced on market participants.

Where Munger goes wrong specifically, in my view, is the inability to separate some of this technology from the others, and his view towards banning the technology wholesale while being unable to articulate any part of the other side of the argument.

**SEC Crackdown**

The US Securities and Exchange Commission has cracked down on cryptocurrency firms recently.

On February 9th, it was [announced](https://www.sec.gov/news/press-release/2023-25) that the crypto exchange Kraken would discontinue allowing crypto staking (as unregistered securities) and pay a $30 million fine. This puts pressure on other exchanges that allow crypto staking as well. This affects usage of networks like Ethereum and other proof-of-stake coins.

Compared to other crypto exchanges, I don’t view Kraken or its chairman Jesse Powell as bad actors. I find it odd that the SEC went after them specifically, although it’s unsurprising given the SEC’s recent inability to spot actual crypto frauds ahead of time while cracking down on normal run-of-the-mill crypto operators.

That being said, I do think centralized retail products around crypto staking are securities, and so it’s unsurprising that this would be a thread that they would pursue. That much is pretty clear within historical law. I’ve been rather critical of proof-of-stake cryptocurrencies for multiple reasons, most of which being technical, but also including the regulatory uncertainties.

In addition, the SEC [proposed rule changes](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/02/15/sec-chair-gensler-crypto-firms-need-to-register-to-custody-assets.html) on cryptocurrency firm custody arrangements. SEC commissioner Hester Peirce was the dissenting vote out of five, and wrote the [dissenting argument](https://www.sec.gov/news/statement/peirce-statement-custody-021523).

Analyst and venture capitalist Nic Carter wrote a [detailed piece](https://www.piratewires.com/p/crypto-choke-point) regarding the current U.S. administrator’s crackdown on cryptocurrency firms’ access to banking services. While I don’t agree with all of the framing, it’s a great summary of the actions that were taken along with general context and precedent, and I recommend reading it.

A semi-rebuttal or clarification to Carter’s piece would be to separate the concepts of bitcoin and cryptocurrencies. The SEC Chairman Gary Gensler has pointed out that bitcoin does not pass the Howey Test and is not a security (it has no issuer and never raised capital), and has referred to it as a real innovation, but has said that most other cryptocurrencies do pass the Howey Test and are securities. Most cryptocurrencies are unregistered with the SEC, and thus most cryptocurrency-related businesses are selling what appear to be unregistered securities. It’s not shocking that companies that sell unregistered securities may have trouble getting access to highly-regulated bank accounts. One may disagree with it, but that’s their view.

In my observation in the industry, bitcoin-only businesses have had less trouble with banking access in the United States. That may change in the future, and indeed if fiat currency problems become bad enough later in this decade, I expect to see the severing of banking rails from bitcoin/crypto rails in many jurisdictions, as lawmakers try to block some of the currency offramps. However, this specific crackdown has been rather different from that scenario so far.

## Travel and Dining Deep Dive Analysis

I am concerned about a U.S. recession and global stagnation, but nonetheless there are certain industries such as energy pipelines, energy producers, industrials, healthcare, banks, tobacco/alcohol, and select travel/dining companies that look reasonably attractive for the long run.

I’ve analyzed banks, healthcare providers, and pipelines quite a bit recently, so it’s time to provide an updated view on travel and dining companies specifically.

**Travel/Dining Updates**

I was actually pretty bullish on travel companies and restaurants in late 2019, and wrote about them. Some of them were significantly off their highs or had chopped along sideways for a couple years due to 2018/2019 recession concerns, and from that point they began to interest me.

This outlook was messed up by COVID-19, especially once it clearly went global by late February and into early March 2020. As everything crashed in early March 2020, I did an emergency assessment on each type of travel company to see which ones were deep bargains and which ones were structurally impaired.

As a result of that analysis, I cut my losses on cruise companies; they seemed structurally impaired to due to high capital costs and being the worst possible business for a pandemic. Indeed, cruise stocks like Carnival (CCL) are lower today than they were in early March 2020, so cutting it away ended up being the right decision.

![CCL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-ccl-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

On the other hand, I added to my restaurant position amid the crash, bought a Mexican airport stock, and bought American Express (AXP), all at blood-in-the-streets levels of value. In my view at the time, which has thus far played out pretty well, these companies were deep bargains in the sense that they were not structurally impaired and would bounce back quickly. They’ve indeed done rather well since March 2020. There were some other cyclical industries like steelmakers that I jumped into as well, which also bounced back. Some of these companies I trimmed or sold after significant rebounds, and others I kept holding.

Here in 2023, we can run through some of these, to see what’s interesting at the current time.

I expected many of them to grind sideways-to-up while paying dividends over the next several years, and with bouts of volatility.

**Cheesecake Factory**

Cheesecake Factory (CAKE) is a restaurant corporation with a diversified set of brands.

Their cash cow business is their namesake Cheesecake Factory chain, which consists of large upper-casual establishments that serve an unusually-large menu and then specialize in high-margin dessert. They have over 200 domestic U.S. Cheesecake Factory locations (each of which being quite large relative to other restaurant chain locations) along with a few dozen international locations. They tend to be open late, and in my experience are often packed. During the pandemic lockdowns, they structurally improved their ability to deliver or provide pick-up orders.

Their moderate growth business is their North Italia chain, which are Italian concepts with an extensive high-margin wine selection. This is an established but smaller retail chain that they are heavily investing in and growing more quickly.

Then, the company owns the incubator Fox Restaurant Concepts, which develops and owns a large number of different restaurants. Concepts that start to do particularly well can be given capital to expand nationally and globally. The company acquired Fox Restaurant Concepts in 2019 for a significant premium, which in hindsight was bad timing given the upcoming pandemic that they couldn’t know about ahead of time. Nonetheless, Fox gives the company a good foundation for growth well into the future with no inherent need for future acquisitions.

I was bullish on CAKE in late 2019 after a considerable consolidation, but then the pandemic absolutely slammed them. While I sold my cruise exposure due to not expecting recovery any time soon, I doubled-down on CAKE amid the crisis and expected a rebound, and wrote about it at the time. At this point, I’m interested in buying again.

And as much as I would like to take credit for trading it well with bottom-buys and top-sells, a lot of it was simply luck. There are other stocks that I didn’t trade as well, and the market just keeps providing irrational pricing on this one relative to fundamentals. By digging into the fundamentals and going to some of the locations occasionally when I travel to see what they’re like, I keep fading extremes of market bearishness or bullishness on it. I’ll take what I can get.

Currently, CAKE is trading at a 14x multiple of 2023 estimated earnings by consensus analysts, a 11.5x multiple of 2024 estimated earnings, and a 8.5x multiple of 2025 estimated earnings (which of course could be very wrong).

Meanwhile, U.S. restaurant revenues have more than recovered since the pandemic in terms of revenue, with a lot more broad money in the system. They do, however, have much higher labor costs now, which constrains their profit potential. I’m moderately bullish with a five-year view as a small part of a diversified portfolio.

## Final Thoughts: Strategic vs Tactical

This decade continues to set up a good case for commodity exposure, select emerging markets exposure, bank stocks, industrial stocks, energy stocks, and other select industries, along with gold and bitcoin and cash equivalents. Select growth stocks may also do well, but the ones that do are unlikely to be the biggest winners of the 2010s decade.

The market sometimes gets way ahead of itself in terms of bullishness or bearishness, and so I generally want to fade the extremes for any of those. This year 2023 presents the possibility of major turbulence or simply just sideways choppiness, as we navigate ongoing tightening attempts by central banks, major structural fiscal deficits by governments, a U.S. debt ceiling situation which can greatly affect liquidity in the middle of this year, and a variety of forward-looking indicators that continue to point towards eventual recession.

We’re currently in a disinflationary cyclical period within what I view as likely being a longer-term structural inflationary period.

One of the biggest sources of alpha, in my view, is being well-studied on the 1940s fiscal and monetary period. I’ve highlighted the 1940s analogue several times over the past three years. Whenever things end up being unusual or “unprecedented” and catch analysts off guard (who are generally only looking back a few decades in the data) here in the 2020s, it’s often well in line with things that happened in the 1940s.

That’s not to say that the 1940s is a perfect analogue. Many things are different now. Technology is completely different, demographics are completely different, fiscal spending has been mostly pandemic-related rather than war-related, and the United States is basically in the role that the United Kingdom was in back then (the established hegemon, issuer of the reserve currency, and running a structural trade deficit).

However, if someone is unfamiliar with the 1940s, and the difference between fiscal-driven inflation and lending-driven inflation more broadly, then they’re going to be repeatedly caught off guard by things. In short, studying the 1940s won’t provide a perfect guide for the 2020s, but it’s basically a starter-kit for being less surprised by things this decade.

And here’s my century chart, which I finally updated from my original May 2021 version. It kind of summarizes the situation for people that don’t have a lot of reading time:

![Century Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-century-chart-annotated.png)

Each of these periods directly contributed to what happened in the subsequent period:

* 1920s and 2000s = booming private credit growth
* 1929 and 2008 = generational financial crises
* 1930s and 2010s = economic stagnation and rising populism
* 1940s and 2020s = deficit-driven inflation and financial repression

A key thing that’s different in the 2020s from the 1940s so far is that the Federal Reserve is (somewhat) more independent now, and is raising rates in the face of fiscal-driven inflation and high public debt-to-GDP ratios. Powell has cited the 1970s and Paul Volcker in terms of quelling inflation, which is interesting considering that the causes of inflation were quite different at that time, and the fiscal situation of the federal government was quite different as well.

Back in the 1940s, the Fed held rates at near-zero regardless of record high inflation, and therefore inflated a lot of the debt away. Interest rates were below the inflation level for a decade, and nominal GDP (fueled significantly by inflation) went up a lot faster than public debt levels, resulting in debt-to-GDP going down. One of the new developments this decade is trying to raise rates to 5% and keep them there for a while in the face of 120% public debt-to-GDP levels.

![CPI vs Interest Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/february-2023-newsletter-cpi-vs-rates.png)

Even after all of this current tightening, however, interest rates have lagged well behind the [Taylor Rule](https://fredblog.stlouisfed.org/2014/04/the-taylor-rule/) (a formula to guide policymakers on where interest rates should be given a few factors), and are below the trailing inflation rate. My base case is that interest rates will spend the majority of their time this decade below the prevailing inflation rate, and below the Taylor Rule, but clearly we’ll be taking a winding path along the way.

![Taylor Rule](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-taylor-rule.png)

This will be an interesting period to navigate, and one that is likely to surprise a lot of people whose inflation worldview is primarily based on the 1970s.
