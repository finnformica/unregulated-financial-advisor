**Chinese Credit Problems**

Some of the biggest headlines in recent weeks have been related to Evergrande, the major Chinese real estate developer. The company is preparing to default on its massive debts. The company has in the ballpark of $300 billion worth of liabilities, including billions in USD-denominated bonds.

As I’ve covered a few times, China’s biggest area of concern is real estate debt in its corporate sector. The sovereign level is relatively unleveraged compared to global peers and has trillions in foreign-exchange reserves, but the real estate sector has been a well-known debt bubble for years. This table of debt-to-GDP ratios is from my 2021 International Report:

![Global Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-global-debt.png)

Chinese officials appear to be letting this play out. Overleveraged companies should fail. That’s why they shouldn’t get overleveraged in the first place. To bail them out would be to promote even more reckless leverage going forward.

However, it’ll be interesting to see where Chinese officials might draw the line in terms of halting contagion and backstopping some counterparties with fiscal support.

This large of a default could bring down weak banks, and there are in the ballpark of 1.5 million Chinese consumers who pre-paid for real estate to build, which are liabilities of Evergrande. Will they be made whole via fiscal support, if Evergrande is unable to meet those liabilities upon restructuring? Plus, suppliers to Evergrande are at risk, consumers holding specialized wealth management products are at risk, etc.

This has sparked some consumer protests in China, from potentially-affected people. Basically, there are certain pain points that Chinese officials may eventually be forced to respond to in order to minimize the probability of widespread unrest, even as they don’t bail out Evergrande itself.

Broadly, this is likely to affect many S&P 500 companies that sell products to Chinese consumers, and has pulled down the prices of iron ore and many other industrial commodities, as the market rationally assumes that China’s real estate cycle will be weak for quite a while as this deleveraging plays out.

**A Theoretical October Treasury Default**

Since the debt ceiling hasn’t been raised yet, the US Treasury can’t issue any more net debt. So, they are drawing down their cash reserves and juggling their books to fund the government’s operations until they get authorization to issue more net Treasuries.

The Treasury Department aimed to maintain $750 billion in cash by the end of this quarter (which ends at the end of September), but they are instead down to $230 billion due to the debt limit:

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-tga.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Treasury Secretary Yellen is on record saying that their cash would likely run out in October without an increase in the debt ceiling, which would theoretically lead to a default on US Treasuries not long thereafter. In previous debt ceiling brinksmanship moments, the debt ceiling was always raised, allowing the Treasury to issue net debt and avoid default. That’s the likely outcome this time as well, but if politicians go further this time and run out of cash without authorization to issue more net debt, the Treasury would default on some of their existing debt, as incoming tax revenue does not fully cover outgoing expenses and they need to continually issue more debt to cover prior debts and outgoing expenses.

The Federal Reserve formed an emergency playbook for this Treasury default scenario back in 2013, which is probably the playbook they would use here if needed. In the playbook, the Fed would ease regulations for banks, allowing the banks to basically pretend that any defaulted Treasuries they own were not defaulted on, as it relates to their capital ratios and other requirements, until the situation is resolved. If needed, a more controversial part of the playbook is that the Fed could also buy defaulted Treasuries and sell non-defaulted Treasuries, to bring the problem onto its own balance sheet and off of purely-private balance sheets.

From the Treasury’s perspective, when choosing which bonds to default on, the Treasury could also aim most of their defaults on the specific bonds that the Fed is holding. And as a related note, the current Treasury Secretary, Janet Yellen, was vice-Chair of the Fed when that 2013 Fed playbook for the Treasury default scenario was thought up.

Once the debt ceiling is raised one way or another, the Treasury will want to fill back up its cash reserves, to around $750-$800 billion based on their previous statements, and that could put some upward pressure on long-duration Treasury yields, as a lot of new supply comes to market. However, first things first, let’s get past the debt ceiling itself and see if the Treasury revises any of its plans for what level of cash reserves it will target in the quarters ahead.

## PulteGroup Deep Dive Analysis

Homebuilder companies are at an interesting point right now. The industry is booming and yet they’re extremely cheap. However, it’s probably “as good as it gets” right now in rate-of-change terms, so the housing market is expecting a slowdown, and figuring out how to price that in.

Due to this, the homebuilder stocks have generally been correcting/consolidating since at least May 2021, and some of them have been correcting/consolidating since January 2021, despite strengthening fundamentals through Q2 2021. It’s the next few quarters that the market anticipates a slowdown in rate-of-change terms, based on softening housing data.

My thesis, with moderate conviction, is that the market is overstating how much the housing industry will slow down in terms of new constructions. In essence, the market seems to think this is end-of-cycle for homebuilders, whereas I think it is mid-cycle. If this ends up being the case, the homebuilders are significantly undervalued. For investors willing to put up with a lot of volatility, I think this is an interesting industry to look into.

Historically, homebuilders are not particularly high-quality companies. They focus on generating a lot of one-time sales rather than recurring revenue. They compete with each other for market share based mainly on price, and so they have little or no “economic moat”. The market is extremely fractured, with even the largest players maintaining a relatively small share of the total homebuilding pie.

The investment case for them right now is that they are basically B- companies trading at D+ prices, and thus they offer compelling bargains for long-term investors. I like them as a small position within a diversified portfolio at the current price points, especially PulteGroup (PHM) and D.R. Horton (DHI).

**Housing Cycle Analysis**

The most important figure for the housing cycle is the number of monthly housing starts. Here’s the data over six decades:

![Housing Starts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-housing-starts.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

In each historical business cycle, the seasonally-adjusted annual rate of housing starts tended to fluctuate between 800,000 on the low end at the bottom of the cycle, and 2+ million at the high end at the top of the cycle.

The recession after the 2000 dotcom bubble was different; housing starts barely fell at all, and the cycle continued in what would become by far its longest stretch ever. It was a double-cycle that lasted over 15 years, in other words. And specifically in the 2003-2007 period towards the end of that cycle, the US housing market became a bubble, with banks and consumers over-leveraging themselves on rapidly-inflating housing prices. This ended spectacularly with the well-known subprime mortgage crisis, which cascaded into a global financial crisis.

Due to that period of overbuilding followed by a credit bubble collapse, the US housing market encountered its weakest-ever period on a nationwide basis. Annualized housing starts fell to a lower level than any previous post-war credit cycle, at below 500,000 for a period of time in 2009. It wasn’t until 2012 that nationwide average home prices bottomed, and the market began truly recovering slowly and steadily.

Nearly a decade later in 2021, we’re still at what is historically a medium level of annualized housing starts.

However, population isn’t growing as fast as it used to either. So, another thing we can do is compare annualized housing starts to the year-over-year change in the number of households. The number of households has some data artifacts in it, but otherwise shows a good representation. In this chart, the blue line is the annualized rate of new home construction, while the red line is the year-over-year change in the number of households estimated to exist. The balance looks reasonable overall:

**Credit Cycle Analysis**

Most analysts like to measure bank credit safety in terms of their capital ratios. That’s the traditional way to measure how well-protected a bank is from loan losses. However, bank capital ratios in aggregate always look fine. Aggregate capital ratios were fine before the 2008 financial crisis, and they were fine before the 1929 financial crisis, for example. That’s why regulators and banks were blindsided by them.

Historically, a better predictor of financial crises is the percentage of aggregate bank assets that consist of risk-free assets (cash reserves and Treasuries). Prior to major financial crises in the US, banks had a much smaller percentage of their assets in these risk-free assets, and a higher-than-normal amount of their assets in loans and securities that are subject to possible loan losses. They were quite aggressively-positioned, in other words.

Even a seemingly adequate capital ratio can quickly become inadequate in practice, if a bank suffers major loan losses from a very aggressive asset book.

During the top of the housing bubble in 2006, US banks in aggregate had less than 17% of their assets held in cash and Treasuries. The other 83%+ was in loans and securities that could potentially be defaulted on, and if 10% of those credit-risk assets get defaulted on, it can blow through an otherwise-decent 8.3% ratio of bank capital to bank assets.

In contrast, US banks in aggregate currently have more than 41% of their assets held in cash and Treasuries. Less than 59% is in loans and securities that can potentially be defaulted on.

This chart shows cash as a percentage of aggregate US large bank assets in blue, and Treasuries as a percentage of aggregate US large bank assets in red:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-bank-cash.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Many investors are fighting the last war, assuming that this housing cycle is likely to end as the previous one did, with an overleveraged crash. However, banks have much less credit risk, and are maintaining rather conservative lending standards, compared to the run-up to the 2008 crisis. It’s as different as night and day.

Instead, banks currently look more like they did in the 1940s, where they were stuffed full of Treasuries as a matter of policy.

I covered that topic quite a bit in my [May 2021 public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/). Although their are some differences in other areas, debt levels and overall fiscal/monetary policy in the 2020s look a lot like the 1940s, after the 2010s also looked a lot like the 1930s:

![Long-Term Debt Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-longterm-debt-cycle.png)

Due to high debt levels, even if inflation runs hot for periods of time, the Fed is likely to be slow to raise short-term interest rates, much like the 1940s and unlike the 1970s. In these charts, I use 3-month T-bill rates as a proxy for the Fed’s short-term interest rate, since it’s basically the same but has a longer available dataset:

![Policy Response](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-policy-response.png)*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The housing boom could slow down. Housing prices could slump. But even when this happens, mortgage banks and homebuilders are unlikely to get slammed here, even though homebuilders are priced as though they will. We have a lot of problems in the global economy and financial system, but the specific subprime housing-related problems of 2008 are not among them. Banks have plenty of defense and are not over-leveraged. Quite the contrary; they have been very reluctant to lend for a long time.

And speaking of bank lending, loan officer surveys for both commercial/industrial loans as well as consumer loans, appear to be in the early stages of a bank lending cycle:

![Bank Lending Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-lending-cycle.png)

Basically, although the stock market has late-cycle valuations as a whole (especially for growth stocks in general), there are areas of economic data, and certain value-oriented stock industries, that look to be early-cycle or mid-cycle. This includes the housing market.

**Homebuilder Comparisons and Valuations**

* PHM: BBB- (Investment grade)
* TOL: BB+ (Non-investment grade)

In many ways, PulteGroup is the most attractively-valued and has the strongest balance sheet of this group. Its credit rating is not the highest because it’s not as big or fast-growing as D.R. Horton, but quantitatively, its balance sheet is as strong as it gets for the industry.

This chart shows the shares outstanding of each company:

![Homebuilder Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-homebuilder-shares.png)

We can see that PulteGroup has been very aggressive with its share buybacks, and that boosts per-share metrics by quite a bit when the valuation is as low as it is. D.R. Horton on has been focused on growth, whereas PulteGroup has been focused on cost-control and shareholder returns. I like both of their strategies here.

In some industries, excessive buybacks would be a bad thing. For example, certain tech companies like IBM (IBM) over-relied on buybacks and fell behind in terms of innovation. However, in a cheap non-tech industry, for a company that has almost as much cash as debt, share buybacks are extremely lucrative. From 2015 to 2021, each share of PulteGroup increased its ownership stake of the company by over 40%, while the company simultaneously strengthened its balance sheet considerably. It’s like the existing owners keep buying out other owners, so the remaining ones each own a larger and larger share of the company, which boosts earnings per share, book value per share, dividends per share, and related metrics.

However, I would ideally like to see an annual special dividend in addition to their regular dividend, in lieu of some of the buybacks. Each of these four homebuilders pays a dividend yield of around 1%, with PulteGroup’s being the highest. But I’d like to see some year-end special dividends as well, instead of buybacks for that quarter.

Here’s PulteGroup’s F.A.S.T. Graph. Analysts’ consensus expectations are for  21% earnings growth in 2022 and 13% earnings growth in 2023. That’s for a stock with a P/E ratio of just over 7x. The market is skeptical of this industry’s ability to match analyst expectations and worries that it is top-of-cycle, which is understandable.

![PHM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-phm-fastgraph.png)

If analysts are correct about PulteGroup’s 2023 earnings (and they may very well not be), and if PulteGroup can go from a price/earnings ratio of 7x back to its historical average price/earnings ratio of 10x (and it might very well not do so), then it would be a $100 stock in late 2023, which is just over double from the current level in a little over two years. I don’t think it would be unreasonable for that to happen, if we are indeed not end-of-cycle for the housing market.

However, we don’t need even that fairly low bar bullish outcome. We can lower the bar a bit more. With a 7x P/E ratio, pretty much all we need from PulteGroup over the next few years is it not to sharply drop its company-wide net income (which, to be clear, is indeed something that periodically happens to homebuilders). It has a 14% earnings yield, which at the moment mainly goes to buybacks, boosting earnings per share considerably even if company-wide income stays flat. But let’s say it undershoots analysts expectations, and only produces $9 in EPS in 2023, and only has an 8x price/earnings ratio by that point. It would be a $72 stock, or 50% above the current level in a little over two years.

PulteGroup has a 20,000+ home backlog, which is great. New orders slowed down over the past quarter compared to the peak in Q1 2021, but remain very strong in absolute terms:

![PHM Backlog](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-phm-backlog.png)

*Chart Source: [PulteGroup Q2 2021 Presentation](https://www.pultegroupinc.com/investor-relations/events-and-presentations/default.aspx)*

PulteGroup has increased its free cash flow generation potential by shifting its emphasis from owning lots to optioning lots. This has improved their ongoing returns on capital, which in my view seems under-appreciated by the market:

![PHM Lots Under Control](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-phm-lots.png)

*Chart Source: PulteGroup Q2 2021 Presentation*

Overall, the combination of low valuation, strong balance sheet, and improved cost control bode well for what is otherwise a very cyclical and somewhat middling-quality business. Adding macro factors such as interest rates that might not catch up with inflation for a while, and low housing inventories, could keep this home-building cycle going on longer than bears expect. In other words, I think we’re in a “double cycle”, similar to the 1990s and 2000s.

**Risks**

Normally, we should expect low/moderate-quality businesses like homebuilders to underperform the S&P over the long run. However, PulteGroup has actually outperformed the S&P 500 on a total return basis over the past 3-year, 5-year, 10-year, 20-year periods, and over a 32-year period as far back as YCharts data go to 1989.

It underperformed the S&P 500 over the past 1-year period, and underperformed the S&P 500 during a 14-18 year stretch if you specifically bought it during the 2003-2007 housing bubble. Other than that, PulteGroup has been a persistent winner.

And yet, PulteGroup has had three different drawdowns of over 80% from its highs. Once was in the 1970s, one was in the 1980s, and one was after the subprime mortgage crisis.

So, when analyzing risks, we have to be aware of its boom/bust nature, and its ability to dramatically outperform or underperform for long stretches of time depending on buying conditions. Investors should consider this type of company to be quite risky, and to expect considerable volatility with it.

*Housing Crash*

If the US homebuilding cycle is actually late-cycle instead of my base case of mid-cycle, then homebuilders will have major problems ahead.

PulteGroup and other homebuilders are already priced for a deceleration in housing growth. However, if we experience more than a slowdown, and homebuilding activities get cut in half from current levels, these homebuilders’ earnings will be badly affected.

It shouldn’t be as bad as the end of the 2003-2007 cycle, but they could very well become unprofitable for a couple years. Their strong balance sheets, especially for D.R. Horton and PulteGroup, should get them through it. PulteGroup’s stock price is already more than 20% down from its recent all-time highs. But overall, this would not have been a great buying moment if we look back a few years from now after a housing crash.

The way to manage this risk is to maintain an appropriate position size for its level of risk and volatility.

*Material Shortages*

House prices have gone up sharply in part because the prices of supplies and labor needed to build them have gone up. Homebuilders have been able to pass those costs onto consumers, and for example lumber prices came back down after their huge supply squeeze.

However, some homes can be delayed due to outright shortages, capping the number of homes that can be built. This is from a [September 8th press release](https://www.pultegroupinc.com/investor-relations/press-releases/press-release-details/2021/PulteGroup-Updates-Select-Guidance-for-Q3-and-Full-Year-2021/default.aspx):

> PulteGroup, Inc. (NYSE: PHM) announced today updated closing guidance for its third quarter and full year 2021. PulteGroup currently expects third quarter closings to increase approximately 8% over last year to 7,000 homes. For the full year, the Company expects closings to increase approximately 19% to 21% to between 29,250 and 29,750 homes.
>
> “Despite the extraordinary efforts of our trade partners, the supply chain issues that have plagued the industry throughout the pandemic have increased during the second half of the year,” said Ryan Marshall, President and Chief Executive Officer of PulteGroup. “We continue to work closely with our suppliers, but shortages for a variety of building products, combined with increased production volumes across the homebuilding industry, are directly impacting our ability to get homes closed to our level of quality over the remainder of 2021. In light of these challenges, we are providing routine updates on build schedules to our backlog of homebuyers, who remain committed to close on their new homes.”
>
> Based on the reduced closing volume and changes in the geographic and buyer mix of homes anticipated to close in the period, the Company now expects third quarter gross margin to be in the range of 26.4% to 26.6%, and SG&A of 9.9% of home sale revenues.
>
> “Industry disruptions have also impacted the timing of community openings such that, even though we continue to limit sales pace in many communities in the face of ongoing buyer demand, our average community count for the third quarter is expected to be down approximately 15% from last year,” said Marshall. “We continue to expect community count at year end to be down 5% to 10% from last year and then gradually expand as 2022 progresses.”

This press release caused the stock price to drop, and caught my attention as a possible value entry, since I don’t currently have a position and the stock is seemingly on sale here. Keep in mind that with a single-digit price/earnings ratio, the stock is already priced for negative growth, assuming top-of-cycle, so positive-but-decelerating growth in a mid-cycle scenario still makes sense fundamentally for a bull case.

If these shortages get bad enough, however, it could have a worsening impact on PulteGroup’s margins and growth rate enough to warrant concern even at these rock-bottom valuation levels.

## Final Thoughts

For several months now there has been weak equity breadth under the surface. Major stock indices like the S&P 500 have been held up by the mega-caps, as most other stocks including small caps, value stocks, and foreign stocks, corrected downward.

That’s a particularly challenging environment for most active investors, including me, because almost anything other than holding the S&P 500 has been weak lately. It has been frustrating for bond bulls as well, with bond yields edging up (prices down) despite decelerating economic data. Gold has been weak. Industrial commodities outside of energy have been weak. Bitcoin has been rangebound. Not a ton of happy faces out there across asset classes recently, outside of the uranium and oil & gas industries.

In the past two weeks, however, this risk-off period has pulled down the broad indices as well. So far, the S&P 500 has repeated its 2021 pattern of moving down to touch the 50-day moving average:

![S&P 500 Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-spx-chart.png)

We’ll see if it bounces again or firmly breaks through to the downside this time.

The case for it to break to the downside is that the combination of decelerating economic growth, waning fiscal stimulus, and high equity valuations, is a more potent mix than the prior bounces off of the 50-day moving average. Sticky inflation combined with a rising 10-year Treasury yield could put some pressure on growth stock valuations. Investors are disillusioned about the prospects for more fiscal stimulus, and worried about China’s downcycle spreading to the rest of the world, which is a very reasonable set of concerns.

The case for it to bounce again is that this recent slump has been during peak delta-variant, which is now improving. There is widespread negative sentiment around China, and basically everyone is concerned about a major correction across global risk assets, right as virus conditions have peaked. For example, the AAII sentiment survey now only has 22% bulls, down from 43% at the start of September. This 22% level is nearly as low as the weakest sentiment point in 2020, and is typically a contrarian indicator (meaning stocks tend to bounce when sentiment is that low).

![AAII Bulls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-9-19-aaii-bulls.png)

*Chart Source: @Ten\_Bagger*

Other than watching price action around this level to get a signal from the market, I don’t have a high conviction view of the next move in the S&P 500. That’s generally not the time frame I tailor my research to.

Instead, my focus is on making sure that the majority of my investments are fundamentally sound and reasonably-valued relative to those fundamentals, so that as we move through various gyrations, even as prices move up and down, the underlying investments continue to make sense.
