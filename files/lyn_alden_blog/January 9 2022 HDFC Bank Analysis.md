**The US Business Cycle**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 and Q4 have shown decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

Updated weekly based on incoming data, the Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow model currently has an estimated 6.7% annualized GDP growth rate for Q4 for 2021, which was recently completed but not yet reported on by official sources.

Overall, Q3 2021 was stagflationary in rate-of-change terms, meaning that economic growth decelerated while inflation accelerated. Q4 2021 had stronger growth and inflation compared to Q3.

Payroll numbers for December came out on Friday. Consensus economist estimates were for 400k net new jobs but the number only came in at about 200k. However, the unemployment rate went down to 3.9%, which was lower than the consensus 4.1% that economists were expecting. So fewer jobs were created than expected, and yet the unemployment rate also fell more than expected.

This is because a lot of people have left the workforce, and so this combination of low payroll numbers and yet also low unemployment continues to be a sign of labor shortages rather than a weak economy.

My base case is for headline year-over-year consumer price inflation to reach a local peak in Q1 2022, although there are some variables like energy shortages that could extend that timeframe.

**The Copper-to-Gold Ratio**

Most central banks (other than China) are tightening monetary policy at the same time as fiscal authorities have been drawing back from fiscal stimulus. This has implications for a variety of industries.

In particular, the price ratio between copper and gold has been a popular global macro indicator for a long time.

Copper is an industrial metal, used in construction, infrastructure, and other industries that tend to do well during “risk-on” periods of strengthening global economic growth. Gold is a monetary metal, often turned to during “risk-off” periods of weakening global economic growth.

As such, copper rising against gold has typically been a decent time for stocks and other risk assets, while gold rising against copper has typically been a decent time for cash, bonds, or other risk-off assets.

The copper-to-gold ratio is getting pretty close to a monthly momentum shift, meaning that copper’s positive momentum is giving way in favor of gold:

![Copper Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-copper-gold.png)

The momentum shift isn’t confirmed yet; there could certainly be another leg up in the existing momentum path first. It’s on my close radar now.

When this momentum shift occurs, it doesn’t necessarily mean that copper goes down or gold goes up. It merely means that gold is starting to do equal to or better than copper, after a significant stretch of copper doing better than gold.

I put green squares on the momentum indicator for the above chart around the four most recent instances of this happening.

The first instance within this time period was in late 2006, which coincided with the slowing housing market and subsequently crashed into the global financial crisis of 2008.

The second instance occurred in 2011, which was the first global slowdown of the post-GFC cycle. It eventually led into the European sovereign debt crisis of 2012.

The third instance occurred at the start of 2015. During this time, the Fed’s QE3 program ended, the dollar strengthened radically, several emerging markets went into severe recessions, the price of oil crashed, and US economic growth slowed.

The fourth instance occurred in mid-2018, which was followed by a period of US market turbulence, a US Treasury yield curve inversion, a mild global economic slowdown, and then by early 2020, the pandemic.

I marked those same green squares on a monthly chart of the S&P 500:

![S&P 500 Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-spx-annotated.png)

While a copper-to-gold momentum shift was not a perfect top indicator, it has consistently presaged a more volatile, choppy, or outright negative period in the broad stock market over the following year or two from that point, after a period of considerable upward momentum. The next copper-to-gold momentum shift will probably occur within 2022, as fiscal stimulus weakens and central banks try to dampen inflation.

In this environment of cooling economic growth rates, I like low-volatility sectors such as healthcare. I also like some of the cheap cyclical areas such as US homebuilders and energy producers and transporters, but mainly due to supply shortages rather than massive demand. I think a significant number of stocks are likely to perform decently in 2022 even if the major indices run into more volatility than they experienced in 2021.

As far as equities are concerned, my overall preference is towards high-quality value stocks in this environment, meaning neither the hyper-growth stocks nor the junk-rated value stocks, but mainly the reliable dividend growers with wide economic moats and reasonable valuations.

## Kazakhstan Note

Those who have been following the news know that Kazakhstan is experiencing violent riots over the past week.

The government ceased price controls on certain energy products, resulting in them going up in price very quickly for Kazakh citizens. This proved to be the straw that broke the camel’s back, or the snowflake that triggered the avalanche, or pick your similar metaphor, for public anger towards the government. People took to the streets in violent protest, not only for this particular energy issue, but with pent up frustration regarding years of policies from the Soviet-era autocratic government that tend to result in high wealth concentration for elites and not necessarily much benefit for the broad public.

The government agreed to put the price controls back in place, but that was not enough to stop the riots. The riots have turned rather violent, with people attacking government buildings across multiple cities. The government shut down the internet and took other measures to try to prevent organization of rioters.

Six ex-Soviet countries including Russia and Kazakhstan are part of the Collective Security Treaty Organization (“CSTO”), which is like a mini-NATO in the sense that these countries agree to defend each other if attacked. Russia has a military that is several times larger than the other five members combined, so in practice what this means is that if any of these nations face external or internal threats, Russia has a framework in place to potentially send military aid.

The Russian-led CSTO has deployed in Kazakhstan to defend the country’s government buildings, government power structure, etc. It remains to be seen how the end-state of this will turn out. Rather than an external attack, this is an internal revolt. The government of Kazakhstan is beginning to claim that they’ve regained a significant amount of stability, but from an outsider’s perspective it’s too early to tell.

Obviously this has implications for residents of Kazakhstan, one way or another. Regionally, it has potential implications for other ex-Soviet countries. Whenever there are sharp rises in food and energy prices, public unrest tends to grow, and tends to spread to related countries as sentiment feeds off itself.

Globally, this situation offers new risks for energy markets. Kazakhstan is a meaningful oil producer in what is an already tight global oil market. Kazakhstan also supplies over 40% of the world’s uranium, making it an absolutely critical source for the world’s nuclear power fleet that already has historically low inventory levels by most estimates. To the extent that these industries get disrupted for more than a short period of time, it could contribute to global energy shortages and consumer price inflation.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin remains in a corrective period. It had a small fake breakout in December 2021 with a higher high, but has since continued a corrective pattern, along with many other crypto assets:

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-btcusd.png)

Current price levels are not high enough to convince longer-term holders to sell, and eventually this long-term holder floor is what creates a liquidity squeeze and sets the foundation for the next bull period. During summer and autumn 2021, the percentage of bitcoin supply that had not moved on-chain in over a year stopped falling and held steady at 54%. Since November 2021, however, this began to move up, and it’s now at 59% and rising:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-hodl-wave.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://lookintobitcoin.com/)*

Now that we reached year-end 2021, the bulk of Asian selling seems complete. The major Asian exchanges were in a big drawdown mode because they were closing their access to mainland Chinese users by the end of calendar year 2021. This chart by [Will Clemente](https://twitter.com/WClementeIII) of Blockware Solutions shows the change in bitcoin’s price during Asian trading hours:

![Asia BTC Selling](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-asia-btc-resized.jpg)

For a while, the Asian market was net-selling during market hours while European and North Americans were net-buying during market hours. At least on the Asian side, this trend seems to have run its course.

NYDIG published an excellent report a couple days ago that among other things had an analysis of bitcoin’s historic correlation to equities. Here was their key chart:

![BTC Correlations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-btc-correlations.png)

*Chart Source: [NYDIG](https://nydig.com/research/2021-in-review-and-look-ahead)*

As the chart shows, from 2011 through 2019 bitcoin had minimal correlation to equities. Ever since March 2020, there has been persistent positive correlation. I think this can be attributed to two main causes. One is that massive stimulus increased the correlation of most assets since March 2020. The second is that bitcoin became an institutional asset during this period, and so the investor profile for the asset shifted quite a bit. I think we’ll eventually see correlations head back down but it’s clear that at the moment, there is positive correlation.

Many on-chain indicators look bullish, but several others look neutral, pointing towards further consolidation. The most bullish metric at the moment is probably the dormancy flow. Whenever it has gotten this low in the past (into the green zone), it generally has represented capitulation of the market and ended up being a great buy:

![Dormancy Flow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-dormancy-flow.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

More specifically, bitcoin has generally been a great buy when dormancy flow hits the green low area in that chart, *and then comes out of it*, which we’re not at yet. It can stay in the zone for weeks or months. And there’s always the potential that this time is different and violates the historical pattern.

The intermediate-term bear case for bitcoin is that at the moment it is positively correlated to equities and is treated as a risk-on asset, and given the purchasing manager’s index and copper/gold ratio rolling over, while the Fed tries to tighten monetary policy, that’s not good. As such, demand for bitcoin is weak.

The intermediate-term and long-term bull case for bitcoin is that after nearly a year of rangebound price action, many bitcoins have already moved from liquid weak hands to illiquid strong hands. Supply dynamics are rather tight, meaning there is comparatively little liquid float available. Whenever demand does reignite, the supply conditions are very favorable for bullish price action.

So in synthesis from a tactical perspective, I am neutral/cautious until we see demand come in, at which point I would likely become very bullish. The dry kindling is building (supply is increasingly tightly-held) but it needs a spark rather than rain (some sort of catalyst for demand coming back in, or merely ongoing mild demand eventually soaking up the liquid supply over time) to move from consolidation/correction back into a bull phase.

I continue to view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the best way to get exposure to bitcoin for most people that want some.

## HDFC Bank Analysis

HDFC Bank (HDB) is the largest bank in India by market capitalization.

The parent organization, called the Housing Development Finance Corporation (aka “HDFC”), was founded in 1977 and is a publicly-traded company. HDFC Bank, which has for a long time been a separate publicly-traded company, was originally founded as a subsidiary of that organization in 1994. It then went onto merge with and acquire other banks to become the massive banking entity that it is today.

![HDFC Org Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-hdfc-org-chart.png)

*Chart Source: HDFC (Parent Company) Investor Presentation*

The parent HDFC organization owns stakes in multiple financial companies, including a 21% stake in HDFC Bank as shown on the chart. The largest entity on that chart by far is HDFC Bank, which has a bigger market capitalization than even the parent company. Unlike the parent organization or the other companies on that chart, HDFC Bank is directly listed on the New York Stock Exchange via an ADR, with ticker symbol HDB.

This analysis is about HDFC Bank (NYSE:HDB) specifically, and not about the parent organization that owns 21% of it.

HDFC Bank provides financial services for retail and corporate clients, offering most types of banking services:

![HDFC Services](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-hdfc-services.png)

*Source: HDFC Bank Investor Presentation*

Their growth rate has been persistently strong both for the company-wide metrics as well as on a per-share basis:

![HDFC Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-hdfc-growth.png)

*Source: HDFC Bank Investor Presentation*

They have thousands of physical branches throughout India, and also have deep online and mobile market penetration.

However, the bank ran into some challenges over the past year. They faced a series of glitches in their online and mobile banking software, which caused India’s central bank to ban HDFC from issuing new credit cards and offering new digital products starting in December 2020 until they address the ongoing problems. By August 2021, after HDFC addressed a number of the problems and overhauled some of their information technology infrastructure, regulators lifted the ban on the company issuing new credit cards. There are still some restrictions on them until they completely resolve the issues, though.

This move temporarily reduced HDFC’s market share of India’s credit card market and led them to underperform some of their large peers for a period of time. However, since August 2021 they have been issuing credit cards at a rather fast pace due to pent-up demand during their 8-month absence from the market.

In addition, the Delta variant of COVID-19 hit India rather hard in the first half of 2021 which slowed economic growth for the country in general.

As a result, HDFC’s ADR (ticker HDB) fell from $83 USD in February 2021 to $63 USD in December 2021, even as India’s broad stock market had positive returns during that time. HDFC Bank stock reached a local bottom in December and has moved up for the past few weeks. As of this writing, the stock is at around the same price level as it was in mid-2019.

India is starting to be impacted by the Omicron variant, and although it has been mild on countries so far, it could slow things down for a couple months by affecting consumer behavior and government policies. As I look out a few months, after the Omicron variant peaks in India at some point, HDFC bank seems reasonably well-positioned for the next several years. The next few months could be quite choppy.

**India’s Banking Market Has Room to Run**

India has a very large and young population, with fast GDP growth. Overall leverage in the country remains rather low.

Specifically, the country has among the lowest rates of household debt to GDP among major economies, at just 36%:

![India Household Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-india-household-debt.png)

Mortgages as a percentage of the country’s GDP are well below other southeast Asian emerging market nations:

![India Mortgages](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-india-mortgages.png)

*Chart Source: HDFC (Parent Company) Investor Presentation*

A key reason for this is because GDP per capita is under $2k USD in India. In comparison, Thailand is over $7k by that metric, while China and Malaysia both are over $10k. India still has a ton of development to do in order to reach even half of the levels of many emerging market peers.

In addition, India has relatively inexpensive housing market compared to many of these countries. Income has been going up faster than property values for several years, and so the average property value to income ratio has dropped to multi-decade lows:

![India Housing Affordability](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-india-affordability.png)

Overall, as India continues to build up more per-capita GDP, and build up more overall property value, the banking sector has a very large runway of growth to reach a typical level of credit saturation for a middle emerging market.

**Valuation Analysis**

Indian equities are usually rather richly valued, because their growth rates tend to be quite high. However, their valuations do fluctuate over time and some entry points are better than others.

HDFC Bank has had a very strong record of growth, and is currently mildly below its long-term valuation relative to its earnings:

![HDB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-hdb-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

The company’s price/earnings/growth ratio (“PEG” ratio) of well under 1.5x is pretty attractive. It’s more risky, but offers much more growth for the price, compared to expensive US tech stocks, for example. I think it makes a good diversifier for a portfolio that otherwise consists heavily of US stocks.

The bank trades at a higher-than-normal price/book multiple for a typical global bank, at about 4x. However, this is in line with their long-term average price/book ratio, and they have been growing their book value at a 15% annualized rate over the past decade in USD terms, which is much faster than the typical global bank.

![HDB Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-hdb-pb-ratio.png)

To put it into perspective, HDFC Bank has about 1/8th the book value of J.P. Morgan Chase, and about 1/4th the market capitalization, since it is growing much faster and markets are forward-looking. Given the fact that India has nearly 1.4 billion people, I can foresee HDFC Bank eventually reaching 50% or more of J.P. Morgan Chase’s size in terms of both book value and market capitalization.

I expect their valuation to decline over time, but for their overall book value size to probably triple in the decade ahead (which would be about a 12% annualized USD book value growth rate) unless India’s structural economic growth gets derailed in some way.

**Risk****Analysis**

An investment in a country’s leading bank is basically a play on the overall economic development of the country, plus any specific risks associated with that specific company. HDFC Bank has outperformed the broader Indian market for a long time, and I expect that to continue in the decade ahead, but this does not come without substantial risks. The bank can underperform for periods of time (as it has recently), and in certain scenarios could underperform over longer stretches of time as well.

*Geography/Currency Risk*

India runs persistent trade deficits, which are mostly due to their need to import large amounts of commodities, especially oil. Since I am rather bullish on oil and gas over the next 5+ years, I expect India to run into challenges with regard to this. The country also faces water shortages and other problems that can be exacerbated by ongoing climate changes.

In short, their geography has a lot of shortcomings compared to how much economic potential they have, and in a bad scenario involving their geography for one reason or another can translate into growth problems and currency devaluation.

Five years ago, a US dollar was worth a little over 68 Indian rupees, and today it is worth over 74 Indian rupees. This is pretty decent for an emerging market currency, especially considering that rupee depositors earned higher yields on their deposits, and so they ended up with more rupees over that period. If India runs into serious energy shortages, water shortages, or other economic problems, the rupee could lose a lot of value, which would be bad for the USD-denominated results of HDFC Bank.

However, if we look at the prior energy bull market (2003-2008), HDFC Bank did amazingly well (up over 700% in USD terms during a five-year stretch), along with overall Indian and emerging market growth. India’s trade deficit blew out during that period, but it didn’t negatively affect the investment. The total amount of growth in a dollar-weakening environment and emerging market growth was more than enough to compensate for a weak trade balance for Indian stocks in general and HDFC Bank in particular.

![India Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-india-trade-balance.png)

Overall, I view India’s energy and currency risks as being quite manageable as long as one holds a decent amount of energy exposure to counterbalance it. I continue to like energy producers and transporters as part of a diversified portfolio. Buying long-dated oil futures is another good way to get energy exposure.

I also consider the India+Russia barbell strategy to be attractive as far as international investing goes, meaning that being long the stock markets of both countries tends to be a good complementary approach. India has fast growth, is expensive, and is a major energy and commodity importer. Russia has slow growth, is cheap, is a major energy and commodity exporter, and has a fortress balance sheet with low debt and high reserves. I’m bullish on both for the long run.

*Single-Company Technical Risk*

HDFC Bank’s technical problems with its online and mobile platforms have hurt its stock performance relative to peers over the past year, because as previously-described, the problem led regulators to curb the bank’s growth until it fixed the issues.

The problems are already well on their way to being solved, and this resulted in a partial release of the regulator’s ban. HDFC also bought a 7.4% stake in Virtuoso Infotech, as part of their ongoing strengthening of their tech capabilities and robustness.

Still, this highlights the single-company risk that any company can face. HDFC Bank may encounter outages, regulatory action, or branding problems in its market.

## Final Thoughts

Back in 2015, a lot of healthcare and biotech stocks became overvalued. The biotech industry in particular took five years to recover from that point:

![IBB Price Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-ibb.png)

The broader healthcare sector only took about two years to recover, because big companies like Johnson and Johnson (JNJ) and United Health (UNH) didn’t really experience the valuation problem and they pulled the rest of the index up. Many individual healthcare stocks weren’t so lucky as the biggest ones.

The large drug distributor AmerisourceBergen (ABC) was a decent example. The stock became expensive after a period of strong growth but then its growth rate mildly decelerated, so the stock had a major six-year-long correction. Its earnings per share and dividends per share continued to increase, but its valuation was cut in half, so stock price stagnated. Many healthcare stocks went through a similar trend as this one:

![ABC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-abc-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Eventually AmerisourceBergen became an attractive buy, and I covered it bullishly in a February 2020 report, and have been riding up this recent bull leg from those cheaper valuation levels, from the $80s to the $130s.

A lot of the US mega-cap stocks currently look similar here in 2022 to how parts of the healthcare sector looked in 2015.

For example, Costco (COST) trades at a 46x price/earnings ratio, which is actually above most of the big tech stocks. It’s a fantastic company and has a lot of ecommerce growth potential, but it’s priced for perfection:

![Costco FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-cost-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

As another example, I’ve been continuously long Microsoft (MSFT) in the newsletter portfolio since the portfolio’s inception in 2018, and yet I’m selling it at these valuation levels with momentum getting weak. I’m not necessarily calling a top but I don’t love the stock’s risk/reward ratio as a long here.

![MSFT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-1-9-msft-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

We have to remember that stock prices can perform poorly even if the underlying company does fine. A stock price can get overexcited in terms of valuation, and then if there is a macro regime change or a growth slowdown for the company, the company can keep growing even as the stock price deflates like a balloon. Valuation itself is not a catalyst; it’s just a condition to be aware of, and makes a stock ripe for a reversal when a catalyst emerges.

About half of the top ten companies in the US in terms of market capitalization look vulnerable for this type of scenario playing out (meaning the company does fine while stock stagnates), although it’s not really a tradeable view until there is a fundamental catalyst or a price momentum shift. The Fed trying to tighten monetary policy into high inflation, followed by the end of the Omicron wave in a few months, could be providing that catalyst here in 2022.

But even if that scenario plays out, it doesn’t mean every stock in the index runs into trouble over the next few years. Some of them are very reasonably valued, while others are quite expensive relative to their growth rates. I view this as a time for defense overall, which in terms of my investment style means making sure I’m primarily invested in my highest-conviction long-term ideas at appropriate prices, and prepared to deal with volatility with cash-equivalents and other defensive investments on hand.
