# Global Equity Rotation Checkup

*April 27, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report provides an update on the US economy and liquidity, then dives into the current status of trade negotiations and the Department of Government Efficiency.

## Macro View

**US Economy Update**

Most near-term data is messy due to tariff-related impacts.

As I highlighted before, many importers brought in extra stuff to the United States during the first quarter to front-run potential tariffs. This included a major burst of gold imports, which should be treated more like capital repatriation than economic imports. Therefore, some measures of the economy like the Atlanta Fed GDPNow calculator have provided alternative calculations to exclude gold imports:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gdpnow.png)

The adjusted GDPNow indicator currently suggests that the US economy is in stall-speed.

Many other economic indicators haven’t updated for April yet, and thus haven’t measured the early impacts from the tariffs which hit at the start of the month.

The housing industry is operating at very low levels of turnover due to high mortgage rates (making existing mortgage holders unlikely to sell, and potential buyers unable to afford). Manufacturers have a lot of uncertainty around tariff policy, especially from their Chinese supply chains, and thus many of them are frozen in indecision. The stock market shifting from an uptrend to a choppy period puts downward pressure on the wealth effect and will likely impair high-end consumer spending around the margins. Some portion of these negatives is offset by ongoing large fiscal deficits, which are a source of inflationary stimulus.

**Domestic Liquidity Update**

Domestic liquidity continues to be rangebound, and this is likely to persist for a while:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-domestic-liquidity.png)

Banks in aggregate continue to have decent levels of cash levels relative to assets:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-bank-cash.png)

The Treasury’s cash account is currently in drawdown mode (which is good for liquidity) due to the ongoing debt ceiling limit. However, they had a $300 billion increase in mid-April during tax season:

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-tga.png)

They will likely go back to cash drawdown mode in the coming months, but this tax haul was a bit higher than consensus expectations and lengthens the estimated runway for how long the Treasury has until they run out of cash due to the debt ceiling. They can likely push into autumn of this year without a debt ceiling deal, although a deal could come before then.

Notably, tax receipts tend to follow asset prices with a lag. The year 2024 was a good one for the stock market and many other assets, and so investors had plenty of capital gains. Additionally, the economy continued to run hot amid fiscal dominance. If 2025 ends up being a lackluster year for the stock market and economy (which is my base case) then April 2026 is likely to have a lackluster burst of tax revenue.

The reverse repo facility is down to $94 billion, and is basically tapped out at this point:

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-rrp.png)

I previously expected the Fed to likely have to resume balance sheet expansion by late 2025. That’s still a significant possibility (especially if the Treasury rapidly refills its cash account in late 2025 after a debt ceiling deal), but because the Fed substantially slowed down their rate of QT this month earlier than I expected, they can potentially extend QT (at the new slower rate) into the first half of 2026. This would still represent having to pivot at roughly the same level of bank reserves, but pushes the time away until they reach that level.

**Global Liquidity Update**

Global liquidity has been solid lately, but it’s been overshadowed by major uncertainty around tariffs.

The biggest booster for global liquidity has been the dollar index rapidly falling from 110 to sub-100:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-dxy.png)

Unlike many commentators that criticize the falling dollar, I do not. The dollar index at 110 was very overvalued on a trade basis, and put pressure on both domestic producers and international borrowers. At 99 it’s still overvalued on a trade basis, because as I described in prior reports, articles, and [Chapter 13 of Broken Money](https://lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/broken-money-chapter-13.pdf), the reserve currency status boosts the value of the dollar above what it would trade at based on interest rate differentials and trade balances alone.

I think there’s a decent chance of 3-5 years of global outflows from the United States, which would likely push the dollar index lower. In the near term, however, I wouldn’t be surprised to see this current level become a local low for a bit of time. I don’t have high conviction in the near-term on the dollar because so many things are headline-driven these days.

To that prior point about a local low, Felix Jauvin, the host of Forward Guidance, humorously highlighted a major support zone at 140 for the dollar/yen exchange rate. I think that level is worth watching pretty closely:

![Jauvin Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-jauvin-april25.png)

**Tariff Walk-Backs**

Since the peak tariff headlines in early April, President Trump and Treasury Secretary Bessent have walked back their stance on Chinese tariffs, both in terms of dialogue and in terms of actions. This has eased market concerns somewhat, allowing stocks to take a breather off their lows, but continues to provide the US and global economy with considerable uncertainty.

The action-oriented walk-backs have mostly taken the form of exemptions. Computers, phones, and automakers have for example been exempted from many tariffs. A challenge with this approach is that while it helps some big corporations including Apple (AAPL), it continues to keep the pressure on all sorts of small and medium-sized businesses that produce many random things we don’t think about.

The dialogue-oriented walk-backs have taken the form of signaling. President Trump has said in interviews that tariffs won’t remain this high, for example. Bessent has also openly talked about de-escalation. Eamon Javers, CNBC’s senior Washington correspondent, [reports](https://x.com/EamonJavers/status/1914731774967431643):

![Javers Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-javers-april22.png)

That last part is important. Multiple statements from shipping companies and shipping analysts have noted a large decline in Chinese container shipments to the United States over the past several weeks. In early April, the scare in the Treasury market seemed to get Bessent’s attention. In late April, the new scare is the risk of product shortages this summer that are potentially on the scale of COVID-era shortages. These deep reductions in shipping volumes will hit us with a lag, and if they persist, will become noticeable to American consumers in the coming months.

However, despite de-escalation on the US side there is still a lot of uncertainty and confusion. Reuters and other news outlets [highlighted](https://www.reuters.com/business/autos-transportation/china-waives-tariffs-some-us-goods-denies-trumps-claim-that-talks-are-underway-2025-04-25/) the conflicting statements between the US and China on whether tariff negotiations are even happening:

> April 25 (Reuters) – U.S. President Donald Trump asserted in an interview published on Friday that tariff negotiations were under way with China, but Beijing denied any talks were taking place, the latest in a series of conflicting signals over what progress was being made to de-escalate a trade war threatening to sap global growth.

Meanwhile, plans for a US-led trading coalition against China are off to a rocky start. Bloomberg [reported](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-24/japan-to-resist-trump-efforts-to-form-trade-bloc-against-china) that Japan is likely to resist joining attempts by the US to participate in that type of anti-China trade bloc. Here were some of the key snippets:

> Japan intends to push back against any US effort to bring it into an economic bloc aligned against China because of the importance of Tokyo’s trade ties with Beijing, according to current and former Japanese government officials. […]
>
> Rather than dialing back trade, Tokyo is in the process of trying to get China to resume imports of seafood and beef from Japan. That has been evident over the past few weeks, with a series of Japanese delegations making trips to China to manage Tokyo’s relationship with Beijing. […]
>
> In a sign of Japan Inc.’s commitment to the China market, Toyota Motor Corp. this week agreed to open a new factory in Shanghai in 2027, with the company reportedly planning to invest around $2 billion in the plant. […]
>
> Around 20% of Japan’s total commerce is with China, larger than its trade with the US. […]

Thus far, this is playing in line with my view from two weeks ago. China is the largest trading partner with the majority of countries in the world. I view it as very improbable that the US would be able to make any sort of broad international trade coalition against China:

> The second is that the rest of the world is unlikely to take that well, and they do have a lot of leverage. For example, China has about $3.6 trillion in annual exports, and only about $500 billion go to the United States. That’s why the latest move by the administration is to try to de-escalate with the rest of the world and try to get everyone to target China together. That’s not an easy gambit to win, so it’ll be the key thing to watch in the weeks ahead.
>
> -April 13, 2025 premium report

Lastly, many small, medium, and large businesses are locked in a period of indecision until they have more clarity on the future of tariffs between the United States and China. These are the long tail of product makers that haven’t been given tariff exemptions like some of the largest corporations have.

As an anecdote, I was in a conversation with a small business about a potential angel investment the other day, and it provided a useful example of what many companies face. This company manufactures some things in the United States and some things in China, depending on specifics. They could move their Chinese-made product line to the United States, but those products would be more expensive per unit to produce, and importantly they would have to change and simplify the product to a slightly *lower* degree of functionality, because they cannot find a manufacturer that can produce it cost-effectively in their current design. A risk is that if they do move it to the United States and then the tariffs go way down due to some sort of deal, then they would have raised costs and reduced product quality for no reason, while competitors that still have their Chinese supply chains would be more competitive. But if they just do nothing and halt production for a period of time until things get clearer, then they hurt their revenues and profits, and tariffs might not come down any time soon. There are tons of businesses with similar dilemmas.

I continue to have a cautious view on the US economy and US stock market until there is more clarity on the tariff situation. There are plenty of individually interesting investments, and selling cash-secured put options continues to be an attractive strategy in these choppy and volatile market conditions.

**DOGE Shake-ups**

On January 20th, 2025, President Trump signed an [executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2025/01/establishing-and-implementing-the-presidents-department-of-government-efficiency/) creating the Department of Government Efficiency. The following key paragraph set it up as a temporary organization that expires after 18 months on July 4th, 2026. That date is symbolically important because it’s the 250th anniversary of Independence Day:

> (b) *Establishment of a Temporary Organization*.  There shall be a USDS Administrator established in the Executive Office of the President who shall report to the White House Chief of Staff. There is further established within USDS, in accordance with section 3161 of title 5, United States Code, a temporary organization known as “the U.S. DOGE Service Temporary Organization”.  The U.S. DOGE Service Temporary Organization shall be headed by the USDS Administrator and shall be dedicated to advancing the President’s 18-month DOGE agenda.  The U.S. DOGE Service Temporary Organization shall terminate on July 4, 2026. The termination of the U.S. DOGE Service Temporary Organization shall not be interpreted to imply the termination, attenuation, or amendment of any other authority or provision of this order.

Initially Elon Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy were going to co-lead it, but Ramaswamy was out before it began. That left Musk as the one spearheading the effort.

So far, despite DOGE being active for over three months, the U.S. fiscal deficit in 2025 has been slightly *larger* than it was by this time in 2024.

Tesla reported rather poor numbers for Q1 2025. Musk said on the [earnings call](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4777030-tesla-inc-tsla-q1-2025-earnings-call-transcript) that he would be significantly reducing his time spent on DOGE, and would spend more time on Tesla. He also said that he would likely continue working on DOGE part time for the rest of the 4-year presidential term, despite the fact that DOGE was initially intended to be complete by mid-2026:

> So, now that said, I do think there’s the large slug of work necessary to get the DOGE team in place and working in the government to get the financial house in order is mostly done. And I think starting probably next month, May, my time allocation to DOGE will drop significantly. I’ll have to continue doing it for, I think, probably the remainder of the President’s term, just to make sure that the waste and fraud that we stop does not come roaring back, which will do if it has the chance. So, I think I’ll continue to spend a day or two per week on government matters for as long as the President would like me to do so and as long as it is useful. But starting next month, I’ll be allocating probably more of my time to Tesla and now that the major work of establishing the Department of Government Efficiency is done.

Throughout January and February, Musk posted a number of times about reducing the fiscal deficit by $1 trillion by 2026:

![Musk Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-musk-february11.png)

![Musk Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-musk-february26.png)

Back in November 2024, when the DOGE initiative was announced, I provided an analysis of it and argued that it was unlikely to make deficit cuts that would be relevant at a macro scale. The reason I analyzed this so closely was because “fiscal dominance” is one of my key investment theses, and so it’s important to monitor concerted attempts to reduce the deficit, however improbable they may be.

Six months later, my conviction continues to be high (and now higher) that DOGE will not be making meaningful deficit reductions by 2026 or thereafter. Here was my analysis at the time, which continues to play out:

> But for this report, I’m going to focus on what I consider the proposal that most directly affects one of my core investment theses: the Department of Government Efficiency, or “DOGE” for short.
>
> Those that have followed my work know that I’ve been emphasizing fiscal dominance since around 2019, along with the broader idea that the U.S. is running unusually large fiscal deficits, is very likely to continue doing so despite various statements by officials to the contrary, and that this has investment implications.  So, my view on this began under the prior Trump term, continued through the Biden term, and as of now I expect it to continue under the next Trump term as well.
>
> Mainly, I’ve mostly avoided the long end of the bond curve (my mid-[2019 article on bonds likely being a bubble](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/) is my starting point for that), and indeed bonds have had basically their worst bear market in modern financial history in these 5+ years. Instead I’ve emphasized stocks, gold, bitcoin, and cash-equivalents or other short-duration paper. This included avoiding long-duration bonds at times where I highlighted risks of economic slowdowns, which goes against most consensus thinking but has been a successful strategy this far.
>
> I continue to expect large fiscal deficits in my forward analysis, which tends to “run things hot” compared to the baseline that we would expect when looking at credit cycles and other private sector metrics alone.
>
> However, Trump plans to set up a presidential advisory committee called the [Department of Government Efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Government_Efficiency) to address that. It’s not going to be an actual department since that requires an act of Congress, but rather it’ll be an advisory group led by Elon Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy. The stated goals are to streamline the government, improve efficiency, reduce the headcount, and cut the fiscal deficits. And notably, they plan to achieve the bulk of the outcome by July 4, 2026, which is symbolic since it’s the 250th anniversary of the country.
>
> The proposed leaders of this advisory committee have said that federal headcount could be reduced to up to 75% and fiscal deficits could be reduced by up to $2 trillion. Here’s the current Wikipedia snapshot, although I’m only using it as a summary source since I’ve seen many of the various social media posts by the advisory committee leaders directly:
>
> “Musk has suggested that the commission could help to cut the U.S. federal budget by up to US$2 trillion through measures such as reducing waste, abolishing redundant agencies, and downsizing the federal workforce. Ramaswamy also stated that DOGE may eliminate entire federal agencies and reduce the number of federal employees by as much as 75%. Musk has also proposed consolidating the number of federal agencies from more than 400 to fewer than 100.” –[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Government_Efficiency)
>
> The federal government has somewhere in the ballpark of [4.3 million employees](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/ap_14_strengthening_fy2025.pdf). About 1.35 million of them are uniformed military, and I don’t expect much if any cuts there. Another 550,000 or so (0.55 million) are postal workers. So that’s 1.9 million uniformed military and postal workers, leaving 2.4 million federal employees in other categories.
>
> ![Federal Employees](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-federal-employees.png)
>
> The number of contract employees that serve federal contracts has grown over time, and most sources I’ve seen place that number somewhere around 4 million, or up to 5 million at the high end. The spending on them in 2023 was [$759 billion](https://www.gao.gov/blog/snapshot-government-wide-contracting-fy-2023-interactive-dashboard), of which 60% was spent by the Department of Defense and 40% was spent by every other department combined.
>
> So 2.4 million cuttable federal employees (let’s round up to 2.5 million, assuming post office could be trimmed) and 4-5 million federal contractors results in 6.5 to 7.5 million jobs as a realistic starting point.
>
> The average compensation (salary + benefits) varies but is generally over six figures, and I’ll round up to $150k-$200k. So as a rough number, every 1 million jobs cut could result in $150-$200 billion less in spending. However, those employees do pay taxes on the pay they get from the government, so we can really only take 70-75% of that figure, so we’re more in the $110 billion to $150 billion range for every million people cut.
>
> They also propose cutting some facilities as well, although that’s likely going to be rather minimal. To include facility reductions, we can err toward the higher end of $150 billion per million people cut.
>
> If these numbers seem small, it’s because the biggest spending areas of the government are light on employees and are heavy on transfer payments. Medicare, Social Security, Interest Expense, Veterans Benefits,  and those sorts of programs are where the bulk of spending occurs, and which are very unpopular to cut ([source](https://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-budget/where-do-our-federal-tax-dollars-go)):
>
> ![Federal Budget](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-federal-budget.png)
>
> The Social Security Administration and the Department of Health and Human Services (which houses Medicare and Medicaid) have under 150,000 employees combined despite accounting for a huge portion of federal expenditure. And Interest Expense isn’t a headcount issue either.
>
> Promise 14 at the top of the [2024 Republican Platform](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/2024-republican-party-platform) is:
>
> “FIGHT FOR AND PROTECT SOCIAL SECURITY AND MEDICARE WITH NO CUTS, INCLUDING NO CHANGES TO THE RETIREMENT AGE”
>
> So they can’t really cut Medicare too much, can’t really cut Social Security at all, can’t really cut interest expense, can’t really cut Veterans Benefits, and the rest of the non-Defense stuff is not all that big (i.e. cut a million employees and consolidate some facilities and save $150 billion/year, back of the envelop).
>
> That leaves the Department of Defense as the big serious honeypot for cutting. The problem there is that untangling a web of failed audits that big, with that many entrenched interests, is a Gordian knot. The defense contractors are major political donors. Basically every Congressperson who has a military base or a factory that produces military goods in their jurisdiction (which is a lot of them) is going to push back on eliminating or moving those facilities. The same is true for the smaller civilian facilities. Cutting every $100 billion increment out of the Department of Defense will be like pulling teeth from an angry tiger.
>
> My initial conclusion from this analysis is that I will take the significant “under” on the proposal that up to 75% of federal jobs could be cut, or that up to $2 trillion in deficits could be saved. I will monitor the actions and progress of this and other things that affect fiscal deficits, but I maintain my thesis that U.S. fiscal deficits will likely be large going forward for many years, and that I will continue to use my existing investing framework unless I see significant evidence to the contrary that changes that view.
>
> -November 24, 2024 premium report

I also commissioned and advised a report by Sam Callahan, published [here](https://www.lynalden.com/full-steam-ahead-all-aboard-fiscal-dominance/) in January 2025, that analyzed fiscal deficits in quantitative detail, which reiterated the difficulty and unlikelihood of significantly cutting them.

As of this past week, House GOP members are [reportedly looking to boost defense spending](https://www.politico.com/news/2025/04/23/house-gop-pentagon-spending-bump-00307100) by $150 billion, rather than cut it. Additionally, President Trump [re-affirmed in an interview](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-25/trump-says-he-would-veto-cuts-to-medicaid-social-security) that he doesn’t plan to cut Medicare or Social Security, other than in cases of outright fraud, which is a GOP 2024 platform campaign promise (and supported by Democrats as well). These collectively are by far the biggest areas of federal government spending.

I plan to continue to monitor DOGE’s impacts on the fiscal deficit to see to what extent it remains in line with my thesis. At this time, I expect deficits to remain large in the coming years, with minimal impact from DOGE. There are individual pockets of the government that have been disrupted, but from a macroeconomic perspective, these were small and haphazardly done. It’s the big dollar flows that relate to fiscal dominance, and they’re all basically untouched.

In short, [nothing stops this train](https://www.lynalden.com/september-2024-newsletter/).

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has shown considerable price resilience relative to stocks throughout the month of April, which has caught a lot of investors’ attention.

Over the past week as stocks have stabilized, bitcoin temporarily reclaimed $95k for the first time since February. Thus, it held up better than expected during the worst weeks of the month, and participated pretty well in the positive weeks of the month.

From an on-chain perspective, the market capitalization has let off a lot of steam relative to its cost basis since it began consolidating in November 2024. This metric continues to indicate that while bitcoin may go through various consolidations going forward, it’s unlikely to experience a massive multi-year drawdown from current levels, because its overall sentiment and euphoria hasn’t reached anywhere near the highs of prior multi-year tops.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

I don’t personally pay much attention to technical analysis. However, I do like to check some of the most basic technical indicators for signs of momentum shifts and sustained price floors or price ceilings. In this environment, global liquidity, on-chain indicators, and technical indicators are all pretty favorable, pointing to the idea that $75k on April 9th was likely the price floor for this period, and that the next 6-12 months look pretty good as a base case.

Of course, bitcoin is volatile and we’re in a headline driven market, so there are no guarantees within that timeline, and proper risk procedures should be followed.

## Global Equity Rotation Checkup

The correction in the S&P 500 has taken steam off of its valuation levels, but it’s still richly priced. In addition, tariffs and a slowing US economy may pressure forward earnings, rendering the rich valuation still somewhat too rich.

![SPY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-spy-fastgraph.png)

Many investors and financial media producers have highlighted the outperformance of foreign equities to US equities this year, but so far it’s overblown.

After 15 years of massive outperformance, US stocks got a further boost in late 2024, and so far the year 2025 has mainly just reversed that last boost. This following ratio chart shows the S&P 500 relative to ex-US stocks, where higher means US stocks are outperforming and lower means ex-US stocks are outperforming:

![SPX ACWI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-spx-acwi.png)

My base case (which I could revise if something materially changes) is that this ratio is likely going to see a rounded top and some years of heading down a bit, similar to the 2003-2007 period. In other words, I expect a flywheel of capital outflows, a weaker dollar, and better international equity returns.

The reason this process acts as a flywheel is that stock performance and the dollar index partially feed on each other. Tons of global capital is stuffed into US markets, which boosts US stock valuations and the dollar index. However, it has reached rather extreme levels, with everyone on one side of the boat.

When the US stock market and dollar index go down together (which is rare, but happened here in 2025), foreign investors get a double-whammy. Their losses, when denominated in their local currency, are significant, since the stocks are going down in dollar terms and the dollar is going down relative to their currency. This can lead to capital repatriation, which further weakens US stocks and the dollar. A weaker dollar also hurts the US wealth effect and high-end consumer spending, while also alleviating some of the pressure on dollar-indebted emerging markets (such as Brazil, for example). This helps emerging markets show signs of life, and attracts some foreign capital, which helps solidify their currency and thus show further signs of life.

If the US economy slows, the Fed is likely to cut rates. Currently, the market is pricing in four rate cuts (100 bps total) through the end of 2025. Unlike prior rate-cutting cycles, however, we’re unlikely to reach a lower-low in interest rates, either on the short end or long end. As such, any sort of homeowner mortgage refinancing cycle is likely to be very small, and not very stimulative for consumer spending patterns compared to prior rate cutting cycles. Thus, a rate-cutting cycle could further weaken the dollar without being very stimulative for the economy, and further help foreign equities.

Meanwhile, I’m pretty bullish on Chinese equities with a 3-5 year view. If I was not American, I’d be more bullish, but as an American I have to consider the possibility within that timeframe that I get somehow sanctioned from my Chinese holdings due to more friction between the US and China.

Notably, the major Chinese indices and ETFs aren’t heavily exposed to manufacturing. The three biggest Chinese equity ETFs are MCHI, FXI, and KWEB.

The iShares MSCI China ETF (MCHI) for example only has 4% exposure to the industrial sector:

![MCHI Sectors](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-mchi-sectors.png)

The iShares China Large-Cap ETF (FXI) has less than 2% exposure to industrials. The KraneShares CSI China Internet ETF (KWEB) has basically no exposure, since it’s a sector-specific ETF.

The two biggest stocks in these ETFs are Tencent (TCEHY) and Alibaba (BABA). Both are historically cheap.

![TCEHY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-tcehy-fastgraph.png)

![BABA FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-baba-fastgraph.png)

I like to keep an eye on JD.com (JD) for good measure, as one of their larger stocks as well:

![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-jd-fastgraph.png)

During a handful of reports in 2024, I highlighted that Chinese stocks were likely bottoming in dollar terms and that Chinese fiscal stimulus would be gradual but persistent as certain red lines in their housing market, stock market, and growth metrics were touched. However, there still wasn’t a strong sign that Chinese stocks had bottomed vs US stocks.

> I think the bottom in Chinese stock indices is likely already in, and there are myriad catalysts that could propel them higher in the years ahead, including thawing tensions between the United States and China, or significant stimulus from the Chinese government. However, there’s no sign as of yet that Chinese stocks have bottomed vs American stocks, as the more relevant comparison. So that’s still in a wait-and-see mode.
>
> -September 1, 2024 premium report

Thus far, the bottom in Chinese stocks does seem to be pretty firmly in. If the intense trade war over the past month couldn’t push them back down to those levels, then it would take a rather massive shock to do so.

![MCHI Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-mchi-chart.png)

In addition, since then there is increasing evidence that Chinese stocks may have bottomed vs US stocks. It’s still too early to tell for sure, but both price action and valuations are supportive of that being the case.

My preferred way to have Chinese equity exposure is simply to own broad emerging markets ETFs like IEMG or VWO. This is partially because as bullish as I am on some of these stocks, I want to limit my overall exposure and complexity due to the non-zero risk of sanctions or other geopolitical (rather than economic) outcomes severing my exposure to those companies.

The iShares Core MSCI Emerging Markets ETF (IEMG) for example has 27% exposure to China, 21% exposure to India, 17% exposure to Taiwan, 10% exposure to South Korea, 5% exposure to Brazil, and a long tail of other exposures. Tencent and Alibaba are the second and third biggest stocks in the MSCI emerging markets index.

The biggest stock in the MSCI emerging markets index is Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM), which makes most of the chips that companies like Nvidia (NVDA) and AMD (AMD) design. They’re by far the largest and most powerful chip foundry operator in the world, and they increasingly have operations in the United States. They also have a fortress AA- rated balance sheet.

![TSM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-tsm-fastgraph.png)

While I don’t know if the full cycle low is in, I think the worst part of Taiwan Semi’s sell-off is likely done. I’m happy to buy a bit here, and buy a bit more if it drops further.

## Final Thoughts: Gold Caution

I have been including gold updates for many of my reports’ final thoughts. Being bullish on gold over long-duration Treasuries has been a winning play for a long time.

Whether I measure it from the date I added gold to the Newsletter portfolio (November 2018), or the date where I published a [long-form piece](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/) about bonds being a bubble (July 2019), gold has kept pace with the S&P 500 and has absolutely crushed bonds.

![Gold vs Bonds vs S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gold-tlt-spy.png)

![Gold vs Bonds vs S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gold-tlt-spy-2.png)

When looking out years, I remain structurally bullish on gold. And I think it can outperform the S&P 500 with a 3-5 year view. However, at current levels I think gold’s risks are increasing for a price consolidation that could last upwards of 6-12 months.

On the monthly chart, it’s nearly as technically overbought as it’s ever been:

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gold-chart.png)

While market turmoil and trade debates are likely to persist for a while longer, I think the market already reached “peak uncertainty” during the opening volley of the tariff escalations. With the stock market, bond market, and dollar index going down together in early April, the current administration has shown some degree of concern and de-escalation.

I don’t plan to sell gold, and indeed plan to keep it as a slice in my portfolios as a “hold” rather than a “buy”. In terms of setting expectations and portfolio allocations, I’d advise caution about chasing it at current levels.
