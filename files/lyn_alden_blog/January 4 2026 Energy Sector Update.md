## Macro View

**US Liquidity Conditions**

As the Fed has shifted away from a multi-year balance sheet contraction toward an indefinite (but gradual) balance sheet expansion plan, it has naturally resulted in a lot of commentary on social media. Much of it, as one might guess, isn’t particularly accurate.

The noise was particularly pronounced on December 31st, due to what’s known as “window dressing”. Financial institutions try to tidy up their balance sheet on the last day of a fiscal quarter, and especially on the last day of a fiscal year, which can result in an uptick in the usage of various financial plumbing mechanisms.

If an institution doesn’t have enough liquidity, they’ll take some actions to get liquidity on their balance sheet. If an institution has too much liquidity, they’ll take some actions to put their liquidity somewhere else and put more assets on their balance sheet. This is all done in order to lock in an optimal balance sheet composition for that brief snapshot of time. They can do this with other institutions, swapping around collateral for cash, and the Federal Reserve now maintains standing facilities to operate in those markets as well.

After years of the Fed reducing its balance sheet (which destroys base liquidity), liquidity has gotten rather tight. There is still technically enough cash for banks to function within the current regulatory environment, but not a ton of extra cash, and there are small pockets of excess liquidity and small pockets of liquidity shortages.

This was all quite predictable in advance, both from the Fed itself and from analysts that watch the financial plumbing closely. From rather simple calculations, it was clear to see that by late 2025 or so, liquidity would become tight. And then either at that time in late 2025, or within early 2026 if the Fed really tried to push it, there would be an end to the Fed’s balance sheet reduction.

![Late 2025](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-late-2025.png)

> Domestic liquidity currently remains rangebound. The overnight reverse repo facility is mostly drained (which puts liquidity back into the system), the Treasury General Account is up to $590 billion as it refills (which pulls liquidity out of the system), and the Fed continues to perform gradual quantitative tightening (which pulls liquidity out of the system.
>
> If the Treasury General Account reaches its $850 billion fully-filled target by the end of September, then it’ll likely drop liquidity to the lower end of its multi-year range.
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> Overall bank cash levels remain above prior acute liquidity events, but in Q4 2025 they will likely start to enter somewhat tight territory.
>
> ![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bank-cash-levels.png)
>
> My expectation continues to be that the Fed will end quantitative tightening in 2026, and possibly move toward gradual balance sheet increases (which they won’t call QE, similar to their balance sheet expansion from October 2019 to March 2020).
>
> So, domestic liquidity looks like it’ll be flat-to-down for a while, potentially running into lower thresholds, and when it does bottom it’ll be a pretty structural event that moves us up out of a multi-year range.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

There are two major trends I’m seeing in social media commentary on this subject recently.

The first and simpler trend is that people are saying things like, “the Fed injected another $75 billion” or other statements of this nature. Some of them are claiming there is a systemic banking crisis brewing. These tend to not be very informed commentators, and for starters, the whole point of overnight liquidity is that it’s not cumulative. It’s not “another” $75 billion. It’s the same tens of billions of dollars being added and taken out and added again.

On December 31st, as financial institutions scrambled for liquidity and collateral amongst themselves, there was an uptick in their usage of the Fed’s standing facilities. The Fed’s repo facility was tapped for about $75 billion, meaning that financial institutions gave collateral such as treasuries to the Fed in exchange for cash, for one day. Interestingly, on the *same day*, the Fed’s *reverse* repo facility was also used for about $106 billion, meaning that financial instructions put excess cash into the Fed and took out some treasuries.

![Repo Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-repo-chart-annotated.png)

![Reverse Repo Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-reverse-repo-chart-annotated.png)

On net, this means that about $31 billion in liquidity was actually sucked *out* of the market by the Fed on December 31st, at the behest of financial institutions. There were simultaneous pockets of liquidity shortage and liquidity abundance in the system, and those pockets didn’t all find each other efficiently and thus relied on the Fed’s two-way facilities to sort out the difference. Here in early January, the numbers have already died down from that window dressing spike.

The second and more nuanced trend is that people are saying the Fed’s shift toward balance sheet expansion is not doing enough to put out these liquidity issues. The Fed’s repo facility is still being regularly used, and the secured overnight financing rate remains persistently elevated above the interest rate on bank reserves, which indicates a liquidity shortage. This is being said by more informed commentators and is a worthy observation.

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-sofr-iorb.png)

However, I think that second trend of commentary is premature. The Fed just started re-expanding their balance sheet in mid-December. They’re only up about $100 billion from their lows, and are at roughly similar levels to when liquidity shortages started to emerge back in September. Bank reserve levels are still near their post-2020 low end. It’ll likely take some time of persistent balance sheet increases to more permanently put out these ongoing pockets of liquidity shortage.

![Fed Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-fed-balance-sheet.png)

![Bank Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-bank-reserves.png)

A few billion, or even a few tens of billions, being sloshed around in the Fed’s standing repo facility on an overnight basis is not much in the context of approximately $3 trillion in system-wide bank reserves.

As we look into 2026, the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet increases combined with mild to moderate interest rate reductions, is likely to be decent for liquidity conditions. I expect there to be plenty of bumps along the way, but I continue to have a constructive view on emerging market equities, as well as on regional and super-regional US banks, and likely on bitcoin as well.

**Strike on Venezuela**

On Saturday morning, the United States carried out an operation in Caracas, Venezuela, that resulted in the capture of Nicolas Maduro and his wife.

The US has since asserted control over Venezuela for the moment.

There is no shortage of macro analysts turning into geopolitical and energy analysts overnight. For example, this swift change in Venezuela potentially impacts the long-term trajectory of Iran (i.e. if the US feels more secure with Venezuelan oil, they might be more willing to risk a closing of the Strait of Hormuz during war with Iran), and the long-term trajectory of Taiwan (i.e. if the US is more assertive with its hemisphere, China might be more assertive with theirs), and people are quick to jump in with their analysis.

Rather than try to game out to what extent the odds of those things have shifted within an investable time horizon, which I don’t think is a particularly effective exercise, for this piece I’ll just put a few facts in context to highlight the potential impact on oil supply.

Venezuela has the largest oil reserves in the world, but it’s a rather heavy crude in the form of deeply-buried oil sands. As a result, their oil is more expensive to extract than, say, Saudi Arabia’s oil, and thus the effective reserves are much lower unless oil prices are sufficiently high to make it economical. This makes it a useful long-term source of oil as the large conventual sources are used up, but it’s not a swing producer.

Due to a combination of the oil being unconventional, international sanctions, and aging infrastructure amid economic mismanagement, Venezuela only produces around 1% of the world’s oil output. After a significant collapse in production a decade ago, their production levels have gradually been rising in recent years.

![Venezuelan Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-venezuelan-oil.png)

President Trump has said that US oil companies will be going into Venezuela. Notably, Chevron (CVX) is already allowed to operate in Venezuela, despite the sanctions and other geopolitical issues.

Overall, this outcome likely increases the base case of Venezuelan total oil production over the next several years, but only to an uncertain and limited extent. Oil production is already rising, there is already a presence of certain large US oil interests there, and the type of oil is not conducive to rapid ramp-ups in production.

## Energy Sector Update

Energy prices in the US and globally continue to be rather low, and certainly lower than my expectations from a couple of years ago.

Global economic growth continues to be on the mid-to-low side, which has been keeping the demand side in check. Meanwhile, the supply side has mostly avoided disruptions, despite the war in Ukraine and the instability in the Middle East.

When looking at the supply side, there have been two major “eras” of the oil age so far.

-The first era started in the late 1800s and lasted until the 1970s. That was the period of rapid growth and adoption.

-The second era, from the mid-1980s to the present, has had a more gradual, linear growth trajectory.

![Global Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-global-oil-production.png)

Oil production is often analyzed on a country by country basis, but it’s also helpful to step back once in a while and categorize it on a continent by continent basis:

![Continent Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-continent-oil-production.png)

As we can see, Europe, South America, and Africa have all been rather stagnant in terms of production in recent decades. The primary growth has come from North America and from Asia (which includes most of the Middle East).

North American conventional oil peaked a while ago, but “unconventional” Canadian oil sands and US shale have both fueled ongoing growth in the 2010s and 2020s. Since US shale ramped up starting in the 2010s, the oil market hasn’t really been the same, as the global balance shifts around to accommodate that new supply.

However, US shale oil is very price sensitive and has rapid depletion rates. That type of oil comes fast and is used up fast, requiring constant re-drilling. And over time, as the low hanging fruit is picked, the depletion rates increase, because the lower-quality wells are the ones left.

At current prices, there is not much economic incentive to drill, except for the most lucrative spots. However, drilling from the past couple of years is still paying off, with production that is mildly up.

![US Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-us-oil-production.png)

The US Energy Information Administration currently forecasts a global oil glut in 2026, and expects US shale production to approximately flatline in 2026 compared to 2025.

**Where to Invest?**

In my portfolios, I separate commodities into two categories.

-The first category consists of commodities that have relatively high existing stockpiles relative to new supply, and usually that are also rather expensive per unit of mass. This includes precious metals and uranium. For this category, I focus my exposure on owning the underlying commodity (either directly or in the form of ETFs/trusts), with much less exposure to the producers of the commodity. These commodities generally do a pretty good job of rising in price over time, and with so much existing stock available, the pricing power of producers tends to be weak.

-The second category consists of commodities with relatively low stockpiles relative to annual new supply. The majority of these tend to be cheaper per unit of mass as well. This includes copper, iron, oil, gas, fertilizer, grains, and other commodities. For this category, I focus my exposure on owning the producers, the transporters, or the service companies, rather than owning direct exposure to the commodity itself.

**Energy Producers**

Some of the large oil and gas producers, such as Exxon Mobil (XOM), are trading near their highs, and have been paying out growing dividends. While their returns have not been great, they’ve done better than the underlying commodities:

![XOM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-xom-fastgraph.png)

My strategy in this category has generally been to avoid the small and price-sensitive ones, and stick to the large ones with strong balance sheets and low breakeven points, meaning they can perform adequately even when oil prices are low. That’s helped with damage control during this weak period of oil prices.

Going forward, I view these as somewhat richly priced, since their stock prices have remained reasonably elevated compared to their profits. Therefore, while I’m not selling my existing positions, these are not areas I’m looking to deploy much more capital into at the moment.

**Energy Services**

Services companies provide a suite of technologies, expertise, and/or asset leases to help producers get barrels out of the ground (or the ocean).

I currently view oilfield services companies such as Schlumberger (SLB) and Halliburton (HAL) to be slightly better deals than the large producers. They increasingly show evidence of a bottom being in, but lackluster growth expectations aren’t exactly signaling a strong buy either.

![SLB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-slb-fastgraph.png)

**Energy Transporters**

Transporters make money from the movement of crude oil, natural gas, NGLs, and refined products, as well as from storage. With overall volumes continuing to do well, and valuations at reasonable levels, I continue to view this as an area to deploy capital in, especially for income-focused investors.

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) continues to be my preferred choice, with close to 7% yields and a quarter-century of consecutive annual dividend increases. It’s a partnership rather than a c-corp, which makes it more complicated for international investors and certain types of retirement accounts to own, but is tax-advantaged and works well as a long-time holding for domestic investors.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-epd-fastgraph.png)

Oneok (OKE) has recently come off of its temporary state of overvaluation, which in my view also makes it a reasonable target for capital. Its dividend yield is lower at 5.5% and its balance sheet isn’t quite as strong as EPD, but it also has a quarter-century of consecutive annual dividend increases, and one of the largest asset footprints in the country. As a corporation rather than a partnership, it tends to trade at a higher average valuation, especially on a quality-adjusted basis:

![OKE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-oke-fastgraph.png)

Overall, there are pockets in the energy space to deploy capital in, but the most interesting ones continue to be on the side that benefits from volumes rather than prices.

## Final Thoughts: Precious Metals

Precious metals reached a crescendo in late December and have since sold off a bit.

Here is silver, for example:

![Silver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-silver.png)

This outcome is not surprising, and is in line with my warning from the prior report:

> My view on precious metals continues to be this: I am concerned about the froth and near-term overbought status of them. However, I think most of this move was a structural repricing from an undervalued status to a fairly-valued status, rather than to an overvalued status.
>
> Thus, I no longer view precious metals as offering the great asymmetric upside-vs-downside potential that they used to, when I started accumulating them in 2018 and 2019 alongside my equities.
>
> Now they have a more balanced mix of upside and downside potential. Silver could easily jump to $100 or fall back to $40, for example. Neither would surprise me. Rebalancing or trimming is warranted, but that’s different than saying I’m bearish. I don’t view these metals as being fundamentally overvalued, but perhaps just over-enthused at the moment.
>
> Thus, I continue to hold the metals physically and in portfolios, but would recommend caution about new capital going into them, and it’s worth checking your portfolio to see if you’re comfortable with your existing percentage allocation after such a strong run.
>
> -December 21, 2025 premium report

Precious metals had a similar correction back in mid-October, so it is not as though a breaking of the momentum is necessarily a kill shot for the whole rally.

However, I continue to view precious metals as a place where risk/reward is no longer asymmetrical. On one hand, precious metals benefit to the upside in my opinion from a combination of fiscal dominance and a world that is becoming more multipolar and distrusting over time, and so I think a lot of this higher pricing is likely to stick even after sentiment washes out. On the other hand, sentiment has become so positive, that there are indeed risks for a multi-month or even multi-year correction from these price levels.

So, while I continue to hold precious metals, my new capital is likely to be deployed elsewhere.
