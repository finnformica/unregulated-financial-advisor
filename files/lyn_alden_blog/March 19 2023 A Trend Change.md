## Macro View

For months, we’ve been in an indecisive, unclear environment.

-The Federal Reserve was pulling liquidity out of the financial system via quantitative tightening, while the Treasury was putting some liquidity back in via spending down its cash balance.

-U.S. economic indicators were decelerating, but only slowly, and were basically at stall speed rather than outright recession. The labor market was holding up well.

-China’s re-opening was providing an upward stimulatory force from the East, but with an unclear magnitude. International flights, for example, were only increasing slowly.

The failure of Silicon Valley Bank and the broader contagion among small/medium/unique banks in the U.S. and Credit Suisse in Europe, along with a re-pricing of the whole financial sector and the renewed liquidity backstop by the Federal Reserve, has given us a rather decisive macroeconomic trend change to analyze.

First of all, domestic liquidity jumped sharply:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Treasury market is now pricing in the idea that the Federal Reserve is nearly done raising rates. Specifically, the 2-year Treasury yield had its biggest 1-day move since 1987. It firmly plunged below the Federal Reserve’s key interest rate:

![2 Year vs Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-2yr-vs-fed-funds.png)

To me, the forward path of interest rates is less clear than the forward path of liquidity. There’s nothing outright stopping the Fed from gradually raising interest rates at the moment, or holding them where they are. However, the Fed’s quantitative tightening will be hard to keep doing without a corresponding increase in the usage of their new BTFP liquidity facility.

In other words, while they have some flexibility around interest rates, I don’t think they have much flexibility to bring bank reserves much lower, if at all. Their quantitative tightening rapidly pulled liquidity out of banks, and for small banks (but not yet large banks) that liquidity is back down to levels not seen since the 2019 repo spike, which ended the Fed’s prior period of quantitative tightening. The current $3 trillion level for national bank reserves is basically the floor.

Cash as a percentage of bank assets:

![Bank Cash vs Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/march-2023-newsletter-bank-cash-vs-assets.png)

The Fed’s balance sheet:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-fed-balance-sheet-updated.png)

Post-2008 regulations force banks to hold more liquidity and safe collateral than they used to. In addition, in the age of rapid wire transfers and social media, it’s very easy for large deposit outflows to happen at any sign of bank distress, and so keeping only 2.5% of assets in cash is a thing of the past. Banks need plenty of liquidity.

In terms of absolute cash levels, small banks were basically unaffected by the 2018-2019 period of quantitative tightening, but got whipped around more-so than large banks by the 2022-2023 period of quantitative tightening:

![Small Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-small-bank-cash-resize.png)

In recent months I was mostly focusing on big banks from a macro perspective since they hold the majority of U.S. banking assets, but this small bank cash volatility is a big deal for that long tail of 4,000+ small and medium-size banks. My base case was that the Fed would tighten until they break something which is what happened here, but small/medium banks weren’t my first guess for what would break first.

What does this mean? To start with, I think the period from September 2019 to February 2020 is important to be familiar with. Prior to September 2019, the U.S. economy growth rate was decelerating, and the Fed was performing quantitative tightening to reduce its balance sheet. When the repo spike happened in mid-September 2019, their plans changed.

From September 2019 to February 2020, the Fed was managing the liquidity problem in the banking system (which they had caused via their quantitative tightening) by 1) participating in the repo market and then by 2) buying T-bills. They refused to call it quantitative easing at the time, because the goal wasn’t to be stimulative for markets or the economy; it was to provide the minimum amount of liquidity to keep things from seizing up. So the balance sheet went up a bit and then mostly sideways.

![Repo Spike Period](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-repo-spike-period.png)

I remember at the time that there were people who were saying it was 1) “merely a technical plumbing issue” or 2) “a massive interbank solvency problem somewhere”. My published view at the time was that it was neither; it was a liquidity floor and a fiscal deficit issue, and I wrote at the time that the Fed would probably buy Treasuries to contain it, which they did in October 2019. It was not a non-issue as many establishment analysts claimed, but at the same time, it was not an existential 2008-style problem either as many of the doomsayers claimed.

Of course, by March 2020 the virus and the lockdowns and the dried-up global capital flows resulted in a crash in asset prices, and the Treasury market went totally illiquid. The Fed immediately brought all of its liquidity bazookas out in response. If the virus and the associated lockdowns had not happened, then that intermediate period from September 2019 through February 2020 would have persisted for longer, with the Fed unable to shrink the balance sheet but not actively trying to increase it either. It was a 5+ month period of flat-to-up liquidity conditions, which was only ended by the pandemic.

With still-high inflation in 2023, the Fed doesn’t want to be simulative at the moment. However, they can’t easily drain bank reserves more than they already have. They can keep interest rates elevated on one hand, and maintain adequate liquidity with the other hand, in a balancing act. How long that period lasts will depend on numerous factors. That’s why this current period is so similar to that prior period from September 2019 through February 2020. It can persist in an intermediate state for quite a while.

Meanwhile, the Treasury general account is shrinking pretty rapidly now, which is positive for liquidity. It won’t necessarily go down every week, and tax season will probably push it up briefly, but it’s headed in a downward direction towards zero until the debt ceiling is resolved. The Treasury can’t issue new debt due to the debt ceiling being reached, and so they have to do things like draw down their existing cash balances to keep paying their bills.

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-tga.png)

I expect liquidity to be flat-to-up until the debt ceiling issue is resolved. The ongoing TGA drawdown is a net positive for liquidity. The Fed’s liquidity facilities are undoing their prior trend of downward liquidity, but are unlikely to go up substantially unless they need to.

Meanwhile, most U.S. domestic economic indicators continue to point downward towards deceleration. The Conference Board leading indicator update for February was a downward move:

**Global Updates**

The U.S. banking problems spread to the usual suspects in Europe, and this time Switzerland had to provide a massive liquidity backstop for Credit Suisse, which is classified as a global systemically important bank. Deposits have been fleeing, and its credit default swaps have been soaring. With the liquidity backstop in place, things have cooled down. Over the weekend, the Swiss government brokered a deal for UBS to acquire Credit Suisse, with the Swiss National Bank offering a $100 billion liquidity backstop to UBS as part of the deal.

In addition, global central banks released statements today about their financial systems being in good order. The U.S. Treasury and Fed released this [joint statement](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy1350):

>

The capital and liquidity positions of the U.S. banking system are strong, and the U.S. financial system is resilient. Japanese bonds saw a classic flight-to-safety move last week while this mess played out, and so their 10-year JGB yields came down below their 0.50% yield cap to around 0.30%.

Pictet Asset Management has been putting out some of the best charts on China, and their chief economist [Patrick Zweifel](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel) put out an updated set last week.

During China’s three-year lockdown, they kept their industrial production and exports consistently up. The parts of their economy that were killed were 1) retail consumption and 2) domestic construction. This was actually the most disinflationary outcome possible; China reduced their own consumption and thus minimized demand for three years, while continuing to keep export supplies flowing almost the entire time, which minimized supply-driven price problems globally.

They are starting to come back in terms of consumption, although I expect a slower growth trend going forward.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin held up rather well against the backdrop of bank problems. A risk-off move generally isn’t ideal for the price, but a boost in liquidity along with the sharp reduction in Treasury yields and forward expectations of the Fed’s interest rate is beneficial for alternative monies such as gold and bitcoin.

The price has reached over $28,000. I don’t know what the next six months will look like in terms of price action since it will mostly be driven by liquidity conditions, but the 3-5 year setup looks similar to when I originally went long in April 2020 at $6,900 per coin.

![Bitcoin Price Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-bitcoin-charts.png)

Bitcoin outperformed most other cryptocurrencies during this banking fiasco. Within the cryptocurrency ecosystem, bitcoin is viewed as the flight-to-safety trade. Additionally, many cryptocurrencies are heavily VC-funded and centralized, and their three major banks all just got taken down (Silvergate Bank, Silicon Valley Bank, and Signature Bank) while companies in the Bitcoin ecosystem tend to be rather separate in terms of financing and the underlying protocol itself is less reliant on funding.

While many people think bitcoin’s recent outperformance compared to other coins is instructive for future trends over the next year, I’m hesitant to read too much into small moves between coin prices like this. I don’t have a firm view on other cryptocurrencies over the next year, since their movements often depend heavily on liquidity and hype. From a fundamental perspective, I remain constructive on bitcoin and stablecoins, and remain less interested in other digital assets.

There is no significant sign of on-chain new wallet accumulation at this time. When Alameda/FTX blew up, there was a rapid accumulation of on-chain bitcoin because people wanted to get their coins off of exchanges and into self custody. Since that is not happening currently, I view the recent price action as being mainly related to the change in interest rates and liquidity, which sparked a spike in demand for it including on custodial exchanges.

## Trend Change Deep Dive

Rather than focusing on one sector, this report section will have updates on a few key sectors in light of this shift in liquidity.

**Banks Update**

As I described in the [March 2023 public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/march-2023-newsletter/), this current banking problem is mostly liquidity-driven rather than solvency-driven.

In 2008, banks made a lot of aggressive loans that began to default. They had unusually low cash and Treasuries (nominally risk-free assets) relative to total assets:

![Bank Safe Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/march-2023-newsletter-bank-safe-assets.png)

In 2023, banks mostly hold safe assets in terms of credit risk, but among those safe assets they bought a lot of Treasury and mortgage securities when their yields were low. These securities trade at discounted prices now if they are sold, so that their effective yield-to-maturity for the buyer is in line with current (higher-yielding) securities of the same type. If a bank holds their securities until maturity, they get all of their money back. But if depositors pull their deposits out, it can force a bank to sell its securities for a loss. That’s what happened to Silicon Valley Bank.

The year-over-year percentage change in the 10-year Treasury rate and the 30-year mortgage rate helps visualize this. Roughly speaking, the higher these go, the bigger the unrealized losses are for holding long-duration Treasuries and mortgage-related securities within a short period of time. Currently it’s bad:

![Treasury Mortgage Change](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-treasury-mortgage-change.png)

In response to this, the Fed set up a liquidity facility so that any given bank can use their safe securities as collateral for a loan by the Fed if they experience a bank run, rather than have to sell those securities and lock in a realized loss.

However, loans made directly by a bank and held by that bank are not liquid securities and cannot make use of the Fed’s new liquidity facility. This is why First Republic Bank (FRC) has been under significant pressure even after the Fed’s new liquidity facility was set up; most of FRC’s assets are loans rather than securities. Banks with that type of balance sheet require different funding sources, and for example FRC has received deposits from the largest banks in order to keep their liquidity profile up.

In other words, people are moving their deposits out of vulnerable banks towards the largest banks, and those largest banks are recycling those deposits right back into some of those vulnerable banks (which they’ll only do if they perceive that bank to have a liquidity problem and not a solvency problem).

It’s actually quite constructive to know which banks deposited money in First Republic, because it confirms which banks have excess deposits rather than being in need of deposits. The “Big 4” were of course known to have excess deposits, as well as the key custodian banks (BNY Mellon and State Street) and the key investment banks (Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley), but it’s notable that the “Next 3” biggest commercial banks (PNC, Truist, and US Bank) are also in shape to provide liquidity:

> March 16, 2023: NEW YORK–(BUSINESS WIRE)–Bank of America, Citigroup, JPMorgan Chase and Wells Fargo announced today they are each making a $5 billion uninsured deposit into First Republic Bank. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are each making an uninsured deposit of $2.5 billion, and BNY Mellon, PNC Bank, State Street, Truist and U.S. Bank are each making an uninsured deposit of $1 billion, for a total deposit from the eleven banks of $30 billion. This action by America’s largest banks reflects their confidence in First Republic and in banks of all sizes, and it demonstrates their overall commitment to helping banks serve their customers and communities. Regional, midsize and small banks are critical to the health and functioning of our financial system.

President Biden’s team has been in talks with Warren Buffett, and flight records showed that over 20 private jets landed in Omaha this weekend. A number of banking executives are likely in talks with Buffett for liquidity provisions. From Biden’s perspective, he wants as much of this to be dealt with by private sector entities so that the perception of a taxpayer bailout is minimized. Buffett’s company has a $129 billion war chest of cash and T-bills, and a history of providing funding for major banks during crises. So far, there’s no action out of Omaha on this.

When we look out over the banking landscape, there are a few key metrics to focus on when it comes to this specific type of duration risk.

-Some banks, big or small, made a bigger bet on long-duration securities than others, and thus have greater unrealized losses.

-Small and medium-sized banks are more prone to have their deposits flow out quickly compared to the giant banks. This is especially true for banks that cater mainly to businesses, who often have deposit amounts way above the $250,000 FDIC limit, and thus tend to be skittish at any sign of a bank problem. It’s harder for a giant bank to have its deposits flow out quickly, unless it really messes up like Credit Suisse.

-A lot of banks and shadow banks have **hidden risks** in the form of underwater illiquid loans. Long-duration loans have the same problem as long-duration securities: if sold today they would get priced at a discount even if they have low default risk. A lot of people are focusing on the unrealized losses on securities of large bank portfolios, but small banks in aggregate have a similar and less visible problem with their loan books. Small banks have 67% of their assets in illiquid loans while large banks only have 50% of their assets in illiquid loans.

Default risk for loans and securities outside of the commercial real estate sector is pretty low for most of these banks. Therefore, solvency is less of a concern than profitability and liquidity, at least for most banks.

Overall, the safest bank by almost any metric is J.P. Morgan Chase (JPM), although investors pay a premium for it. It’s the largest bank in the U.S. and took less duration risk than other large banks. It’s also the only bank with a G-SIB score of 4, meaning it’s classified as the single most global systemically important bank in the world.

Down from there, Bank of America (BAC), Citigroup (C), and Wells Fargo (WFC) all have different risk profiles but are solid. Bank of America has the biggest unrealized losses of the group, Citi has the most uninsured deposits due to their bigger focus on business banking, and Wells Fargo is kind of in the middle based on most metrics. They’re all G-SIBs of varying levels, and are gaining deposits rather than losing deposits.

Truist Financial (TFC), U.S. Bancorp (USB), PNC Financial Services (PNC), Fifth Third Bancorp (FITB), Huntington Bancshares (HBAN) and other large banks are quite safe in terms of size and deposit diversity, but they are getting punished by the market for holding a significant amount of these longer-duration assets with unrealized losses, which is fair but in my opinion overblown. I consider them to have more of a profitability risk than a solvency risk; if they have to raise deposit rates to keep depositors, or if they have to use the Fed’s BTFP facility, then it will increase the interest rates on their liabilities and will squeeze their profit margins. I would be careful about owning too big of a stake in any one of them, but spreading exposure among a basket of them is an interesting value play in my view.

At the bottom, the long tail of small and medium-sized banks are riskier. This category includes over 4,000 banks and so of course there is a huge quality differential between them. People who are investing in small banks or holding deposits over $250,000 in small banks should do considerable research to look at the unrealized losses and liquidity risk that their banks have. A [recent study](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=4387676) found that around 190 banks may have similar risk profiles as Silicon Valley Bank, out of thousands of banks.

Overall, I think the sell-off in large regional bank stocks down to their COVID-19 lows is overdone.

## Final Thoughts

We’ve exited an uncertain market regime, and entered another uncertain market regime, but one with different characteristics. The Fed balance sheet is likely to trend sideways-ish for months, unless or until some big catalyst forces it higher. They don’t want to flood the system with liquidity but they want to keep the wheels from falling off.

The price action of gold (up), bitcoin (up), the Nasdaq 100 (up), and the dollar index (down) have been pretty clear that this banking event and the response by the Fed represents a gain in liquidity. Some analysts are quick to point out that the Fed’s recent uptick in balance sheet size is not QE and that’s correct, but that doesn’t change the fact that it’s positive for liquidity compared to the prior week and the prior year-long downward trend.

I think some people are overestimating how much liquidity the Fed is likely to provide within a 3-month period, while other people are dismissing it as a bank-specific problem and therefore not something that has consequences for the Fed’s tightening attempts. My view currently lands in the middle; all else equal it’s mildly positive for liquidity. The Fed’s pulldown in total liquidity is probably over, but that doesn’t mean liquidity is going to go straight up, either.

Going back to my “century chart” that I’ve posted a number of times (with some new annotations this time), the Fed has been fighting 1940s-style fiscal-driven inflation (when debts are high and most of the monetary expansion is coming from fiscal deficits) with hawkish 1970s-style monetary policy (which works when debts are low and most of the monetary expansion is coming from bank lending).

![Nope Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-nope.png)

Raising rates that quickly and sucking liquidity out that quickly, when debts are still that high, leads to financial chaos. They now have a decision to either keep trying to tighten and damage the long-tail of small and medium banks and large swaths of the shadow banking system that they can’t even measure, or pause and risk letting inflation run hot. It’s rough for Powell because everyone is Monday-morning quarterbacking with these bank crises going on, and this type of “long term debt cycle” situation is the hardest environment for a central bank to operate in.

Due to ongoing uncertainty and volatility, I continue to view a “three pillar portfolio” as the way to go for this decade, at least as a baseline. A classic 60/40 stock/bond portfolio benefits from disinflation, while this type of portfolio is more balanced, with significant protections against inflation.

-The first pillar consists of high-quality profitable equities, with an emphasis on dividend-paying companies at reasonable valuations. A slice of emerging market exposure is also interesting here.

-The second pillar consists of commodity and hard money exposure. This includes energy producers, commodity producers, royalty companies, direct exposure to energy or commodity futures, precious metals, and/or bitcoin.

-The third pillar consists of cash or cash-equivalents like T-bills and money markets, and serves as a volatility-reducer for the other two pillars.
