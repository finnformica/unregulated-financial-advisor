## Macro View

**US Liquidity Conditions**

Base liquidity in the United States continues to be mildly strained. The Fed’s standing repo facility was used a number of times over the past couple of weeks, although it closed Friday without being used. Meanwhile, SOFR rates are still elevated compared to the interest rate on bank reserves, which indicates a collateral glut and a liquidity shortage:

![Repo Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-repo-facility-usage.png)

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-sofr-iorb-spread.png)

Absolute bank cash levels remain at the lower end of their post-COVID range, which is the level I’ve previously highlighted to be the pain area (around $3 trillion):

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-bank-cash.png)

Bank cash levels relative to total bank assets also remain at the lower end of their post-COVID range, although the problems are occurring despite there being more relative liquidity in the system than there was back in 2019:

![Bank Cash Percentage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-bank-cash-percentage.png)

This liquidity issue can be resolved in the longer run by the Fed expanding its balance sheet (i.e. printing base liquidity) or by the Fed adjusting the supplementary leverage ratio for commercial banks (i.e. letting banks lever up their base liquidity more). It can also be improved in the short term if the Treasury Department lowers its cash account back to normal levels of around $850 billion or less:

![TGA Level](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-tga.png)

This is relevant because repo is a huge source of leverage for hedge funds. Hedge funds borrow trillions of dollars, and for that leverage they primarily use equities as collateral with primary brokers and bonds as collateral for repo activities. With repo rates being elevated, it gradually puts pressure on these hedge funds and can lead them to de-risk their positions by selling some of their assets and reducing leverage:

![NY Fed Post](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-ny-fed-post.png)

Overall, the combination of tight base liquidity in the US along with AI capex concerns (discussed later in this report) is likely contributing to the recent weakness in US equity markets. Until we get one or both of those Fed actions (expanded balance sheet or supplementary leverage ratio tweak), or some other positive catalyst of similar scale, I’d err toward being somewhat defensive.

**Japanese Stimulus**

In my report from four weeks ago, I highlighted the election of Japan’s new prime minster and her fiscal policy, which is expected to be rather substantial:

> Japan elected a new prime minister, Sanae Takaichi. The fact that she is the country’s first female prime minister and the fact that she is on the conservative end of Japan’s political spectrum have both made global news, but for the purposes of this report, the notable fact is that she is in favor of substantial fiscal deficits for certain purposes (such as expanded military spending) and a firm continuation of Abenomics. Overall, her policies are likely to add mildly to global liquidity.
>
> -October 26, 2025 premium report

As an update, there are now rather tangible policies announced. They will be doing the biggest fiscal injection in years. Reuters reports:

> TOKYO, Nov 21 (Reuters) – Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s cabinet approved a 21.3 trillion yen ($135.40 billion) economic stimulus package on Friday, marking the first major policy initiative under the new leader, who has pledged to pursue expansionary fiscal measures.
>
> The package includes general account outlays of 17.7 trillion yen, far exceeding the previous year’s 13.9 trillion yen and representing the largest stimulus since the COVID pandemic. It will also include 2.7 trillion yen in tax cuts.
>
> Growing concerns about the nation’s worsening fiscal position brought about by Takaichi’s lavish stimulus package have sent the Japanese currency to 10-month lows and super-long government bond yields to record highs.

In rough numbers, Japan has a GDP of a little over $4 trillion while the US has a GDP of around $30 trillion. Relatively speaking, a $135 billion stimulus in Japan is nearly equivalent to a $1 trillion stimulus in the US, in terms of percentage-of-GDP extra deficits. That’s a rather sizable jolt.

Japan’s currency has sold off recently (i.e. a rising USDJPY ratio). It’s part of a longer-term devaluation trend that began in late 2012 when the country ramped up its monetary base and the policy set broadly known as “Abenomics” began:

![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-usdjpy.png)

![BOJ Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-boj-balance-sheet.png)And their bond yields have risen (meaning their bonds have sold off), both recently and as part of a multi-year trend:

![Japan 10-Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-japan-10year.png)

In recent years, the effect of these types of currency moves have been substantial. We can see this when we look at Japan’s GDP in yen vs dollar terms.

In yen terms, Japan’s GDP is up substantially since the pandemic:

![Japan GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-japan-gdp-yen.png)

However, in dollar terms, Japan’s GDP has fallen from its long-term level of around $5 trillion to around $4 trillion:

![Japan GDP Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-japan-gdp-annotated.png)

Notably, 2012 is also when their stock market structurally bottomed:

![Nikkei 225](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-nikkei225.png)

The money-printing represented a shift from private sector debt to public sector debt, overall devaluation of the currency, and a reflation in asset prices. Companies and households greatly deleveraged, that leverage shifted to the public sector, and then that leverage has increasingly been devalued.

Since Japan has a stable economy and huge foreign-exchange reserves, neither their currency nor their bonds are in serious intermediate-term danger. They can choose to sell plenty of US treasuries to defend their currency. However, that would negatively impact the US bond market, which is already wobbly given these recent liquidity issues. Additionally, the Bank of Japan can resume its balance sheet expansion to contain their bond yields, but that comes at the risk of further currency weakness, unless as previously mentioned they sell some reserves to support their currency.

Basically, they have plenty of levers to pull, but they all end up leading to central bank balance sheet expansion by some combination of the Federal Reserve and the Bank of Japan in the years ahead. The European Central Bank will be joining the party too. A lot of this will probably start in 2026, but over the coming weeks and months I’ll be monitoring these developments for more precise timing.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin and the broader cryptocurrency space faced immense selling pressure this past week. The price started November at around $110,00 and has dropped below $90k.

Most metrics of euphoria/capitulation show that it’s the most oversold in years. It’s not as wrecked as it gets in multi-year bear markets, but the combination of quantitative metrics and social media commentary is pretty intense.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

I continue to view there to be somewhat of a self-fulfilling prophecy going on, because people are worried that a 4-year cycle is over. Bitcoin previously peaked in Q4 2013, Q4 2017, and Q4 2021, and so many bulls fear that they’ll have to wait a few years for new highs here in Q4 2025.

While bitcoin does have all sorts of opportunities and risks, and can go down for any number of reasons, I don’t view the 4-year cycle as being fundamentally valid now in the way that it was early in its lifecycle. Overall mining rewards are small, and now it’s driven by other factors. My base case continues to be for a more muted but longer period of price expansion than the asset as historically had.

**Potential MSCI De-listing**

On November 20th, JP Morgan released a research note about how MSCI might de-list Strategy (MSTR) and other crypto asset treasury companies from their indices. This made its way around financial media. It’s not a confirmed decision yet and is still an ongoing discussion, but will be confirmed in January.

However, MSCI actually made that announcement on October 10th, though it wasn’t widely known at the time until JP Morgan brought public attention to it. What’s notable about October 10th is that there was a flash-crash in crypto that night, with $19 billion worth of leveraged liquidiations occurring.

At the time, many people thought it was because of President Trump posting about an escalation in the trade war with China. When other markets are closed, crypto is the market that trades 24/7 and allows various funds to de-risk positions even on a Friday night. However, with this current knowledge, it’s possible that institutions were making major moves specific to the crypto industry in response to that MSCI announcement as well. A one-two punch may have occurred.

Can bitcoin survive with MSTR and other treasury companies de-listed from major indices? Yes. MSTR only holds around 3.2% of all bitcoin. All together, the percentage of bitcoin held by all crypto asset treasury companies is pretty low.

However, treasury companies were the major source of demand over the past year, along with ETFs. The top ones with low leverage will continue to exist, but being excluded from various indices would reduce demand for their shares and thus weigh down their overall ability to acquire bitcoin via share dilution.

## AI Capex and the Stock Market

The full stack of AI companies is broad, but for the big things we can put them in a few main buckets.

One bucket is the chip designers and foundries. These are companies like Nvidia (NVDA), AMD (AMD), and Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM). They are profitable companies and make money primarily by selling high volumes of GPUs. Their opportunity is to sell a ton more GPUs, and their risk is that if there is a cooldown in GPU purchases by various AI companies, their revenues and margins will suffer.

Another bucket is the large language model creators. These are the newer research arms and “pure play” AI companies. They are generally private and unprofitable, such as OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI. They create consumer-facing or business-facing AI platforms and applications, and despite enormous demand for their services, they generally don’t have a vision toward profitability within the next 5+ years. Their money is primarily raised from massive venture capital investors.

A third bucket that sits in the middle of those are the hyperscalers. These are the biggest companies in the United States that run datacenters, and are increasingly turning those datacenters toward hosting AI hardware as they partner with the pure-play AI companies. Microsoft (MSFT), Amazon (AMZN), Alphabet (GOOGL), Meta (META), and Oracle (ORCL) are the biggest hyperscalers. Some hyperscalers also have large language models of their own, such as Alphabet’s Gemini and Meta’s Llama.

The first and the third buckets are most relevant for the US stock market, while the second bucket is primarily for private venture investors. My October 12th report focused on some of the chipmakers (and announced the selling of TSM and AMD after quick doubles in stock price), and this report will focus on the hyperscalers.

**GPU Depreciation**

The famous portfolio manager and short seller Michael Burry made headlines recently by drawing attention to the lengthening depreciation estimates among hyperscalers. Many of them expect their GPUs to depreciate over 5-6 years, whereas he thinks 3 or less is more reasonable.

![Burry Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-burry-depreciation-tweet.png)

In plain English, it means there is some uncertainty around how long GPUs will typically last and retain value. Will these hyperscalers get a useful 5-6 year life out of them, or will they be replacing them in 3 years to stay at the forefront of processing efficiency and/or due to physical burnout?

Those in favor of the longer GPU useful lives say that once they get a bit out of date, they can be rotated toward less intensive functions, and that on average they really will last 5+ years. Those against longer GPU useful lives say that while this rotation might indeed be the case, the vast majority of the depreciation will have happened by that point and so a 3-year life is still the reasonable number to assume.

I err toward Burry being correct on this, but I’m certainly not a subject matter expert on the topic. In my engineering days I bought tons of GPUs for the aircraft simulation facility that I was the head engineer for and they typically lasted 5+ years as we rotated the oldest to the applications where they fit best and put the newest in the most advanced sims, but we weren’t at the rapid cutting edge of intense AI competition or running them constantly.

And from my knowledge of bitcoin mining, the depreciation bears were generally wrong for that industry. Alex de Vries in particular made headlines about bitcoin mining rigs only having average lives of around a year and a half, and Nic Carter and others debunked that claim pretty heavily. Bitcoin miners indeed rotate their mining rigs away from the most intensive parts to the scrappier portions of the industry, and typically do get many years of life out of their mining rigs on average.

To some extent, while I think the debate over GPU depreciation is valid, it’s only a subset of the bigger question. The bigger question is how much will all of this capex pay off over say, the next five years in terms of revenue and free cash flow? These five companies collectively issued $120 billion worth of bonds this year to fuel their AI capex, which is 4x their typical annual rate of bond issuance.

-A bear would say that these hyperscalers won’t get quite the ROI they are hoping from it all. Their prior software growth had much lower capital requirements, which allowed them to channel most of their profits toward share buybacks and juice their stock prices. Now all of this hardware capex will lead to lower share buybacks, a lot of new debt issuance, and mixed revenue and profit results to show for it after several years.

-A bull would say that they will get the ROI they hope from it. All of this capex, while indeed quite expensive, will greatly boost their revenue, profits, and free cash flow in the future. They’ll have tremendous pricing power and companies all around the world will need to use their services to remain competitive.

I’m increasingly shifting toward the bearish/neutral side, at least at current valuations. There are certainly price levels that would draw me back on the bull side, but right now I think the overall risk/reward balance is looking less attractive than it was a year or two ago. I’ll walk through some examples for the remainder of this section.

I’ll start with Alphabet (GOOGL), since I’ve had a bunch of premium reports primarily or partially focused on Alphabet from a bullish perspective. It’s the hyperscaler I’ve covered most closely, and it’s the best stock performer out of the hyperscalers in 2025.

My main point was that Alphabet was a growing company, with a fortress balance sheet, trading at a lower earnings multiple than the likes of Procter and Gamble (PG) and other slower-growing businesses.

> The company had an artificial growth spurt in 2021 due to so much fiscal stimulus working its way through the economy. The broad money supply increased rapidly, and Alphabet with its leading position captured quite a bit of that. The 2022 year is kind of the hangover year, where analysts are expecting flat performance.

>
> -May 15, 2022 premium report

-The second was [November 10, 2024](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2024-11-10/) at around $175/share. By this point ChatGPT had been out for a while, and many people were concerned with large language models taking market share from the Google search business. I presented my view on why that’s overblown, especially relative to their other businesses.

> Finally, the newest threat to Alphabet’s search business is the rise of AI. If we want to know something, rather than search for the relevant website to tell us about it, we can now ask ChatGPT. That can indirectly eat into Alphabet’s market share by changing how we search for information. In response, Alphabet has gotten into the AI business itself, incorporates AI into Google searches, and has had to spend a lot of capex money on this.
>
> One thing that I think is important to realize is that not all searches are equal in terms of add revenue. Some types of searches inherently have more value to advertisers and thus to the advertiser platform. If I search for “plumber near me” then it’s pretty clear I want to find a plumber and spend money on plumbing services, and so plumbers are going to compete to get my attention. But if I want to know “what’s the difference between Sci-Fi and Fantasy?” then whether I type that into Google or ChatGPT I’ll get an AI answer that helps explain the difference and it’s not nearly as monetizable. So if Google loses market share to people searching for various less-monetizable information, then that’s not really a big issue.
>
> And AI in general “expands the pie” for what’s possible. So for example we can ask AI to summarize something for us, or create something for us, which wasn’t possible with search engines. Since Alphabet is getting into the AI business, it expands the areas in which it can provide value. And so in that sense, new entrants like ChatGPT are a challenge, but also the technology itself is harnessed by Alphabet and thus multiple entities can grow larger at the same time.
>
> That’s not to say that AI isn’t a threat to Alphabet’s search business, but rather it’s to say that not all searches are equal and that the pie is growing, and thus it may take more time for AI to eat into Alphabet’s search revenue than bears think, which by extension gives Alphabet more time to expand its cloud and AI businesses.
>
> -November 10, 2024 premium report

-A number of other reports have included Alphabet rather substantially. Some of the most recent ones were back-to-back on [March 30, 2025](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2025-3-30/) and [April 13, 2025](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2025-4-13/). The March report focused on AI art generation, and highlighted Alphabet as a company I like because AI makes video generation cheaper and they run the largest video hosting network, YouTube. I also reiterated that I think Google’s search profitability is somewhat resistant against AI. The April report focused on other things, but re-iterated Alphabet as an investment idea. The stock had dipped to under $160/share during these reports despite having good forward prospects, which is why I was increasingly highlighting it.

Well, Alphabet’s Gemini continues to maintain major market share among LLMs, their YouTube business continues to do great, and their search business is still holding up. The stock closed at nearly $300/share on Friday.

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-googl-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (an 18.62x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Now, when everyone is bulled up on Alphabet, I’m getting less enthused. The higher valuation no longer leaves a margin of safety, and their massive capex is forecasted to have lackluster free cash flow results over the next few years.

That’s not to say I’m a bear on Alphabet, but rather it’s that the risk/reward is no longer skewed enough for me to be bullish like I was from 2022 until now. The thesis has already played out, in other words. They even initiated a dividend like I hoped they would in my 2022 report. Here’s the free cash flow FAST Graph:

So, I’m shifting my stance to neutral on the company for a while, and view it as a decent name to take profits in.

## Final Thoughts: Gold

Gold remains in consolidation since its peak in mid-October, and I view that as a healthy consolidation after such a huge run this year:

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-gold-price.png)

Despite the massive gains in gold, and my temporary concerns about it being overbought, I continue to view it as likely to outperform the S&P 500 over the next five years. That’s especially true if the hyperscaler capex bear risks described in the earlier section end up being directionally correct, since those stocks are basically the anchors of the S&P 500.

Notably, the 50-month moving average of the S&P 500 to gold ratio is negative this year for the first time since 2002. Stocks aren’t just having a bad year versus gold; they’re showing signs of a structural rollover:

![SPX Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-23-spx-gold.png)

Given the massive outperformance of US stocks over the past fifteen years relative to international stocks and relative to most other large assets, it wouldn’t be surprising at all for them to take a breather for a few years relative to other types of assets. What would be surprising is for them not to.
