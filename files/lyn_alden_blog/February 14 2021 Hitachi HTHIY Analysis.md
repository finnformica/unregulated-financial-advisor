## Macro View

Coronavirus cases continue to trend down sharply in the United States, and this is true for most of the world as well:

![Daily Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-14-daily-cases.png)

*Chart Source: Worldometer*

This is also true for similar metrics, like hospitalizations and deaths. There is still headline risk; various strains keep emerging, and there are occasional reports of re-infection or strains being more resistant to a particular vaccine.

So that’s a persistent tail risk, that eventually one of these variants might be particularly bad, push cases back up, make vaccines less effective, and that governments would go through another round of lockdowns trying to address it. Statistically speaking, that’s not my base case.

In related news, a big study has shown that Russia’s vaccine is more effective than folks initially assumed.

Russia’s vaccine was rolled out quickly before final tests were completed (while the Pfizer and Moderna vaccines were still undergoing final tests) and they received appropriate criticism for doing so. Many folks thought it wouldn’t be particularly safe or effective, and indeed for example China’s vaccine ended up not being very effective.

However, a [recent peer-reviewed study](https://www.thelancet.com/journals/lancet/article/PIIS0140-6736(21)00234-8/fulltext) in *The Lancet* found that Russia’s vaccine is actually among the top three vaccines in terms of efficacy, and with no unusual side effects. More than two dozen countries have approved it now, and Russia is emphasizing emerging markets with it. It can be stored in the fridge rather than requiring deep-freezing like some of the other vaccines require. It can also potentially accelerate vaccination in Europe this spring/summer. It’s challenging to produce, so Russia is working with other countries to manufacture it as well.

Overall, we have good news on the virus front. Multiple vaccines are going out, albeit not at the same rate everywhere, and the virus is on the downtrend at the moment.

**Dollar and Treasury Updates**

A month ago in my January 17th report, I wrote that although I remain long-term dollar bearish (since October 2019), I was turning dollar neutral-to-bullish for Q1 2021 as a tactical bounce within that bearish trend.

This tactical shift was because the US Treasury was planning to perform massive bond issuance in Q1 compared to what the Federal Reserve was prepared to buy, which would suck liquidity out of the system.

However, on February 1st, the Treasury (now under Janet Yellen, rather than Steven Mnuchin) [announced](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy0011) plans to revise down their Q1 net Treasury issuance from $1.127 trillion to $274 billion, which is a huge $853 billion (75%) reduction. They also plan to have low net Treasury issuance in Q2, although that may change based on stimulus decisions.

Instead of large Treasury issuance as initially planned, they will draw down the Treasury General Account or “TGA”.

The TGA is the Treasury’s checking account at the Federal Reserve; money that they’ve collected but not yet spent, and the Yellen Treasury seems more intent than the Mnuchin Treasury on drawing down this figure closer towards normal levels. In other words, they can let out a surge of cash without a corresponding bond issuance by drawing down the TGA, since they’ve already collected this cash from prior bond issuance.

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-14-tga.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

All else being equal, drawing down on the TGA and keeping Treasury issuance light is a relatively dollar bearish plan. Since my brief tactical dollar neutral-to-bullish view from a month ago, we indeed got a little bounce and then a pullback, and overall the dollar has been flat for the month. I’m shifting back towards my baseline neutral-to-bearish dollar position.

The one dollar-bullish variable to be aware of, however, is that if the long end of the Treasury curve keeps rising along with inflation expectations, and thus keeps inflation-adjusted yields from getting more negative, it can keep a temporary floor on the dollar.

With very loose fiscal policy (relative to other governments), moderate monetary policy (relative to other central banks), and a large trade deficit (relative to major trading partners), the longer-term structural force for the dollar likely remains downward. This monetary policy variable by the Fed, particularly in regards to their rate of Treasury purchases, remains the only sticking point.

There are potentially a few decision points in the coming months to watch as it relates to the Fed’s monetary policy, because if they don’t buy enough Treasuries and yields keep pushing up, that could temporarily stabilize the dollar for a while but also cause a liquidity issue at some point.

Overall, until the dollar index either breaks firmly above 92 (previous consolidation level) or firmly below 88 (making a new multi-year bottom), it’s relatively uninteresting.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-14-dxy.jpeg)

So, I’m back to being neutral-to-bearish on the dollar, but watching certain support and resistance lines pretty closely here.

## Hitachi Ltd Deep Dive Analysis

Hitachi Ltd (TYO: 6501, OTC: HTHIY) is a Japanese multinational technology and infrastructure conglomerate that is getting near the end of a decade-long restructuring process. This restructuring already helped over the past decade and should provide a good foundation for the next decade to grow from.

**Hitachi History and Japanese Corporate Reform**

The company was founded over a century ago by an electrical engineer, and the first product was an induction motor.

Over time, Hitachi became the “General Electric of Japan”, as a sprawling conglomerate with hundreds of businesses, engaged in everything from consumer electronics to industrial automation to mechanical parts to construction equipment to nuclear energy to information technology. Many of the businesses had no synergy with each other, other than generally being technical/industrial as a category.

This was in-line with what the rest of the Japanese corporate sector looked like for decades. Many conglomerates operated in unfocused ways, and owned multiple joint ventures between themselves in a big interlocking puzzle.

However, during the 30-year bear market that the Japanese economy and stock market have gone through, there has been a gradual shift towards better corporate governance. Particularly over the last decade, Japanese firms deleveraged, spun off non-core businesses and joint ventures, increased dividends, and began emphasizing higher returns on capital and better total returns for shareholders in general.

Many companies also increased the number of independent directors on their boards, and brought in non-Japanese board members to complement their Japanese board members and have a global perspective, since many of them operate globally.

Hitachi, under the leadership of Hiroaki Nakanishi, has been one of the most aggressive companies in Japan in terms of restructuring and re-focusing its company along this path.

**Hitachi’s Restructuring**

Beginning in 2010, after a series of massive losses, Hitachi began a decade-long restructuring process. Hiroaki Nakanishi, who had been with the company since 1970, became the company’s president in 2010, and currently he is the executive chairman.

This restructuring emphasized profitability and good returns on capital, and has involved a series of divestitures and acquisitions to shift the company from being a random pile of tech/industrial businesses to being a large and diversified technology/industry business with a set of core lines of business that complement each other in a more unified way.

They divested many lower-margin or legacy businesses, like consumer electronics, chemicals, and their hard drive business, and doubled-down on information technology, internet of things “IoT”, and global infrastructure. They also began steadily increasing their dividends per share, and have indeed become consistently profitable.

As part of this process under the current management plan, they identified five of their lines of business as “social innovation businesses” including IT, Energy, Industry, Mobility, and Smart Life.

The company’s annual reporting period ends on March 31 of each year. If we look at the latest full year figures (from March 31, 2020), we can see a breakdown of their revenue and operating income, with the social innovation businesses highlighted in red:

![Hitachi Segments](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-14-hitachi-segments.png)

*Chart Source: Hitachi Integrated Report 2020*

About half of their revenue comes from Japan, and the other half comes from a combination of North America, Europe, Asia, and elsewhere.

Since that fiscal year in the charts above, Hitachi divested their chemical business, and announced plans to divest their metals and construction businesses.

On the other side of the coin, they acquired the majority of ABB’s power grid business, which will enhance Hitachi’s small energy segment. This business was carved out of Switzerland’s ABB, and became a joint venture called Hitachi ABB Power Grids Ltd, of which Hitachi owns 80% and ABB owns 20%. In time, Hitachi will probably fully absorb it but they already effectively own it now.

A key thing that all of the red social innovation businesses in the charts above have in common is that they incorporate Lumada, Hitachi’s platform of digital solutions. This helps focus and standardize the various business segments towards complete solutions with a lot of technical overlap, rather than being a pile of separate businesses within a shared corporate umbrella.

**The Focus on Lumada**

Here’s how the company defines Lumada:

> Lumada consists of a core of platform services, architecture and technologies that enable the quick development and implementation of advanced digital solutions.
>
> Lumada is comprised of six main layers form the architecture of the Lumada IoT platform: Edge, Core, Data Management, Analytics, Studio, and Foundry. Together they serve as a software platform that is intelligent, composable, secure, and flexible.

![Hitachi Lumada](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-14-hitachi-lumada.png)

*Chart Source: Hitachi.com*

Hitach is now very focused on internet of things “IoT” technologies, along with the analytics, data management, and enterprise-wide integration between IT systems and operational field assets.

In other words, rather than being involved in various unrelated low-margin businesses, they are doubling down on being a system-wide integrator for major companies and projects, relating to information technology, industrial automation, smart grids, infrastructure, and home products.

![Hitachi Lumada 2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-14-hitachi-lumada-2.png)

*Chart Source: Hitachi.com*

One thing that all of these areas have in common, ranging from industrial automation to enterprise IT to their newly-acquired power grids business, is the need for a ton of sensors and control systems within these assets (IoT), along with big data technology to tie it all together to make sense of it and help company leaders effectively manage their assets towards their business goals.

**Global Electrification**

A lot of people are excited about electric vehicles and renewable energy, going so far as to pay extraordinarily high valuations for companies in those industries. By most ways of measuring it, those areas became bubbles in 2021. For example, Tesla revenues grew by 30% in 2020 while their stock price soared over 700%.

Hitachi, however, represents a cheap way to play that trend towards electrification of the world. It’s the equivalent of selling picks and shovels to prospectors in a gold rush, as the popular analogy goes.

In order for the world to shift more towards electric vehicles over time, electrical grids need to be vastly improved and overhauled.

The electrical grid represents a big chunk of our energy consumption. Heating and cooking energy often comes from natural gas, but can also come from electricity. Our cars, however, run on gasoline, which involves a lot of energy transported by pipeline, rail, truck, etc.

The shift towards electrification will see this automotive energy gradually shift over to the electrical grid. In addition, solar panels and other factors make the grid more complex. The existing electrical grid in most places is not robust enough or smart enough to handle these changes, and needs a lot of infrastructure spending to modernize.

One of Hitachi’s core areas of expertise is electrification, which is one of the areas they are emphasizing across their business lines to ensure they are part of this mega-trend. Hitachi is involved in robotics, power grids, electric vehicle car parts, data centers, sensors and IoT technologies, and digital platforms to tie them together.

In terms of dividends, Hitachi shifted from a stagnant dividend policy pre-2011, to a persistent dividend growth policy since then. Their dividends depend on business conditions and can be flat or go up as appropriate, but overall, we see a clear trend, with a 10-11% annualized dividend growth rate since the company’s turnaround efforts began:

After those divestitures, the company will be relatively “complete” and ready to move forward as a Lumada-focused and more streamlined tech/industrial company involved with several long-term trends of power grid and infrastructure enhancement, robotics, electrification, data management, and IoT.

Notably, the stock in yen terms just broke out of a 15-year price consolidation, that previously had what was basically a quadruple-top:

I have no idea what the stock will do over any multi-month period but for a 5+ year perspective, I think Hitachi is a good buy-and-hold candidate, along with the various Japanese trading companies that I’ve been pounding the table on in recent months.

**Company Risks**

Although Hitachi is a relatively conservative investment at this point, it does have notable risks.

*Economic Cyclicality*

Although Hitachi was successful in turning around its business over the past decade, it has seen slow progress in terms of profit growth because the macro headwinds were against them.

The 2010s decade was disinflationary in a global sense, with a strengthening dollar that kept a lid on global growth and liquidity, along with a period of deleveraging in Japan. In other words, it wasn’t a pro-commodity, pro-industry type of decade at all, and instead was more of a software decade, with industrial/commodity stagnation.

I think the 2020s are shaping up to be the opposite, with more of a weaker dollar, pro-commodity, pro-industry style. Many countries will likely turn to publicly-funded infrastructure projects to fuel growth and modernize aging infrastructure, and a weaker dollar could ignite the next leg up in emerging market earnings and activity.

In other words, I expect tailwinds for Hitachi now, which combined with their new corporation direction, should make this decade a lot different than the past few decades for the company.

However, if this doesn’t come to pass, Hitachi could indeed have another slow decade, as it grinds along with good management but against macro headwinds.

*Global Competition*

Hitachi operates in a number of highly competitive industries. They serve markets all over the world which means they have competition from American, European, Chinese, Taiwanese, and South Korean firms in many areas.

I would classify them as having a moderate moat, meaning they have a lot of entrenched advantages, but can’t just sit back and ride cash-cow businesses. They have to constantly innovate and be well-managed in order to succeed, so if the current or next generation of managers fail to perform well, Hitachi could lag its peers and stagnate.

The 10-year yield has been rising, and just broke out over 1.20%, which makes it the highest 10-year yield since the pandemic hit in early 2020.

All else being equal, a steepening yield curve is a sign of reflation, tends to be good for bank stocks and value stocks more broadly, and tends to be somewhat painful for very expensive growth stocks, as described in [my article](https://www.lynalden.com/interest-rate-effects/) on interest rate effects on equities.
