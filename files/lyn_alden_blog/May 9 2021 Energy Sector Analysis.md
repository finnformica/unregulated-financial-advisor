**The Jobs Report**

The US April payroll report came in lighter this past Friday than economist expectations.

In fact, it was the biggest negative gap between consensus expectations for the numbers and the actual numbers in over twenty years. The report showed 266,000 in net new jobs created between March and April, compared to the 1 million net new jobs that consensus economist expectations were set for. Ouch.

![Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-payrolls.png)

Economists also expected the official unemployment rate to tick down from 6.0% to 5.8%, but instead it ticked up to 6.1%.

On the news, the dollar had a notable decline for the day, and shorter-duration Treasury yields ticked lower. Basically, the market interpreted it to mean that this will delay any potential hawkish behavior from the Fed, since they are favoring maximizing employment rather than minimizing inflation at this time when it comes to their policy approach.

There has been a divergence between job openings and payrolls recently, meaning that jobs are available but the wages offered are not high enough to attract people back to the workforce. Unemployed Americans are receiving state aid and then an additional $300 in weekly additions from the federal government. This aid offered folks a valuable lifeline during the pandemic and shutdowns, but now is tightening the labor market for low-pay professions, especially including fast food workers. Fast food wages barely pay any better than the combined federal/state unemployment benefits. Plus, a percentage of the population is still concerned about the virus, although over time this effect should diminish.

The federal portion of the unemployment benefits runs out in September, so employers are reluctant to raise wages before then. For example, if they have to offer $15/hr to someone now in order to entice them to work instead of receive unemployment benefits, but think they can hold out and offer only $12/hr when unemployment benefits expire, then they will be reluctant to raise the wages to a necessary level to hire now.

Some of them are instead offering sign-on bonuses, so they don’t lock themselves into permanently higher wages for new workers, but provide a temporary extra incentive up front to start the job now.

The local Chipotle near me has some “online order only” days during each week because they have acute labor shortages, leaving them unable to run the full location on random days.

Other parts of the stimulus bills don’t compete with jobs too much. For example, the three rounds of stimulus checks were handed out to both folks who have jobs and who don’t have jobs (as long as they’re under an upper middle class income level), so there wasn’t an incentive mechanism there to keep people out of the labor force. Whatever folks got from the checks is on top of other income rather than competing with it.

Instead, it’s specifically the extra unemployment benefits that only unemployed people get, that are competing with low-wage jobs and likely contributing to some labor shortages.

So, labor data could continue to be a bit weird for the next several months until September, and it’s something to monitor as we move forward.

US median wages have barely moved at all over the past four decades in inflation-adjusted terms, as automation, offshoring, and other factors put a lot of downward pressure on the power that workers have to earn higher wages. Labor’s share of the US economy (measured in terms of compensation as a percentage of the country’s economic output) diminished gradually from the 1960s to the 2000s, and then dropped more quickly starting in the early 2000s. This happened to some extent to most developed countries, but it was particularly strong for the United States and certain Southern European countries.

Meanwhile, on the other side of that situation, China’s exports accelerated, as a sizable chunk of the US industrial growth trend was instead shifted to them.

## Ethereum and Altcoin Note

As strong as Bitcoin’s performance has been this year (up 96% YTD in 2021, after being up 347% in 2020), alternative cryptocurrencies have done even better in terms of percent gains, especially starting about a month ago in April.

This has historically occurred during the heart of bull runs in the industry, as speculation reaches a high level of excitement and retail traders move out on the risk curve to try to find the “next big thing” that will go up a lot from a small starting level.

Ethereum, the other mega-cap cryptocurrency besides bitcoin, had a big price breakout in April that still continues until now. And then smaller tokens along the risk spectrum have done even better.

Caution is warranted at whichever point this bull cycle ends; if we look back into previous bull cycles like 2013 and 2017, the number of coins from those eras that went on to hold their value over the long run is a very tiny percentage. Bitcoin has done well, Ethereum has done well, and only a few others out of the thousands.

I have some concerns about long-term tail risks related to Ethereum (lack of sufficient decentralization and large changes in the protocol planned in the years ahead), but in terms of price performance, whenever Bitcoin does well, Ethereum and other coins at the time historically have a period of time of doing even better. So far, this cycle has been no different. And unlike the other altcoins, Ethereum is the second-largest protocol with a significant network effect.

And when we look out past that on the risk curve, Ethereum Classic has actually done even better than Ethereum this year. It’s up by a crazy percentage:

![Ethereum Classic](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-classic.png)

*Chart Source: [Coindesk](https://www.coindesk.com/)*

Back in 2016, Ethereum suffered a major setback when an [attacker exploited a bug in smart contracts](https://www.coindesk.com/understanding-dao-hack-journalists) and took a ton of coins from a large entity that had raised $150 million in investor money. It was a significant minority percentage of total Ethereum coins at the time, numbering in the millions of coins. The attacker argued that it was a fair and legal exploit of a poorly-constructed digital contract after carefully reviewing it for shortcomings, rather than actual theft.

The Ethereum Foundation intervened to roll this attack back by hard-forking the Ethereum blockchain to a new chain. This took the coins back from the attacker to the original owners.

Ethereum Classic is the original chain, the version where the the attack was never rolled back. Ethereum Classic is a much smaller chain with a much weaker developer ecosystem and much weaker network effect. The hard fork that rolled back the attack became the main Ethereum chain in terms of popularity, size, and ongoing development despite being the hard fork itself. Since then, Ethereum continues to hard fork to make changes, but those other forks didn’t cause a community split like the 2016 one did to some extent.

And yet, in this environment of high crypto speculation, the lesser-developed Ethereum Classic chain is outperforming what is considered the main Ethereum chain in percent price gains this year.

Since April, Bitcoin Cash has also been outperforming the main Bitcoin blockchain as well. Bitcoin Cash forked away from Bitcoin back in 2017 and has since underperformed, but is currently enjoying a speculation price spike like many other tokens.

And of course Dogecoin, which was originally created as a joke years ago, but started to get pumped by Elon Musk last year, has even bigger percent gains:

![Doge](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-doge.png)

*Chart Source: Coindesk*

So far, 2021 has had an inverse relationship between quality and price action for the crypto space. Bitcoin and Ethereum have lagged Ethereum Classic, Dogecoin, and one called Cumrocket in terms of percent price gains. A lot of this is… very bubbly. When Ethereum Classic, Bitcoin Cash, and meme coins are going vertical, it’s a good time to check risk management for the space overall.

I remain focused on a tax-advantaged and risk-adjusted method of holding Bitcoin in my portfolio, rather than actively trading various altcoins.

## Energy Sector Analysis

It has been several months since I last covered energy (oil and gas) stocks, and they are worth reviewing at this time in my opinion, especially from a longer-term perspective.

I’ll touch on some specific names like Exxon Mobil (XOM), Lukoil (LUKOY), and Enterprise Products Partners LP (EPD) in this report, but the broad topic is about the sector as a whole. Energy stocks had a nice bounce in recent months but overall, many large pools of capital are structurally underweight the sector.

Here’s the iShares Global Energy ETF (IXC), showing the sector’s dismal performance during the past fifteen years. The purple line is the price and the orange line adjusts it to include reinvested dividends:

![IXC Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-ixc-performance.png)

From 2001 to 2008, that ETF and ones like it were big outperformers, but from 2008 onward they deeply underperformed. The sector had negative total returns since 2008, including reinvested dividends.

The industry was disrupted by a number of events, creating a perfect storm for producers. The global financial crisis hit in 2008, the European sovereign debt crisis hit in 2011, North American shale added a lot of new supply to the market from 2010 to 2015, and a strong dollar suppressed commodity prices and the global growth rate (and thus oil demand) from 2015 through 2019. The pandemic temporarily obliterated oil demand in 2020, culminating in what was probably the ultimate bottom for the sector in this cycle when WTI crude futures went into negative pricing in April 2020 due to oversupply.

As we look out longer, many pools of capital are avoiding the sector due to ESG mandates, and many investors are concerned that oil and gas will face secular stagnation as renewable energy sources displace them.

**The Long-Term Oil Cycle**

Here’s a chart with 150 years of data that shows the rolling 5-year cumulative percent price change for crude oil:

![Oil Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-oil-longterm.png)

The period of time over the past several years was historically about as bad as it gets for the oil market within the past century, comparable to the mid-1980s and mid-1930s.

If we look at bellwethers in the industry, Exxon Mobil (XOM) for example never previously had a drawdown as bad as it had from 2014 to 2020, in five decades of data:

![XOM Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-xom-drawdown.png)

Ironically but not surprisingly, it has historically been a good idea to buy Exxon Mobil and other oil stocks whenever the energy outlook looks terrible. This next chart shows Exxon’s share price compared to the EIA’s assessment of OPEC spare production capacity.

Whenever there is a ton of spare capacity (too much oil supply vs weak demand), that’s been the time to buy. Even the one “early” signal still greatly outperformed the S&P 500 over the next several years.

![XOM vs OPEC Spare Capacity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-xom-vs-opec-spare-capacity.png)

*Chart Source: EIA*

Future supply may not be so abundant at current prices, though. Ever since energy prices collapsed in 2014 due to structural shale-driven oversupply and dollar strengthening conditions, global oil capex has diminished significantly:

![Oil Capex](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-oil-capex.png)

*Chart Source: [Rystad Energy](https://www.rystadenergy.com/)*

As Rick Rule of Sprott often says about the natural resources sector, bear markets are the authors of bull markets, and bull markets are the authors of bear markets. Because prices have been low, there has been less investment to bring new supply to market. The sector gradually started to recover, but then got knocked down again by the events of 2020.

In 2019, global oil demand reached just over 100 million barrels per day. After falling very fast in 2020, demand has since recovered to about 95 million barrels per day, with various forecasts that generally point to late 2021 or sometime in 2022 that it’ll reach previous peak demand levels.

China and India still present significant oil demand potential. They have a lot of consumption growth that is offsetting the stagnant levels of energy consumption in developed markets. Here’s China’s pre-pandemic oil consumption per year:

![China Oil Consumption](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-china-oil-consumption.png)

*Chart Source: [CEIC](https://www.ceicdata.com/en/indicator/china/oil-consumption)*

To put things into perspective, the United States consumes more than 3x as much oil per capita as Thailand, and Thailand consumes more than 2x as much oil per capita as China. If China ever reaches the point where it consumes as much oil per capita as Thailand does now, that change alone would add 15 million barrels of daily demand.

India consumes a little over a third as much oil per capita as China, which totals about 5 million barrels per day. If they ever reach China’s current consumption levels (which, again, are only half as much as Thailand and one sixth as much as the US), that would add nearly 10 million barrels to daily global demand.

And then there’s Indonesia, Nigeria, and other large population countries with low per capita demand that have a lot of upward demand potential as well.

But then offsetting that growth, Europe and Japan are gradually rolling over in oil demand from their historical peak levels, and the United States is likely next. However, those countries that are shrinking their oil demand for a while now, are generally increasing their natural gas demand or holding it steady. So, the overall oil and gas demand is pretty stable in developed countries and still rising in emerging countries.

Plus, one of the main ways that developed markets were able to reduce their domestic oil consumption was by exporting parts of their energy-intensive manufacturing supply chains to China and other emerging markets. A portion of China’s fossil fuel consumption “belongs” to developed markets in that sense, in terms of final consumption. The same is true for other export nations. It’s hard for a country to actually reduce its consumption, when all the moving parts are considered including the parts they exported out of their borders but still benefit from.

**The Global Energy Mix**

The general consensus is that global oil demand will peak in the 2020s or 2030s at roughly the current level, and then gradually or quickly roll over as the world shifts towards renewable energy.

Governments have various targets for carbon emissions, and so most oil majors have slides like this in their presentation decks that show the various possible scenarios regarding how quickly the world will move away from oil. Even with the most aggressive scenario of oil demand destruction, capex will be needed to bring new supply to market to offset current decline rates.

![Oil Demand Scenarios](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-lukoil-demand-scenario.png)

*Chart Source: [Lukoil April 2020 Investor Presentation](https://www.lukoil.com/InvestorAndShareholderCenter/ReportsAndPresentations/Presentations)*

What’s interesting is that if this big oil reduction target is achieved, it would be a global first. Historically, whenever humanity finds a new energy source, it doesn’t replace the previous one, but instead *adds* to it:

![Global Energy Mix](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-energy-mix.png)

*Chart Source: [Our World in Data](https://ourworldindata.org/energy-mix)*

Humanity burned biomass for energy, and then we added coal but didn’t stop burning biomass. Then we added oil, but coal stayed around as well, especially in emerging markets. India and China are still growing their coal usage each year. Then we added natural gas, but still use oil. Then we added nuclear power and hydroelectric, but we’re also still growing our natural gas usage as well. Hydroelectric, solar, and wind energy have been additive onto that as well.

In addition, each new primary energy source was more energy dense than the previous one, until recently. Fossil fuels are concentrated ancient biomass. As you go from wood to coal to oil to nuclear, you move up the spectrum of energy density. Hydroelectric power is also extremely energy dense when the right type of river is present.

Solar and wind, however, are less dense energy sources. If humanity successfully transitions to getting half or more of its energy from wind and solar, it would not only be a first in terms of displacing previous energy sources rather than adding to them, but would also be a first at successful moving the world to a less dense primary energy source.

Plus, oil and gas derivatives are used for all sorts of materials, including clothing, plastic, wrappers around wires, wind turbine blades, and all sorts of other things we don’t think about. Those use-cases would need to be replaced as well, with plant-based chemicals instead.

What I’m saying is that there’s implementation risk here in terms of global oil demand being able to roll over and fall off a cliff. I support renewable energy usage and carbon capture technologies. But I’m also watching what happens in the market.

Throughout the 2010s decade, tons of pensions and other funds invested into shale oil financing, and destroyed a lot of their capital as oil drillers kept focusing on growth rather than profitability and never became reliably free cash flow positive.

More recently, in addition to that history of losses, the proliferation of ESG mandates to address CO2 levels and other environmental concerns made many large pools of capital decide to stop investing in oil.

![Carbon Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-carbon-levels.png)

*Chart Source: [NASA](https://climate.nasa.gov/vital-signs/carbon-dioxide/)*

So with less external investment demand, oil producers are more focused on being free cash flow positive and self-sufficiently reinvesting into their businesses, rather than running on perpetual free cash flow destruction and relying on external capital to keep them going to grow as fast as possible.

Kind of like how the gold industry has recently shifted towards focusing on free cash flow generation more-so than fast growth, I think the oil market is headed in that direction too, out of necessity.

So, in order to meet even aggressive disruption scenarios with lower oil usage, more production has to come online to offset natural decline rates from existing wells, and this reinvesting will likely have more of a profit mandate in this cycle. Oil prices likely need to be at a level that justifies new capex and production.

In other words, I think there is a good probability that the 2008 oil price spike was not the last one that will ever occur. This current period of low prices and low capex has a decent shot of causing supply constraints and the next upswing in price levels as we head deeper into this decade.

![Oil Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-oil-longterm.png)

In the meantime, I like to focus on oil producers and transporters that are profitable at current oil price levels. Ones with low production costs and strong balance sheets. If oil plods along sideways or mildly up, and producers keep paying big dividends, then they have the potential to outperform the highly-valued S&P 500.

**Lukoil Update**

I can’t hold it in my M1 Finance portfolios because it trades over the counter, but I have a core position for that stock in the No Limits portfolio and my Fidelity/Schwab accounts. Lukoil is Russia’s second largest oil producer. They are not state-owned, they have a westernized business model, they have huge insider ownership, and they have outperformed all other oil and gas majors globally during the past 20-year, 10-year, 5-year, and 3-year periods.

Unlike many other oil majors, they haven’t done capital-destructive acquisitions at the top of a business cycle, or other things like that. They just produce oil and gas throughout various places in Eurasia at low production costs, along with refining and distribution businesses, and have the longest reserve life compared to other oil and gas majors at 20 years for current production levels.

In addition, their capital structure is great. As a larger company, Exxon Mobile has about 3x as much revenue and 2x as much net income as Lukoil in a good year. **Enterprise Products Partners Update**

In addition to producers, I like some oil, gas, and petrochemical transporters at current price levels.

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) has been one of my favorites. They transport oil, gas, natural gas liquids, and petrochemicals, including for exporting to emerging markets.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-5-9-epd-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

From the mid-1990s to the mid-2010s, pipeline infrastructure grew like wildfire across North America, and then slowed ever since 2015 when oil prices fell due to structural shale oil oversupply.

Pipeline businesses historically haven’t generated much free cashflow in official terms during that rapid expansion phase. A lot of their cash flows are offset by deprecation for tax purposes, and they kept raising capital and reinvesting it into more pipelines while paying distributions which made sense in that environment.

After 2015 with low oil prices and less growth, however, many pipelines transitioned towards being self-sufficient rather than relying on external capital, meaning that they grow more slowly, at lower valuations, with lower distributions and yet still-high distribution yields due to those lower valuations, and reinvest some of their capital into growth and sustainment projects as needed.

Pipelines that were less-indebted and better-managed navigated that transition more gracefully than ones that had too much debt, blew up, and cut distributions to preserve capital.

And so we see with EPD, historically one of the best-of-breed pipeline businesses that handled the transition well (without having to cut their distribution), they are becoming more consistently free cash flow positive. Here’s the free cash flow version of its F.A.S.T. Graph:

**Energy Sector Risks**

The energy sector has a lot of risks, which is why it is so cheap and out of favor. There are three categories of risk in particular to focus on, with one of those risks being cyclical and the other two being more structural.

*Global Growth Rates*

The cyclical risk with oil and gas stocks is that with such a fine balance between supply and demand, any downturn in the global economic growth rate can reduce oil and gas consumption, and thus reduce prices for a period of time.

Investors should make sure they understand the time horizon associated with their oil and gas investments.

*New Technology*

The energy sector of course depends on oil/gas prices and oil/gas volumes remaining robust. Earlier in this report I discussed the challenges of renewable energy sources quickly displacing oil and gas. However, we need to monitor the possibility of new technologies accelerating the shift faster than expected.

For example, if new types of solar cells are invented that are big game-changers, or if ultra-deep geothermal energy becomes economical in places that aren’t normally associated with geothermal energy availability, it could eat into oil and gas market share a decade earlier than whatever the base case is.

This risk of rapid technological growth above expectations can be mitigated by focusing on the oil and gas companies with low production costs and strong balance sheets. It can also be mitigated by holding a broad commodity basket, since many commodities including silver, copper, aluminum, and uranium are important for various alternative forms of energy besides oil and gas. Plus, some of the oil and gas majors, like Total (TOT) are gradually adding renewable energy divisions to their portfolio of operations.

*Taxes and Jurisdictional Risk*

Countries could choose to raise taxes on their oil and gas sector, or on all companies in their borders. For example, Russia increased taxes on oil companies in 2020, and the Biden Administration is seeking a moderate sector-wide US corporate tax increase.

I like to diversify my oil and gas exposure to companies across the United States, Canada, Europe, and Russia. This reduces jurisdictional risk.

## Final Thoughts

We’re in the heart of a strong commodity market, and the April CPI report (coming out next week) and the May CPI report (coming out in June) will have high year-over-year prints due to the combination of fiscal stimulus, supply chain issues, and low base effects from last year.

Some commodities like lumber are going parabolic in terms of price action, and are concerning for a highly-probable snap-back at some point. Other ones, like oil and gas, have heated up but still lag the broader basket of them.

Investors should make sure they understand the timeframes associated with their investments. I expect a longer-term positive environment for commodities, but there will be pullbacks along the way, and localized areas of excess.
