## Macro View

**Tariff Update**

In my prior report, I highlighted that with the stock market back at all-time highs, it’ll give the Trump Administration more incentive to dial up the tariff negotiations again:

> However, high equity valuations and new nominal highs in the S&P 500 give the Trump Administration more flexibility to press harder on tariff negotiations, which are ongoing. Those were the primary focus back during the springtime, temporarily took a backseat during this first half of summer, but now are likely to be back in focus as some deadlines are reached.
>
> ![SPY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-6-spy-fastgraph.png)
>
> **F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**
>
> * black line: the current and historical stock price
> * blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
> * orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
> * yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
> * dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers
>
> As a base case, I don’t expect tariff-related turmoil of a magnitude that was experienced in April of this year, but I do think it could pick up a bit relative to the past month or two.
>
> When the stock market is selling off and the bond market is experiencing high volatility, the administration has a strong incentive to put out the fire. However, when the stock market is pumping and the bond market is in a lower-volatility state, it gives the administration more ammo to test some of their pro-tariff negotiation tactics.
>
> -July 6, 2025 report

Starting in the few days right after that report, President Trump sent out a series of letters to many world leaders indicating that they’ll be subject to higher tariff rates starting on August 1st. The levels varied, but many were in the 20-50% range. Reed Smith LLP [maintains a tracker](https://www.tradecomplianceresourcehub.com/2025/07/17/trump-2-0-tariff-tracker/) of ongoing tariff numbers by country.

There is some noise out there regarding who is actually paying the tariffs. Are foreign exporters discounting their prices to absorb the cost? Are US consumers paying it? Are US businesses’ profit margins absorbing it?

Most early data suggest that US consumers are paying the bulk of it, followed by US businesses, followed by foreign exporters. It’s a polarized political environment which can complicate research, but we can cut through that with some quantitative analysis.

For starters, ChatGPT’s deep research mode and Grok both say, with citations, that US consumers and businesses are paying the bulk of tariffs, with minimal impact on foreign exporters:

![ChatGPT](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-chatgpt-ai.png)

![Grok](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-grok-ai.png)

Secondly, we can look through individual analyses. In general, I’ve found Bob Elliott (formerly of Bridgewater) to have been providing good charts on the subject.

![Bob Elliott Tweet 1](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-bob-elliott-tweet1.png)

![Bob Elliott Tweet 2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-bob-elliott-tweet2.png)

However, it’s a bit more complicated than that. As I’ve covered in earlier reports this year, US importers also front-ran a lot of tariffs:

![Trade Balance: Goods](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-trade-balance-goods.png)

The US is now collecting tens of billions of dollars in monthly tariffs, but tens of billions of extra inventory was already brought in ahead of that, which means that many businesses have some wiggle room to keep prices low before they have to raise them. Any sort of delay or lag like this always complicates analysis.

May and June saw a de-escalation in tariff policy after April market turmoil, so some of the really big tariff proposals that were ramped up in April aren’t yet implemented. But, after this July wave of tariff letters, there are now very big rates set to go into effect in August unless they are delayed or re-negotiated again.

![Justin Wolfer Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-justin-wolfers-tweet.png)

Roughly speaking, if the US puts 20% average tariffs on its $3.4 trillion worth of annual imports and leaves them there persistently, that’ll be $680 billion in annual tariff revenue. I would argue that the $200-$300 billion level that has been in place post-April is absorbable without major notice, but $600+ billion is far more likely to be impactful.

Of course, to some extent trade would reorient around these rates, and that’s already happening to a degree in May and June. Volumes would decrease in some areas, for instance. That dynamic aspect would likely reduce the annual tariffs revenue from the headline static calculation but show up in other areas.

In my view, the market is being too complacent about tariffs. It’s the #1 macro topic I’m monitoring currently, and there are a few key reasons for that:

-Large US fiscal deficits exist in large part due to political polarization. In general, Republicans push back on tax hikes and Defense cuts, and Democrats push back on entitlement cuts, leaving little to truly negotiate about on large budget items. However, President Trump is using emergency powers to enact his tariffs without legislation, which bypasses this baseline level of  polarization. Thus, big numbers can indeed happen quickly.

-Trump’s usage of emergency powers for tariffs are subject to ongoing court deliberation and appeal, which reintroduces headline impact (e.g. major court outcomes could notably move markets by taking away or reinforcing hundreds of billions of dollars worth of new tax revenue).

-The market seems to want to “look through” the upcoming August 1st tariffs. Market participants likely assume that if there are any disruptions to the economy or markets, the president will back off on tariff policy, and is likely using these high tariff letters as a hard negotiating tactic to work down from. However, that introduces a catch-22; if the market remains complacent and looks through any potential issues, then what incentive would the president have to back down from his preferred high-tariff position? In that sense, the market would be telling him to keep it up.

![Tariff Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-tariff-meme2.png)

Overall, I’m a bit more risk-averse than average over the next couple of months, or until there is more clarity on August tariffs. While I’m not making taxable trades in portfolios around that concern, and I’m generally quite heavily invested in assets across the board, I am building up some cash around the margins and being slow to deploy to new ideas, to top off my dry powder for the moment.

**US Business Cycle**

The Conference Board’s leading economic index is back in mild recession mode, although in general this period of fiscal dominance has made the historically-accurate indicator a source of false positives as of late:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-lei.png)

To cut through the noise of fiscal dominance with regards to the business cycle, I’m primarily watching the labor market. Payroll numbers can be revised in hindsight, whereas initial jobless claims represent an actual count of the number of people filing for unemployment each week. Until there’s a rise to around 300,000/week, it can largely be considered contained. So far, no problem:

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-initial-claims.png)

**Domestic Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity conditions continue to operate in the three-year band that policymakers have been optimizing within:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-domestic-liquidity.png)

Bank cash levels as a percentage of total assets remain above levels that have caused historical liquidity problems:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-bank-cash.png)

Despite the debt ceiling having been raised, the Treasury has thus far been slow to refill its cash balance back to baseline levels:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-tga.png)

In late July, the Treasury Department will release its funding plan for the next quarter. Since the economy is in fiscal dominance, these quarterly refunding announcements are generally as impactful or even more impactful than the Fed’s twice-quarterly FOMC meetings. A dovish (pro-liquidity) outcome would mean they announce a lot of T-bill issuance. A hawkish (negative-liquidity) outcome would mean they announce a lot of T-bond issuance. The influx of Tariff revenue could also temporarily reduce issuance requirements, which is another dovish (pro-liquidity) outcome.

The NY Fed’s 2024 annual report (published [here](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/markets/omo/omo2024-pdf.pdf) in 2025) restated the fact that within the next year, they plan to stop performing quantitative tightening and go back toward gradual balance sheet expansion. This would keep their portfolio holdings fixed to around 20% of GDP, which grows over time:

![NY Fed Projected SOMA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-projected-soma.png)

For that process, they expect to continue to let mortgage-backed securities mature off their balance sheet, while restarting the process of monetizing US treasury securities:

![NY Fed UST vs MBS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-ust-vs-mbs.png)

There’s nothing immediately concerning about liquidity on the near-term horizon. It will, however, be interesting to see how financial media responds to a period of resumed central bank balance sheet growth if consumer price inflation is still trending above the central bank’s 2% annual target. We’ll cross that bridge when we get to it.

**Altcoin Rally**

Most bitcoin bull markets are eventually accompanied by an “alt season”. During an alt season, bitcoin has generally already run up a lot in price, and capital starts flowing toward other parts of the crypto ecosystem to bid altcoin prices up, too.

This cycle has seen a rather strong bull market without much of an accompanying alt season. I think that’s pretty healthy, because other than stablecoins and some tokenized real-world assets, there’s not much sustainable value or utility to be found in the cryptocurrency industry. The vast majority of projects have one big pump cycle for insiders (founders and token VCs) to use retail traders as exit liquidity, and then the project never reaches a new high against bitcoin again.

Each cycle generally comes with a main narrative. In early cycles the narrative was alternative monies to bitcoin, such as litecoin or XRP. In 2017 it was initial coin offerings. In 2020 it was DeFi. In 2021 it was NFTs. Those narratives all eventually rolled over into smaller markets as bitcoin kept reaching higher highs over the years. The latest narrative was meme coins, meaning that the projects admit up front that they have no utility and are basically gambling.

If there’s an emerging narrative currently, I would say it’s probably about the renewed interest in stablecoins. With the passage of the GENIUS Act, the US has embraced certain regulated forms of stablecoins, and they were on a growth path anyway. Thus, some altcoin proponents point to their preferred coin’s current or future capability to serve as the tech rails for stablecoins as a reason to own them.

I’ve been bullish on the market cap of stablecoins since my original January [2021 piece on Ethereum](https://www.lynalden.com/ethereum-analysis/):

> Stablecoins are particularly important, in my view. I’m bullish on the amount of money locked up in stablecoins. It’s a space to watch, for both good developments and bad developments. The US Office of the Comptroller of the Currency [now officially permits US banks to use stablecoins](https://www.occ.treas.gov/news-issuances/news-releases/2021/nr-occ-2021-2.html). They’re a much more liquid form of fiat currency, and can have various implications for central bank digital currencies and the existing global monetary system.
>
> -January 2021

In January 2021 when I wrote that, the total USD-collateralized stablecoin supply was in the ballpark of $35 billion. Today it’s around $250 billion, representing a 7x increase. I expect the number will likely exceed $1 trillion in the not-too-distant future, and I would note that some of our portfolio companies at Ego Death Capital make use of stablecoins.

![Stablecoin Supply](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-stablecoin-supply.png)

However, it only takes tens or hundreds of billions of dollars worth of blockchain infrastructure to maintain trillions in stablecoin supply and/or volume. Stablecoins generally migrate to whatever chains are cheap with decent network effects, especially the stablecoins that are used for medium-of-exchange purposes or crossborder payments rather than DeFi.

As a point of comparison, the entire ETF industry collectively has about $12 trillion in assets under management, but the value of all major ETF businesses is a couple hundred billion dollars in market capitalization at most, and generates only tens of billions in annual revenue. That’s because their fees are highly competitive and trend toward zero. That’s part of what makes them so efficient and desirable in the first place.

Similarly, when people tell me they’re bullish on a given blockchain because stablecoins run on it, my response is to generally point out that the major blockchains where the $250 billion worth of stablecoins run are *already* larger in collective market cap than the collective market cap of all ETF companies that manage $12 trillion in AUM.

The past two months have seen a recovery in a number of altcoins. A particularly noteworthy development is the rise of Ethereum treasury companies, which are public equities that buy and hold ETH as a treasury asset, mirroring the type of activity that Strategy (MSTR) and others have been doing with bitcoin since 2020. There have also been announcements of companies buying other cryptocurrencies down lower on the market capitalization spectrum.

![Treasury Companies](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-treasury-companies.png)

As a result, there’s been a surge of altcoin price action:

![Meltem Demirors Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-meltem-demirors-tweet.png)

While I wouldn’t stand in the way of this altcoin trade off of its multi-year low point, I continue to view bitcoin as by far the most investable asset in the ecosystem over the long run.

## Latin American Equity Updates

Latin America remains an interesting region for value opportunities. It’s under-owned, inexpensive, and with many signs of stabilization.

**Itau Unibanco: Tariff Resistant**

Brazil’s largest bank stock dipped a bit. That’s a dip I’m happy to keep accumulating:

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-itub-fastgraph.png)

Brazil’s government received a letter from President Trump telling them that they’ll be subject to 50% tariffs, which is on the punitive high end of the recent tariff letters. The reason stated in the letter is that Trump does not like how Brazil is handling its internal politics and legality around their former President Bolsonaro.

![Trump Brazil Tariff Letter](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-trump-brazil-tariff-letter.jpg)

A challenge here is that the US already has a trade surplus with Brazil rather than a deficit with them, and referencing internal politics of a country argues counter to the president’s justification of emergency tariff powers which could end up circling back in court. Nonetheless, Brazil’s market has shown some concern for the tariffs, and Brazil’s President Lula has vowed to combat the US tariffs with reciprocal tariffs of their own if they go into effect in August, which could then result in higher US tariffs according to the letter.

However, it’s worth pointing out that Brazil has a $2.2 trillion GDP economy, and they export about $41 billion annually to the US:

![Brazil Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-brazil-exports.png)

Brazil’s exports are concentrated primarily in commodities, including agricultural commodities, and there is no shortage of global demand for them. Thus, the small share of GDP that those exports represent, and the ability to redirect those exports elsewhere, should keep the overall effects of tariffs minimal in terms of the national scale, even if they do disrupt some individual exporters. Thus, nothing about the latest round of tariff threats derails my bullish view on Brazilian financials.

**Copa Airlines: Cheap and Well-Run**

Airlines in general are having a bit of a resurgence following their disastrous 2020-2021 period.

Founded in 1947, Panama’s Copa Holdings (NYSE: CPA) is one of the smaller, undervalued, and interesting airline companies in my view. They have a single-digit price/earnings ratio, a hefty dividend, and decent growth:

![CPA FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-cpa-fastgraph.png)

Located in Panama, which is right between North America and South America, they have a great geographic position as a travel hub.

![Americas Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-americas-map.png)

They have over $900 million in cash equivalents, they have total net debt equal to about one year’s worth of net income, and they operate with a solid balance sheet broadly.

![CPA Assets and Liabilities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-cpa-assets-liabilities.png)

One of Copa’s smart strategies is that all 106 airplanes in their fleet are the same type: Boeing 737. They have some different versions/ages of the 737 family, but every single aircraft is a 737.

The Boeing 737 is the most common commercial aircraft globally. It’s ideal for short-to-medium range trips, which are the types of trips that Copa operates. Other larger types of aircraft are generally used for cross-ocean trips, which Copa doesn’t do. And they don’t use a mix of Boeing and Airbus.

By specializing in short-to-medium trips with Boeing 737s, they are able to unify their maintenance and pilot training all under one type and keep costs lower than competitors. Although it’s funny to apply a Steve Jobs quote to an airline, it’s relevant here:

![Jobs Quote](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-jobs-quote.jpg)

Even though I used to work in the aviation simulation industry (including working a lot with Boeing 737 avionics), I have historically not been a fan of investing in airline stocks. When I’m bullish on the industry, I generally buy airports or travel agencies (e.g. OMAB and BKNG) which have stronger economic moats and less cyclicality. And one tragic accident or other negative headline can severely disrupt a small airline.

So, to the extent that I’m interested in Copa, it would only be with a small position. A scattering of small individual uncorrelated bets are risky on their own, but within the context of a diversified portfolio, can reduce overall risk.

## Final Thoughts: Precious Metals

Kind of like how altcoins have been playing a bit of speculative catch-up with bitcoin over the past couple of months, alternative precious metals like silver, platinum, and palladium have been playing catch-up with gold.

Gold has been a stand-out multi-year performer, but it became rather overheated in April, and I began cautioning about a potential correction or consolidation:

> When looking out years, I remain structurally bullish on gold. And I think it can outperform the S&P 500 with a 3-5 year view. However, at current levels I think gold’s risks are increasing for a price consolidation that could last upwards of 6-12 months.
>
> On the monthly chart, it’s nearly as technically overbought as it’s ever been:
>
> ![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gold-chart.png)
>
> While market turmoil and trade debates are likely to persist for a while longer, I think the market already reached “peak uncertainty” during the opening volley of the tariff escalations. With the stock market, bond market, and dollar index going down together in early April, the current administration has shown some degree of concern and de-escalation.
>
> I don’t plan to sell gold, and indeed plan to keep it as a slice in my portfolios as a “hold” rather than a “buy”. In terms of setting expectations and portfolio allocations, I’d advise caution about chasing it at current levels.
>
> -April 27, 2025 premium report

Indeed, gold has been consolidating for three months now, since April:

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-gold.png)

Back in May, gold was the best year-to-date performer among the precious metals, but since then, the others all leapfrogged it:

![Precious Metals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-7-20-precious-metals.png)

While I view the altcoin rally as inevitable but not particularly healthy (since I think most of them are overvalued), I view a broader precious metals rally as both inevitable and healthy, so it’s nice to see some activity there.

I continue to be long-term bullish on precious metals as part of a diversified portfolio.
