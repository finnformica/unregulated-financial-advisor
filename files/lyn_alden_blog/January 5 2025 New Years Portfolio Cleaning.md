# New Year’s Portfolio Cleaning

*January 5, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report covers liquidity conditions, the U.S. business cycle, and headwinds for 2025.

The digital asset section covers bitcoin price action and what’s going on under the hood.

The investment analysis section takes a look at a couple healthcare stocks to see what value traps we may be able to sweep away from the model portfolios, and then finishes with an update on gold.

## Macro View

**Domestic Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic bank cash levels have trended sideways, and are about $100-$200 billion above the level where the March 2023 regional bank crisis occurred:

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-bank-cash.png)

In terms of napkin math, the Fed took out a little over $3 trillion in liquidity since they started reducing their balance sheet in mid-2022. Meanwhile, there was over $2 trillion stored in the reverse repo facility, and that began coming out and *back into banks* in mid-2023, which offset a lot of that balance sheet reduction, result in a $1 trillion net reduction from over-filled levels. Along with a small handful of other items, this means that commercial bank cash levels peaked at a little over $4 trillion in late 2021 and are down to a little over $3 trillion currently.

If we look at bank cash levels as a percentage of assets or as a percentage of deposits, the numbers aren’t quite as low as March 2023 but are getting there. And they’re still above 2019 levels:

![Bank Cash Percent](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-bank-cash-percent.png)

Meanwhile, there have been some noises happening in the liquidity-related financial plumbing, similar to what was occurring in 2019 prior to the repo spike.

During the end of each fiscal quarter, especially the end of the fourth quarter, there is a lot of “window dressing” that occurs where financial institutions want to lock in good numbers for the reporting period. Entities with very liquid balance sheets can provide liquidity to other entities, but the available system-wide liquidity is getting tight lately.

During the end of Q3 and just recently during the end of Q4, the secured overnight financing rate (SOFR) spiked well above the rate of interest on reserve balances (IORB), which should generally not happen and indicates a tightness in balance sheet space within the financial system:

![SOFR vs IORB](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-sofr-iorb.png)

And during the end of Q3 there was a brief $2.35 billion usage of the Fed’s standing repo facility. This facility stands ready to provide liquidity but is rather stigmatized, so various financial entities would rather pay higher rates elsewhere (e.g. SOFR) than use it. It seemed likely that we’d see another brief usage of the repo facility at the end of Q4, but that didn’t materialize. It seems entities *did* find enough private sector balance sheet space to make use of, as long as they were willing to pay a premium to find it:

![Repo Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-repo.png)

Usage of the Fed’s reverse repo facility also tends to increase at each quarter-end period. This facility isn’t stigmatized; it’s where financial institutions can temporarily store *excess* liquidity. On December 20th, as little as $98 billion of the Fed’s reverse repo facility was used, but by December 31st it briefly spiked up to $473 billion. It’s back down to $237 billion now, and in general we should expect it to inch down over the course of this quarter:

![Reverse Repo Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-rrp.png)

Overall, this indicates that the Fed will likely end its balance sheet reduction in 2025, but there are no signs that they’ll have to do it imminently. We should probably look to around mid-year as the likely timeframe for that, but there are a number of variables that can affect the precise timing. For example, if a debt ceiling fight emerges later this year, it could result in the Treasury General Account draining its liquidity back into the financial system, which is temporarily (and unintuitively) *good* for bank liquidity when it happens.

**Global Liquidity Indicators**

The dollar index has been strengthening against other major currencies since the election. The strengthening paused in November, but then resumed in December, and this second leg has seemingly put more strain on asset prices:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-dxy.png)

The S&P 500 peaked in early December and has been in correction mode since then. Gold and bitcoin have both been in correction too. Basically, the dollar is the most liquid global unit of account and the one that most assets are traded against, and so when it hardens, it puts stress on other asset prices more often than not.

Eventually, a strong enough dollar index would cause a liquidity strain in the U.S. financial system, and be alleviated with Treasury or Fed action (such as the end of quantitative tightening by the Fed or something like a Mar-a-Lago exchange rate accord by the Treasury). However, until that time we should expect global liquidity to remain a bit strained.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator suggests 2.4% annual real GPD growth for the quarter that just ended (but was not yet reported), which is recently down but still solid:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-gdpnow.png)

The Conference Board’s Leading Economic Index had been signaling recession for the past two years, but is no longer signaling it:

![Leading Economic Indicator](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-lei.png)

Many indicators show that recession-like aspects materialized in the U.S. economy during the past two years, but that large fiscal forces overrode them, which is why the ongoing theme of fiscal dominance is so important to track. For example, this was the first period where more than 40% of banks were tightening credit to businesses without an NBER-defined recession having occurred:

![SLOOS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-sloos.png)

Many business cycles are characterized by identifying where credit is flowing in the economy. However, in this fiscally dominant environment, it’s more about identifying where fiscal deficits are going, since that’s now a bigger force than bank lending. Individuals and businesses on the receiving side of fiscal deficits are in aggregate doing pretty well, while individuals and businesses that are not on the receiving side are generally struggling.

2025 is set up to be riskier than normal for real GDP growth and stock market performance, due to the larger-than-average uncertainty around government policies during that timeframe.

-To what extent will high import tariffs be enacted in practice or merely used as a negotiation tool?

-To what extent will House fiscal conservatives stand off against President Trump on the debt ceiling?

-To what extent will the Treasury Department consider an intentional dollar devaluation to improve its trade terms (i.e. a Mar-a-Lago accord)?

Given that the presidency and both houses of Congress are in on the same party, we should expect some rapid actions within the first year, before midterm election season ramps up. During Trump’s first term, that was mostly stimulative (e.g. the anticipation for tax cuts was of a larger scale than the concern around tariffs), whereas in this second term it might be more of a mixed bag (e.g. the anticipation for tax cuts might be fully balanced out by the concern around harder-line tariffs). These all remain to be seen but are the types of things I’m monitoring in 2025.

In addition, state-level spending is [set to be a bit more austere](https://www.pewtrusts.org/en/research-and-analysis/articles/2024/07/15/state-budgets-are-downsizing) in 2025.

In general, I have a more cautious stance on 2025 U.S. equity returns than I had for 2024. It’s not that I expect a massive crash or deflationary bust or anything like that as a base case, but I expect a lot more choppiness, corrections, and heightened risk of stagflation in rate of change terms (i.e. slower real GDP growth combined with sticky inflation and less-stellar asset price returns).

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

At the start of November, Bitcoin was about $69k, and by November 22nd it surged all the way to about $99k. It then corrected for a couple weeks, then spiked over $100k, and by mid-December had touched as high as $108k before correcting again.

Currently the price sits close to where it was on November 22nd, and overall we can say that the price has been consolidating since that time.

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-btc-chart.png)

In my November 24th report, I discussed how one of my favorite valuation measures, the MVRV Z-Score, had reached levels that started opening up the significant possibility (but not guarantee) of a consolidation:

> Bitcoin broke to fresh new highs since the prior report, and has at times reached over $99k:
>
> ![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-bitcoin-mvrv.png)
>
> So far, this rally looks moderate; none of the major on-chain indicators that I track are showing signs of major cycle-top sentiment extremes yet. A lot of the indicators currently look similar to March 2024, which resulted in a 7-month consolidation. However, in prior cycles, indicators like this went on to break to much more extreme levels before finding a multi-year top. I have a positive view on bitcoin price over the next year, but no particular view on the path or timing it follows along that way.
>
> And I would remind readers that bitcoin can have rather violent corrections during bull runs, and so I would caution against excessive leverage or position sizes that are above your volatility tolerance.
>
> -November 24th report

In that report, I also used the opportunity to trim another part of the MicroStrategy (MSTR) position from the No Limits portfolio at around $400/share and 3x its net asset value. Both the price and public sentiment around it had gone parabolic:

![MSTR Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-mstr.png)

At the current time, we’re in a month-and-a-half bitcoin price consolidation, with initial valuation metrics for that consolidation that look similar to the March 2024 period that was followed by a 7-month consolidation. That of course doesn’t mean this consolidation has to follow the same timetable. It just means it was starting from a similar situation by this particular high signal metric.

Here’s the good news. On November 22nd, the bitcoin market capitalization or “market value” was $1.95 trillion. The on-chain cost basis, or “realized value” was $718 billion. Since that time, the market capitalization has gone sideways. However, the on-chain cost basis has risen to $815 billion, which is a massive $97 billion or 13% addition to its cost basis during this month-and-a-half period. This is similar to what happened over months during that post-March consolidation. In other words, coins are changing hands at these price levels and gradually raising the average cost basis, helping to make this price range more “real” and gradually ratchet up the effective floor price. I discussed this type of cycle analysis in my [December 2024 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/december-2024-newsletter/).

As a result, I view this period as a healthy consolidation similar to the post-March consolidation. It may seem like nothing is happening, but under the surface the market is digesting this new price range. While anything can happen, I see fairly low risk of a multi-year correction ahead, and instead see it as another mid-cycle correction, which can build a new base to launch higher in price from.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

![Bitcoin MVRV Zoomed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-bitcoin-mvrv-zoomed.png)

I’ll continue to monitor this as we progress, from numerous different perspectives and metrics.

## New Year’s Portfolio Cleaning

Every once in a while, it’s useful to go through a portfolio and clean out any value traps that might exist there. Or at least develop an updated gameplan for some of the troubled holdings.

Sometimes when a stock price falls, its fundamentals are doing fine, and it’s time to double-down on it. However, it’s important not to just kneejerk double-down on anything that goes down in price. Sometimes a stock falls both in terms of price and in terms of fundamentals, and needs to be let go.

In an article called “[Most Investments Are Actually Bad. Here’s Why.](https://www.lynalden.com/most-investments-are-bad/)“, I explored the math behind how concentrated the performance of the stock market has historically been. As described in that piece, there was a study done on 26,000 stocks that covered 1929 through 2019, and they found that 4% of stocks accounted for virtually all of the stock market’s aggregate outperformance over T-bills. And just 86 stocks, or a little over 0.3% of them, accounted for half of that outperformance.

If we look through the stocks in the various model portfolios, as we would expect there’s a broad range of performance. The majority of them grind up and to the right over time, since we mostly stick to large wide-moat companies. Some of them are major outperformers, and some of them are disappointing underperformers.

At the top of the list is MicroStrategy (MSTR) which I’ve had in the Newsletter portfolio since they adopted their bitcoin strategy in August 2020, and have it in the No Limits portfolio as well. That’s actually the best-performing large/mid stock during that period, beating even Nvidia (NVDA). And we generally trimmed it at opportune times to take out some of the concentration while letting the rest of it run.

![MSTR and NVDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-mstr-nvda.png)

Beneath MicroStrategy, many of the growth names have done well. Booking Holdings (BKNG), for example, keeps grinding higher and is now bouncing around $5k/share. A lot of other names that don’t get a lot of publicity, like Fiserv (FI), American Express (AXP), and Traveler’s Companies (TRV) quietly soared higher in 2024.

Near the bottom of the list lately have been oilfield service and drilling stocks (SLB, HAL, VAL) as well as healthcare names (CVS, CI, BMY).

In my [November 24th report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2024-11-24/), I provided an update on oilfield services and drilling companies, and why I’m not very bearish on them despite near-term headwinds. I view them as a case where the stock price has gone down but the fundamentals are okay.

The healthcare sector has been more of a mixed bag, with some stocks selling off more than their fundamentals suggest they should, and others experiencing deteriorating fundamentals. So, I’ll use this report to highlight two of the most problematic names and what I’m planning to do with them.

**Bristol-Myers Squibb**

Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMY) is undergoing a portfolio transformation. Some of their older cash-cow drugs are coming off patent protection, while some of their newer drugs are coming online and growing rapidly. The hard part is predicting how quickly those old drugs’ cash flows will decline compared to how quickly those newer drugs’ cash flows will ramp up.

![BMY Portfolio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-bmy-growth-legacy.png)

The market has been pretty bearish on that expectation, paying less than 8x free cash flow for the company. I’ve had the view that this view leans too bearish, and that it’s a good value stock.

![BMY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-bmy-fastgraph-fcf.png)

Fundamentals have held up okay, but have undershot prior expectations. I view this recent bounce in price as a reasonable time to exit the position, not because I expect them to perform poorly per se, but because at the current time I don’t have a high conviction on their performance either way.

I don’t see a strong positive catalyst over the next two years, unless they meaningfully outperform analyst expectations, which I don’t have a strong way to judge the probability of. And so, I see this as a useful time to cut the loss and reinvest in opportunities elsewhere.

In the Newsletter portfolio, BMY is 3% of the dividend stock pie, which is itself 27% of the total portfolio, and so BMY represents 0.81% of the portfolio. This will be sold and dispersed into other stocks.

If we look at Bank OZK (OZK) for example, we can see that they’ve had similar stock price headwinds (although they’re about flat since we entered in mid-2024), but their fundamentals have held up well enough. They might face some rocky volatility in mid-January when they report their latest earnings, but overall I continue to be more positive on that bank than consensus expectations, since I view their type of lending as being unfairly categorized the same way as other types of commercial real estate lending. I went into detail on Bank OZK in my [May 12th report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2024-5-12/).

![OZK FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-ozk-fastgraph.png)

![OZK YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-ozk-ychart.png)

**CVS Health**

CVS Health (CVS) has been quite a loser lately. Luckily we’ve completely avoided Walgreens (WBA) which has done even worse, but CVS just keeps breaking down fundamentally and in terms of price.

![CVS FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-cvs-fastgraph.png)

In general, the pharmacy benefits management industry that CVS and a few other companies engage in is under challenge, and rightfully so. The health insurance industry has also been facing some post-pandemic headwinds, although I expect those to be more temporary.

In addition, I’m no longer particularly compelled by the company’s acquisition strategy. They performed some major acquisitions, and then entered a period of debt consolidation where they digested those acquisitions, focused on debt reduction, and were positioned as one of the most diversified and vertically integrated healthcare companies around. It was during that phase where I became bullish on them. But then they opened up their wallet and started to do more acquisitions, and took on more net debt even in an environment of higher interest rates.

![CVS Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-cvs-debt.png)

I do expect the company’s income to stabilize in 2025, and I think some of the aggressive stock selling recently is related to tax loss harvesting. And so, I’m not strongly inclined to eliminate the position at this specific time, and will hold for the moment. They’ve replaced their CEO as well.

However, CVS is firmly in the “show me” camp for 2025, and my long term view on its prospects has diminished. They need to demonstrate stabilization both in terms of fundamentals and stock price to indicate that they’re not a value trap for the time being, and even then will still be viewed with scrutiny in the portfolios and are a potential candidate for trimming.

CVS represents 0.81% of the Newsletter portfolio.

## Final Thoughts: Gold Update

Several of my reports throughout September and October expressed some concern that gold was overbought and the dollar index was oversold. This is despite the fact that I was (and am) long-term bullish on gold.

September 1st:

> I’ve been bullish on gold for a while, although it is beginning to show signs of tactical overbought status. These levels of relative strength often lead to a price consolidation:
>
> ![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-gold.png)
>
> During gold’s bull run in the 2000s decade, it reached overbought status a number of times, would consolidate a bit, and then continue to keep pushing up higher. That’s how most big bull runs work, and for example that’s what the S&P 500 has done throughout the 2010s decade and half way into the 2020s here as well.
>
> I don’t see any signs that gold is in bubble territory like it was in 2011, so I plan to remain long gold as part of a portfolio. We could push a bit higher and then consolidate for a time, or consolidate directly from these levels, but either way I remain structurally bullish as the U.S. Federal Reserve begins a rate-cutting cycle, as U.S. fiscal deficits continue to run at about 7% of GDP structurally, and as global central banks diversify their holdings around the margins including substantially into gold as an allocation.

September 29th:

> With gold’s relative strength getting close to 80 on the monthly chart, and the dollar index near the bottom of its recent range, I do have some concerns again for a period of potential consolidation in the price of gold. I don’t expect it to have a 3+ year consolidation like it did after August 2020, but I just wouldn’t be surprised to see it cool off a bit. As a bull who is long gold, I hope that I’m incorrect and that it just keeps grinding higher.
>
> I don’t really plan to trade around the position unless it reaches true extremes; I structurally prefer gold over long-duration Treasuries with a 5+ year view.
>
> Meanwhile, bitcoin has been consolidating since March of this year. So I like that mix of bitcoin and gold, because the two assets often undergo price pumps and price consolidations at different times.

October 13th:

> In my prior report and a couple other reports, I’ve been a expressing a view that gold over $2,600 is looking a bit overbought, and the dollar index under 101 is looking a bit oversold, in the near term. This is despite the fact that I’m long-term bullish on gold, and think we could have more of a weaker dollar cycle ahead.
>
> I’m not trading around those near-term views, but I express them to manage expectations.
>
> Within the past two weeks, gold went down slightly, and the dollar index jumped from a bit under 101 to a bit under 103, which released some of that overbought/oversold condition. I’m not surprised to see some consolidation moves like this, and I think it’s healthy for the market to digest some of its strong prior moves. My base case is to expect a bit more consolidation before the moves continue onward.
>
> ![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-gold-chart.png)
>
> ![USD Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-13-usd-chart.png)

November 10th:

> Gold finally had a bit of a pullback:
>
> ![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-gold.png)
>
> I’ve been in the camp that’s bullish on gold but a bit worried about it being overbought and expecting some pullbacks as a base case, but not really trading around that view.
>
> With a renewed surge in the dollar and long-end rates, it seems we finally have some headwinds against gold. It doesn’t change my long-term bullish thesis, and if anything I’d like to see it chop around for a bit and release some of its overbought momentum and sentiment so that it can continue to climb a “wall of worry” over the longer-term.

December 8th:

> Gold and bitcoin have been going through inverse patterns lately.
>
> Bitcoin reached new highs earlier this year in March 2024, and then consolidated sideways until November 2024, which healthily released a lot of the ETF-launch enthusiasm.
>
> Gold on the other hand broke out in March 2024, and steadily powered upward until October 2024, and lately it has been consolidating. Much like the bitcoin consolidation, I think it’s healthy for gold to consolidate a bit here and let people stop talking about it for a moment.

> ![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-gold.png)
>
> And as gold has been consolidating, bitcoin broke out to the upside in November, jumping from about $70k to over $100k in a span of a month. It’s nice owning some of both assets, in my view.
>
> I don’t have a firm view on what gold will do in the next 3-6 months, but I continue to have a long-term constructive view on it relative to bonds and cash.

As an update, the gold price has been in a correction for over two months now since peaking the day before Halloween. But that price correction put the metal back to prices it was in mid-September, so we’re approaching four months of consolidation in that sense.

![Gold Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-gold-longterm.png)

![Gold 1 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-gold-1yr.png)

The funny thing, though, is that gold has outperformed long duration Treasuries during this consolidation. If we measure it from the absolute gold peak on October 30th, then gold is down mildly vs Treasuries, but if we measure it from mid-September when it became noticeably overbought, then gold has outperformed Treasuries, since Treasuries ran into even bigger headwinds than gold.

![Gold vs 10 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-5-gld-vs-ief.png)

Basically, the strong dollar index (101 in mid-September when I warned about it being oversold vs up to 109 now) and the rising long-duration Treasury yields (3.7% in mid-September vs 4.6% now for the 10-year Treasury) have been substantial headwinds against gold, which trades in many ways like a competing currency.

Gold has no yield but has much slower supply growth than dollars and other currencies, and so when the dollar hardens significantly like this, it’s often pretty rough for gold temporarily. This gold consolidation continues to be healthy in my view, and was expected.

Investors have to be careful about listening to overly bullish cheerleaders of assets like gold and bitcoin. While I’m very bullish both for the long run, I do try to emphasize periods of overbought sentiment and price action when they occur, not necessarily because I strongly trade around those moments, but rather because I want to set realistic baseline expectations for myself and my readers.

So when gold is touching overbought levels that it has only reached five times in my lifetime (based on monthly RSI), I make sure to bring attention to it when few others are, because the probability of at least a multi-month consolidation is pretty high when that happens, and even the risk of a consolidation lasting a year or more is not insignificant. Gold is still working through that.

I don’t view gold as being in a bubble (i.e. it’s not like 2011), but from a trading perspective it got a bit rich for a period of time, and now the market is focused on tighter liquidity, uncertainty around tariff policy, and other such matters. I expect gold to positively climb that wall of worry, but it will take some time first to stabilize (which is already arguably happening), then to take out its prior highs, and then to surge to new levels of price discovery.
