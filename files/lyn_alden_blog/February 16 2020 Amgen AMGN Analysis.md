# Amgen Deep-Dive Analysis

*Published February 16, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report provides an update on the U.S. business cycle, and then dives into a healthcare stock, Amgen, that has been on our buy list for a while and still looks attractive at current levels.

## U.S. Business Cycle Status

The United States economy continues to exist at an inflection point, close to stall speed, but still growing, and still looking for a potential rebound towards faster growth.

**Problematic Job Openings**

The latest release for the number of job openings was concerning. There has been a sudden two-month downtrend in the number of job openings, although the absolute number of job openings remains at a high level.

![Job Openings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-job-openings.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/JTSJOL)*

If we look at that chart in year-over-year percent change terms, and overlay it with year-over-year real GDP growth, it doesn’t look great:

![Job Openings YoY and GDP Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-job-openings-gdp.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/graph/?g=qa7c)*

Lastly, if we overlay job openings with the broad U.S. stock market level, we get a really fascinating chart that has a complete divergence at the end:

![Job Openings vs Stock Market](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-job-openings-stock-market-divergence.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

On Twitter, I’ve jokingly dubbed this above chart the “Bull/Bear Rorschach Test”. Market bears will look at it and see that job openings are reflecting the actual economy, while the stock market is in some oblivious la la land, about to get a rude awakening and come crashing down to reality. Market bulls, on the other hand, may notice that from previous low points, the market generally leads job openings, and the market may simply be leading and pricing in an expected rebound. In addition, it’s worth re-emphasizing that even after this decline, the job openings figure is still high in absolute terms.

One way to bridge the gap between the two viewpoints is to point out that most of the recent stock market gains have been in growthy mega-cap stocks with strong balance sheets in attractive industries (e.g. cloud computing, software, or payment processing) like Microsoft, Amazon, Mastercard, Adobe, and so forth. With emerging markets, small cap stocks, and commodity producers under pressure (classic riskier or more cyclical plays), the fact that the market is flooding money into mega caps is probably more of a defensive move than a big risk-on move.

However, with the valuations that many of those stocks are reaching, it may not be as defensive as some hope. It’s important to have realistic expectations.

**The Dollar Anchor**

With the dollar currently in a strengthening phase since early January, it will pressure global growth and S&P 500 earnings (since they receive over 40% of their revenue from international sources) until it reverses to the downside. For this and other reasons (mainly the virus), consensus analyst expectations for 2020 S&P 500 earnings have been declining recently.

For a while, the Federal Reserve was performing quantitative tightening, meaning they were reducing the size of the balance sheet, and the dollar had an upward trend during that time compared to a basket of foreign currencies. Tightening monetary conditions reduces dollar liquidity, and all else being equal, is dollar-positive.

Starting in September 2019, however, the Fed suddenly reversed course due to the spike in overnight lending rates, and began expanding their balance sheet. In particular, they added a bunch of liquidity into year-end to make sure there was no liquidity shortage during that time. This balance sheet expansion quickly broke the dollar bullish trend, and shifted it down. This extra liquidity meant that the supply of dollars was more abundant, which was dollar-negative, and was great for emerging markets.

From the beginning of January 2020, after the year-end flood of liquidity, the balance sheet has been in a sideways trend, and the dollar suddenly began strengthening again. Global liquidity is tightening.

I marked this on the chart below. The red line is the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet, the blue line is the trade weighted dollar index, and the black vertical line marks the beginning of January 2020, which starts the recent trend change where the balance sheet turned flat and the dollar turned up:

![Dollar vs Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-dollar-balance-sheet-correlation.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/graph/?g=qa7f)*

The Federal Reserve continues to buy Treasury bills (additive to balance sheet), offset by amortizing mortgage-backed securities and slowing down repurchase agreements (subtractive to the balance sheet). Within a month or two, as repurchase agreements reach a steady state while Treasury bills continue to be added, I expect the balance sheet to likely resume its upward trend. Until it does, liquidity will be tight and the dollar can be relatively strong, and this puts pressure on global and U.S. growth.

## Amgen Inc. (AMGN) Deep Dive Analysis

We have another healthcare stock this week, with Amgen for the deep dive analysis. Amgen (AMGN) is one of the largest biotechnology companies, and here is their overview from their 10-k:

> Amgen Inc. (including its subsidiaries, referred to as “Amgen,” “the Company,” “we,” “our” or “us”) is committed to unlocking the potential of biology for patients suffering from serious illnesses by discovering, developing, manufacturing and delivering innovative human therapeutics. This approach begins by using tools like advanced human genetics to unravel the complexities of disease and understand the fundamentals of human biology. Amgen focuses on areas of high unmet medical need and leverages its expertise to strive for solutions that improve health outcomes and dramatically improve people’s lives. A biotechnology pioneer, Amgen has grown to be one of the world’s leading independent biotechnology companies, has reached millions of patients around the world and is developing a pipeline of medicines with breakaway potential. Amgen was incorporated in California in 1980 and became a Delaware corporation in 1987. We have a presence in approximately 100 countries worldwide. Amgen operates in one business segment: human therapeutics.

The company is not solely dependent on the success of any one product, but 78% of their sales come from their top 8 products, and 50% of their sales come from their top 3 products:

![Amgen Product Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-amgen-product-sales.png)

*Chart Source: Amgen 2019 10-K Form*

The company has shown continued pipeline strength and revenue diversification over the past few years, but its top 3 products are very important for the company. Here is their description from the company’s 10-K:

> Enbrel® (etanercept)  
> We market ENBREL, a tumor necrosis factor blocker, primarily in the United States. ENBREL was launched in 1998 and is used primarily in indications for the treatment of adult patients with moderately to severely active rheumatoid arthritis, patients with chronic moderate-to-severe plaque psoriasis who are candidates for systemic therapy or phototherapy and patients with active psoriatic arthritis.
>
> Neulasta® (pegfilgrastim)  
> We market Neulasta®, a pegylated protein based on the filgrastim molecule, primarily in the United States and Europe. Neulasta® was launched in 2002 and is used primarily in the indication to help reduce the chance of infection due to a low white blood cell count in patients with certain types of cancer (nonmyeloid) who receive anticancer medicines (chemotherapy) that can cause fever and a low blood cell count. In 2015, the Neulasta® Onpro® kit became available in the United States. The Neulasta® Onpro® kit provides physicians the opportunity to initiate the administration of Neulasta® on the same day as chemotherapy, with drug delivery of the recommended dose of Neulasta® at home the day after chemotherapy, thereby saving patients a trip back to the doctor.
>
> Prolia® (denosumab)  
> We market Prolia® primarily in the United States and Europe. Prolia® contains the same active ingredient as XGEVA® (denosumab) but is approved for different indications, patient populations, doses and frequencies of administration. Prolia® was launched in the United States and Europe in 2010. In the United States, it is used primarily in the indication for the treatment of postmenopausal women with osteoporosis at high risk of fracture, defined as a history of osteoporotic fracture, or multiple risk factors for fracture; or patients who have failed or are intolerant to other available osteoporosis therapy. In Europe, Prolia® is used primarily for the treatment of osteoporosis in postmenopausal women at increased risk of fracture.

Regarding geographic diversification, 74% of Amgen’s revenue comes from the United States, which is down from 79% two years ago.

The company grows from a combination of internal research and external acquisitions. The company spends about $4 billion per year on research and development. In most years, there are no sizable acquisition costs, but in late 2019, the company closed a $13.4 billion acquisition of the anti-inflammatory drug Otezla, from Celgene. This drug will further diversify Amgen’s top drug lineup and product sales in 2020 and beyond, and seems to be prudent from a financial perspective.

**Management and Performance**

As it grew from a mid-cap to a large-cap, Amgen soundly outperformed the S&P 500:

![Amgen Historical Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-amgn-performance.png)

Investor expectations should be more muted going forward, as the company is now a larger, safer, dividend growth stock rather than a high-growth mid-cap company. I expect solid long-term risk-adjusted returns as my base case, but there are of course risks to that outcome.

Like many of the most successful healthcare companies, Amgen has strong free cash flow generation, high returns on invested capital, and sizable profit margins. The key focus of the company, therefore, is on keeping this engine growing, ideally with increasing levels of product diversification to reduce risk.

The company has a wide economic moat due to patents and strong R&D operations, but must renew itself over time or face stagnation, reduced market share, and eventually revenue and earnings declines.

The company has consistently demonstrated strong returns on invested capital, returns on equity, and returns on assets:

![Amgen ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-amgn-roic.png)

This chart is very instructive for why I focus heavily on returns on invested capital (ROIC) more-so than returns on equity (ROE). ROIC measures profitability against both equity and debt funding, while ROE only measures returns against equity funding, which means ROE metrics can be boosted heavily simply by taking on more debt leverage (i.e. shifting the funding structure away from equity).

Amgen’s ROIC levels in the mid-to-high teens are very strong. The year 2017 saw a dip in GAAP income for accounting purposes, but free cash flow remained strong in that year, so it wasn’t an issue fundamentally.

Until 2017, the company had an enormous pile of cash, over $40 billion, giving them an unusually strong balance sheet, but arguably wasting the time value of that capital. In 2018 and 2019, they put most of that cash to work, buying back about 10% of the company’s shares in each of those two years (at sensible valuations), and buying the rights to Otezla for $13.4 billion to grow and diversify their product mix. Currently, they have about $9 billion in cash. Now that they’ve cleared out their excess cash, their returns on equity are way higher, while their more important metric of returns on invested capital remain largely the same.

At the current time, the balance sheet remains relatively strong. They have about $30 billion in debt and $9 billion in cash, giving them $21 billion in net debt. They currently earn $7-$9 billion or more in net income and/or free cash flow per year (it varies), which gives them debt/income and debt/FCF ratios of well below 3x.  Their S&P credit rating is A. Overall, it’s quite a strong balance sheet, but not excessively strong like it was in 2017 and before.

In my view, Amgen’s approach is quite sensible. They invest substantial amounts in research and acquisitions, and use excess cash to pay a growing dividend and reduce the total share count to make each share worth a larger stake in the company. Good for long-term shareholders.

**Valuation Analysis**

Amgen stock has generally traded at a reasonable valuation, with occasional deviations. For example, it became a bit expensive in late 2014, and went into a choppy sideways pattern for about 3 years, and then continued moving up as fundamentals crossed the price and pulled it up. At the current time, I view it as fairly-valued against a broader stock market that is quite pricey.

Amgen has turned into a fine dividend growth stock, with a 2.86% dividend yield and consistent annual dividend growth.

With a blended P/E ratio of about 15, a moderate dividend yield, and an average analyst-consensus forward earnings growth rate in the high single digits, the company has a fairly attractive dividend-adjusted PEG ratio that is well below 2.

The stock’s trailing P/E ratio and P/FCF ratio are within the historical norm. Note that in 2017, the company reported an accounting-related decline in GAAP earnings, even as free cash flow remained robust. Overall, the company usually produces slightly higher free cash flow than earnings, which is indicative of good earnings quality.

![Amgen PE and PFCF Ratios](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-16-amgn-pe-pfcf-ratio.png)

The company’s price-to-sales ratio remains at a healthy level. Its price-to-book ratio, however, has increased substantially in recent years:

This increase in book value is a result of the company’s aforementioned cash-spending in 2018 and 2019. The company had a higher book value before, as it hoarded cash. However, it spent a large portion of that cash to reduce its share count and acquire Otezla, which cleared out excess equity from the balance sheet to boost the company’s revenue and various per-share metrics.

Overall, the company has a reasonable path towards 10% annualized returns over the next several years, which is better than my base-case for the broader market over the next several years.

**Risks**

The company operates in an industry that is not very cyclical, but that does have two substantial risks.

*R&D Pipeline Risks*

The company must continually reinvent itself with successful R&D for new products to replace existing products that either reach patent expiration or face new competition.

It is challenging to forecast in advance which products will be duds (fail clinical trials, or quickly become irrelevant despite working as intended due to a better competing product), marginally profitable (plenty of R&D spending followed by only modest sales to recoup the initial expenses), or wildly successful (where the product becomes the leader in its category, and brings in billions and billions of free cash flow and pays for itself many times over). The big profitable drugs help pay for all of the smaller drugs that are important for patients but that don’t fully pay for their own R&D efforts.

The company is spending sizable amounts on R&D, has decent existing patent protection for many of their products, and can use acquisitions to further diversify and grow as needed.

This risk is primarily offset by better returns if the company is successful. For example, Amgen has a lower valuation, higher growth rate, and higher dividend yield than the bluest of blue chips, Johnson and Johnson. In exchange, Johnson and Johnson has more product diversification and a stronger balance sheet. I like both companies at current levels, with Amgen being the slightly higher risk/reward choice with more upside potential.

*U.S. Political Risk*

With 3/4ths of the company’s sales coming from the United States, Amgen is vulnerable to U.S. healthcare reform. Amgen’s 10-k risk assessment explains it well:

> Sales of our principal products are dependent on the availability and extent of coverage and reimbursement from third-party payers. In many markets around the world, these payers, including government health systems, private health insurers and other organizations, remain focused on reducing the cost of healthcare, and their efforts have intensified as a result of rising healthcare costs and economic challenges. Drugs remain heavily scrutinized for cost containment. As a result, payers are becoming more restrictive regarding the use of biopharmaceutical products and scrutinizing the prices of these products while requiring a higher level of clinical evidence to support the benefits such products bring to patients and the broader healthcare system. These pressures are intensified where our products are subject to competition, including from biosimilars.

Medical costs have increased at a much faster rate than broad consumer inflation over the past few decades, and people in the United States pay far higher per-capita healthcare costs than any other country in the world. Many U.S. political candidates are in favor of significant healthcare reform. The current regime is very favorable to healthcare companies, but future programs may be less beneficial to them.

Unlike health insurance companies that could potentially face existential risk if something like Medicare for All is passed into law, healthcare product companies like Amgen mainly would face margin pressure in certain reform scenarios. So, the risk is notable, but less severe for companies like Amgen.

In addition, it helps to keep in mind the process in which healthcare reform would take place. First, a candidate that favors substantial healthcare reform would need to win the U.S. presidency. Then, they would likely also require a majority in the U.S. House of Representatives, and likely a filibuster-proof 60 U.S. Senate seats (which almost no political forecast currently expects in the current election cycle). So, the potential for healthcare reform needs to be on the radar of healthcare stock investors, but proper diversification, position-sizing, and a focus on companies that make important products, can mitigate the risk.

**Conclusions**

Amgen likely has a profitable future ahead as it diversifies its product line and gains a larger share of its revenue from outside of the United States.

As a healthcare stock that relies heavily on R&D success and has a few key products it relies on rather significantly, I would not put an outsized amount of capital into it as a core position. However, when combined as a small or medium position with other appropriately-valued and growing healthcare stocks, it seems suitable for the defensive and non-cyclical portion of a portfolio.

I particularly like Amgen at the current time because many top stocks in the S&P 500 are becoming quite stretched in terms of valuation, and there remain significant cyclical risks at the current time, related to the U.S. business cycle and to the impact on Asia and potentially elsewhere from the coronavirus. Remember, even if the coronavirus ends up not being a big deal globally compared to the seasonal flu, China’s dramatic response to it (shutting down large portions of the country, quarantining cities the size of New York, turning away copper and oil shipments, etc) certainly is.

So, I’m certainly happy to hold some stocks that are not very cyclical at the moment, like Amgen, and that instead have a very different risk profile, related to their own R&D pipeline as well as U.S. political risks. All else being equal, it’s preferable to have a collection of assets with uncorrelated risks rather than all sharing similar risks.
