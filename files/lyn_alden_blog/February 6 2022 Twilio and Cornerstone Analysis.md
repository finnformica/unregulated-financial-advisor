**The US Business Cycle**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 and Q4 have shown decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Inflation and Policy Update**

Here’s the Fed’s dilemma in three charts.

First, the Fed is the furthest behind the curve on inflation that they’ve ever been since 1951, with rates at zero while CPI is at 7%:

![Real Fed Funds Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2022-1-real-rates.png)

Second, now that they are trying to tighten policy, they are doing so into a period of economic softening. Rather than being counter-cyclical, they are forced by their dual mandate to be pro-cyclical:

![US PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/business-confidence)*

Third, debt is so high that although they can raise rates somewhat, they can’t realistically “normalize” rates to positive real levels on any sort of long-term basis. The only way to make the math work when sovereign debt as a percentage of GDP is over 100% is financial repression (a.k.a. persistently negative real yields), and yet it’s not something they can really say out loud:

![National Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-national-debt.png)

The same situation is true for the European Central Bank and the Bank of Japan. Italy for example has over 150% sovereign debt to GDP; there’s no way to manage that debt load with structurally positive real rates, but it’s not something that Christine Lagarde can just say publicly, given the supposed independence of the central bank from government policies.

So, the major developed market central banks are in a corner where every potential move is a policy error. Leaving rates at zero and buying bonds while inflation is running much higher than their target is a policy error. On the other hand, raising rates to positive real levels amid a softening economy with this much debt in the system is a policy error. They had better choices decades ago but now they have basically a set of bad choices to choose from.

My base case continues to be that the Fed and other central banks will continue to tighten around the margins until it breaks something, such as credit markets. They’re tightening policy, but won’t be able to *normalize* policy, and they’re starting from a position of being way behind the curve.

Meanwhile, the lack of energy capex will likely keep oil prices relatively high. Even though I expect year-over-year US consumer price inflation to probably peak by early Q2 due to base effects and a pullback on stimulus (unless a huge energy price spike occurs this summer, which is quite possible), I also expect consumer price inflation to average higher in the 2020s decade than the 2010s decade.

And as such, I want to hold real assets such as productive value stocks, commodity producers, real estate with a fixed 30-year mortgage, emerging market equities, gold, and bitcoin. I’ll also buy small positions in oversold growth stocks if the market gives them to me on sale, and I’ll hold a nonzero amount of cash equivalents as a volatility shock absorber for rebalancing whenever liquidity dries up and good assets go on sale (which can easily happen in this difficult environment).

Cash and bonds in this long period of financial repression are basically just a very expensive form of optionality, and I treat them as such.

And, while it’s not my highest-conviction position, I continue to observe that gold is holding up well in the face of rising real rates and Fed tightening and remain relatively bullish on it relative to risk assets in this environment. I think the gold market sees through the near-term to the bigger picture regarding the inability of central banks to structurally normalize monetary policy despite all sorts of inflationary pressures.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin is showing potential signs of having put in place a price bottom.

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-btc-chart.png)

However, due to my view of risk-off conditions for 2022, I don’t necessarily “trust” this rally from a traders’ perspective. I consider it to be a coin flip whether or not we’ve seen this cycle’s lows for bitcoin already.

From an investors’ perspective though, I consider this to be a great range for [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/). I remain structurally bullish on bitcoin with a 5-year view, regardless of whether the bottom is already in place or not. If that structural view is correct, then this is a great accumulation zone.

The bearish argument is that as financial conditions are worsening, and as the Fed tightens monetary policy into a decelerating economy, this environment does not exactly scream “it’s a great time to buy a highly-volatile asset”. Indeed, bitcoin’s major rallies tend to occur in rising US PMI (economic accelerating) environments with rising levels of liquidity, rather than the opposite. Economic deceleration and worsening financial conditions tend to not be great environments for the price of bitcoin.

The overall point is that if traders try to get too “cute” with trying to precisely time bitcoin’s price bottom, they risk missing out on the long-term big picture filled with a lot of upside potential. For this reason, while I try to manage expectations conservatively around intermediate-term macro headwinds when it comes to bitcoin, I will generally err on the side of bullishness for bitcoin. That’s my structural view unless or until I see evidence to the contrary.

Bitcoin is towards the value end of this historical range. While I don’t rule out a lower low into capitulation at some point this year, I do like the long-term prospects of bitcoin here. It didn’t overshoot as much in this cycle as it historically has tended to do, and it might not undershoot as much either. Time will tell.

I marked some “eras” on that long-term moving average chart, and I’ll give some context below.

**Era 1: Peer-to-Peer (est. 2009-2010)**

The first era of bitcoin consisted of peer-to-peer usage between cypherpunks and other enthusiasts, mainly out of academic, ideological, and/or technical interest. For the first year and a half, bitcoin didn’t even have a dollar price, and exchanges didn’t exist yet.

There was very high friction for getting it, a.k.a. basically bad user experience for anyone who wasn’t a techie enthusiast. You could download a full node and mine bitcoins on your home computer, find someone to transfer some to you in exchange for cash, and engage in niche discussion boards, for example. It wasn’t on most peoples’ radar.

Satoshi Nakamoto was still active during this time, discussing things with the community and making software updates.

**Era 2: Proto-Usage (est. 2011-2014)**

By this point, Satoshi Nakamoto disappeared, and the bitcoin network became self-sustaining by the rest of the community.

The online marketplace Silk Road was founded in 2011, which allowed people to buy drugs with bitcoins. This gave the asset a lasting reputation for illicit activities even though less than 1% of bitcoin transactions are used for that today. Similar to the early usage of pagers by drug dealers when they came out, people began experimenting with the usage of bitcoin as a censorship-resistant payment method for the dark web. Like any powerful technology, bitcoin could be used for good or ill.

In addition, Wikileaks began accepting bitcoin early in this era as well, because Wikileaks was shut off from its other payment channels such as PayPal. In fact, Satoshi referenced this in one of his last forum messages, because he was worried that this sort of thing would bring negative attention to bitcoin while it was still in its early stages.

The early bitcoin exchanges came into existence around this time. In 2010, Mt. Gox (a website for exchanging *Magic: The Gathering* trading cards) launched a service for bitcoin trading, and by 2011 it really ramped up. Kraken was founded in 2011 and Coinbase was founded in 2012, and they were tiny back then.

There was a lot of friction for using exchanges at this time. User interfaces were challenging, and many early companies had trouble getting bank connections due to the perception of bitcoin/crypto mainly being a criminal thing. A lot of initial altcoins were created to try to ride on bitcoin’s coattails.

The first application-specific integrated circuits “ASICs” were made for bitcoin mining during the later stage of this era. By this point, it was uneconomic to mine bitcoin on a home PC, and instead bitcoin mining became a specialized niche business operation.

The Silk Road was shut down by authorities in 2013, and its founder Ross Ulbricht was later convicted with more than two life sentences in prison. Meanwhile, Mt. Gox was plagued with problems, suffered a massive hack, and trading was closed in 2014.

Bitcoin was thought to be dead by many people after these setbacks, and went through a long bear market.

**Era 3: Speculative Retail (est. 2015-2017)**

Over time, the bitcoin network refused to die, and started to get more mainstream attention by early retail traders.

Some of the early hardware wallets were created, which allowed users to easily self-custody their bitcoins rather than leave them on exchanges, even if they didn’t run their own full node.

The larger exchanges were now able to maintain banking relationships, and put in place serious security standards by cybersecurity professionals. The user interfaces in the industry were improving.

Early venture capitalists began providing serious capital to bitcoin startups and other digital asset startups.

Bitcoin went through the “fork wars” during this era, where developers and industry leaders debated about changing bitcoin’s block size. Most big companies like exchanges were in favor of increasing the block size, while the majority of users were not, and the users won.

Bitcoin cash “BCH” hard-forked away from original bitcoin “BTC” with a larger block size, but most of the network remained with original bitcoin. Increasing the block size with a hard fork increases centralization risks because it increases the hardware and bandwidth requirements to run a full node. Bitcoin cash lost tremendous value compared to original bitcoin over time, and today has less than 2% of the market capitalization and hash rate of original bitcoin.

This era basically ended with the initial coin offering “ICO” bubble. Tons of altcoins raised capital by selling tokens to retail investors, and some of them were subsequently sued for selling unregistered securities. People yet again proclaimed bitcoin and the broader digital asset industry to be dead, and it went through another long bear market.

**Era 4: Institutional Custodians (est. 2018-present)**

Institutional managers quietly took notice of bitcoin in Era 3, and began researching the industry. During the end of that period and then mainly through Era 4, they began building out institutional-grade custody solutions.

Large investors, corporations, insurance companies, pensions, and other pools of capital could now safely put millions or billions of dollars into bitcoin, via firms like Fidelity and NYDIG. Bitcoin became large and liquid enough to absorb these institutional-scale capital inflows.

In addition, venture capitalists remained active through the bear market, and all sorts of other infrastructure continued to be built up. Collaborative multi-signature custody solutions, mobile wallet apps, crypto-native financing companies, bitcoin rewards cards, and other companies were built and expanded.

Early macro analysts including me began covering bitcoin and the digital assets industry in eras three and four. Some of us were invited to give presentations to Wall Street firms and other institutional sources of capital to ramp up their knowledge of the industry.

MicroStrategy (MSTR) became the first publicly-traded company on a major exchange to start holding bitcoin on its balance sheet in 2020. Coinbase (COIN) went public in 2021. China banned bitcoin mining in 2021, and much of the hash rate shifted over to North America. Dozens of bitcoin miners grew to considerable size and went public. Block (SQ) began incorporating bitcoin as a key component of its business plans.

The retail market around bitcoin shifted more towards automatic recurring purchases and viewing bitcoin as something to hold rather than to trade, with more of the speculation shifting to altcoins.

In the altcoin space, DeFi, DAOs, and NFTs experimented with different speculative use-cases for blockchain technologies with some degree of early mainstream adoption. The industry became big enough to start capturing serious regulator attention and also reached into pop culture.

The Lightning network launched in early 2018 as a second layer on top of the bitcoin base layer, with multiple open source implementations, and reached significant levels of liquidity by early 2021. El Salvador adopted bitcoin as its second legal tender along with the US dollar, mainly by using Lightning.

Many countries such as India and Russia flip-flopped on decisions regarding whether to ban digital assets or accept and regulate them during Era 4, and over time generally shifted towards accepting and regulating them. Thailand proposed a crypto-specific tax and then walked that idea back when it faced public backlash. Singapore was an early adopter with digital asset regulations and institutional-grade custody solutions. Multiple US senators and congressmen explicitly began to support bitcoin and the digital assets industry, and a number of mayors and governors positioned their cities and states as hubs for bitcoin and digital assets.

Basically, digital assets became big enough in this era that countries had to articulate their policies on the industry so that companies knew whether or not they could participate. Some initially hostile jurisdictions faced pushback from the private sector to take a more open position on digital assets.

I continue to monitor the bitcoin’s surrounding ecosystem, and it continues to show signs of maturation and structural adoption. Many companies in the space were successfully able to raise rounds of venture capital throughout 2021, which gives them big cash piles for ongoing development in the upcoming years.

## Twilio Analysis

Twilio (TWLO), founded in 2008, is a company that offers cloud-based solutions that other companies can use to engage with their customers through various communication channels.

For example, Twilio’s product suite allows a company to send text messages, phone calls, and emails to customers, and organize those communications together into individual customer profiles to track their engagement and other details regarding their interaction with the company. They also have customer support chat software and customer authentication software.

Here’s a summary from their latest 10-k filing:

> Twilio spent the last 12 years building the leading cloud communication platform, but communications is just the beginning. Twilio’s vision is to be the leading customer engagement platform, ultimately providing businesses with the holy grail—a single view of the customer journey. We believe the future of customer engagement will be written in software, by the developers of the world—our customers.
>
> Cloud platforms are a category of software that enable developers to build and manage applications without the complexity of creating and maintaining the underlying infrastructure. These platforms have arisen to enable a fast pace of innovation across a range of categories, such as computing and storage. We are the leader in the cloud communications platform category. We enable developers to build, scale and operate real-time customer engagement within software applications.
>
> We offer a customer engagement platform with software designed to address specific use cases, like account security and contact centers, and a set of Application Programming Interfaces (“APIs”) that handles the higher-level communication logic needed for nearly every type of customer engagement. These APIs are focused on the business challenges that a developer is looking to address, allowing our customers to more quickly and easily build better ways to engage with their customers throughout their journey. We also offer a set of APIs that enable developers to embed voice, messaging, video and email capabilities into their applications, and are designed to support almost all the fundamental ways humans communicate, unlocking innovators to address just about any communication market. The Super Network is our software layer that allows our customers’ software to communicate with connected devices globally. It interconnects with communications networks and inbox service providers around the world and continually analyzes data to optimize the quality and cost of communications that flow through our platform. The Super Network also contains a set of APIs giving our customers access to more foundational components of our platform, like phone numbers.
>
> In February 2019 we acquired SendGrid, Inc. (“SendGrid”), the leading email API platform. Email is an important channel for businesses to communicate with their customers. Incorporating SendGrid’s products into our platform allows us to enable businesses to engage with their customers via email effectively and at scale.
>
> In November 2020 we acquired Segment io, Inc. (“Segment”), the market-leading customer data platform. Segment provides businesses a unified customer view to better understand their customers and engage more effectively. The acquisition expands and strengthens use cases across customer service, marketing, sales, product and analytics and accelerates Twilio’s journey to build the world’s leading customer engagement platform.

## Cornerstone Building Brands Analysis

Homebuilding stocks have been punished in recent weeks by rising mortgage yields. The thought is generally that rising yields will make homes less affordable, which can slow down building activity in the industry.

![Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-mortgage-rates.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Around the margins that is certainly an issue, but I think it is dwarfed by the trend of millennials buying homes and people moving from some states to other states, and into more suburban and rural areas.

Plus, on an inflation-adjusted basis, mortgage rates have never been lower than they are right now:

![Real Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-mortgage-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Of course, the market doesn’t expect year-over-year CPI to remain as high as 7%. Forward inflation expectations are much lower than that.

But we can think of it as a dichotomy. If inflation indeed rolls over and levels out, then we probably won’t see much more upward pressure on mortgage rates, especially in this era of financial repression. On the other hand if inflation remains higher for longer than the market expects, likely due to severe energy shortages, then mortgage rates might go up but they would likely remain cheap relative to the ongoing devaluation of currency. Having a negative-real-yield mortgage against a hard asset isn’t a bad trade.

The homebuilding industry has been sluggish since the bottom of the subprime mortgage crisis, and even after a length recovery is nowhere near prior cyclical peaks of annualized home construction.

![Housing Starts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-6-housing-starts.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

I think too many analysts are fighting the last battle and are afraid of a subprime crisis 2.0 happening. As a result, homebuilding stocks are constantly priced as though their entire industry will collapse again.

Overall, I acknowledge near-term headwinds in this declining-PMI cycle, but remain structurally bullish on the US homebuilding industry. I manage the volatility by using small position sizes.

One way to get exposure to the industry is through Cornerstone Building Brands (CNR). Cornerstone is one of the biggest manufacturers of exterior housing products in the United States. They specialize in residential siding products, residential windows, and various commercial exterior products. This includes things like vinyl siding, stone siding, metal roofing, exterior trim, shutters, and fencing.

In other words, rather than build homes, they make products that homebuilders use on the exterior of the home. Their products are also heavily used for exterior remodeling, rather than just first construction.

The company’s legacy stretches back to the 1980s, but the modern conception of the company began in early 2019 as a result of a merger between two companies. Since then, the company has made some targeted acquisitions, and a large $1 billion divestiture of their insulated metal panels business to Nucor (NUE). They are shaping their portfolio of product brands around their core expertise.

**Valuation Analysis**

Like many companies related to the homebuilding industry, the legacy pre-merger version of Cornerstone was completely nuked during the subprime mortgage crisis. This was the biggest housing crash since the Great Depression, and it completely reset the homebuilding industry.

A risk for the company is that they operate in a cyclical industry and have a substantial debt load. As such, their debt is rated at only B+, which is junk grade.

However, the company is prioritizing deleveraging. After the merger in early 2019, the company had $3.2 billion in net debt, which as of mid-2021 was down to under $2.4 billion. They continued growing the company as well, so debt to adjusted EBITDA fell from 6.1x in early 2019 to 3.7x in mid 2021. The company plans to continue its rapid deleveraging throughout 2022. This will increase after-interest profits, and eventually should result in an upgrade in the credit rating, which leads to lower refinancing costs.

So, this company’s debt load is not a result of a struggling business or bad management; it’s the result of the company coming into its current form after a merger in early 2019. It is now whittling itself down to a more streamlined and less-leveraged business.

The company recently had a smooth CEO transition as of late 2021. The former CEO, James Metcalf, retired and is now the executive chairman of the board. The new CEO, Rose Lee, was previously an executive at DuPont. Rose Lee has a bachelor’s degree in aerospace engineering from Cornell, a master’s degree in mechanical engineering from Rensselaer, an MBA from MIT, and about three decades of combined engineering and management experience across multiple companies with rising levels of seniority. Governance looks solid in my view.

With a roughly $2 billion market cap, this smallish company is off of most peoples’ radar, and is not the sort of structural growth stock that benefits from a decade of disinflation, which is what has captured the market’s attention over the past decade. It’s a real-economy type of stock, connected to the homebuilding industry, and is incredibly cheap as it improves its post-merger capital structure and optimizes its portfolio of products and brands through acquisitions and divestitures.

It won’t show up favorably on many stock screeners, and has been rangebound since 2009. However, its fundamentals have been improving for years to the point of major undervaluation, it benefits from macroeconomic tailwinds, and I think it’s only a matter of time until the ongoing improvements of the company force the share price to go higher.

In the meantime, they will continue to deleverage their balance sheet and strengthen their company. If the share price remains very low by the time their deleveraging is finished, they could eventually transition into aggressive share buybacks at these low valuation levels, which would further accelerate per-share growth.

## Summary Thoughts

As the Fed and other central banks continue to tighten monetary policy into an economy that is showing signs of ongoing growth deceleration, I remain quite cautious. This is an environment that benefits from portfolio hedges and nonzero cash positions, to rebalance into any volatile events that we encounter. I would not be surprised to see capitulation/liquidation events at any time during this year or next, including among high quality assets that I like with a 5-year view.

However, I am cautiously optimistic on several parts of the global market, including Chinese equities, Brazilian equities, gold, bitcoin, energy pipelines, healthcare stocks, homebuilders, domestic and global value stocks in general, and a handful of oversold hypergrowth stocks.

We’re at a rather pivotal moment in the world of macroeconomics, with developed market central banks facing the prospect of serious inflation for the first time in decades, and with a sovereign debt situation at high levels that haven’t been seen since the 1940s. That sort of encounter is unlikely to be smooth, and my focus in this environment is on owning high-quality real assets with a cash buffer for occasional rebalancing.
