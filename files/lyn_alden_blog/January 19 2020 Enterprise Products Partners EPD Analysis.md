# Enterprise Products Partners L.P. (EPD) Deep-Dive Analysis

*Published January 19, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This issue takes a look at the status of the U.S. business cycle, makes a case for platinum, and then explores arguably the best-managed midstream company in the United States.

## U.S. Business Cycle Status

The U.S. economy continues to show an inconclusive-but-slowing pattern for economic output, even as the stock market pushes to new highs.

**Housing Starts: Solid, but Over-Reported**

The most surprising number from this past week, reported on Friday, was that housing starts came in with a blockbuster number. They had a massive spike up, which was way above estimates and received quite a bit of attention:

![January 2020 Housing Starts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-housing-starts.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The reduction in interest rates over the past year has been helpful for the housing market. Thirty-year fixed mortgage rates had increased to cycle highs of about 5% in late 2018, but have since fallen back down to between 3.50% and 3.75% within the past several months, which makes a big difference for home affordability. This seems to be helping housing demand.

However, digging into it a bit more, December’s big gain was mainly due to seasonal adjustments. The non-seasonally-adjusted figure was far less spectacular:

![Housing Starts Non Seasonal](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-housing-starts-non-seasonal.png) *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

December is usually the lowest month for housing starts, because it’s cold in many parts of the country and because it’s the holiday season. However, this December was among the warmest and least-snowy Decembers on record for most of the United States, meaning it was a much “less-bad” home-building month than a typical December. So, housing starts declined, but did not decline as much as they usually do in December, and thus the seasonally-adjusted figure was remarkably high.

**Job Openings: Not So Good**

It was also reported on Friday that the total amount of U.S. job openings fell from over 7.3 million to 6.8 million, which was well below expectations. The job openings metric is now in its deepest year-over-year percent decline of this business cycle.

The year-over-year percentage change in job openings tracks very closely to year-over-year percentage change in real GDP. Real GDP is reported quarterly with a considerable lag (we’re still waiting on the Q4 2019 figure, for example, which will come out at the end of January and then will be subject to revisions for a while), while job openings is a monthly metric, and therefore it gives an information lead on what the GDP report might look like or where it’s potentially headed. Job openings is only one economic variable and thus what it says is not perfect, but what it does say isn’t good:

![January 2020 Job Openings vs GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-job-openings.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Some analysts are still referring to this as a solid number because they are looking at in absolute terms rather than year-over-year change terms, and the latter is more sensitive and informative of the two ways of organizing it.

In absolute terms, the 6.8 million number of job openings remains high, and remains above the number of people who are officially unemployed. However, there is a skill/education/salary mismatch between job openings and people looking for work.

Most economic indicators look great in absolute terms at this point in the economic cycle (earnings, employment, spending, etc). The moments that matter are when they start to fall from those great levels, which is why viewing the numbers in year-over-year percent change terms is usually more informative.

**Jobless Claims: Some Stabilization**

Lastly, the moving average of initial jobless claims improved/reduced last week to resume a flattish trend rather than pushing forward with a worsening/upward trend, while the moving average of continuing jobless claims worsened:

![January 2020 Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The unemployment rate, along with its various indicators like jobless claims and job openings, are the key areas to watch throughout 2020 because this is what will be the deciding factor for whether the U.S. business cycle has another spurt of growth left in it, or if it is likely to enter a significant downturn.

## ETF Spotlight: PPLT

I recently became a bit more bullish on platinum, so I want to take a moment to highlight it.

Long-time readers know that I’ve been significantly bullish on gold and silver and their various miners and financiers since October 2018 (and to a more limited extent prior to that) as allocations within a diversified portfolio, but I haven’t written much about platinum.

Gold and silver both “bottomed” right around the start of 2016 and have been in a choppy uptrend since. More importantly, I am bullish on them for fundamental reasons, and have an article dedicated to precious metals [here](https://www.lynalden.com/precious-metals-investing/).

Platinum also reached a low point back in the same 2016 time-frame, but reached even lower lows in late 2018, so it has been on its own bearish trend.

While I’ve been bullish on platinum with a very long-term outlook, the problem was that I did not see a bullish catalyst for a trend reversal yet, so I was less interested in it than I was with gold and silver, and thus had no position in it.

Platinum has historically traded at a higher price than gold, but roughly within the past decade, it has traded at a discount to gold. So it has been attractive from a valuation perspective for some time, but has had a very ugly price chart for a while. Here’s a 10-year chart:

![Gold, Silver, Platinum](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-metals.png)

Platinum has had a perfect storm of bearishness until recently. The metal is heavily used for catalytic converters in vehicles to reduce harmful emissions. Electric vehicles do not need catalytic converters, so that is a long-term demand destruction risk for platinum usage. Car sales in general have been in a significant downtrend over the past year; that industry is one of the weakest parts of the global economy. In addition, another platinum-group metal, palladium, has been substituted for platinum in the past several years for a lot of catalytic converter usage. Historically, palladium was cheaper than platinum, but now palladium trades for well over twice the price of both platinum and gold, which is historically extremely unusual.

This hasn’t had much financial media attention, but within the past two weeks alone, the prices of both palladium and its rarer counterpart rhodium (both platinum-group metals) went absolutely vertical:

![Rhodium and Palladium](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-rhodium-palladium.png)

*Chart Source: Kitco*

Unlike gold and silver that are mined all over the world, a very small number of emerging market countries produce almost all platinum-group metals, including platinum, palladium, and rhodium. So, supply/demand balances are quite strict. New emissions standards in China and elsewhere are likely a factor in the increased demand and prices since the beginning of 2020.

Palladium and platinum are able to be substituted for each other for catalytic converters in many cases, with some engineering work involved. Palladium has historically been the cheaper metal, but in this current cycle where most designs are made for it, palladium demand has been strong, driving the price up. As long as palladium prices remain elevated, it makes the engineering case for switching to platinum more attractive. These things tend to go in cycles.

In addition, while platinum remains historically cheap compared to gold (about $1,000+ vs $1,550+ at the moment), platinum’s usage in jewelry should become increasingly attractive as a more cost-effective option, even though ironically it has generally been considered the more premium material. For most of history, platinum has been the more expensive metal compared to gold.

With palladium and rhodium at very high prices, and gold priced well above platinum, platinum is arguably the true value play among the precious metals. In the past couple weeks, it has shown small signs of life, indicating that the past year may have been the actual bottom for the metal, and that the expensive prices for rhodium and platinum are spilling over into platinum.

Last week, I added some platinum bars to my bullion collection for the first time. I may also add a small allocation to the Aberdeen Standard Physical Platinum Shares ETF (PPLT) to some of my portfolios for a more liquid and cost-effective source of exposure. Although it’s not certain, there are signs that platinum, and subsequently the PPLT ETF, may have formed a bottoming process.

![PPLT January 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-PPLT.png)

When it comes to metals, especially physical bullion, I am extraordinarily patient. Frankly, I don’t know how platinum will do this year or next, for example. However, I now consider the long-term risk/reward ratio of platinum and the PPLT ETF to be attractive as part of a diversified and regularly rebalanced portfolio, and thus consider it to be a “buy” for a small allocation, with ongoing dollar-cost averaging.

## Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) Deep Dive Analysis

Enterprise Products Partners LP (ticker: EPD) is one of the largest midstream operators in North America. They own a huge set of infrastructure for transportation, processing, storing, and exporting natural gas, crude oil, natural gas liquids (NGL), and petrochemicals.

![EPD Overview](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-overview.png)

*Chart Source: EPD December 2019 Investor Presentation at Wells Fargo*

Since inception, EPD has absolutely crushed the S&P 500 in terms of total returns:

![EPD Outperformance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-outperformance.png)

Plus, it currently pays a distribution yield of over 6% per year with two decades of consecutive annual distribution increases, which makes it an attractive higher-yielding investment.

However, within the past five years, EPD has significantly under-performed the S&P 500:

![EPD Five Year Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-5-year.png)

In the [previous report about oil company Lukoil](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-1-5/), I showed how the U.S. energy sector dramatically outperformed the S&P 500 during the 2000-2009 decade only to dramatically underperform it during the 2010-2019 decade, especially during the second half of that latter decade. EPD is in a similar situation that I believe could begin to turn favorable.

The key factor for EPD is that the *fundamentals* of the business have done very well this whole time since inception; it’s merely the *price* that has done poorly in recent years. In other words, it’s a high-quality business at a historically low valuation.

![EPD Distribution Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-price-distributions.png)

I’ve been bullish on EPD in recent years as an income investment after its big price decline, and wrote about it from time to time, but haven’t invested in it myself yet. The reason is that it requires filing an additional tax form, and the relative attractiveness of the business compared to the broader market hasn’t been compelling enough yet for a younger and more aggressive total-return investor such as myself (compared to someone who specifically wants the reliable distribution income from it, such as a retiree, for whom it would be great). I’ve been buying ENB, KMI, and OKE for my midstream exposure, which has been enough for me.

However, with the recent major run-up in the S&P 500 and the comparative flat performance from EPD, my expected value of long-term forward returns for EPD has improved relative to the S&P 500. So, now I’m getting seriously more interested in it.

**Management and Performance**

The important aspect of EPD, unlike the previous recommendation of Lukoil, is that EPD primarily makes money from energy volumes, not energy prices. Their job is to transport, process, store, and/or export it, regardless of how cheap or expensive it is. So, as oil prices and natural gas prices have gone up and down, EPD has continued to increase its distributable cash flow per share and its distributions (dividends) per share.

EPD also happens to be heavily focused on natural gas, natural gas liquids, and petrochemicals, with only about a quarter of its business exposed to crude oil volumes.

Over the past two decades, there has been a major build-out of North American energy infrastructure, which is starting to flatten out. Therefore, it does make sense for midstream companies to trade at lower valuations than they use to, and to focus on total returns rather than aggressive growth. However, EPD is arguably the highest-quality business in the sector, and they’ve been grouped in valuation-wise with many of the more poorly-managed and highly-leveraged midstream businesses. This gives discerning investors a good opportunity, in my opinion.

The oil price crash of 2015 due to oversupply of new shale oil was the big bearish moment for energy stocks and midstream companies, from which the U.S. energy industry still has not fully recovered. Many midstream businesses were over-leveraged, and had to cut their dividends, sell assets, and destroy unitholder capital.

However, EPD was one of the few midstream businesses that emerged almost unscathed in terms of its fundamentals. Although its overvalued unit price came back down to reality (which is outside of management’s control), the business continued to raise dividends and grow distributable cash flow.

![EPD Fundamental Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-performance.png)

*Chart Source: EPD December 2019 Investor Presentation at Wells Fargo*

This was because, even during the boom times of high energy prices and strong growth, EPD management never over-leveraged the business, and never tried to maximize returns at the cost of financial safety. In both good times and bad for the energy sector, EPD management continued to prudently operate the business with proper risk management and a focus on sustainable growth with 12% or better returns on invested capital while maintaining a fortress balance sheet.

During bull markets (whether in stocks, or energy, or junk bonds, or cryptocurrencies, or whatever market you’re looking at), the most leveraged and risk-taking members look like they’re the smartest ones for a while, with the best growth. Then when the bear market comes and the leveraged ones go down in flames, the prudent ones turn out to be the real winners. It’s the classic “[tortoise vs hare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Tortoise_and_the_Hare)” analogy, in other words. That was what EPD did compared to many of its midstream competitors, and is a good way of thinking about investing in general.

In addition, EPD has been near the forefront of good management practices in the midstream sector. They eliminated their 50% incentive distribution rights (IDRs) in 2002, which made the partnership more financially sustainable for limited partners.  More recently, they have shifted towards a mostly self-funding model, which means they have intentionally slowed-down their distribution growth to let distributable cash flow per share outpace cash distributions per share, so that the distribution payout ratio would decrease. That’s the gap between the blue and gray areas in the chart above.

EPD is one-third owned by management, including the Duncan family, which partially explains why it has been so well-managed. The late Dan Duncan was the co-founder and chairman of EPD, and now his descendants have his big multi-billion-dollar stake in the business, and continue to oversee it with a long-term view rather than quarter-by-quarter short-sightedness.

EPD currently has a BBB+ credit rating, which places it as being tied in first place, along with ENB and MMP, as having the highest credit rating in the midstream industry. EPD has among the lowest debt-to-EBITDA ratio in the industry, and is currently below the bottom range of its typical debt-to-EBITDA relative to its own history, meaning they have a very strong balance sheet:

![EPD Historical Debt Level](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-debt-ebitda.png)

Right now, their debt interest rate is very low at 4.5% with a long 19-year average maturity. They’ve lengthened their average duration while simultaneously lowering their average interest rate, so they have locked in the benefit of this low interest rate environment without leveraging their balance sheet up like many other businesses have.

**Valuation Analysis**

EPD became quite overvalued in 2013 and its stock price hasn’t recovered since, even as its fundamentals continue to march upward. Fortunately, unitholders have been paid a high distribution yield while they’ve waited.

![EPD Fast Graph Earnings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-fastgraph-earnings.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

* *Black line = actual stock price*
* *Blue line = price if at its average P/E  of 21.63*
* *Orange line = price if at a P/E of 15*
* *White line = dividends*

As is the case for most midstream companies, earnings are not the best measure of profitability. Unique metrics like distributable cash flow per share are more accurate for such asset-heavy businesses, and price-to-EBITDA is a decent proxy for it. However, there were some accounting artifacts for EBITDA during the financial crisis that throw the chart off a bit:

![EPD Fast Graph EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-fastgraph-ebitda.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

* *Black line = actual stock price*
* *Orange/blue line = price if at its average P/EBITDA  of 9.98*
* *White line = dividends*

Considering both the earnings chart and the EBITDA chart, EPD’s unit price is relatively attractive, and below its historical average valuation level.

YCharts confirms how the EV/EBITDA ratio has changed over time, showing it currently to be near its cheapest level since 2009:

![EPD EV-to-EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-ev-ebitda.png)

So, EPD is currently trading near the lowest P/EBITDA and EV/EBITDA ratios it ever has, while simultaneously having debt levels below its historical average, even as the typical valuations of companies within the S&P 500 continue to climb to extraordinary heights while building higher-than-average amounts of leverage.

This makes EPD look pretty good at current levels in comparison, for patient long-term fundamental investors.

Due to EPD’s high yield and low-but-predictable distribution growth, the [dividend discount model](https://www.lynalden.com/dividend-discount-model/) is likely the best way to value it, and it is one of the options within the [StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/stockdelver-book/) spreadsheet.

EPD has grown its distribution by over 5% per year on average during the past decade, but I’ll use a lower 4% figure going forward. This assumes that 2% growth comes from gradual price increases, and 2% comes from either infrastructure growth or unit buybacks (which spreads the existing fundamentals over a smaller base of units).

Considering that EPD only pays out about 2/3rds of its distributable cash flow as distributions, leaving the other 1/3rd for growth reinvestment or potentially for forward unit buybacks, this should be a fair baseline.

![EPD Dividend Discount Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-19-epd-ddm.png)

The results show that EPD should be able to provide about 10% long-term annualized returns from current levels. This should be intuitive: a business that yields 6% in cash distributions and grows those distributions by 4% per year, gives about 10% annualized returns over the long run, assuming no major changes in valuation.

Even in a more bearish scenario, where distribution increases slow down to 2-3%, it still has a solid chance of outperforming the S&P 500 for the long run from current levels, particularly within a diversified portfolio, and especially for investors that need investment income.

**MLPs vs Corporations, and a Potential Catalyst**

EPD is a master limited partnership (MLP) rather than a standard corporation, so the tax situation is a bit more complex. Instead of being broken into shares, it is broken into units, and investors are called unitholders rather than shareholders.

Historically, the MLP is a tax-advantaged structure compared to corporations. However, investors will receive an extra tax form during tax season, and will need to file that as part of their tax return. Tax professionals and programs like Turbo Tax can generally do these forms without a problem, but it does represent an extra thing to deal with. Investors also need to be careful about putting a large allocation of an MLP into a retirement account, because if it creates more than $1,000 in unrelated business taxable income (UBTI), investors may have to pay a tax bill on their retirement account for that holding.

This makes some investors not want to own MLPs, and most funds don’t include MLPs. So, a company like EPD can be under-owned, which can push down its valuation.

As corporate tax rates have decreased over decades and as growth has slowed in the midstream sector, the comparative advantages to being an MLP compared to a corporation have diminished. Many other midstream businesses, such as KMI and the various subsidiaries of ENB have consolidated into corporate structures. EPD, however, remains an MLP.

EPD has reported that they have carefully considered the possibility of a transition to a corporation, but have not seen a strong reason to do so yet. If it were to occur, it could be a catalyst for a moderate valuation increase as more investors and funds buy into it, but there have also been instances of big tax bills associated with MLP-to-corporation conversions. However, because EPD is so heavily family-owned, they likely wouldn’t make any move that would be tax-disadvantaged for unitholders as some MLPs have in the past.

Whether they ultimately convert or not will surely be a well-reasoned decision, and performed in the most tax-efficient way. If converted, it could result in a moderate valuation increase, serving as a potential catalyst for improved stock performance.

**A Self-Funding Model**

Some businesses are [net buyers of their own stock](https://www.lynalden.com/share-buybacks/), meaning they reduce their share/unit counts over time, which boosts earnings per share and dividends per share. All else being equal, a lower share price benefits their per-share growth because they can buy back a larger percentage of their shares each year with a given amount of money than if their shares were expensive.

Other businesses like EPD are net sellers of their own stock, meaning they regularly issue new shares and use that capital to invest in new projects and make acquisitions. Traditionally high-yield industries like real estate investment trusts (REITs), master limited partnerships (MLPs), yieldcos, and business development companies (BDCs) usually fall under this category. They’re paying out most of their cash flow as dividends/distributions, so in order to raise new capital and expand, they need to issue new shares. All else being equal, they do very well when their shares are expensive.

Here’s the problem. Companies that are heavily reliant on selling shares to fund growth can quickly collapse if their share price gets too low, because they can no longer profitably sell their shares to fund projects. There’s a red line somewhere for each of these net sellers of stock at which they can not grow per-share value by selling new shares to raise capital. For example, if their share prices sink and their dividend yield is 12%, they can’t issue new shares to fund projects that give them only 10% returns. They then have to rely on issuing debt or cutting the dividend to raise liquidity.

Most dividend investors are aware of what happened to Kinder Morgan (KMI) and a bunch of other MLPs a few years ago during the oil price crash. Kinder Morgan was considered the blue chip version of a master limited partnership; the long-standing gold standard of its industry. They built extensive pipelines throughout the United States, and funded that growth by issuing new units and by using high levels of debt leverage. For a very long time, this was a market-beating strategy with an amazing combo of yield and growth.

But when oil prices crashed in 2015, the industry ran into a serious problem and MLP valuations fell dramatically.

For a normal corporation, this would not have been as big of a deal. But because Kinder Morgan was reliant on continually issuing new shares to fund growth, and its share prices were suddenly cut in half, it quickly fell into a liquidity trap by losing its usual source of new capital. And because they were already highly leveraged, they couldn’t take on too much new debt.

As a result, they had to cut the dividend and sell some of their assets at bad prices to raise liquidity and remain solvent, and years later they are still recovering.

EPD avoided that problem by not having excessive leverage; they were able to operate normally and let their unit price recover before issuing more units for further growth projects. They used a mild increase in debt to get through that harder period, which they have since reduced.

Now, many midstream companies including KMI and EPD have shifted towards a self-funding model. (Of course, the way EPD did it by choice rather than necessity was the smarter move.) This means that they have purposely slowed down their distribution growth (or cut them in the case of KMI and many others) to create a larger source of inside capital.

In other words, if a company earns $1.00 in distributable cash flow per share and pays $0.95 in distributions, then it only has 5% of its cashflow left over to fund growth projects. So, it needs to dilute its unit base, sell units, and raise cash, if it wants to grow. This works very well in a strong growth environment, but causes problems during recessions when unit prices are low.

If they decide to become self-funding, and distributable cash flow increases at, say, 6% per year, they can keep their distribution growing at, say, 3% per year. Over several years, they’ll push down their distribution yield to maybe 60-80%, which leaves 20-40% of their cash flow available to internally fund growth projects. It’s beneficial in the long run once they reach their target lower payout ratio, but it slows down distribution growth while it happens. Again, this is the divide between the blue area and the gray area in the chart higher up in this report.

EPD has been in that phase for a few years already, and now is in a pretty good self-funding place for the most part. They pay out about 2/3rds of their distributable cash flow as distributions, and retain the other 1/3rd for growth or flexibility. Investors that have been unhappy with EPD’s slower distribution growth rate should keep in mind the reasons they have been doing it. In the long run, it makes the structure more stable and avoids unit dilution. A lot of this work is behind them now, and the business is stronger than ever based on most metrics like debt levels, distribution payout ratios, and so forth.

**Risks**

EPD has fortunately been one of the most boring midstream companies, meaning they haven’t made dramatic mistakes. Still, they face the typical industry-wide risks.

*Renewable Energy- Lower Gas, Oil, NGL, and Petrochemical Volumes*

Midstream assets are expensive and need to operate for decades to justify their cost of construction. This requires a long view towards future energy demand, to ensure that they don’t build unnecessary assets. The long-term risk for midstream companies is for renewable sources of energy to reduce the need for oil and natural gas. Additionally, some plant-based plastics and chemicals could replace plastics and chemicals derived from fossil fuels.

However, natural gas is replacing coal among developed countries, since it is cheaper and cleaner. Emerging markets are further behind in this process; many of them are still building new coal plants, even as they have an eye towards eventually moving away from coal. EPD has a growing export business to send propane and other natural gas liquids to emerging Asia and elsewhere; India in particular is a growing source of demand.

So, it is likely still a long process before natural gas, natural gas liquids, and petrochemical demand start to wind down, even if electric vehicle adoption accelerates and takes market share from gasoline specifically.

Still, it’s a trend worth being aware of when deciding how much energy exposure you want to have in your portfolio. North America already had a very large build-out of midstream assets and overall oil/gas production, so the glory days are likely behind this sector. However, from current price levels, EPD is in a strong position to operate as a high-yielding, slow-growing provider of solid total returns.

*Market Risk*

Because EPD is not very reliant on issuing new equity, and has mostly fixed-rate investment-grade debt with low leverage, they have a lot of stability in the face of a bear market. However, their unit price will most likely take a beating along with most everything else in that sort of market environment.

By many indications, the S&P 500 is overbought at the moment and ripe for at least a correction in the coming months, so investors should be prepared for volatility and be willing to dollar-cost average into their high-quality investments. Maintaining some defensive elements in a portfolio, like cash-equivalents, short-term bonds, and precious metals, can be of use during major market declines.

It’s worth noting that in the aftermath of the 2000 dotcom bubble, value stocks had a much shallower decline and a faster recovery to new highs than their more overvalued growth/tech counterparts. Growth stocks led the bull market up, and led the bear market down.

The situation reversed in the aftermath of the 2008 crisis, where growth stocks recovered quickly while value stocks took a much bigger beating and required many more years to reach new highs. Value stocks had strongly outpaced growth stocks leading into the 2007 market top, and then led growth stocks on the way down.

Although nothing is guaranteed, the current environment looks more like the 2000 era, where growth has once again significantly outpaced value, indicating that solid value stocks like EPD should hold up better than many of their more expensive growth counterparts if there is a broad market sell-off.

*Other Risks*

EPD’s business is very well-capitalized, but there are other tail risks to be aware of.

Changes in energy sector regulation, tax policies for MLPs and corporations, or export agreements could negatively affect EPD. In addition, their assets are exposed to the possibility of natural catastrophes that could halt their operation.

The mitigation for this, as with any investment, is to keep EPD as an appropriately-sized position within a diversified portfolio if you choose to invest in it. A good investment strategy is about the success and reliability of the process, rather than the booms or busts of any one particular investment.

**Conclusions**

Overall, EPD is a steady investment with a high yield. It has a low payout ratio, debt level, and valuation relative to its long-term history.

With the S&P 500 starting to reach particularly high valuations, businesses like EPD that have a blend of quality and value are becoming ever more appealing. However, while MLPs are a tax-efficient structure, it does require an extra form during tax season.

Investors can decide for themselves whether EPD fits within their investment plan, but I think there’s a lot to like for these units while they remain under $30. It could make a good investment for dollar-cost averaging into at current levels and through any rough patches that might be ahead.
