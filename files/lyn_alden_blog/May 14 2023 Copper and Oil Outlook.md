## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity indicators continue to trend sideways:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Federal Reserve continues to pull liquidity out of the market, while the Treasury Department continues to push liquidity into the market, which has resulted in a sideways liquidity situation overall. We’re still on the liquidity playbook that I outlined back in January.

> Going forward, this sideways liquidity situation is likely set to continue for the next few months. The Fed is still reducing its balance sheet (negative for liquidity) while the debt ceiling issue is likely to result in lower Treasury General Account balances (ironically positive for liquidity) until the debt ceiling is resolved, as described in the next section.
>
> When the debt ceiling is raised and the Treasury Department begins refilling its Treasury General Account with more bond issuance later this year, then that would likely be very negative for liquidity, assuming the Fed is still reducing their balance sheet at that point. That would be a negative double-whammy for liquidity.
>
> -January 23, 2023 Premium Report

However, the Treasury is running low now. They have less than $150 billion in their general cash account, even after the gains from April tax season. This chart shows the weekly average but the end-of-week figure is lower:

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-tga.png)

The upcoming debt ceiling deadline later this spring or in early summer creates a significant amount of market uncertainty. If they run out of cash without a deal to expand the debt ceiling, they’ll need to either miss some Social Security payments, miss some military paychecks, or miss some bond payments temporarily. Tax revenue is smaller than expenditures, and so without being able to draw down cash or issue new debt, they would have to temporarily cut expenditures down to incoming tax revenue levels.

Alternatively, President Biden could pull a power move and invoke the 14th Amendment to the U.S. Constitution to override the debt ceiling and direct Secretary Yellen to issue more debt anyway. The 14th Amendment [states](https://constitution.congress.gov/browse/essay/amdt14-S4-1/ALDE_00000849/), “The validity of the public debt of the United States, authorized by law, including debts incurred for payment of pensions and bounties for services in suppressing insurrection or rebellion, shall not be questioned.” If he does that, it would be up to the Supreme Court to decide whether that’s valid or not.

However, once they raise the debt ceiling one way or another, the Treasury will no longer be offsetting the Federal Reserve’s cash drain since they’ll have no more cash reserves to empty into the market. They will likely be pulling liquidity out of the market to rebuild their cash reserves. If that Treasury cash build comes out of reverse repos (which can only be done with an uptick in the issuance of short-term T-bills) then it is neutral for liquidity. If that Treasury cash build comes out of bank reserves, then that’s very negative for liquidity.

There are going to be some tough decision points ahead between the Treasury and the Federal Reserve, because bank reserve liquidity is already near the minimum workable level.

**Credit Indicators**

The quarterly senior loan officer survey came out for Q2 2023, and doesn’t look great.

Banks continue to report tightening credit standards to businesses (with gray areas representing recessions):

![Credit Tightening](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-credit-tightening.png)

Banks also continue to report weakening demand from businesses:

![Credit Demand](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-credit-demand.png)

A bullish counterargument to this is that by the time that credit demand and credit tightness were this bad in past periods, it was already a good buying opportunity for equities.

A related point about this is that we’re not in a very credit-driven cycle this time. This 2021-2023 cycle was instead driven by pent-up demand, extra savings from stimulus, and gains in nominal wages. So, credit conditions may need to be weaker for longer in order to have the types of negative economic effects that such a credit-constrained environment normally has.

I currently hold seven bank stocks across various portfolios. They represent a low single digit percentage of each portfolio, with different exposures in different portfolios. There is also some indirect bank exposure through ETFs.

* HDFC Bank of India (HDB)
* DBS Group Holdings of Singapore (DBSDY)
* Bank of Nova Scotia (BNS)
* Itau Unibanco of Brazil (ITUB)
* Truist Financial of the United States (TFC)
* U.S. Bancorp of the United States (USB)
* Discover Financial of the United States (DFS)

Year-to-date, all seven have outperformed the U.S. regional bank ETF. In particular, the four non-U.S. banks have been fine, and Discover Financial within the U.S. has been fine. The two big and diversified U.S. ones, Truist and U.S. Bancorp, have ironically been the weak ones.

![Bank Stock Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-bank-stock-returns.png)

I’ve not sold any bank stocks, but I’ve not been directly “buying the dip” at this point either. Many banks in the United States have a real vulnerability depending on the Federal Reserve’s actions. The worst of the liquidity crisis may be over, but profitability risks remain. Banks may need to cease buybacks and slow their dividend growth in order to retain and grow their capital over the next year or two as a form of defense or due to regulatory actions.

I expected last year that they would increase rates by maybe 300 basis points at most and not cause financial instability, but instead they’ve increased by 500 basis points and caused quite a bit of financial instability.

Ironically, those with the safest, lowest-yielding assets have been among the most hurt, while those with more credit exposure (higher rates, shorter duration, but higher risk for default) have held up better. And international bank peers have greatly outperformed. For example, U.S. bank stocks quickly gave up five years of outperformance vs European bank stocks with this crisis.

When a central bank holds interest rates at zero for a prolonged period of time, it encourages a lot of debt accumulation. If they encounter inflation and raise rates sharply, they risk causing massive defaults that threaten the solvency of the entire system. In the 1940s, they eased off of the zero bound gradually, while here in the 2020s they chose a different path of ham fisting the interest rate up, which puts large swaths of the financial sector at risk.

![Century Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-century-chart-annotated.png)

In other words, in the Fed’s quest to quell inflation via aggressive monetary tightening, they increased financial instability compared to our international peers. These actions also increased federal interest expense and reduced asset prices, which combined to blow up the deficit:

![Deficit and Interest](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-deficit-interest.png)

However, unlike the 2008 crisis (which was driven by credit defaults), this one is easily controllable by the Federal Reserve if it gets bad enough. The crisis has been a duration/rates problem rather than credit losses, and the Fed always has the option to address that type of duration/rates problem. What it may take, however, is giving up on their inflation fight earlier than they’d like to. They currently have to balance between inflation and banking stability.

As a separate but related matter, the big area of credit that remains a well-known concern is commercial real estate exposure. Higher interest rates threaten that industry, and office exposure particularly. This tweet by Amy Nixon shows a large number of banks’ exposure to commercial real estate:

![Bank CRE Exposure](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-bank-cre-loans.jpg)

The large banks like TFC, PNC, HBAN, FITB, and USB have very little commercial real estate lending exposure, and of that, only a small percentage is in office or other highly-stressed properties. Their main concern instead remains on interest rate differentials between deposits and their low-yielding assets, along with the need to build and strengthen their capital positions against profitability headwinds.

However, the long tail of small and medium banks have quite a bit of commercial real estate exposure and thus should be analyzed for serious credit risks in addition to any liquidity/duration risks they have been facing.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has recently been consolidating again, down from its highs of over $30,000 that it reached in April. It remains cheap relative to its moving averages.

![Bitcoin Averages](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-bitcoin-averages.png)

For most people who want some bitcoin as part of their asset mix, I think dollar-cost averaging is the optimal way to do it rather than trying to trade it too much. [Swan.com](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/)‘s internal analysis found that users who have an automatic purchase plan are more likely to be positive relative to their cost basis than those who do not.

As an example, if someone set up a plan to put $50/week into bitcoin over the past four years, they’d be up 72%. They would have bought at the highs, bought at the lows, and generally made use of the notorious volatility without trying to time it.

[![](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-swan-dollar-cost-averaging.png)](https://www.swanbitcoin.com/alden/)

**Ordinals BRC-20 Speculation**

The 2021 Taproot soft fork for Bitcoin enabled some new features, which this year have been explored in a rather speculative manner.

The first round of it came earlier this year with an Ordinal/Inscription framework that allowed people to easily insert large NFT images into the discounted signature portion of the Bitcoin blockspace. The second round of it came in recent weeks with the BRC-20 protocol, which allows people to make meme coins on Bitcoin. The coins don’t really do anything other than exist and be trade-able, and there are thousands of different ones with low liquidity and market capitalizations.

This second round caused quite a speculative mania, and briefly drove fee rates up considerably. These speculators burned a lot of capital to pay Bitcoin miner fees, and a subset of the speculators unethically used their social media followers as exit liquidity (buying a coin, and then promoting it to their following to pump the price while quietly selling it). Meanwhile, proponents of relatively unused blockchains with a lot of spare capacity used the opportunity to criticize Bitcoin and draw attention to their own blockchains instead.

There might be further waves of it, but the current wave has died down, as shown by the fee pressure. Fees themselves are what serve as protection against spam:

![Bitcoin Fees](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-bitcoin-fees.png)

In the long run as usage of the capped block space grows, fees will serve as the way to prioritize transactions and keep the blockchain secure. For now, occasional bursts of fees serve as catalysts for various layer-two network development and adoption to increase. Binance for example announced they would integrate the Lightning network for withdrawals, and a number of other companies in the space accelerated their Lightning integration plans as well.

Layers built on top of the Bitcoin network, such as Lightning, allow various transactions to occur and then settle in batches on the base layer, which compresses fees and makes more dense usage of base layer block space.

**MicroStrategy Conference**

MicroStrategy (MSTR) held their annual MicroStrategy World conference in Orlando a week and a half ago. The first half of the conference focused on enterprise analytics (their primary software line), and the second half focused on Bitcoin and Lightning for corporations. I spoke on both the Bitcoin and Lightning days, and participated in a number of networking events with executives from various companies, fund managers from various institutions, and a number of startup founders. Overall, it was very constructive, with an emphasis on emerging technologies.

For people who want to hear my overview of why I think Bitcoin is an important technology, you can see my first-day [panel discussion here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=5PuPf6SBXAA). I was joined by Andrew Moss (Vice President, Global Digital Strategist at Bank of America), Mark Palmer (Equity Research Analyst, Fintech and Digital Assets at Berenberg Capital Markets), and Shirish Jajodia (Vice President, Treasury and Investor Relations at MicroStrategy).

MicroStrategy also announced a new Lightning product offering in the form of [Lightning rewards](https://www.microstrategy.com/en/lightning/lightning-rewards). Their enterprise software will enable corporations to turn every email address at the corporation into a Lightning address, and setup programmable Lightning rewards for employees or users for certain actions. The product integrates into the backend of various applications, so for example MicroStrategy internally set up a system where employees who are on time to Zoom meetings automatically earn a reward.

![Lightning Rewards](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-mstr-lightning-rewards.jpg)

-Lighting rewards for **employees** can be budgeted and programmed to be given out to people who are on time to meetings, reach fitness app goals (e.g. walk 10,000 steps in a day), write company blog posts, respond to company forum posts, or anything else a company might want to incentivize.

-Lightning rewards for **users/customers** can be budgeted and programmed to be given out to people who sign up to a free subscription, fill out surveys, write product reviews, refer other customers, reach loyalty milestones, participate in events, and so forth. They can serve as part of a marketing budget to draw actions towards their sales funnel or product offering.

Unlike airline points or other closed-system rewards, fractional bitcoin (known as “satoshis” or “sats”) can be withdrawn by the recipient into the full open-source interconnected ecosystem and saved or spent wherever they want. And because they can be received instantly upon performing the triggering action (e.g. joining on time for a Zoom meeting and seeing the rewarded sats hit the wallet immediately), they have a rather satisfying feedback connection to the behavior that caused the reward.

A moat that MicroStrategy has, is that they have multi-decade software sales relationships with major enterprise and government customers. So, providing additional software solutions such as various Bitcoin/Lightning tools is a natural fit. It remains to be seen what adoption will look like once the product is launched, but I like the concept and I think it’s smart for the company to blend its software and Bitcoin interests by releasing software related to Bitcoin for its enterprise customers.

## Copper and Oil Outlook

The next six months remain highly uncertain. The debt ceiling impasse (potentially disruptive for markets), the refilling of the Treasury General Account (potentially negative for liquidity), and the possibility of a U.S. recession remain uncertain variables to navigate.

And what makes them particularly uncertain is that they all involve policymaker decisions at certain inflection points. The outcome cannot be known just from current data.

**Copper Outlook**

I continue to monitor the U.S. business cycle closely, along with the European business cycle and Chinese business cycle more loosely. And along with that, I am monitoring the copper-to-gold ratio. The dynamic between gold being a safe haven asset and copper being an industrial/construction metal tends to create a price ratio that closely follows the U.S. business cycle in rate of change terms, which can be summarized with the purchasing manager’s index:

![Copper to Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-copper-gold-ratio.png)

From 2020 to 2021, the U.S. economic growth rate accelerated (purchasing manager’s index heading up), and copper outperformed gold. Since 2021, the U.S. economic growth rate has decelerated (purchasing manager’s index heading down) while copper has been underperforming gold. I think it probably has a bit more to go before it reverses, but we’ll see.

I bought Southern Copper (SCCO) in 2019, and then in [June 2020 I emphasized copper more significantly](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-6-21/), being long both Southern Copper and Freeport-McMoRan (FCX). I expected to hold these positions for years, but then they immediately doubled and tripled.

For my newsletter portfolio, I got out of the positions by early 2021. Here was FCX being bought and sold from the newsletter portfolio, for example:

![FCX Buy/Sell](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-fcx-buy-sell.png)

I watched it grind up and down and go a bit higher after that point, and wondered at times if I sold a bit too early. It has now been rangebound for two years, and so exiting the positions ended up not being a bad move.

I’m starting to get interested in copper and copper stocks again, but am not re-buying quite yet. I expect to probably be a buyer within the next 3-6 months, so I want to take a moment to put copper on our radar again.

Most estimates suggest that global copper production is set to stagnate while global demand is set to gradually increase:

![Copper Outlook](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-copper-outlook.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Rystad Energy](https://ajot.com/news/article/copper-supply-deficit-of-6-million-tons-by-2030-threatens-renewables-evs-as-investment-lags-demand)*

Demand growth comes primarily from the developing world. Countries like India, for example, are rapidly growing economies with far less per-capita copper usage than the developed world. As countries like India eventually become middle-income countries, it means a lot more copper consumption.

Renewable energy systems and electrical vehicles necessitate a lot more copper consumption if they become larger shares of the market. Wind turbines use a ton of copper per unit of power produced, and an electric vehicle uses a lot more copper than an internal combustion vehicle. In addition, if a large portion of society shifts to electric vehicles, it requires the electrical grid to have more throughput, which means more copper and related metals.

This is all somewhat offset by the likelihood that China’s copper demand will stagnate, as they’ve already performed massive amounts of construction. In addition, engineers keep finding ways to use a little bit less material for their designs, which leads to copper demand generally coming in slightly under estimates.

On the supply side, the main issues are decreasing ore grade and uncertain jurisdictions. Some of the most copper-rich deposits have already been heavily exploited, leaving the lower-grade ore deposits remaining (which means higher costs of extraction). There are new discoveries, but they tend to have lower ore grades and/or exist in risky jurisdictions. Plus, it’s just a matter of time and money; discovering, permitting, and developing large new copper mines is a decade-long process. We will likely need another long capex cycle to bring more copper supply to market.

Meanwhile, copper inventories are historically quite low. If supply exceeds production demand, there isn’t much of an inventory buffer:

![Copper Inventory](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-copper-inventory.png)

*Chart Source: [MacroMicro](https://en.macromicro.me/charts/943/lme-copper-warehouse-stock)*

FCX used to have upwards of $20 billion in net debt, and as recently as June 2020 still had over $8 billion in net debt. In recent years, they’ve gotten down to sub-$3 billion in net debt, which is low for how much money they make in a good year.

![FCX Net Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-fcx-net-debt.png)

The company is reasonably priced, but has room to get a bit cheaper this year:

![FCX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-fcx-fastgraph.png)

I’m bullish on copper and copper stocks for the long run, but want to see more evidence of a capitulation point in the economy and price. Copper prices are decreasing, the copper/gold ratio is decreasing, and various economic measures continue to decelerate. As there are signs of a bottom in some of these metrics, copper stocks are likely to be a useful bullish position to have from that point forward.

**Oil Outlook**

WTI crude has been chopping along between the high $60s and low $70s a barrel since late last year:

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-wti-crude.png)

Positioning in adjacent markets (gasoil futures in this case) is pretty wiped out:

![Gasoil Positioning](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-gasoil-futures.png)

The United States continues to draw down its Strategic Petroleum Reserve, although the energy secretary [said](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/us-could-start-buying-oil-reserve-after-june-sale-energy-secretary-2023-05-11/) they might begin refilling it in the second half of the year.

![Strategic Petroleum Reserve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-strategic-petroleum-reserve.png)

Recessions can reduce demand for oil around the margins, although the low economic growth rate (PMI below 50, for example) has likely already done a lot of that. There are all sorts of things that could push oil lower in the next six months or so, but the [long-term supply and demand picture](https://www.lynalden.com/energy-problems/) remains very attractive.

The iShares Global Energy ETF (IXC) is one of the easiest ways to get diversified long-term exposure to oil stocks. Exxon Mobil (XOM) and Chevron (CVX) make up a little over a quarter of the fund, followed by the European and Canadian oil majors, and then various other North American producers and pipeline operators. It has a 0.40% annual expense ratio which is reasonable.

Aside from that, BP is becoming interesting at current levels.

![BP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-bp-fastgraph.png)

BP (BP) is one of the least-liked oil majors, partly because of the [2010 Deepwater Horizon disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deepwater_Horizon) (which they have paid tens of billions of dollars over) and partly because BP has gone back and forth a bit on the energy transition topic. While Exxon Mobil takes a stance that they are an oil and gas company, BP has attempted to take an approach of embracing the energy transition theme in a more diversified way.

In addition to producing oil and gas, BP is heavily investing in electric charging stations, biofuels, hydrogen fuels, and truck stops with convenience stores and fueling stations. In general, the European oil majors have invested more into non-hydrocarbon energy than U.S. oil majors have, and the market hasn’t really liked that. BP trades at a lower valuation than its peers, at under 6x forward earnings.

The company pays a dividend yield of over 4%, is aggressively buying back inexpensive shares, and has sharply reduced its net debt. While I don’t think the management is exceptional, the assets have a wide capital moat, the company is cheap, and actively making use of its cheapness by returning capital to shareholders.

I think a number of oil major stocks have a good probability of going up 2x or 3x in price this decade, while paying out 3-5% dividend yields along the way.

## Final Thoughts: China’s Auto Exports

This isn’t super recent but in my opinion is not getting enough attention. China’s auto export sector came together strongly in recent years, resulting in a rocket ship of automobile export growth:

![China Auto Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-5-14-china-auto.jpg)

We don’t really see these cars in the United States, but for example I see them frequently when I visit family in Egypt each year. A large portion of global car demand is now being served by China, to the point where they are basically #2 after Japan now. A skeptic may say Chinese cars are of low quality, but then again, the same things were said about South Korean cars initially and they now compete solidly at the middle quality level.

One of China’s goals over the past couple decades has been to move up the value chain. They began by exporting inexpensive items very cheaply, but have increased their industrial and technical prowess to capture higher value export markets. The emergence of a serious auto export market is an example of ongoing success in this regard.

However, countries generally run into the [middle income trap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_income_trap) along this path. After a successful initial industrialization period, they become wealthy and developed enough that they are no longer a source of super-cheap labor, but they are also not developed enough to compete for the high end of the market, and thus begin to economically stagnate at that middle level. China is currently near the top end of the middle income bracket.

Due to this and other factors, there’s only a small number of countries over the past fifty years that have actually “emerged”. Very few countries go from being classified as emerging markets to developed markets.

On one hand, if there’s a country large and powerful enough to break out of the middle income trap, it’s China. They now surpass the United States as the largest economy in the world by some metrics, and are #2 in others.

On the other hand, they face considerable demographics challenges as a big hurdle. The natural path would be to shift from an industrializing export powerhouse to a more balanced production/consumption economy, but it’s unclear that their political/economic model is flexible enough to make that transition. Their international trade connections are on the right path, but it’s unclear that their internal policy is.

When forecasting what the results will be for China’s ongoing efforts to internationalize their currency for payments and be held in reserves by trading partners, one question that pops up by critics is, “what would countries do with the yuan?” China is not exactly well known for its rule of law and depth of capital markets like the United States has been historically. The answer, generally, is that countries can hold the currency for a time and plow it back into Chinese goods, since China is the largest trading partner for most countries.
