# Brookfield Infrastructure (BIP/BIPC) Analysis

*Published April 26, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report provides a macroeconomic update, discusses the latest portfolio changes, and then dives into a relatively high-yielding global infrastructure company.

## U.S. Business Cycle Update

Nations around the world continue to deal with the COVID-19 economic shutdown, with job losses and cessation of economic activity hitting record-setting levels worldwide.

The United States now has over 26 million cumulative initial jobless claims in the past 5 weeks and growing, from a starting pool of over 152 million nonfarm payroll jobs in February 2020.

The number of weekly new claims had a baseline of 220,000 per month before the crisis (normal job churn), quickly jumped to 3.3 million in the first week of the crisis, then hit a peak of 6.8 million in the second week, and is now coming in with lower (but still extremely high) weekly numbers. It looks like this will reach over 30 million jobless claims before quarantines start to reverse.

Weekly jobless claims:

![Initial Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-initial-claims.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/ICSA)*

In comparison, the largest weekly number of initial jobless claims in the 2008/2009 recession was less than 700,000. The total job losses during that recession were smaller and more spread out over time.

Analysts expecting a sharp V-shaped recovery are likely unrealistic. The sheer speed and magnitude of this decline is specific to the virus, but we were in a late-cycle business environment before the virus struck, with very high business and household leverage ratios and declining positive growth rates. This will take time to normalize in terms of jobs and earnings.

Historically, it takes years for jobs to fully return:

![Nonfarm Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-nonfarm-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/PAYEMS)*

We’ll likely get some sharp bounces in job recoveries whenever restaurants, hotels, and other service-oriented gathering places can reopen. But overall, it’s likely to be a grind to get the remaining jobs back as many weak companies fail to re-open and many strong companies learn to do more with less.

Companies can’t really hire more people until they have good prospects for sales/customers, and they won’t have good prospects for sales/customers until more people have incomes, and a lot of people won’t have incomes (other than government support) until companies hire more people. It’s the classic recession negative feedback loop that takes time to recover from even when the initial catalyst for a recession is no longer ongoing.

**Global PMIs**

Composite purchasing manager’s indices are among the best monthly indicators for economic growth or contraction, because unlike many economic indicators, they are reported without much of a lag and represent close to a “real time” monthly assessment.

Anything below 50 is a contraction, and anything above 50 is an expansion. Metrics over 55 are considered pretty strong growth, while about 47 or less gets firmly into recession territory. Below 40 gets into severe recession or depression territory.

PMIs are divided into manufacturing and service portions, or the “composite” which includes both. Historically, manufacturing tends to be more cyclical than services. During a recession, manufacturing takes a bigger hit than restaurants, for example. However, this virus has specifically hit the services sector harder than the manufacturing sector, which is even more problematic for many countries because more people are employed in services than manufacturing.

Since the virus originated in China, they are a couple months ahead of the United States on this economic timeline, but their economic reports are not considered very trustworthy, so they offer limited but still useful insight into the virus that we need to collaborate with other data.

China’s composite purchasing manager’s index went off a cliff and partially rebounded, but still remains at a contraction/recession level.

Back in February and March, traffic congestion in Wuhan where the virus originated was nonexistent, while cities like Beijing and Shanghai remained closer to normal.

Now in late April, Wuhan traffic congestion during workdays is back near normal 2019 levels, but weekend traffic congestion remains nonexistent. In other words, people are working but not doing many discretionary trips.

## Brookfield Infrastructure (BIP/BIPC) Deep-Dive Analysis

Brookfield Infrastructure Partners LP (BIP) and its newer equivalent Brookfield Infrastructure Corporation (BIPC) is a business that focuses on acquiring, developing, and operating critical infrastructure throughout the world.

Brookfield Asset Management (BAM) is the parent corporation that owns the general partner of BIP and nearly a third of its limited partnership units. BAM supplies the executive team to operate BIP in exchange for getting a management fee and incentive distribution payouts that are tied to distribution increases. BAM itself has a very large amount of insider ownership; the executives are strongly aligned with investor interests in BAM and BIP.

BAM owns a collection of partnerships in different areas:

![BAM Structure](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-bip-structure.png)

BIP is structured as a partnership that issues a K-1 form during tax season, while BIPC is a newly-formed corporation that holds equivalent assets to BIP. As a normal corporation, it has a simpler tax situation, equivalent to any typical stock. Shares of BIPC are exchangeable for units of BIP, and pays the same dividend/distribution. They’re basically the same thing, but in different wrappers. Many investors only invest in corporations, not partnerships, so this option gives more investors an opportunity to get exposure to the business. In addition, BIPC will be eligible for inclusion in various equity indices, unlike BIP due to its partnership status.

The company owns electrical utilities, natural gas pipelines, toll roads, railroads, shipping ports, data centers, fiber backbones, and cell towers, with these assets spread around on five continents:

![BIP Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-bip-map.png)

*Source: [March 2020 BIP Corporate Profile](https://bip.brookfield.com/)*

Their portfolio of assets is divided as follows into these different types:

![BIP Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-bip-chart.png)

*Source: [March 2020 BIP Corporate Profile](https://bip.brookfield.com/)*

BAM and BIP use a counter-cyclical value-oriented investment approach. During recessions (whether it’s Europe in 2009 or Brazil in 2016, as two examples), BAM and BIP invest heavily in cheap assets from distressed sellers. During periods of strong economic growth, they sell fully-valued assets and raise liquidity. Since inception, BIP has sold 15 businesses, generating $4.5 billion in gross proceeds at an average internal rate of return of 22%.

Management and Performance

BIP was launched as a partnership by BAM during the global financial crisis over a decade ago. At the time, BAM management acquired a lot of good infrastructure assets from bankrupt companies for pennies on the dollar, and has continued to accumulate (and then occasionally sell) good assets since. They recycle capital from selling fully-assets into other cheap assets during downturns or areas of industry weakness.

In terms of performance, BIP has crushed the market since inception with a collection of “boring” assets:

![BIP Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-bip-performance.png)

I initially invested in BIP in 2010, and it has been my best investment in terms of total absolute returns due to the size of my position. I let it ride for many years and collecting distributions, briefly sold BIP to buy the parent company BAM instead, and then ended up buying BIP back in a different account, so I have exposure to both BIP and BAM and have been following the company for a decade now. The same two CEOs of BAM and BIP are still in place after this past decade.

BIP has a BBB+ credit rating and a moderately strong balance sheet. They also have a liquidity lifeline to their parent company BAM as an extra layer of protection. 90% of their debt is fixed-rate, and 85% of their debt is non-recourse, meaning the debt is tied to individual assets rather than connected to the top structure of BIP itself.

Ever since the business matured a few years after inception, their debt/EBITDA ratio has been very stable:

![BIP Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-bip-debt.png)

Of their cash flow, 95% is contractually regulated, 75% is indexed to inflation, and 60% is without volume risk.

Valuation Analysis

In my view, due to focus of BIP on the growth of its distribution/dividend payouts, the dividend discount model is an ideal framework for valuing the business compared to other alternatives. The company spends 60-70% of its FFO on dividends and uses the other 30-40% for reinvestment and growth.

BIP targets 5-9% annual dividend growth, but they have achieved an average of 11% annual dividend growth. From 2009 to 2010 they had sluggish distribution/dividend growth because it was a big period of investment in cyclical assets, but once the global economy began to stabilize, BIP’s payouts increased as it harvested the fruit from its investments. We could very well see a rocky or flattish period from 2020-2021 for the dividend as well.

If the company merely achieves the low end of its target distribution growth range and therefore achieves 5% annual long-term dividend growth (alongside the current 5.7% forward dividend yield), it can provide 10%+ long-term annual total returns.

Here’s a dividend discount analysis:

![BIP Dividend Discount Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-bip-ddm.png)

The company is trading at an above-average dividend yield at the current time, at about 5.7% for the forward yield and nearly 5.5% for the trailing yield. It has historically been a good buy whenever the yield is over 5%, and especially when over 6%.

Company Risks

*Cyclical Risk*

Although 60% of their cash flow has no volume risk, the other 40% of their cash flow does. This includes shipping ports, railways, toll roads, natural gas processing facilities, and so forth. These more cyclical assets are balanced out by some of their more defensive portfolio assets like electrical transmission lines, natural gas midstream pipelines, and cell towers.

Historically, Brookfield management has been exceptional at managing their operations counter-cyclically. They raise capital in good environments and buy assets on the cheap in bad environments. However, the global economic shutdown related to COVID-19 will put that to the test. Commodity prices are crushed, global trade is sharply reduced, traffic levels are way down, and world energy usage is significantly decreased. This is a significant near-term risk.

BIP’s capital structure is resilient and should get them through this, but nothing is for certain. This is an asset-heavy business model that requires a sizable amount of leverage to operate, and BIP makes use of leverage but historically never too much, and always with plenty of liquidity. This liquidity should be enough but we’ll see for sure in the quarters ahead.

*De-globalization Risk*

Over the longer run, this COVID-19 crisis may be a catalyst for a trend towards more localized supply chains and partial de-globalization, which was a trend that was already forming for political and economic reasons. Railways and shipping ports are still critical infrastructure but BIP management must monitor this trend for their transport assets and ensure they have the right geographic mix for the transport portion of their business and that they are not caught off guard by structural changes that could put negative pressure on the volumes of key transport assets.

*Currency Risk*

Since BIP holds assets around the world, they are exposed to a broad basket of currencies. They generally contact their cash flows to be indexed to inflation, and they hedge a portion of their currency exposure, but the fluctuations between currencies do impact their earnings each year for better or worse. As an analyst that views the U.S. dollar as structurally overvalued for the past 5 years, I like having a broad basket of currency exposure at this time, but it still can add volatility.

Brookfield only does business in countries that they view as having a strong commitment to respecting capital, meaning little or no history of forced nationalization of assets or other things of that nature, which is important in emerging market investing.

*Dividend Risk*

BIP has never had a dividend cut, but this is the biggest global slowdown in modern history and will put that to the test.

If the crisis lasts a long time and has a sustained and deeply negative outcome for toll roads, shipping ports, rail freight, and other volume-exposed assets, BIP/BIPC would have a dividend cut risk. And if they cut their dividend, we’d likely see a sudden single-day decline for the stock price of BIP/BIPC as some dividend investors exit their positions. The traffic data in some countries, such as China, appears to bounce back within a few months.

Personally, if they cut the dividend and the stock falls, I would likely buy more after reading management’s explanation and forward plans, depending on the details of the situation. However, if that happens, it will shift more towards a deep-value investment rather than a quality investment.

*Accounting Risk*

Brookfield has often been criticized for exceptionally complex accounting and corporate structures. The parent company owns multiple partnerships and private funds, and the ownership percentages of the assets themselves are often divided between these various investment vehicles.

The layers of ownership and leverage here are very complex. There’s the parent company, partnerships, private funds, and then various assets that have non-recourse debt connected to them individually. BAM often initiates an acquisition as a consortium of buyers, including its partnerships and funds, along with some outside companies occasionally. Due to the complexity, fraud would be easy to pull off, and so investors rely in part on the executive team acting in good faith. They are of course audited by outside auditors, but that is never a perfect defense.

The company can re-value its estimated asset values each quarter. Because Brookfield sometimes owns a portion of an asset while another company owns the other portion (e.g. let’s say each one has a 50% stake in a Brazilian toll road, for example), there are sometimes cases where one firm has the asset booked at one price, and the other firm has the same asset booked at another price.

If Brookfield were to have a history of over-stating its asset values, it would be a major problem. However, BAM CEO Bruce Flatt has shown that for the past couple decades, the criticisms against the organization haven’t been valid. Their accounting/valuations are consistently shown to be accurate; on average, they have been able to sell assets at or above the book value that they had them listed as, and well above the purchase price. They consistently walk away from bidding wars if the price for desirable acquisitions gets too high, and they walked away from several bidding wars in 2019. They have been careful to buy assets only when they are priced below their calculated replacement costs. Their long-term returns, especially for the infrastructure assets within BIP, have been very strong.

Although the company has had a long history of appropriate valuation, I do think it’s possible that some of their commercial properties could be valued lower in the years ahead. However, Brookfield tends to be willing to re-purpose retail space when appropriate and to focus on premium properties rather than discount properties. At the current time, I like the infrastructure assets in BIP/BIPC more than some of the commercial property elsewhere within the BAM network of assets.

Still, it’s important to reduce single-stock risk in a portfolio, especially for complex businesses. Generally speaking, no individual stock position should be large enough as a percentage of a portfolio such that if it were to face problems, it would substantially affect a portfolio.

Conclusion

Many defensive assets have been bid up to extraordinary prices. For example, Clorox stock is trading at a price/earnings ratio of nearly 30, despite having low single-digit earnings growth and a dividend yield slightly over 2%. This is a very high [PEG ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/pe-ratio/).

![Clorox FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-4-26-clx-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

This type of stock works well in a deflationary and fear-driven economic environment, especially when everyone suddenly wants disinfectants. If 10-year Treasuries yield only 0.60%, and many companies will be unprofitable this year, Clorox with an otherwise low 3.5% earnings yield and 2.2% dividend yield is not so hard to justify, as people still need their products. With safe alternatives like Treasuries offering very low returns for buy-and-hold investors (below the Federal Reserve’s inflation target of 2%), these defensive dividend payers have been understandably bid up to high prices.

However, an otherwise safe stock, when priced at that level, faces its own set of risks on the other side of this crisis. If inflation increases in the years ahead, for example, stocks like this would likely be re-priced at lower valuations. In other words, a safe company at a price that’s too high, is no longer truly safe.

For me, the sweet spot for risk and reward in this environment is finding companies that do have economic exposure and have gone down in stock price, but that have solid balance sheets, adequate liquidity, and enough resiliency and asset quality to get through the crisis and come out strong on the other side. I like Brookfield Infrastructure for that purpose, but it is not without risk, and like all investments, is best balanced by other assets within a diversified portfolio.
