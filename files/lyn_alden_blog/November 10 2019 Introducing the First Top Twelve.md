## U.S. Business Cycle Status

S&P 500 earnings for Q3 came in better than some feared, with 75% of companies beating estimates so far. However, the estimates were very low to start with. With 90% of the S&P 500 having reported third quarter results as of this writing, the average year-over-year revenue growth was 3.7% and the average EPS growth was -1%. This was inclusive of buybacks propping up EPS, so company-wide net income dropped a bit further than just -1%.

Despite this, the S&P 500 roared to new all time highs this past week. With stock earnings down and stock prices up, the cyclically-adjusted price-to-earnings (CAPE) ratio of the S&P 500 is back over 30:

![CAPE Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-cape-chart.png)

*Data Source: Prof. Robert Shiller*

Putting the stock market aside, here is a heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy for the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms:

![Business Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years (20 quarters), and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

When you see red on the chart, it means an indicator is near its slowest period of growth during the five-year period (some of which are negative). When you see green, it means the indicator is growing at around the fastest pace in the past five years.

We had a significant economic slowdown back in 2015/2016, which you can see with all the red around that time period. However, construction spending was still at its peak growth rate, payroll growth was solid, and the yield curve was still steep.

Then in 2017/2018, we had a strong period of growth. Tax cuts were introduced, and business optimism increased.

Currently in 2019, we have a slowdown that is more broad-based than was the case in 2015/2016, with construction in the red, payroll growth slowing, and the yield curve flat or inverted, but some of the other areas, while slowing, haven’t gotten quite as bad as they were in 2015/2016.

During the the 2012/2013 period and the 2015/2016 period, the U.S. had significant economic slowdowns, but they were not enough to cause an actual recession. You can see these slowdowns in the U.S. ISM manufacturing purchasing manager’s index:

![PMI Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Right now in late 2019, we’re experiencing the third slowdown during this decade-long economic expansion. There are some indications that the economy is stabilizing and potentially heading back up for now after this soft patch, rather than falling deeper. Or it could be a fake-out with a small period of stabilization before falling deeper. Based on the evidence, I lean towards the latter as being more probable (a fake-out to the upside followed by a deeper slowdown), but it’s still not clear. We need to remain data-dependent.

Growth estimates by economists and analysts are very low for the fourth quarter of this year and into early 2020. As of this writing, the Atlanta Fed has a GDPNow estimate of 1.0% for Q4, while the New York Fed has a Nowcast of 0.73% for Q4. Both of these figures are lower than the GDP we just had in Q3.

In addition, consumer and business debt has continued to accumulate for the duration of the business cycle, which puts more and more pressure on each of these “mini cycles” to eventually be hard to recover from without an actual recession and a period of deleveraging.

As new monthly data come in, we’ll keep getting an updated picture of what the economy is doing in rate-of-change terms. Right now, we should be prepared for both scenarios of 1) upside stabilization and a 2) downside deeper fall, and we can start leaning one way or another more heavily as more data comes in.

## International Spotlight: Turkey

Last month, I [wrote about](https://www.lynalden.com/trade-deficit/) how currency crises can bring about positive changes in a country’s trade balance and current account balance.

After this past year’s currency crisis, Turkey recently reported a positive current account balance over a 12-month period for the first time in at least 15 years.

![Turkey Current Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-turkey-ca.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

The international report in the Google Drive (linked to from the [members area](https://www.lynalden.com/members/)) shows more detail about Turkey, but this is a positive development.

Turkey remains a risky investment, but it’s showing signs of stabilization. At current low valuations, the Turkish market could benefit dramatically if the U.S. dollar were to weaken, because one of Turkey’s main economic problems right now is the large amount of external dollar-denominated debt in their corporate sector. (Their government and household sectors both have low debt levels.)

I consider the iShares MSCI Turkey ETF (TUR) to be a buy, currently hold it in the international ETF section of my newsletter portfolio, and am continuing to dollar-cost average into it. However, this is a volatile and risky investment, suitable only for aggressive investors, and for a small position at most.

By keeping close track of a bunch of high-quality companies and putting money into them when they get cheap, either because they are out of favor or because they have run into short-term issues, investors can build substantial wealth and dividend income over the long run.

The market tends to overshoot both up and below fair value. The market, being rather emotional overall, frequently magnifies those fundamental bumps and spikes, so the stock price is often more volatile than the underlying fundamentals.

**Amgen (AMGN)**

The healthcare sector appears to be one of the few reasonably-priced areas of the market at the moment.

However, there is a good reason for that, because healthcare affordability is a major topic going into the 2020 U.S. elections. Americans pay a far higher per-capita cost of healthcare than any other country, including highly-advanced countries with longer average lifespans and lower average infant mortality rates than Americans, so healthcare companies are directly in the cross-hairs of many U.S. politicians.

High-quality biotechnology companies like Amgen are well-positioned even in the face of some cost pressure. Amgen has a robust research pipeline with a reasonable degree of drug diversification, high returns on invested capital, and net debt equal to about one year of company free cash flow. I’d be more concerned about health insurers than pharmaceutical companies and medical device makers from a political standpoint.

Now that we’re over a decade into this business cycle, many investors are rightfully concerned about the prospects of a recession in the next year or two. The healthcare sector, however, is very non-cyclical, and the only thing that generally matters is how the individual products of each company perform against competition. For example, Amgen’s stock price didn’t do anything during the 2008 financial crisis:

The company recently exited from neuroscience research, which is a bit of a disappointment for growth, but this will allow the company to focus more heavily on its core competency areas and return more cash to shareholders.

**HDFC Bank (HDB)**

India consistently has one of the highest GDP growth rates in the world, and doesn’t have the demographic or debt problems that China has.

However, they have a commodity problem, and particularly an energy problem. India has to import most of its energy and several of its key commodities, which is a big drag on the country’s current account balance, which weakens the currency. The amount of energy they have to import relative to GDP is one of the worst in the world.

Starting with my April 2019 Global Opportunities Investment Report (included in the Google Drive link, found in the [members area](https://www.lynalden.com/members/)), I started highlighting Russian and Indian markets as a good complimentary exposure pair in a portfolio.

Russia is a slow-growing economy, but it is super cheap and has an extraordinarily stable financial system, which they’ve built up steadily since their late 1990’s financial crisis. Most importantly, they’re one of the largest producers and exporters of oil and gas, which means their economy does better when energy prices are high. They’re also a big producer and exporter of many agricultural commodities and metals.

India couldn’t be more different. They’re fast-growing, their market is usually on the expensive side, and they prefer an environment of low energy and commodity prices, as a big importer of them. I’m cautiously bullish on India for the long run, but their Achilles’ Heal is their vulnerability to high commodity prices.

Since I’m picking twelve stocks at once for this first article, I wanted a bit of balance between them. This list includes an oil producer, a copper producer, a gold financier, a natural gas transporter, a Canadian bank, and a Russian bank. All else being equal, those stocks prefer high commodity prices, either because they produce them, transport them, or finance them. On the other hand, India’s largest private bank prefers lower commodity prices, indirectly.

HDFC Bank is basically a pure-play on India’s growth and currency stability. The better India grows and the fewer currency problems they have from energy price spikes and other things, the more comfortable of a ride HDFC Bank will likely have, all else being equal. Maintaining a mix of companies that prefer higher or lower commodity prices helps spread out risk.

![HDFC Bank Fast Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-hdb-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

* *Black line = actual stock price*

India is still under-banked, meaning that many people don’t have access to a bank account, and consumer debt as a percentage of GDP is extremely low. Therefore, HDFC Bank and other well-run banks in India can grow faster than India’s GDP for quite a while as India develops a more advanced (and leveraged) financial system.

Based on raw earnings multiples, HDFC Bank is an expensive growth stock. However, based on the [PEG ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/pe-ratio/), the bank is reasonably priced. It doesn’t have as big of a margin of safety as I’d like, but it offers a solid risk/reward balance for long-term investors in my opinion, especially when hedged with commodity producers for balance.

I don’t have a large position yet because I suspect we’ll see better entry prices, but I’m happy to start nibbling at current prices.

**Sandstorm Gold (SAND)**

My favorite small cap stock at the current time is Sandstorm Gold.

Sandstorm is a financier of gold and silver miners; they provide upfront capital to miners so they can develop their projects. In exchange, Sandstorm gets a royalty or a stream; either a percentage of the output or a contract to buy a certain amount of the metal at a very low price. It’s a much safer business model than actual mining.

Miners have been relying on this type of financing heavily because it’s less risky for them than traditional financing in many cases. If they take out fixed debt, then they have to pay that amount back regardless of whether the price of gold goes up or down. It’s hard having fixed liabilities and fluctuating income based on commodity prices they can’t control. However, with a gold royalty contract, their liability is payable as a percentage of output or an amount of gold, so in other words, their liabilities are also tied to the price of the metal they are mining.

The big three gold royalty companies are Franco-Nevada (FNV), Wheaton Precious Metals (WPM) and Royal Gold (RGLD). These three have done very well over the past decade, even during a decade-long bear market in precious metals.

I consider Sandstorm to have a high probability of becoming the next “big” gold royalty company over the next decade. The company was founded by Nolan Watson in 2008 and is still run by him with strong record of success so far. Before founding Sandstorm, Watson became the CFO of Wheaton Precious Metals in his 20’s as the youngest CFO for any NYSE-listed company at the time, and now he’s around 40. Perhaps more than any other sector, management quality in the commodity sector is extremely important, and having the founder of the company on board with a good track record gives this stock an edge.

My shift into this company started when they stopped needing to issue shares to fund their deals:

![Sandstorm Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-sand-shares.png)

Once enough of their previous deals became cash flow positive (because it takes several years for their financing to result in an active mine that sends cash back to Sandstorm), Sandstorm hit a critical point where they can fund new deals from current cash flow. They might still issue shares, but it’s optional now.

**Southern Copper (SCCO)**

Southern Copper is a major copper producer with a low cost of production (bottom quartile in 2019) and the longest mine life (70 years) of any major copper producer in the world.

Some copper producers have more explosive upside potential, but this is one of the safer ones. Still, I consider it an aggressive pick due to the high variability of copper prices and the fact that nearly half of all global copper consumption is by China.

Copper prices have been in a bear market since their peak in 2011.

![Copper Prices](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-copper.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Too much supply was put online in the 2000’s, and as China and other emerging markets started to have a slower growth rate in the 2010’s, commodity prices collapsed worldwide. However, a long-term copper deficit looks likely to start developing in the early 2020’s, with copper producers set to benefit.

As famous natural resource investor Rick Rule often says regarding the materials sector, “*bear markets are the authors of bull markets, and bull markets are the authors of bear markets.*” When commodity prices are high, companies invest heavily in new exploration and mine development, which floods the world with supply and ends up causing a price bust. After years of low commodity prices and nobody willing to invest in new projects, eventually a supply/demand deficit starts to form, driving prices up, and starting the cycle anew.

Eight years into this copper bear market, I’m just now starting to scale into copper exposure, but slowly. Southern Copper will be a bumpy ride with highly variable dividend payouts, but it’s one of the safer ones overall.

One of my deep dives in the future will likely be about copper and copper producers.

**Invesco (IVZ)**

Asset managers have been under pressure for years because low-cost passive investment strategies have eaten their high fee structures. Very few active managers can consistently outperform, and therefore investment management fees are being scrutinized more and more, and rightfully so.

There are a handful of asset managers that have navigated this transition reasonably well. Invesco is a big player in ETFs, including factor ETFs. They own the famous QQQ ETF, but also own various factor-based strategies, including [equal weight ETFs](https://www.lynalden.com/equal-weighted-index-funds/), of which many have performed very well.

However, Invesco has been on an acquisition spree, and the market didn’t like its recent purchase of OppenheimerFunds and its mostly active AUM. Invesco’s growth is having a rough time right now, but the stock price utterly dropped off a cliff:

![Invesco Fast Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-ivz-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

* *Black line = actual stock price*
* *Blue line = price if at its average P/E (15.4)*
* *White line = dividends*

Should Invesco have a relatively low valuation at the current time? Yes.

Should it have a P/E ratio of 7? Probably not.

It takes very negative estimates about the company to justify a valuation this low. It’s certainly *possible* that those very negative scenarios could come to pass, but so far I don’t see significant evidence that they will occur.

Based on my analysis, Invesco’s acquisition made decent sense. They are streamlining the acquired company’s operation and cutting out a layer of redundancy between the two companies to improve their overall economy of scale. It adds risk, but also opportunity. A portion of this active AUM may shift over to their ETF products.

So, Invesco has exposure to problem areas that are being disrupted (actively-managed AUM) as well as to opportunities that are causing that disruption (relatively cheap passive and factor-based ETFs).

With a dividend yield of nearly 7% that is well-covered by earnings, Invesco has a low hurdle to step over in the coming years to be a good investment at today’s prices.

**Enbridge (ENB)**

Enbridge is a Canadian-based transporter of oil and gas across North America, and has grown its dividend by 12% per year on average over the past 20 years. While many midstream companies slashed their dividends during the energy price crash of 2015, Enbridge kept growing theirs despite facing headwinds of their own.

Today’s biggest challenge for Enbridge is that environmental groups keep working with regulators to block them from replacing old pipelines or building new ones, particularly around the border between Canada and the United States. In addition, it’s simply a slower-growth environment all around for energy infrastructure compared to their peak days of massive buildout in the 1990’s and 2000’s.

However, the slower growth rate and the political uncertainty has arguably been more than factored in to today’s low valuation. With a cheap stock price and a yield of about 6%, Enbridge doesn’t need to do much to give investors decent returns going forward. A little bit of growth, some price increases to keep up with inflation, and a 6% yield is a pretty good recipe for low double-digit total returns in a world of high equity valuations and low forward return potential for the broader market.

**PJSC Sberbank (SBRCY)**

Sberbank of Russia is not a company that many investors are deeply familiar with, but it’s a company worth keeping an eye on. When I gave a presentation at the Philadelphia Money Show in September, this is the one stock I choose to emphasize. It trades over the counter (OTC) as an ADR.

![Sberbank Fast Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-sbrcy-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

* *Black line = actual stock price*
* *Blue line = price if at its average P/E (10.0)*
* *White line = dividends*

Sberbank is the largest bank in Russia by far. They have a market share of 25-50% of banking activities depending on the line of business in question. Their returns on equity are often over 20%, and they remained profitable (albeit at a reduced level) through the latest two recessions in Russia. Sberbank currently has a P/E ratio of about 6, a dividend yield north of 6% with a payout ratio below 50%, and they are growing.

They have slightly more employees than the U.S. banking giant J.P. Morgan Chase (over 250,000) but they have a market capitalization of just $80 billion compared to J.P. Morgan Chase’s gargantuan $400 billion market capitalization. There’s a lot of potential here.

And yet, the past has been good as well. If you had invested in Sberbank back in 2004 when it became available over the counter (OTC), and reinvested all dividends, you would have made 16.8% compounded returns with Sberbank compared to only 7.7% from the S&P 500. For each dollar invested in Sberbank back then, you would have more than ten dollars today, compared to about three dollars from an investment in the S&P 500. Still, the stock only has a single-digit P/E ratio today.

The Russian stock market is one of the cheapest in the world by most valuation metrics, and the country has quite a bit of corruption along with slow growth. However, the low price levels and high dividend yields more than compensate for those issues according to my analysis.

Russia was tied in the first place rank back in my April 2019 Global Opportunities Investment Report (which is available for members in the Google Drive, linked from the [members area](https://www.lynalden.com/members/)). The country earned the top scores for low valuation, strong currency fundamentals, and low debt levels. This was partially offset by poor scores for low growth and low stability (mainly from corruption and sanctions rather than actual instability), but the overall score was very strong.

The key with that sort of situation is to be diversified. Would I put half of my money into Russian equities? Of course not. Would I put a small percentage into them, especially the highest-quality companies? Sure.

Sberbank is likely going to be one of the high conviction deep dives, where I’ll go into more detail.
