## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

Liquidity was constrained through tax season, as expected. Especially on tax day itself, there tends to be a liquidity drain from the private sector toward the Treasury general account.

> My expectation is to see another positive liquidity cycle over the next two years, but I continue to look for a breakout of the actual data. In the meantime, liquidity is still rangebound, and overall liquidity data tends to get messy around tax season (April).
>
> -March 31, 2024 report

But while that is relevant for traders, it’s just noise for long term investors. Overall, liquidity continues to be rangebound. As inflation expectations and economic data comes in mixed-but-hot, it has led the bond market to price out rate cuts, and thus strengthen the dollar index. All else being equal, this puts downward pressure on liquidity when it happens.

Here’s the latest snapshot of domestic liquidity:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-domestic-liquidity.png)

And here’s a measure of global liquidity:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-global-m2.png)

In the coming days, we will get more information from the Treasury for their next quarter of bond issuance, which could affect liquidity one way or the other depending on how much short-term or long-term debt they want to issue, and whether they plan to keep their cash account as high as it is now or drain it a bit.

I don’t have a firm view on liquidity over the next 3-6 months, but I have a positive view on liquidity over the next 18 months, and will adjust that view accordingly as more data come in.

A couple weeks ago, the New York Fed [released](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/annual_reports) their 2023 annual report for open market operations. Notably, this includes their projections for what the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet will look like in the years ahead.

They expect a tapering of the rate of balance sheet reduction in 2024, and then a bottoming and resumption in growth in 2025, at a rate that is in line with the growth of nominal GDP:

![Projected Soma](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma.png)

And specifically, they expect a reduction in mortgage-backed securities as a share of holdings, and an increase in U.S. Treasury securities as a share of holdings:

![UST vs MBS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma-ust-vs-mbs.png)While the actual outcome will likely be bumpier and higher than forward expectations (both the Fed and the Congressional Budget Office always assume smooth growth with no recessions ever in their projections), I think these overall projections make sense as a middle range or lower bound, and are in line with my expectations.

And specifically, because I view the United States as being in fiscal dominance mode (running large structural federal deficits that will be accommodated by monetary authorities whenever needed to ensure a functioning liquid Treasury market), I expect nominal GDP to run on the hot side, and for the Fed’s balance sheet to grow roughly in line with it.

The government’s deficits and the Fed’s accommodation of those deficits has a stimulatory effect on nominal GDP, and their balance sheet growth is expected to be in line with nominal GDP. This means that their own balance sheet growth to accommodate ongoing federal deficits and bond issuance contributes to nominal GDP, in a self-reinforcing feedback loop.

In short, nothing stops this train.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The U.S. GDP report for the first quarter of 2024 came out on April 25th, and it was sharply below consensus expectations of 2.5%, at just 1.6% annualized.

And measures of inflation simultaneously came in above expectations. Core PCE came in at 3.7% compared to the expected number of 3.4%.

While this report is not stagflationary in absolute terms since real growth is still positive, it is stagflationary in directional terms. In other words, the growth rate turned lower while inflation simultaneously turned higher and remains above-target.

A lot of the weakness in the GDP report was from the import/export mix. Imports to the U.S. were strong, while exports from the U.S. were weak. This is likely tied to the current strong dollar index. The stronger the dollar gets, the less competitive many U.S. exports become on the global market.

![Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-trade-balance.png)

Meanwhile, the Conference Board released their March numbers on April 18th. Their leading economic index continues to re-accelerate from low levels:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-lei.png)

This reinforces my theme that every sector should be analyzed separately in this unusual economic environment. Since fiscal policy and monetary policy are more divergent than usual, we should expect to see larger than usual differences in performance between various sectors in the economy.

> So, unless something meaningfully changes, I expect to see continued strength in sectors that are not very sensitive to interest rates, and continued weakness in sectors that are sensitive to interest rates, speaking in terms of their fundamentals (revenue and earnings). With a bigger-than-normal divergence between loose fiscal policy and tight monetary policy, comes a bigger-than-normal performance gap between sectors that are more tightly correlated with fiscal policy and monetary policy respectively.
>
> -February 2024 Public Newsletter

Running loose fiscal policy tends to support consumption, while running tight monetary policy helps strengthen the dollar. This combination is pretty good for importers, pretty bad for exporters, and thus pretty bad for the overall trade balance. It’s also pretty good for businesses that are on the receiving side of fiscal deficits or whose customers are on the receiving side of fiscal deficits (such as healthcare or travel-related companies), and it’s pretty bad for levered entities with short duration debt that need to refinance (such as commercial real estate operators).

More conceptually, running loose fiscal policy and tight monetary policy at the same time is like the driver of a car pushing down the gas pedal and using the break at the same time, causing some burnout on the tires. Between the two opposing forces, I expect monetary policy to eventually blink and become more accommodative (in terms of balance sheet capacity more-so than rates per se), and so the fiscal side will win and the car will go forward. The NY Fed’s open market operations report supports that view.

I expect the dollar index to lose some steam once the Federal Reserve is forced to go back to structural monetary base expansion, or if there is some multilateral international agreement to ease the dollar a bit. There are already tensions with the dollar index at current levels. But the current set of forces supports a strong dollar for the rest of 2024 or until such time that this sort of intervention occurs.

Meanwhile, Japan’s yen-to-dollar exchange rate broke out to fresh multi-decade highs (meaning a weaker yen):

![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-usdjpy.png)

Japan currently has 0.1% short term interest rates despite experiencing above-target inflation, at a time when most other developed countries have 4-5% interest rates or higher. This makes holding yen unattractive, and thus hurts the exchange rate. The Bank of Japan has thus far chosen not to meaningfully tighten monetary policy in the face of this, although they previously intervened when the exchange rate moved faster than they prefer.

That’s the problem with being in fiscal dominance, which applies even more to Japan than the United States. Japan has such large government debts (over 250% public debt to GDP) that if they raise interest rates meaningfully in line with the rest of the developed world, it would blow out their public interest expense to huge levels, which in large part flows to the private sector and is nominally stimulatory and would require support from the central bank.

If Japan wants to defend its exchange rate at some point again, they could sell some U.S. Treasuries and buy back some yen. They have a strong current account surplus and accumulated positive net international investment position, and are the biggest foreign holder of U.S. Treasuries in the world. Thus far, the yen has weakened because they have *allowed* it to weaken. They have plenty of ammo to deploy if the devaluation gets too disorderly for their preferences, even if interest rates are not necessarily the ideal tool for them to use.

In the meantime, a weaker yen is good for Japanese exporters and for inbound tourism. It’s bad for Japanese savers, importers, and those who want to travel abroad. Sometimes subsidies are used to minimize the most obvious effects for consumers and businesses, such as with energy prices.

More broadly, whenever the dollar index gets too strong, foreign holdings of Treasuries tend to decrease across the board, because those foreign countries trim some of their Treasuries to buy back and defend their own currencies around the margins, or at least stop buying additional Treasuries. Foreign countries only tend to accumulate Treasuries when the dollar weakens. The inverse correlation between dollar strength and foreign demand for Treasuries remains strong:

![Fed Custody Securities vs the Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-securities-vs-dollar.png)

In summary, when the dollar starts to get too strong as has it been recently, it starts pressuring U.S. exports, and starts contributing to higher U.S. yields and sloppy U.S. Treasury auctions as foreign countries take defensive action. If it gets disorderly enough, either the Treasury or Fed eventually steps in to provide liquidity and smooth out market functioning. They’ve done this multiple times since 2019.

I don’t think we’re quite there yet, but as we reach the later stages of the Fed’s balance sheet contraction amid ongoing large deficit spending, we should expect some Treasury market disorder, growing frictions in liquidity measures, and then eventually a pivot from the Fed and/or Treasury to maintain adequate functioning and liquidity in the Treasury securities market.

That’s why I currently find the next 3-6 months to be challenging to forecast, but have more confidence over an 18 month forecast. I expect liquidity to be higher 18 months from now, but the timing to get there for the rest of 2024 is harder to predict.

Overall, I continue to find long-duration Treasuries uncompelling on a risk-reward basis. I prefer short-term or intermediate-term Treasuries, inflation-protected Treasuries, gold, bitcoin, and appropriately valued equities.

**Runes Launch**

The Bitcoin halving occurred in mid-April, which means that now only about 450 new bitcoin are created per day compared to the 900 that were created each day prior to the halving. The halving occurs every 210,000 blocks, which takes about four years with ten-minute block times.

At $60,000+ USD per coin, 450 new coins created per day is $27+ million USD worth of new coins per day. If more demand than that wants to flow into the network on a sustained basis, then that demand has to be met with existing supply from existing holders, which generally means prices have to go high enough to convince them to sell.

On the halving block, a new protocol called Runes was launched. Runes isn’t a software change to Bitcoin itself, but rather is a separate protocol developed by Casey Rodarmor that makes use of the Bitcoin blockchain. It allows various entities to create tons of new tokens with special transactions. It was pretty clear ahead of time that this would have significant demand upon launch, and that there would be a burst of activity that elevates fees on the Bitcoin network, and so I mentioned that in my prior report:

> It’s likely that the initial burst of usage of this new protocol will result in a period of network congestion and higher transaction fees. If that occurs, it would be temporarily beneficial for miners due to them receiving higher fees (thereby potentially mitigating the impact of the halving event), but temporarily harmful for users because they would be the ones paying higher fees amid the network congestion.
>
> -April 14, 2024 report

Indeed, Bitcoin had its highest-ever transaction fees for several hours after the halving, and had by far the highest fees within any 24-hour period so far. As of this writing, fees remain elevated relative to the pre-halving weeks but have returned to being reasonable.

![BTC Fees](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-btc-fees.png)

Right now, the Runes protocol is used for speculation. People are making meme tokens with it. Its usage is starting to fade from its initial burst, since fads burn themselves out eventually. Over time the people creating, selling, and buying all of these coins run out of money to speculate with. The creators pay fees to the miners, and buyer demand is eventually exhausted after thousands of new meme coins come to market to saturate that demand.

Some developers are looking to make more utility use out of Runes. For example, tokens can conceivably represent stablecoins or other securities that have some real-world connection. However, there are other protocols like Taproot Assets that make more efficient use of block space for utility purposes by keeping more of the information off-chain.

It remains to be seen how some of these protocols will be used, but in general I expect speculative use cases to burn themselves out over time as a share of the network.

**The Inscriptions/BRC-20/Stamps/Runes Controversy**

The fact that Inscriptions, BRC-20 tokens, Stamps tokens, and Runes tokens result in more demand for block space and drives up transaction fees has been a point of controversy ever since Ordinals theory was developed and these things became popular over the past year or two.

A little over three years ago, I wrote about how non-monetary use cases might re-assert themselves in the years ahead and contribute to fees:

> **Other Uses of Block Space**
>
> Block space in bitcoin’s blockchain is just information; it doesn’t all have to be used purely for payment transactions. Messages can be and have been included in various transactions within blocks, including by Satoshi Nakamoto in the genesis block.
>
> A given block on the bitcoin blockchain, as an immutable distributed public ledger, can be thought of as virtual real estate, and only 144 blocks are available per day on average. Most uses are for settling bitcoin payments, but the space within a transaction can be used for other purposes too, because any information you put there becomes permanent and publicly-available.
>
> There’s potentially a use-case for that, because you can put something there as a matter of public record, as an arbiter of truth that is stored on countless devices around the world that can never be changed once it’s buried under more blocks.
>
> Over time, various services have been willing to pay transaction fees to secure messages inside of the bitcoin blockchain. Veriblock, for example, has a service that allows weaker blockchains to “inherit” bitcoin’s security by using the OP Return operator. Veriblock and similar solutions accounted for a double-digit percentage of Bitcoin’s ongoing transactions for a couple years, although in recent years this practice has tapered off:
>
> ![OP Return Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bitcoin-security-op-return.png)
>
> *Chart Source: [transactionfee.info](https://transactionfee.info/charts/output-opreturn/)*
>
> Overall, non-transactional messages are no longer a big portion of bitcoin’s block space usage, but in the future this could conceivably pop up again if new use-cases are identified. Many of the previous use-cases have migrated to become ethereum tokens and trading around in that space instead.
>
> [-Bitcoin: Fee-Based Security Modeling, March 2021](https://www.lynalden.com/bitcoin-security-modeling/)

If we think about what the Bitcoin blockchain is at its foundation, it’s a distributed database.

Bitcoin is the most decentralized and immutable database the world has thus far made. And it’s open source and therefore widely available in a permissionless context.

When you pay a bitcoin miner to put something into a new block on the Bitcoin blockchain, this information gets stored on tens of thousands of nodes around the world that sync with each other constantly. And it’s accessible to anyone in the world with an internet connection. Depending on precisely how/where you put that information into the blockchain, it might be prunable by nodes (i.e. some of them can decide not to store it and still remain synced to the network) or it might not be (i.e. nodes are basically stuck with it whether they want it or not). Unprunable information will generally be more complicated and costly to insert into the blockchain than prunable information.

For the Bitcoin network, there is a block space limit of four megabytes every ten minutes. If the block space limit is designed to be too big, then the bandwidth and storage requirements to run a node would become too high, and thus only institutional entities could run a node, and the network would become less decentralized and permissionless. If the block space limit is made too small, then the number of people who would be able to pay for and get a transaction into the blockchain on a regular basis may be insufficient relative to demand, resulting in those people looking elsewhere for a solution.

The main way to address those two design criteria is to have a moderately small block size and then scale through layers; layers can and have been built that have higher throughput, speed, privacy, and/or programmability than the base layer, in exchange for less security on those layers. To varying degrees, those layers inherent some of the properties of the base layer, but not all of those properties. This keeps the base layer for high-density valuable transactions, and pushes smaller transactions to the upper levels. For example, if you want to store 10 bitcoin as long-term savings, it’s best to do it on the base layer. But if you want to set aside 0.01 bitcoin to use as a medium of exchange, then storing that on the Liquid network, on Cash App, on a noncustodial Lightning channel, or on a Chaumian ecash custodian wallet for fast and private transactions is good for that purpose, since base layer fees will be too high on a per-transaction basis.

The primary design goal of this distributed database by its creator Satoshi Nakamoto was for it to be used as money. The largest monies in the world (the dollar, the euro, the yuan, and all fiat currencies) run on centralized permissioned ledgers. Bitcoin is a decentralized permissionless ledger with a high degree of immutability, and so naturally a good killer app for that is to use it as decentralized money. It’s not the *only* use case, but it’s the obvious use case, and I would argue it’s by far the largest use case with the largest total addressable market.

Although Bitcoin as a database was primarily optimized by Nakamoto to be used as money rather than non-monetary information storage, it’s likely impossible to design a blockchain in such a way that it can \*only\* be used as money. And even Nakamoto himself put non-monetary information into the first block of the Bitcoin network when he timestamped an article headline about bank bailouts (“The Times 03/Jan/2009 Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks”).

There are almost always clever ways to store non-monetary information on a blockchain, no matter how restrictive the code is. A given blockchain can be designed with more or less flexibility on purpose, so that it’s harder and more expensive or easier and less expensive to stick that non-monetary information in it. Bitcoin in its current form is designed mainly for money but with some allowance for non-monetary information. If anything, it’s good to have some efficient prunable space where non-monetary information can go, to encourage it to go there if it’s going to exist. That way, nodes that don’t want it can prune it out, and nodes that do want it can keep it. The Bitcoin network currently does that with defined OP\_RETURN space, and people use that and also find more expensive ways to stick unprunable non-monetary information elsewhere in the blockchain as well.

Decentralized money and savings is potentially a $100+ trillion global total addressable market. The demand for it is enormous, if a good enough solution proves itself, which the Bitcoin network has gradually been doing for 15 years now. Decentralized non-monetary information storage for utility or speculation is a much smaller market, probably a few trillion dollars or less. That’s why over time I expect monetary uses to price out the majority of non-monetary use cases and remain the largest use for the blockchain.

Some bitcoin users want to strengthen the various filters against non-monetary information, to try to keep more of the blockchain reserved for monetary transfers. However, that’s extremely hard to do if there is real demand by paying users for those non-monetary use cases.

A subset of bitcoin critics often say that bitcoin has no intrinsic value (meaning to say that it has no non-monetary use case), in contrast to gold which can be used for various ornamental and industrial purposes in addition to its role as money.

However, the reality is the Bitcoin network as the world’s most immutable and decentralized database *does* have non-monetary uses cases, and bitcoin is the network’s unit of account. And ironically, some bitcoin proponents are unhappy with the fact that the Bitcoin network has non-monetary use cases, since those non-monetary use cases compete with fees for their monetary use case.

There’s a common human trend to overreact to the current thing or trend and fail to anticipate response functions to shocks, which in my view is at the heart of this controversy.

In macroeconomics, investors and analysts often think that the current trend will continue. For example, they see high debt and slow growth and assume that disinflation is inevitable. But then, *oops*, in response to disinflation the government and central bank print and distribute trillions of dollars and generate inflation, and that disinflationary thesis goes out the window. In other words, they failed to appreciate the incentives and power behind the likely response function to disinflation.

When the Bitcoin network had low fees, many of its critics said that the *low* fees would be a long-term problem, and that it’ll become insecure from lack of fees. This was despite the fact that overall fees were cyclically growing over time, and that the potential demand is nearly infinite while block space is fixed at four megabytes per ten minutes. Some of them breathlessly suggested all sorts of fixes for *low fees*, like to add a permanent tail emission to the network.

And now that it went through several months of sustained high fees, there are people (sometimes the same critics, and sometimes other bitcoin proponents) complaining about *high* fees and saying that the network is damaged from all of these *high* fees. Many of them are breathlessly saying there should be immediate fixes to try to filter out more of these non-monetary use cases.

The human urge to tinker is strong. As soon as a system doesn’t act exactly as people want, they immediately want to tinker with it. But some of the best things survive precisely because they are *hard* to tinker with, like the U.S. Constitution, and I would argue like the Bitcoin network. The Bitcoin network requires rather large supermajorities of opinion by node operators and miners to make major changes to its core functionality, which allows the network to remain consistent against the rapidly changing tide of human opinion, which tends to oscillate between extremes.

In my view what both sides of this controversy fail to appreciate are the response functions. When fees were low, it incentivized people to find other uses for the block space, which led to higher fees. Now that fees are high, it is accelerating interest in layered solutions, and various companies have raised capital to build out more of those layers to address the high fees on the base layer and make more efficient use of block space.

At [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/) back during the bear market in 2022, we were expecting higher fees in the future even when fees were low at the time, which is why we invested in companies that are building out various scaling methods even at a time when they seemed unnecessary. The math is pretty straightforward; if Bitcoin as a network is going to continue to grow as it has been for 15 years now, meaningful base layer fees are going to develop, and so a variety of scaling methods are needed and will be demanded by the market.

-Do I think that people should invest in meme tokens and NFTs on the Bitcoin network, which are currently responsible for causing higher fees on the network? No, I don’t. And I think the vast majority who do so will lose capital because they are buying into an expensive fad that is not scarce.

-Do I think that miners and developers and node operators should rush to find ways to filter out these non-monetary use cases that are currently in demand? No, I don’t. And while they are welcome to try, I think that attempts to do so will continue to be unsuccessful. Filtering works well for spam that hardly anyone is willing to pay for, but doesn’t work well for spam that people *do* want to pay for.

Instead, like any robust protocol that is resistant to rapid change and has value *because* of that fact, I think it will take time to develop an equilibrium. I view Bitcoin as a network to be well-designed as it is, meaning that it’s purposely simple and optimized for monetary transfers, but with the ability to put some non-monetary information into it, which goes through periods of fads as it matures.

**The U.S. Government’s Privacy Crackdown**

On April 24th, the U.S. Department of Justice [announced](https://www.justice.gov/usao-sdny/pr/founders-and-ceo-cryptocurrency-mixing-service-arrested-and-charged-money-laundering) that they seized assets of Samourai Wallet and arrested the co-founders for money laundering and unlicensed money transmission. They are extraditing one of the defendants from Europe.

And then on April 25th, the [FBI put out a warning](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/markets/fbi-warns-americans-against-using-non-kyc-crypto-money-transmitting-services) saying that people should avoid non-KYC crypto services and only use crypto services that do indeed collect all their users’ identity information.

Samourai Wallet focuses on adding privacy back to bitcoins via a process known as a “coinjoin”. If you withdraw bitcoin from an exchange like Coinbase to your own self-custodial onchain wallet and spend that bitcoin at various merchants, then Coinbase (or any other exchange or custodian that you happened to withdraw from) can continue to track all of your spending details. In addition, those merchants that you pay for goods and services can check to see how much bitcoin you have in that address, and where it came from. It would be like if you withdraw physical cash from a bank, but the bank can see everything you spend the cash on going forward, and you have to write the amount that your savings account has on every dollar bill that you hand to merchants so that they can see that number and what bank it came from. That would be worse privacy than what we currently have with the current banking system. Coinjoins and similar privacy methods help to eliminate that fungibility issue with bitcoins and make them so that neither side can see how much bitcoin you have or where you are spending it. The vast majority of usage of coinjoin services like what Samourai offers is for legitimate privacy purposes. Various scaling solutions such as Lightning channels, the Liquid network, and Chaumian ecash wallets also provide good privacy for legitimate purposes.

Due to various things that Samourai Wallet’s co-founders have said on social media over the years which were included in the prosecution’s case, they make for what are known as “unsympathetic” defendants. But the case is very noteworthy because unlike prior lawsuits against coin mixing services, Samurai is a *noncustodial* service; at no point did the defendants have custody of the funds that they are alleged to have laundered. Ultimately, they operate a messaging service for others to coordinate transactions in a noncustodial way, and Samourai can’t see the messages that they coordinate.

Similarly, SWIFT is a [messaging service provider](https://www.swift.com/about-us/legal/compliance-0/swift-and-sanctions), not a money transmitter.

In addition to the DOJ and FBI actions, the U.S. government prosecutors filed [a memo](https://twitter.com/jp_koning/status/1784191018973134977) in response to the defendant’s pretrial motions in the related Tornado Cash case (a privacy service similar to Samourai Wallet but for the Ethereum network), which clarified their view on money transmission in this type of context. Specifically, they argue that bitcoin and cryptocurrency is indeed money, and that a service provider need not be custodial in order to be charged with unlawful money transmission.

> The definition of “money transmitting” in Section 1960 does not require the money transmitter to have “control” of the funds being transferred. The definition extends to “transferring funds on behalf of the public by any and all means.” 18 U.S.C. § 1960(b)(2). Under the ordinary meaning of the word “transfer,” there is no requirement that the transferer exercise control over the funds being transferred.
>
> […]
>
> Courts have uniformly held, and the defendant does not dispute in his motion, that “funds” and “money” for purposes of this statute include cryptocurrencies. E.g., Murgio, 209 F. Supp. 3d at 705-10 (Bitcoin exchange covered by 18 U.S.C. § 1960); United States v. Faiella, 39 F. Supp. 3d 544, 545 (S.D.N.Y. 2014) (“Bitcoin clearly qualifies as ‘money’ or ‘funds’” for purposes of 1960 prosecution); United States v. Harmon, 474 F. Supp. 3d 76 (D.D.C. 2020) (applying Section 1960 to Bitcoin mixing service).
>
> -The Government’s Opposition to Defendant Roman Storm’s Pretrial Motions

However, neither Samourai Wallet nor Tornado Cash transmit cryptocurrency per se. The prosecution would have to win in court with the view that providing messaging services for cryptocurrency holders is a form of money transmission, despite not at any time having control of the funds.

But in the meantime, a number of other businesses, including noncustodial ones, closed their services to U.S. residents, including Phoenix Wallet, which is a leading noncustodial Lightning wallet. Even rollup sequencers, atomic swap facilitators, sidechain operators, and similar technologies can potentially be classified as money transmitters under this expanded context. Some industry operators have argued that this type of US-pullout is an overreaction, but it’s the most conservative action for them to do.

This type of legal action can be effective in a few ways. By picking an unsympathetic defendant to start with, they decrease the types of entities that will assist with their defense. And then if they win against the defendants, they establish new precedent and broaden the definition of money transmission for which they can go after other companies down the road. And in the meantime, many companies, developers, and financiers withdraw or reduce their activity, and so prosecutors have a chilling effect on the industry without further expense and enforcement for a while.

Taking legal action against noncustodial software that provides a messaging service is an interesting intersection, because the software can be open source and thus is very hard to outright eliminate. However, by going after companies that earn fees on the service, they can reduce liquidity and availability for those types of services.

The government is not a monolith, and so there are various policymakers and civil servants and agencies that take differing views on certain matters. However, from a large portion of the government’s perspective, they want all commerce to go through centralized, surveillable, and permissioned corporate/government gateways. Peer-to-peer exchange, especially in any sort of large scale, private, or cross-border context, is something that they tend to view as a threat, even though financial privacy has all sorts of legitimate (non-criminal) use cases.

I continue to expect that while governments will increasingly make allowances for ETFs and various custodial store-of-value functionality (even if just via losing court cases that force them to accept it, as the SEC did last year), they will continue to apply pressure to services that support the self-custody of funds, the privacy of funds, and/or that facilitate peer-to-peer medium of exchange. As I wrote a few months ago:

> Governments in general have been increasingly focused on financial surveillance over the past several decades. Continuous inflation has helped them with that mission, because many governments set specific thresholds above which reporting is necessary, and then never adjust those threshold for inflation. Therefore, the threshold effectively tightens each year.
>
> *“The 1970 Bank Secrecy Act, enacted into law by the U.S. government and still in effect, compels banks to file reports with the government if a customer’s daily transactions exceed $10,000.*
>
> *When this law was enacted in 1970, the median American annual income was less than $10,000. Therefore, the law only covered rather large sums of money moving within a day — worth well over $80,000 in today’s weaker dollars. However, there was no inflation adjustment embedded into the law. As the value of the dollar eroded over time, banks effectively had to file reports regarding smaller and smaller levels of transactions, since $10,000 worth of transactions occurring in a day became more and more commonplace. Every year, the government effectively lowered the threshold regarding its financial surveillance, simply through inflation, without passing further legislation.*
>
> *Over the next fifty years, if the rate of inflation averages the same amount it has over the past fifty years, then the reporting threshold will shrink by another 8x or so in terms of purchasing power. When the law was enacted, the government granted itself the ability to keep tabs on house-sized transactions. Over time, inflation enhanced the law so that they can keep track of transactions the size of used cars. If this keeps up, it will enable them to keep track of transactions the size of lawnmowers or bicycles.”*
>
> *–[Broken Money](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR), page 446*
>
> Most of the increasingly strict anti-financial-privacy rules are operational on the institutional level which makes them easy to enforce, but cryptocurrencies throw a curve ball into the situation because they enable people to send money peer-to-peer using free open-source software. This leads the government try to enforce similar anti-privacy rules on the individual level, which is orders of magnitude more difficult to enforce because there are orders of magnitude more potential enforcement points.
>
> *“The 2021 Infrastructure and Jobs Act requires individuals to report information about people who send them cryptocurrency if the amount is over $10,000 (even if spread among multiple transactions). However, this requirement didn’t become active until January 1, 2024.*
>
> *The report must include, among other things, the name, address, and Social Security number of the person from whom the funds were received, the amount received, and the date and nature of the transaction. If you don’t file a report within 15 days of receiving the transaction, you could be found guilty of a felony offense.”*
>
> *–[Coincenter](https://www.coincenter.org/new-crypto-tax-reporting-obligations-took-effect-on-new-years-day/)*
>
> A challenge for this legislation is that cryptocurrencies can be sent anonymously, so sometimes such reporting is not even possible by the person who received it. Coincenter is currently suing the government regarding this rule being potentially unconstitutional, but that’ll take time to work its way through courts.
>
> For years, a lot of people have been hung up on the question of whether the government will “ban bitcoin” or not. That’s extremely difficult to do because ultimately, holding bitcoin just means holding a private key, which is a large number. Bitcoin is basically just a decentralized spreadsheet, with its users updating the spreadsheet by signing updates with their large numbers. The draconian levels that a government has to go to to ban the usage of a decentralized spreadsheet are very large and optically unfavorable. Even China has had a tough time with it.
>
> Instead, the main question is how many frictions the government will add to using it, including all sorts of reporting requirements that are hard to comply with and hard to enforce, but that can be selectively forced to make examples out of people and thus put downward pressure on usage, liquidity, development, and so forth.
>
> I think this friction is going to last quite a while. Cryptography inherently enables people to send value to each other, including across borders and including via private means, which is disruptive to the legacy system that has 160 different centralized (and for the most part rapidly-devaluing) fiat currencies with various capital controls built around them.
>
> Additionally, the entire apparatus of basing government revenue primarily on income taxes came into being with modern centralized banking, and is based on the idea that everyone’s finances can be readily surveilled by central authorities at all times. If more commerce happens peer-to-peer and outside of banks and across borders, and if ubiquitous financial surveillance is no longer easy to accomplish due to shifts in technology, then that is a long-term disruptive change for governments.
>
> In the face of these changes, governments are likely going to keep trying to apply bank-style rules on peer-to-peer transactions unless or until such technology becomes so widespread as to make those rules unenforceable, at which point they’ll have to adjust their tactics.
>
> -January 7, 2024 premium report

Back in the 1980s, law enforcement was increasingly concerned about pager or “beeper” usage among criminals. Pagers had various legitimate use cases among doctors, journalists, delivery people, and so forth, but were also increasingly used by drug traffickers. As a result, law enforcement and trade groups sought to find ways to reduce pager availability to criminals, and to gather identity information on anyone who purchased a pager. The ACLU then pushed back on it, arguing that it was overreach by law enforcement or trade groups to determine what kinds of messages can be sent over devices and who can access messaging devices. This [1988 Washington Post article](https://www.washingtonpost.com/archive/politics/1988/07/11/message-is-out-on-beepers/58840caa-523e-413b-9224-60ad94d7803f/) describes it well.

Of course, over a long enough timeline of technology improvements, the whole issue became moot. Virtually everyone in the world now has 100x the digital communication capabilities that they had in the 1980s. It was like a sand wall being built against a rising tide.

And then in the 1990s, as the internet became popular, some people used it for pornography, and it was hard to make sure certain people couldn’t access it. The U.S. government went after that with the [Communications Decency Act of 1997](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reno_v._American_Civil_Liberties_Union). Specifically, they tried to criminalize any service provider for knowingly transmitting indecent material to persons under 18. While that of course seems reasonable on the surface, it basically means that internet service providers would have to surveil and be responsible for all information that flows through them. As a result, the American Civil Liberties Union went after that, and the Supreme Court struck that aspect down as being in violation of the first amendment, and it set a precedent that *publishers* of material are responsible for those types of things but not *operators* of transmission services, since the latter are merely allowing bits of information to move across their infrastructure.

Also in the 1990s, the U.S. government tried to crack down on open-source software that allowed for end-to-end encryption of information. Specifically, they launched a criminal investigation against [Phil Zimmerman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phil_Zimmermann) for creating and distributing open-source software that enables that. The government basically wanted a backdoor built into all peer-to-peer messaging in the world so that they could surveil it for criminal activities. In response, Zimmerman published his code in a book via MIT Press, and thus protected its existence under the first amendment. In addition, people protested the overreach by putting encryption code on [T-shirts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adam_Back#/media/File:Munitions_T-shirt_(front).jpg). The U.S. government ended up dropping its criminal investigations and pulling back its encryption control attempts as they became untenable to enforce in practice or in law. Peer-to-peer encryption subsequently became a key piece for e-commerce to flourish.

The current legal battleground is around open source noncustodial methods of making transactions private, or that facilitate the movement of money without a license. Since in this newer context cryptographic money is just bits of information, and open source software enables the movement and anonymization of that information without taking control of it, enforcing a widespread ban on this activity is challenging, and is potentially in violation of the first and fourth amendments.

However, prosecutors can go after companies that attempt to earn money from such noncustodial services starting with the least sympathetic ones, trigger a number of legal defenses that take a long time to be concluded, and see where these things shake out in terms of constitutional law. Doing so puts pressure on most types of current or prospective noncustodial private scaling solutions including Lightning nodes, sidechain operators, rollup sequencers, atomic swap facilitators, and so forth.

I think this will continue to be an important area to monitor, and is more important than the Runes/Ordinals/NFTs controversy that has received a lot of attention among bitcoin proponents in online discourse.

And to bring some themes together, it’s important to note that during periods of fiscal dominance, countries almost invariably turn to capital controls to try to maintain a captive audience for their government debt at below-market interest rates. In the modern era, a key part of enforcing capital controls is to try to reduce the liquidity and privacy and scaling potential of cryptographic value transfer methods.

## Equity Valuation Deep Dive

A rule of thumb I use for equity valuation is to look for companies with dividend-adjusted PEG ratios below 2x, and preferably below 1.5x. That sentence contains a bit of jargon, so we can break that down a bit.

-The PEG ratio is the price/earnings to growth ratio.

-The price/earnings ratio is the price of a stock divided by its earnings per share, and is basically a measure of how many years it will take you to earn your principal without growth.

-The growth rate is the expected annual earnings growth rate, based on a combination of past history and forward analysis.

-The PEG ratio divides the price/earnings ratio by the growth rate, so that we take into account both valuation and expected growth to see if the valuation makes sense relative to the expected growth.

-The dividend-adjusted PEG ratio adds the dividend yield back to growth expectations, because an investor that receives a regular dividend can choose to reinvest it into more shares, and thus grow their earnings on their investment via share accumulation. In other words, a company that grows earnings by 5% per year while also paying a 3% dividend yield that the investor can reinvest into more shares of the company, is very different than a company that grows earnings by 5% per year while not even paying a dividend.

This valuation method does not work for 1) unprofitable early-stage tech growth companies or 2) highly cyclical businesses that go without earnings for long stretches of time and that have unpredictable growth rates. Different valuation methods apply for those types of businesses. Instead, this valuation method applies mainly for consistently profitable buy-and-hold companies that have economic moats. And it doesn’t tell you what a stock will trade at next year, since valuations can be volatile, but rather it provides a rough idea of what it is expected to return if you act like an owner and hold it for many years.

It can be applied to individual stocks for stock selection purposes, or to see if a given stock index is overvalued or not based on the valuations and growth expectations of many of its largest components.

A reasonable dividend-adjusted PEG ratio will depend on what you can expect to get on government bonds over the next 5-10 years. Right now you can get about 5.3% on T-bills or you can lock in about 4.7% on ten-year T-notes. So it only makes sense to invest in more volatile and risky equities if you can get a substantial margin above that, like 8% expected returns or better in exchange for taking on that additional equity risk and volatility.

**Baseline Example: A Slow Growth Company**

Suppose that a company has no growth in real terms, and grows only in line with inflation. It raises prices maybe 3% per year in line with inflation and passes all of its earnings through to its investors as dividends or share buybacks. So, it has 3% average annual top-line earnings growth as a result of inflation, but no other growth. It’s a toll-road type of business.

If the stock trades at 10x earnings, it could pay all of its earnings out as a 10% annual dividend yield, and grow the earnings and dividend by 3% per year.

Alternatively, it could pay out half of its earnings as a 5% dividend yield and use the remaining cash to buy back some of its shares each year and thus grow the earnings and dividends per share by an additional 5%. As a result, it’ll have 8% annual earnings and dividend growth per share, and a 5% annual dividend yield.

Either way, you can expect about 13% annualized returns from this stock over the long run if bought at that low valuation, assuming your growth expectations are correct. If the company outright shrinks or fails to raise prices in line with inflation, it would underperform. If at some point down the line it trades at a much higher valuation and you sell it into that strength, you could earn higher returns on top of the 13% annualized fundamental baseline.

This would be a dividend-adjusted PEG ratio of 0.77x. The price/earnings ratio is 10, and the sum of the dividend yield and growth rate is 13. Ten divided by thirteen is about 0.77.

Since this stock produces 13% expected returns at a 10x price/earnings ratio, someone might decide that they will pay more for this company, such as a 15x price/earnings ratio.

In that case, their dividend yield would be reduced by about one third, and the company’s rate of share repurchases would be diminished since each share is more expensive to purchase out of the company’s total cashflow. So in the 100% dividend payout version, the company would pay a 6.66% dividend yield and grow earnings at 3% per year. In the 50% dividend payout and 50% share buyback version, it would pay a 3.33% dividend yield and grow earnings per share at 6% per year. Expected returns are about 9-10% per year either way. The dividend-adjusted PEG ratio would be about 1.5x or 1.6x or so.

If a company has a higher return on invested capital and is able to grow faster, then higher earnings multiples make sense, but with similar overall assessment between total growth and the dividend.

With that in mind, the rest of this section will review some popular examples of current stocks from expensive to cheap, to see where opportunity lies in the market, and why I am overweighting or underweighting certain areas.

**Microsoft Valuation**

Microsoft (MSFT) is the largest stock in the United States, with a market capitalization of over $3 trillion. They have a powerful position in business software, cloud computing, and artificial intelligence. They are one of only two AAA-rated companies, with the other one being Johnson & Johnson (JNJ).

Currently they trade for about 35x earnings, which is pretty high. Over the past ten years, their revenue growth rate was about 10% per year. Over the past five years, the average was higher at 14%. This was from a combination of cloud computing growth and rapid money-printing. They also buy back some shares, and so earnings per share growth has been somewhat higher than revenue growth.

![MSFT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-msft-fastgraph.png)

Analysts expect about 16% earnings growth on average for the next few years, and at this valuation the dividend yield is well under 1%. The larger the company gets, the harder it is to continue growing at a rate that is faster than the overall economy. So there’s a risk that this could slow back down in the future, but it’s hard to say precisely at what point their mega-trends of cloud computing and AI market share will run into saturation, competition, or government attention.

Right now, the dividend-adjusted PEG ratio based on analyst expected earnings is over 2x. I would have to be a bit more bullish than consensus analysts in order to want to buy this stock with significant conviction, and there’s no margin of safety if the earnings come in weaker than expected in the years ahead.

I’ve looked through the fundamentals and the major trends, and while I can’t make any sort of high conviction claim that Microsoft must underperform in the years ahead, I also can’t comfortably make a case for its outperformance either. I used to be long the stock for quite a while, but after its price growth surpassed its fundamental growth for a while, I eventually rotated out of the position for a sizable gain, albeit a bit too early. It’s not that I’m concerned about a Microsoft crash per se, but rather I am concerned about the likelihood of a 3-5 year period of stagnant price action as fundamentals catch back up over time.

I have instead been holding Alphabet (GOOGL), which has been trading at a lower valuation with similar growth expectations. The margin of safety has been better on that one in my opinion, and the recent price pop after its latest earnings and dividend announcement has been nice.

**Visa Valuation**

I bought Visa (V) back when it fell below a 2x PEG ratio. It has since performed well, and the price has moved a bit more quickly than its fundamentals:

![V FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-v-fastgraph.png)

I don’t generally rotate out of high-quality companies for minor valuation overshoots, but it means I switch it to being a “hold” rather than a “buy”. Analysts expect its growth to be in the mid-teens, the dividend yield is under 1%, and the price/earnings ratio is about 29x.

So, there’s not a lot of margin of safety at this price level if growth starts to slow down, but as a base case I expect continued long-term compounding from here.

**American Water Works Valuation**

I’ve almost completely avoided utility stocks over the past several years. When bond yields went very low, utility stock valuations went very high.

American Water Works (AWK) is an example of a high-quality utility that became overvalued for a while. At one point it traded at over 40x earnings! Now it is back down to about 24x earnings:

![AWK FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-awk-fastgraph.png)

But is that sufficiently cheap?

The stock can reasonably grow earnings by 7-8% per year and pay a 2-3% dividend yield along the way. So total dividend-adjusted growth is maybe 10% per year combined.

The price/earnings ratio is currently 24x which puts it above my preferred dividend-adjusted PEG ratio range at about 2.4x. A decade ago, it used to trade in the 17x to 22x price/earnings range, even though Treasury yields were lower. As a result, it had a better equity risk premium relative to Treasuries back then than it does now.

This valuation can make sense if markets expect rates to return to very low levels, but overall it doesn’t leave much margin of safety relative to Treasury securities at this time. Investors tend to pay up for utilities due to their stability, but 24x earnings is near the top end of what is reasonable in my view. It’s not insane anymore like it was a few years ago, but it’s not a compelling buy either.

**Enterprise Products Partners Valuation**

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) has been my baseline hurdle comparison for any high-yield stock for a while.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-epd-fastgraph.png)

It pays a 7% distribution yield, has grown distributions for 25 consecutive years, and has a reasonable expectation of growing distributable cash flow and actual distributions by 2-5% per year going forward. It has the strongest credit rating in the midstream industry, and has locked in a lot of long-duration debt at low interest rates, which are backed up by hard infrastructure assets.

The price/earnings ratio is below 12x, which compared to the 10% or so expected distribution and growth combination, gives it a PEG ratio of around 1.2x. Investors earn a comfortably higher active yield than Treasuries and enjoy the expectation of ongoing growth of that income, in exchange for taking on tail risk relative to Treasuries.

It issues a K-1 form during tax season since it’s a partnership rather than a corporation, which is an issue for a lot of foreign investors, but that offers tax deferment benefits for domestic U.S. investors. Foreign investors are generally better off investing in the Alerian MLP ETF (AMLP) if they want midstream exposure.

**Bancolombia Valuation**

In my prior report, I provided an overview of Bancolombia (CIB). It’s a well-run bank that serves Colombia, El Salvador, Guatemala, Panama, and elsewhere in Latin America, and has been trading on the NYSE since the 1990s.

It operates in an emerging market in a difficult sector and has a BB+ credit rating, so it naturally trades at a very low valuation. It’s risky and any position size should reflect that.

But while its fundamentals have bounced back to pre-pandemic levels, its stock price has not. It’s a company that is likely worth more than a price/earnings ratio of just 6x, and yet is trading below that.

![CIB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-cib-fastgraph.png)

So much capital has fled regions like that, and that capital is currently piled into the U.S. mega-caps at 30x or 40x price/earnings ratios or more. While I don’t know that it’ll happen in 2024, I suspect that in 2025 and beyond we’ll see some mean reversion in that trade.
