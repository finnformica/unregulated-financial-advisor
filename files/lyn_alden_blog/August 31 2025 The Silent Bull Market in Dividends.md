## Macro View

**US Business Cycle**

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index, which was historically accurate but that continues to be a source of noise (false positives regarding recession) here during fiscal dominance, continues to show rangebound weakness:

![LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-lei.png)

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator popped up to over 3%, and this was widely covered across social media. However, this pop was mainly from advanced expectations of the trade balance, rather than because of a broad shift in overall domestic indicators, and is mostly noise:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gdpnow.png)

Initial jobless claims remain under 250k/week, which indicates no sign of acute labor market weakness:

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-initial-claims.png)

Overall, it continues to be a two-speed mixed economy. Large fiscal deficits continue to act as stimulus for some sectors, while high rates and some other factors continue to put downward pressure on other sectors (e.g. real estate).

**Tariff Updates**

Tariffs remain in effect, currently at a rate of around $360 billion annualized (and my [August public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2025-newsletter/) focused on them and their current/potential impacts).

Back in April, a coalition of small businesses and US states filed a lawsuit against President Trump, arguing that his unilateral imposition of broad and permanent tariffs was illegal, and that outside of certain narrower contexts, that’s supposed to be a power of Congress. The Trump administration has been arguing that they are being used in an emergency context for the purpose of national security, whereas the plaintiffs argue that they’re too broad and persistent to really be for a national security emergency, and thus should go through Congress.

In May, the US Court of International Trade agreed with the plaintiffs; they determined that the tariffs are outside of unilateral executive power. As expected, however, the Trump administration filed an appeal.

Two days ago, the Federal Circuit Appeals Court [upheld](https://www.cafc.uscourts.gov/opinions-orders/25-1812.ORDER.8-29-2025_2566157.pdf) the decision of that lower court with a 7-to-4 ruling; they also found that the tariffs exceeded executive powers and should go through Congress. However, the tariffs remain in effect as the administration is appealing that decision to the US Supreme Court, which will settle it one way or the other.

In theory party affiliation is not supposed to matter, but it’s worth noting that the Federal Circuit Appeals Court that ruled against the administration has a majority of judges appointed by Democratic presidents, while the Supreme Court has a majority of justices appointed by Republican presidents. So, on one hand the President has the party advantage for this next and final decision, but on the other hand he can’t realistically call the decision partisan (as he has for the Appeals Court) if it does decide against him.

Over the coming couple of months, I’ll be keeping an eye on this case and its final fate with the Supreme Court, since the outcome is likely to have some material macroeconomic outcomes. In what is quite a noisy and headline driven world these days, tariffs are currently in my top five important variables to monitor when it comes to investing and commentary.

**Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity currently remains rangebound. The overnight reverse repo facility is mostly drained (which puts liquidity back into the system), the Treasury General Account is up to $590 billion as it refills (which pulls liquidity out of the system), and the Fed continues to perform gradual quantitative tightening (which pulls liquidity out of the system.

If the Treasury General Account reaches its $850 billion fully-filled target by the end of September, then it’ll likely drop liquidity to the lower end of its multi-year range.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-domestic-liquidity.png)

Overall bank cash levels remain above prior acute liquidity events, but in Q4 2025 they will likely start to enter somewhat tight territory.

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bank-cash-levels.png)

My expectation continues to be that the Fed will end quantitative tightening in 2026, and possibly move toward gradual balance sheet increases (which they won’t call QE, similar to their balance sheet expansion from October 2019 to March 2020).

So, domestic liquidity looks like it’ll be flat-to-down for a while, potentially running into lower thresholds, and when it does bottom it’ll be a pretty structural event that moves us up out of a multi-year range.

Meanwhile, with the dollar being on the weaker end of its recent range, and China’s credit impulse on the upswing, global liquidity is in pretty decent shape, and I expect it to continue to be going forward.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has corrected back down to early/mid July levels.

![Bitcoin Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bitcoin-price.png)

This has helped to reset overall levels of sentiment, and I continue to view it as being in a healthy, gradual bull market with ebbs and flows.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

The general pattern is that corporations are currently gobbling up bitcoin, while long-term large holders are gradually selling their coins, as they do every cycle. When people hold for many years, they end up very wealthy in bitcoin terms, and a significant subset of them either want to upgrade their consumption level or they want to rebalance into a broader set of assets, which is normal.

![Bitcoin HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bitcoin-hodl-wave.png)

And while many macroeconomic analysts focus only on price action, I also pay close attention to the business and technical details of the ecosystem. In my work at [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/), which [raised $100+ million](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/news/ego-death-capital-closes-100-million-raise-to-scale-bitcoin-companies) for what is so far the largest dedicated venture fund for bitcoin-focused startups to date, I continue to see plenty of high-quality startups building on the Bitcoin network with healthy product/service traction, in what overall is a healthy funding environment.

## Dividends: Still Cool

Much of the market focus remains on AI, but year-to-date here in 2025, broad US stock indices have all performed pretty similarly to the Magnificent 7 mega-cap tech stocks.

![Equity Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-equity-returns-ytd.png)

And foreign markets have quietly outperformed US markets:

![US vs Foreign Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-us-foreign-equity-returns-ytd.png)

On one hand, the US remains in structural fiscal dominance, with ongoing large deficits that tend to fuel outperformance of scarce assets versus currency and bonds. But on the other hand, people increasingly point to speculative froth in the form of retail equity participation, a sharp rise in the volume of newsletter services to cater to them, and generally high equity valuations.

Both concerns are valid, but in my view the low-hanging fruit currently is to buy high-quality yet inexpensive equities that aren’t exactly big growers but that return a lot of capital to shareholders. This group benefits from and offers protection from fiscal dominance and debasement, but isn’t over-owned and doesn’t have speculative froth that exists in many of the popular names.

**Enterprise Products Partners**

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) continues to be my gold standard example of a long-term dividend payer.

It has a distribution yield of a bit under 7% and has grown that distribution for 25 consecutive years. This compares favorably on a risk-adjusted basis to a 10-year Treasury note that pays a bit over 4% and comes with no growth.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-epd-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

It issues a K-1 during tax season, so it’s best held by long-term investors rather than traders. In fact, pipeline companies that are organized as partnerships tend to have lower valuations (for any given level of quality) than their counterparts that are organized as corporations. This is because the tax treatment disincentives certain foreign holders, and certain funds, from bothering to own them. However, the benefit is that those who can comfortably buy them can get a larger income stream per dollar invested, without sacrificing quality or growth.

In a world where the US stock market is currently north of 200% of US GDP, where cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratios are near their historical peaks, an inexpensive high-yielder like EPD continues to be interesting.

![Shiller PE](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-shiller-pe.png)

**Banks**

US Bancorp (USB) and Truist Financial (TFC) are the 5th and 9th largest US banks by assets respectively. They sold off like many stocks did in the aftermath of Liberation Day (April 2025), but have both quietly reached new post-Liberation Day highs:

![USB FASTGraph Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-usb-fastgraph-annotated.png)

When zooming out, they’re still cheap and in a weak rangebound state, but with otherwise solid and improving fundamentals. Notably, both of them pay higher than 4% dividend yields while investors let them accumulate capital and eventually break out to the upside (hopefully).

Bank OZK, meanwhile, is quietly touching new cycle highs. As a smaller bank, they should also benefit more from expected interest rate cuts in the year ahead.

![OZK FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-ozk-fastgraph.png)

Investors are currently avoiding them because they’re considered to be specialists in commercial real estate lending (and commercial real estate is in rough shape), but as I’ve been highlighting for a while, their lending is generally focused on short-term loans for new construction activities rather than providing financing for old out-of-use office buildings and things like that.

![OZK Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-ozk-book-value.png)

![OZK Price to Book](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-ozk-price-to-book.png)

## Final Thoughts: Gold Mini-Breakout

We’re now over 5 months into a gold consolidation, since I highlighted the risk of a potential 6-12 month gold consolidation.

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-google.png)

> When looking out years, I remain structurally bullish on gold. And I think it can outperform the S&P 500 with a 3-5 year view. However, at current levels I think gold’s risks are increasing for a price consolidation that could last upwards of 6-12 months.
>
> On the monthly chart, it’s nearly as technically overbought as it’s ever been:
>
> ![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-gold-chart.png)
>
> While market turmoil and trade debates are likely to persist for a while longer, I think the market already reached “peak uncertainty” during the opening volley of the tariff escalations. With the stock market, bond market, and dollar index going down together in early April, the current administration has shown some degree of concern and de-escalation.
>
> I don’t plan to sell gold, and indeed plan to keep it as a slice in my portfolios as a “hold” rather than a “buy”. In terms of setting expectations and portfolio allocations, I’d advise caution about chasing it at current levels.
>
> -April 27, 2025 premium report

The good news lately is that gold is back to the top end of that consolidation range, and has touched above the psychologically important $3,500/oz range. Additionally, the increasing consensus around a dovish Fed is beneficial for the price, all else being equal.

The bad news is that it’s not really a true breakout yet, more like a test, and it’s still structurally overbought on the monthly chart:

![Gold Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-stockcharts.png)

Now, because I think we’re going through major long-term changes (the end of a four-decade structurally declining interest rate cycle, a global shift in sovereign interest from US Treasuries to gold around the margins in an increasingly multi-polar world, and persistent US fiscal dominance which fuels the attractiveness of hard assets), gold remaining in overbought status for unusually long periods of time is not unreasonable.

That’s why since autumn 2018 when I added precious metals to the newsletter portfolio, I’ve happily erred on staying long even through periods where I expect intermediate-term weakness.

Money Metals visualizes it well in their [quarterly newsletter](https://www.moneymetals.com/uploads/content/MoneyMetalsInsider_Summer2025.pdf). The dollar is no longer expanding as a share of global reserves, and gold is no longer falling, in what is likely the bottom of a trend that basically began after WWII:

![Money Metals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-money-metals.png)

After all these 5 months of price chop, I am increasingly uncertain as to whether to expect a breakout or more consolidation. Given my prior comments, I continue to be structurally long, and at the very least I am less negative on the intermediate-term prospects for the metal than I was in April.
