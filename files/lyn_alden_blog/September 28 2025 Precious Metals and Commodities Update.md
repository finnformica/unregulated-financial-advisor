# Precious Metals and Commodities Update

*September 28, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report discusses the US business cycle and liquidity conditions, with an important liquidity milestone reached.

The digital asset section discusses the recent bitcoin price correction and provides an option trade idea for Strategy (MSTR).

The investment analysis section provides an update on commodities ranging from precious metals to energy.

## Macro View

**US Business Cycle**

The Atlanta Fed’s GDP Now indicator suggests that Q3 (which we’re finishing now) had well over 3% annualized real growth, which is quite strong:

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-gdpnow.png)

The two-speed economy remains in effect, with large fiscal deficits continuing to support spending by seniors and by employees of the healthcare, defense, and financial sectors, while tight monetary policy continues to weigh on residential real estate, commercial real estate, and overall bank lending.

Initial jobless claims, which were flashing a bit of a warning sign in recent weeks with a fresh multi-year breakout, have fallen back into their healthy range:

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-initial-claims.png)

Next up: the country is set for another government shutdown by October 1st if Congress does not pass funding legislation by then. This isn’t a debt ceiling dispute, so it won’t come with a major liquidity boost as those tend to do. Mainly, it’ll inject some uncertainty and will freeze paychecks for many federal employees, which could around the margins impact their spending into the economy as they delay purchases.

I don’t expect the shutdown to be particularly material from a macroeconomic analysis standpoint, but if it goes for an above average length of time, it could eventually become a variable to watch.

**Liquidity**

The Treasury’s cash account reached $800 billion last week. Their target was to reach $850 billion by the end of September, and while this is the last update we’ll get until October, this figure is within a reasonable margin of their target. I had some doubts that they’d refill it so relentlessly (since it drains liquidity when they do), but I have to hand it to the Treasury staff: they did.

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-tga.png)

The good news is that this particular pain is over now (and wasn’t too bad while it occurred).

In my report four weeks ago, I outlined the effect that this would have on domestic liquidity indicators:

> Domestic liquidity currently remains rangebound. The overnight reverse repo facility is mostly drained (which puts liquidity back into the system), the Treasury General Account is up to $590 billion as it refills (which pulls liquidity out of the system), and the Fed continues to perform gradual quantitative tightening (which pulls liquidity out of the system.
>
> If the Treasury General Account reaches its $850 billion fully-filled target by the end of September, then it’ll likely drop liquidity to the lower end of its multi-year range.
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

Indeed, that’s exactly what this particular measure of liquidity looks like currently, at a hair below $5.8 trillion:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-domestic-liquidity.png)

More simply, as a subset of domestic liquidity, we can see that total bank cash had a sharp drop to the lower end of its recent range:

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-bank-cash.png)

My expectation is that the Fed will end its balance sheet reduction in 2026 and transition to a slow pace of balance sheet growth, as they try to “land the plane” a bit smoother than they did in 2019.

![Fed Balance Sheets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-fed-balance-sheet.png)

In mid-September, the Fed cut interest rates by another 0.25% (the first cut in nine months), bringing the rate to a bit over 4%. The FOMC has a range of expectations, but on average expects to get closer to 3% over the next couple of years:

![Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-fed-funds.png)

![Dot Plot](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-dot-plot.png)

Commercial bank lending, meanwhile, is gradually re-accelerating. It was tight throughout 2022 amid the rapid Fed tightening and regional bank crisis, then started to accelerate a bit in 2024, briefly cooled according to some metrics in the first half of 2025, and here in the second half of 2025 is showing signs of re-accelerating:

![Bank Lending](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-bank-lending.png)

![SLOOS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-sloos.png)

I continue to be moderately bullish on select bank stocks on a total return basis (capital appreciation and substantial dividends).

## Digital Assets Note

After recovering in the first half of September, bitcoin had a bit of a sell-off here in the second half of September.

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-btcusd.png)

Since the price of bitcoin has a strong correlation with liquidity, I’m not surprised to see some weakness in the price. Global liquidity is pretty decent right now, but most of the buying this year has been concentrated into US corporates, and those are more negatively impacted by the Treasury’s domestic liquidity drain that I mentioned earlier.

![Bitcoin vs Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-bitcoin-vs-global-m2.png)

![Bitcoin vs Global M2 YoY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-bitcoin-vs-global-m2-yoy.png)

Many people on social media are asking if the cycle is over, and in my view the answer is no. There are no major signs of speculative froth in the bitcoin market, and what little signs there were of froth on the margins (high premiums for bitcoin treasury companies and a sharp rise in altcoin treasury companies) have decreased considerably in recent weeks.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

Bitcoin has to shake tourists off from time to time. When it goes up significantly, some people are willing to overleverage it or overpay for leverage on it, and companies that buy and lever up any other altcoin also get rewarded by the market, which is unsustainable. It makes sense for a period of pain to wash out those attempts at free money, which can build the next foundation for another leg higher.

![DeNiro Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-deniro-meme.png)

**MSTR Cash-Secured Puts**

A trade I like currently is to sell cash-secured MSTR puts at a strike price of $250 or $260 for January 2026, which is a moderately bullish trade on the stock. Volatility is pretty high currently. Investors can earn a decent income from premiums if the stock remains above the strike price for the next 3.5 months, or can enter at a cost basis that is 20-25% below current prices if the stock dips and remains below the strike price.

If bitcoin remains at the current price and MSTR dips to that cost basis, the mNAV would be in the 1.1x to 1.2x range, which I consider low. If bitcoin also dips significantly, then it increases the odds that MSTR will fall to those strike prices, but it also provides more of a bullish coiled spring in the months that may follow.

I’ve been long MSTR since August 2020 in the Newsletter portfolio, and built a position during 2021 and 2022 in the No Limits portfolio. During 2024 I trimmed the position in the No Limits portfolio after major gains, specifically during times of exuberance when the mNAV reached around 3x. In my March 16, 2025 report I provided a cash-secured put sale (expiry in June 2025) to benefit from the volatile dip at the time which indeed gave good returns, and this cash-secured put sale will be my second. We’ll see how it goes.

## Precious Metals and Commodities Update

Gold continues to perform very well in this cycle, and in particular is up 42% year-to-date.

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-gold-price.png)

I’ve been bullish and long gold since 2018 in the newsletter portfolio due to the prospect of fiscal dominance and a gradually shifting global order, though at times I have been concerned about it becoming tactically overbought (mainly in mid-2020 and again here in early/mid 2025). Recently I’ve been in the cautious bull camp.

> The good news lately is that gold is back to the top end of that consolidation range, and has touched above the psychologically important $3,500/oz range. Additionally, the increasing consensus around a dovish Fed is beneficial for the price, all else being equal.
>
> The bad news is that it’s not really a true breakout yet, more like a test, and it’s still structurally overbought on the monthly chart:
>
> ![Gold Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-stockcharts.png)
>
> Now, because I think we’re going through major long-term changes (the end of a four-decade structurally declining interest rate cycle, a global shift in sovereign interest from US Treasuries to gold around the margins in an increasingly multi-polar world, and persistent US fiscal dominance which fuels the attractiveness of hard assets), gold remaining in overbought status for unusually long periods of time is not unreasonable.
>
> That’s why since autumn 2018 when I added precious metals to the newsletter portfolio, I’ve happily erred on staying long even through periods where I expect intermediate-term weakness.
>
> Money Metals visualizes it well in their [quarterly newsletter](https://www.moneymetals.com/uploads/content/MoneyMetalsInsider_Summer2025.pdf). The dollar is no longer expanding as a share of global reserves, and gold is no longer falling, in what is likely the bottom of a trend that basically began after WWII:
>
> ![Money Metals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-money-metals.png)
>
> After all these 5 months of price chop, I am increasingly uncertain as to whether to expect a breakout or more consolidation. Given my prior comments, I continue to be structurally long, and at the very least I am less negative on the intermediate-term prospects for the metal than I was in April.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

Perhaps more newsworthy recently is the breakout in silver and platinum, which I maintain positions in for various portfolios. Historically these are somewhat later-stage breakouts during a gold bull market, as they are purchased primarily by investors and traders rather than by central banks.

Silver has practically gone vertical, having entered June at around $33/oz and now being $46/oz (a 39% gain) after just a few months:

![Silver Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-silver-price.png)

Platinum had an *explosive* June, peaked in July, spent a couple of months consolidating, and just broke out again which brings it to a 62% gain so far this calendar year:

![Platinum Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-platinum-price.png)

I’m one of those weirdos who actually holds some physical platinum as part of my precious metals stash, so that’s certainly nice to see for a change.

**Precious Metals vs Equities**

The S&P 500 priced in dollars is pretty close to its all-time high, but priced in gold it remains below its Dotcom-bubble peak. There have been four major peaks in the US stock market relative to gold, and we seem to be rolling over from the fourth one:

![SPX vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-spx-vs-gold.png)

It’s important to note that, inclusive of reinvested dividends, US equities have solidly beaten gold over the ultra long term. That chart above is *without* reinvesting dividends, which doesn’t make a huge difference over a 3-5 year period (since the S&P 500 dividend yield is so low at only 1.2%) but it does make a massive difference over multi-decade periods, especially from back when yields were higher.

Notably, however, even with dividends reinvested, one would have been better off buying and holding gold for 25 years from the peak of the Dotcom Bubble through this year:

![SPX TR vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-spx-tr-vs-gold.png)

I continue to view the equity-to-gold ratio as likely to gradually roll over during the next five years, meaning I tend toward being a bit more bullish on gold than equities. However, as readers know, I treat each stock/sector separately, and I think there are plenty of equities capable of outperforming gold.

When we look at broad US stocks, valuations by many metrics are either the highest they’ve ever been (such as the market capitalization to GDP ratio) or they’re the second highest they’ve ever been (like the cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio):

![Shiller PE](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-shiller-pe.png)

![Market Cap to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-market-cap-to-gdp.png)

Now, one has to be careful with these metrics, because for the past decade various perma-bears have been citing high valuations as a reason to short or otherwise avoid US equity indices as they’ve continued to grind higher over time. During fiscal dominance, asset bubbles can persist longer than one might expect, because the currency itself is part of the bubble. When the denominator itself is weakening rather persistently, the numerator (prices of assets) can levitate and find a new higher equilibrium.

Another way of putting that, is if a government is running 6-7% deficits as a percentage of GDP as far as the eye can see, do you really want to go all-in on bonds yielding 4%? And if not, what else do you want to buy, and what price is reasonable to pay for those other things?

As an extreme example of this, one might look at the stock market of several developing market countries when they have recessions and currency crises. Those stock markets will typically perform poorly in dollar terms and gold terms, but often pretty well in their own local currency terms, because the local currency is performing even worse than the equities in those cases. The equities have at lease some degree of inflation-adjustment built in.

When the US or other developed market encounters fiscal dominance, it typically looks like “emerging market lite”, meaning the numbers are unlikely to be as extreme as may commonly be found in developing countries during crises, but there are some directional similarities in that run-it-hot type of environment with some stagflationary characteristics.

Equities treading water against gold for long periods of time, or underperforming gold, is one of those telltale similarities between a developed country in fiscal dominance or other malaise, and a developing country in a currency crisis.

**Precious Metals vs Energy**

With major equity indices rather expensive, and gold up big and looking pretty overbought too (despite still having a bullish thesis ahead), what’s cheap?

Certain US value sectors (e.g. financials) are pretty cheap, as are emerging markets (e.g. Brazilian and Chinese equities are quietly doing amazing this year from a very low base). But in the commodity realm, the answer is energy. Energy-related commodities are pretty out-of-favor at the moment.

The gold-to-oil ratio is the highest it has ever been outside of March 2020 when the global economy basically shut off.

![Gold vs Oil](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-gold-vs-oil.png)

Energy supply continues to flow pretty well (i.e. no major geopolitical disruptions or shortages, despite all that’s going on), and the two-speed economy that’s happening in the US and many other countries is keeping a lid on demand for the time being.

I continue to be long-term bullish on the oil and gas majors, although many of them are still near their highs so they’re not my primary interest at the moment.

![XOM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-xom-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

**Oilfield Services**

The area I’m paying a bit more attention to now is oilfield services and drillship owners.

It used to be that platinum was the dud in my portfolios, but with that breaking out finally, the more recent dud has been oilfield service companies like Schlumberger (SLB), Haliburton (HAL), and Valaris (VAL).

However, some of these have quietly put in bottoms that are worth watching.

On the surface, the world’s largest (and arguably most premium) oilfield services company is in a significant share price downslope since mid-2023:

![SLB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-slb-fastgraph.png)

However, it hasn’t put in a new low since April:

![SLB Stock](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-slb-stock.png)

The same is true for HAL and VAL:

![HAL Stock](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-hal-stock.png)

![VAL Stock](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-val-stock.png)

US shale oil production continues to flat-line, because existing wells deplete over time and current energy prices don’t support aggressive-enough drilling to replace and expand that capacity:

![US Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-us-oil-production.png)

I currently view the oilfield service companies as interesting places to deploy capital. Their narrow economic moats somewhat limit how much can safely be put in, but topping up a small position at these inexpensive levels is reasonable in my view.
