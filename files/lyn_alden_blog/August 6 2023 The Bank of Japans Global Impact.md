## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

U.S. domestic liquidity continues to be relatively flat:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity, on the other hand, has been mildly upward, thanks to a weaker dollar index and continued policy easing in China and Japan.

When the dollar strengthens, it pressures various countries’ finances largely due to their dollar-denominated debt. Many of them stop buying U.S. Treasuries or trim their reserves a bit in order to backstop their currencies and get dollars. When the dollar weakens, it gives those countries some breathing room, and they usually go back to reserve accumulation. We can see this inverse relationship on the chart of the nominal dollar index and the subset of U.S. securities owned by the foreign sector that are custodied by the Fed:

![Fed Custody and Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-custody.png)

Last week, the Treasury provided their [quarterly financing projection](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy1662). They expect to borrow over $1.85 trillion during the July-December 2023 period on net.

* During the July – September 2023 quarter, Treasury expects to borrow $1.007 trillion in privately-held net marketable debt, assuming an end-of-September cash balance of $650 billion. The borrowing estimate is $274 billion higher than announced in May 2023, primarily due to the lower beginning-of-quarter  cash balance ($148 billion) and higher end-of-quarter cash balance ($50 billion), as well as projections of lower receipts and higher outlays ($83 billion).
* During the October – December 2023 quarter, Treasury expects to borrow $852 billion in privately-held net marketable debt, assuming an end-of-December cash balance of $750 billion.

$1.85 trillion within six months is a remarkably large number, especially outside of an emergency situation. And since the Fed is still reducing their balance sheet, the actual number of Treasuries that need to be absorbed by the domestic private sector or the foreign sector is even larger than $1.85 trillion, and is instead closer to $2 trillion. Higher interest rates have greatly increased the Treasury’s interest expense and mildly decreased their revenue.

So far, the Treasury Department has been focusing on T-bill issuance even though that’s the most expensive part of the curve, because that can easily be funded out of reverse repos and thus is neutral for liquidity. However, they also announced that they plan to increase longer duration note and bond issuance going forward, which could put more strain on domestic liquidity conditions.

More and more analysts and capital allocators are talking about the United States’ [growing fiscal issues](https://www.lynalden.com/july-2023-newsletter/). These large deficits are a form of stimulus, and they push back potential recession timelines, but contribute to longer-run inflationary potential.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy has been decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, and is beginning to reach stall speed or becoming recessionary in many metrics. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as most of them are now), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms. Imports and exports are additionally negative in nominal terms.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

The purchasing manager’s index is currently slightly over 46, which is historically associated with recessionary or near-recessionary conditions. Anything below 50 represents contraction:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-ism-pmi.png)

The labor market continues to be the strongest indicator. However, the labor market is prone to revisions, and temporary help services (a leading indicator for overall payrolls) is in negative year-over-year territory already. When business conditions are weak, it’s typically the temps that get let go first:

![Temps](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-temps.png)

However, the [Atlanta Fed’s model](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow) is looking rather bullish for Q3, with a 3.9% annualized growth rate forecast. I expect this to drift lower as more quarterly data comes in:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-gdpnow.png)

The way I would characterize the market is that of two giant forces pushing against each other.

-Monetary policy is relatively restrictive, at least for interest rate sensitive areas (commercial real estate, unprofitable companies that rely on high equity valuations and a lot of share issuance, and small businesses that rely on bank financing).

-Fiscal policy is strongly expansive, with huge deficits that are pouring out into the economy, mostly in a top-down manner. The large deficits primarily pour money out in the form of Social Security, Medicare, Interest Expense, and Defense, and three out of four of those are primarily directed at seniors.

Both of these forces will *strengthen* even if the Fed merely holds rates where they are. As more and more debt matures and gets refinanced at these higher rates, it’ll continue to pressure certain parts of the private sector with higher effective rates, but will continue to increase the government’s interest expense as well.

What types of equities do I want to own in this environment? Mostly things that seniors spend on: healthcare companies, travel companies, restaurants, and so forth. They’re the ones with the money, and fiscal policy continues to be supportive of their spending as certain other parts of the market are pressured.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has been rangebound lately, which makes sense given the neutral liquidity conditions.

The price is up remarkably year-to-date, from the post-FTX price implosion of late 2022. So far, the pattern has been major moves higher in a matter of days, followed by months of gradually declining, until the next move up comes.

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-btc-ytd.png)

The supply side continues to be tight, with about 70% of coins held in addresses that have not moved in at least one year. In order to convince those holders to sell, it usually takes a massive price increase. During bull markets, some of these long-term holders sell their coins into strength. During bear markets, they hold tight, accumulate more, and basically set the price floor.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-bitcoin-hodl-wave.png)

I continue to view bitcoin as having put in its price bottom and being in a position to grind higher to new highs over the next few years. However, the price will likely be very much tied to liquidity conditions, and so I expect considerable volatility and periods of stagnation or correction along the way. I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the most sensible approach for most investors that want a bitcoin allocation.

## Japan’s Impact on Global Assets

This section will look at recent actions by the Bank of Japan and how they might affect global equities and bonds.

**Japan’s Yield Curve Control Update**

For the past several years, the Bank of Japan has been engaging in a yield curve control, meaning that in addition to controlling short-term interest rates, they are also controlling long-term 10-year government bond yields.

![JGB Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-jgb-yield.png)

The mechanism to achieve this is that the BOJ basically has to maintain an open bid for 10-year bonds that go above their yield target (which is another way of saying that they need to be willing to buy any bond that goes below their price target). This keeps financing costs low for the government, but results in constant central bank balance sheet expansion. The central bank has to keep printing new base money in order to buy more bonds, with no end in sight, even when inflation is above the bank’s 2% target (as it is now).

About a week ago, the BOJ surprised markets with a tweak to its yield curve control policy. They previously had used a yield cap of 0.25% for the 10-year bond, but ever since late 2022 they had increased it to 0.50%. Well, this past week they effectively changed the target again, but in a different way.

Specifically, the BOJ announced that although 0.50% is still their target, they would now be more flexible about defending that target. They announced they would have an open bid for 10-year bonds at a 1.oo% yield, which effectively draws a hard cap at that level. But, by leaving the target unchanged, it purposely makes bond shorts uncomfortable if they are within the 0.50% to 1.00% range. The BOJ has discretion to intervene and hammer down the yields closer to their target at any time, which means trying to get too aggressive as a bond short is risky.

There is now a safe zone under 0.50%, a gray zone between 0.50% and 1.00%, and a hard limit (via an unlimited central bank bid) at 1.00%.

**Global Implications**

A fraction-of-a-percent move like this might not seem important, but BOJ policy is among the most important in the world. This is because Japan is the second largest creditor nation in the world, meaning that they own a *lot* of foreign financial assets. And with so much financial repression domestically, Japanese investors go out and buy bonds of other countries in search of higher yields. When possible, they often hedge the currency risk out of the bonds, while still picking up the higher yields.

However, in certain market environments, hedging costs are higher. Right now, a 10-year U.S. Treasury bond that is currency-hedged offers a *lower* yield than Japanese 10-year bonds. As the BOJ gets slightly less dovish, it helps incentivize some capital around the margins to come back home and to stay with domestic assets. If the effect is large enough, it can stabilize the currency and is generally not good for foreign bond markets.

In the days after the BOJ’s announcement, the U.S. bond market became somewhat disorderly. The 30-year bond had a particularly sharp move:

![U.S. Long Bond](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-us-long-bond.png)

The argument for lower U.S. Treasury bond yields ahead is that when the U.S. enters a recession, long-duration bonds will be a source of defense as they normally are. The argument for higher bond yields ahead is that huge fiscal deficits and re-accelerating energy inflation will put pressure on bond yields even in a recession, much like what happens to many emerging markets.

At these prices, I don’t like the risk/reward of long duration bonds, either with a short view or a long view. For the low volatility part of my portfolio, I prefer T-bills or short-duration notes (roughly 5 years or less), as well as defensive value stocks and gold. For my higher volatility part of my portfolio, I prefer commodity producers, select growth stocks, and bitcoin. Long bonds just aren’t very compelling to me here.

Japanese banks, for example, have been weak performers for a while. In an environment of suppresed yields, it’s hard for them to grow. However, as Japan moves towards a slightly less dovish policy stance with some actual steepness to its yield curve, it should benefit the country’s banks. So far, Japan isn’t showing any intentions of doing what the Federal Reserve did, where they jacked short-term interest rates up so quickly that they hurt many of the banks.

## Final Thoughts: Apple Earnings

In my prior premium report, I highlighted Apple (AAPL) as an example of a company with a risky valuation:

> In general, I view most of the mega-cap stocks as being overbought at the moment, especially compared to the expected returns from T-bills and T-notes. So far, stocks have pushed up higher than I would have guessed, and the overall risk/reward doesn’t seem attractive.
>
> The recent price rise of Apple (AAPL), for example, has been entirely valuation-based amid sluggish fundamentals:
>
> ![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-aapl-fastgraph.png)
>
> -July 23rd premium report

This past week, Apple reported quarterly earnings with some mixed results. Revenue came in-line with consensus expectations and earnings per share came in slightly better than consensus expectations, although the consensus expectations are usually low-bars for companies to beat. It was the mix of revenue that concerned investors, with declining sales of iPhones, iPads, and Macs, (the core business) which were offset by solid growth in wearables and services.

All-in-all, it was not a bad result given the relatively slow growth rate of the global economy at the moment. But what makes it a problem is the valuation: a stock trading at 30x earnings can run into significant price challenges if its growth stagnates for too long.

![Apple Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-apple-revenue.png)

As a result of the earnings numbers, the stock dropped nearly 5% on Friday, and various momentum indicators appear to be rolling over from overbought levels.

![AAPL Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-8-6-aapl-chart.png)

Overall, I continue to see similar risks among most of the mega-cap tech stocks. As companies they are doing fine, but the high valuations and sluggish growth means they don’t really have a margin of safety at current share price levels.

There are two main ways that the mega-cap stocks can do poorly for the rest of the year and into next year, and one main way that they can do well.

Scenario 1) The U.S. business cycle continues to slow down until it enters a recession. That’s not likely to be good for most stocks in general, including the mega-cap growth stocks.

Scenario 2) The U.S. business cycle remains in a goldilocks scenario, with falling inflation but still positive real GDP growth thanks to large structural deficits that serve as a form of ongoing stimulus. That can be good for many stocks.

Scenario 3) Energy inflation comes back for a second round, and/or heavy bond issuance drives up bond yields. That’s an okay environment for certain value-oriented stocks, but may put a lot of downward valuation pressure on mega-cap growth stocks.

We’ve been in Scenario 2 for this year so far, but with energy prices showing signs of firming up and refinancing risk still ahead for many companies even if interest rates remain where they are, I expect either scenario 1 or 3 (or a mix of both) to start asserting itself a bit more.
