## Macro View

A significant part of this report is going to be bearish, so I’m going to start with some good news.

**COVID-19 Update**

I used to track COVID-19 numbers in each report, and especially their rate of change, because they were a significant macro factor. As mutation waves came and went, various regions would lock down and open back up, and that had significant influence on many types of companies.

For many months, I haven’t been tracking it at all, because other than China with their outlier zero-COVID policy, the virus hasn’t been a notable global macro factor for a while now.

However, I want to take a moment to highlight that another wave came and went this summer with little fanfare. The global reported death count was quite low:

![COVID-19 Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-covid.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

There are still some policies in place that matter economically, however.

For example, there are very different travel policies around the world ranging from nothing to a strict ban. Travelers to the US need to be vaccinated. Japan still has a complete individual tourist ban in place, although they are expected to end that soon. Months ago they opened back up to tourist groups, but individual tourists are still banned. The chart of Japan’s tourist arrivals is stunning, as they dropped from 3 million tourist arrivals per month to effectively zero, for two and a half years now:

![Japan Tourists](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-japan-tourists.jpg)

Considering that Japan currently has a record trade deficit and a currency devaluation that borders on being disorderly (mainly due to the combination of performing formal yield curve control and being a net energy importer amid an energy crisis), they could certainly use those foreign tourist money inflows again. There are, however, [some concerns](https://www.washingtonpost.com/world/2022/09/16/japan-covid-restrictions-tourist-xenophobia/) about Japan being able to regain its former status as a major tourist destination.

I’m currently in Egypt visiting family and friends for the month, as I do each year, so I’ll use this country as somewhat of a counter-example that I have both numbers and personal experience with. Here are Egypt’s tourist arrivals, which are currently at healthy levels, at 850,000+ per month:

![Egypt Tourist Arrivals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-egypt-tourists.jpg)

I have some annotations on this one because it’s more complicated than Japan’s situation, with some major events that caused large but brief drops in tourism.

During the entirety of COVID-19, Egyptian tourism was largely unaffected. I skipped what would have been my 2020 trip here, but during 2021 it was business-as-usual, albeit with a negative covid test requirement for entry. For 2022, there was no test requirement at all.

Egypt has fewer cumulative reported covid deaths per capita than Japan in total, with two major caveats that Egypt has a much younger median population than Japan, and reporting accuracy in a country with $3k USD GDP per capita is inevitably challenging. But overall, it doesn’t seem to have been a major issue compared to other countries.

Overall, I’m glad to see that the virus is a lot less of a global concern now than when it began, and that many countries have relaxed or removed various restrictions associated with it. It’s not something that I need to cover in each report like I used to. There are a handful of countries like Japan and especially China that continue to have outlier strict policies in place, but for the most part in my macro analysis, the virus is no longer a factor that I consider much, unless something changes.

The stock fell over 20% in a single day, which was the biggest drop for the stock in over four decades.

I’m not thrilled about this for two reasons.

The first reason is that I have about 1% of the newsletter portfolio in FedEx as one of my dividend holdings, so that’s not great. Overall, most indicators continue to suggest global economic weakness ahead, particularly in the United States and Europe. China has some green shoots since it is further along in a downcycle already, but its zero-covid policy and drought-related power generation issues are pressuring growth prospects there as well.

**Inflation and Fed Policy Update**

Price inflation continues to be a problem. The August numbers were reported last week, and they caught the market by surprise.

By every measure, price inflation came in above consensus expectations, even though it is down from the peak. In other words, it’s falling, but not nearly as fast as expected. The month-over-month headline and core figures, and by extension the year-over-year figures as well, were notably above expectations.

While the reduction in energy prices brought down headline numbers, the month over month figures show sticky core inflation. Market expectations were for 0.3% month over month core inflation, but the actual number was closer to 0.6%:

![CPI MOM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-cpi-mom.jpg)

Similarly, the market was expecting 6.1% year-over-year core inflation, but got a 6.3% print instead:

![CPI YOY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-cpi-yoy.jpg)

[John Authors](https://twitter.com/johnauthers) from Bloomberg had a good summary of the issue:

![John Authors Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-john-authors-tweet.jpg)

Laggy data is part of the problem. Headline shelter inflation (rent and owner’s equivalent rent) accounts for about a third of CPI, but the numbers are a laggy and smoothed-out version of what already happened with actual house prices and rent prices. Shelter CPI continues to explode higher, and despite this, it still understates what actually happened in hindsight with house prices and nationwide rents:

![Shelter CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-cpi-shelter.jpg)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Even factoring out laggy shelter inflation, however, services inflation has been substantial recently.

Due to a combination of supply-side issues and fiscal/monetary stimulus, I’ve been warning about upcoming inflation since 2020 in multiple articles and newsletters and reports ([and as early as mid-2019 was hinting at it](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/), but without a catalyst yet at that point, just noticing the kindling). And my view has been that several aspects of it will be longer-term in nature than most market participants currently realize (lack of sufficient energy investment, peak globalization at least in rate-of-change terms if not absolute terms, and high sovereign debt loads that necessitate structurally negative real yields).

Many people cite supply chain issues or transitory factors, and they certainly have been a variable. But widespread fiscal stimulus and broad money supply growth throughout 2020 and 2021 at levels relative to GDP not seen since World War II, were significant demand-side factors, resulting in currency devaluation. And due to how the money was spent, it didn’t benefit the working class as much as it could have, even though they take a big hit from the resulting inflation. Rents, for example, did not go up due to supply chain issues. That was more of a broad money supply issue.

My [March 2021 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/) was probably my most direct description of what at the time was a nascent price inflation issue, which has since become the biggest issue in the domestic and global economy. And in this section, I compared what was starting to unfold here in the 2020s, to the 1940s inflation:

> Inflation that is truly transitory in absolute terms would mean that a lot of prices go up due to a temporary supply shock of some sort, and then come back down when the supply shock is over.
>
> On the other hand, inflation that is only transitory in rate of change terms would mean that a broad set of prices jump quickly and then stop going up quickly, but never actually come back down. Instead, they go through a permanent step-wise increase in price levels and reach a new equilibrium at a higher level.
>
> This first chart here shows the year-over-year consumer price index change (aka price inflation) in the 1940s. Indeed, each inflationary spike was transitory. However, the second chart shows the absolute level of the consumer price index. After each inflationary spike, prices remained at that permanently higher plateau. In other words, inflation was transitory in rate of change terms, but not absolute terms.
>
> ![Transitory Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-transitory-inflation.png)Chart Source: St. Louis Fed
>
> The current inflationary spike that we’re seeing in 2021 is being dismissed as being caused by supply shocks. But of course, all inflationary events involve supply shocks. That’s one of the inherent catalysts of why inflation happens, every time.
>
> The 1940s had all sorts of war-related commodity and labor shortages. The 1970s had the oil embargo. The 2020s have semiconductor shortages, various commodity shortages, and shipping constraints.
>
> External/accidental supply shocks on their own eventually result in prices returning to normal, when the anomalous supply shock is resolved. They involve specific goods/services.
>
> On the other hand, rapid increases in the broad money supply that boost demand for goods and services without boosting the supply of goods and services, result in supply shocks and cause price inflation. As the market adjusts over time, this price inflation becomes transitory in rate of change terms, but with prices that ultimately settle at a higher level, due to more money permanently being in the system. This second type of inflation is likely what we’re experiencing at this time.
>
> -May 2021 Newsletter

And since December 2021, I’ve been looking for a disinflationary period within a longer-term inflationary decade. At first I wrote that the year-over-year US CPI peak would likely be in late Q1 or early Q2, but had to push it back a quarter to late Q2 or early Q3 due to Russia’s war and the associated energy price volatility. That local peak indeed came in the last month of Q2. With fiscal and monetary stimulus wearing off, and policymakers aiming for demand destruction to rein in inflation, we’re back in a disinflationary environment for the moment, albeit from high levels.

However, I eventually expect the strong dollar, lack of sufficient Treasury security buyers, and/or recessionary conditions to force the Fed to be unable to continue tightening monetary policy even if inflation is still a problem. The way I’ve been phrasing it is that the Fed can certainly tighten monetary policy, but that they can’t *normalize* monetary policy back to structurally positive real rates with debts relative to GDP at the levels they are at. And when they fail to fully normalize policy, I view that as a potential catalyst for the next round of inflation due to the supply side issues that will likely still exist.

**Don’t Forget About Oil**

Oil prices have been declining from their spring highs, but remain elevated at $85/barrel.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-wti-crude.jpg)

This is with the dollar at unusually strong levels vs the euro and the yen (and as such, the oil price in euro or yen terms remains more elevated), and with central banks trying to curtail inflation via demand destruction, and with China maintaining zero-covid policies and locking down cities on a regular basis (which results in oil demand destruction), and with the US aggressively selling oil from its Strategic Petroleum Reserve (resulting in temporary extra supply to the market):

![Strategic Petroleum Reserve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-spr.jpg)

The Biden Administration wants gasoline prices under control in the run-up to the midterm elections. Gasoline prices are some of the most visible and thus politically-sensitive signs of inflation.

Once we’re past the midterms, however, this Strategic Petroleum Reserve drawdown will likely be over. They’ve talked about potentially refilling it next year at lower prices, but those lower prices might or might not materialize, especially without the Strategic Petroleum Reserve actively selling oil into the market. It’s not out of the question that they’ll end up refilling it at higher prices than they sold it.

Once they stop selling, or even more impactfully start buying, that removes one of the significant downward forces on oil prices and potentially creates an upward force on oil prices. They’ve signaled a willingness [to buy under $80/barrel](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-13/biden-officials-mull-buying-oil-at-around-80-to-refill-reserves) to protect US oil production.

There is no major oil supply response yet, with US oil production still below its 2019 peak, and with OPEC+ consistently failing to meet its output targets. Furthermore, OPEC+ recently decided to trim production by 100,000 barrels/day to maintain upward pressure on oil prices.

The new UK prime minister is changing course on UK energy policy, with a renewed focus on domestic fracking and North Sea production. However, this will take some time. Those are some of the early signs of supply-response, but at the moment it’s still policy talk rather than physical reality.

I continue to view the global oil market like a beachball being held underwater by numerous policy choices (Fed tightening, SPR drawdowns, China recurring lockdowns, etc). The supply limitations are still there and largely unaddressed, with growing emerging market demand, and are ready to re-emerge as soon as some of those downward forces can no longer be maintained. In addition, both the Biden Administration and OPEC+ potentially have a floor at $80/barrel, where the SPR could turn into a buyer and OPEC+ could cut production further.

Therefore, I am agnostic about oil over say the next 6-12 months, but bullish on oil, oil producers, and pipelines over the next several years. In recent months I have been emphasizing pipelines as the more conservative way to play a bear market.

**Geopolitical Bifurcation Continues**

The mostly unipolar geopolitical world that was in place from the early 1990s to the early 2020s, and that helped to accelerate globalization (and thus structural disinflation), continues to seemingly transition towards a more bipolar or multi-polar world.

Iran is moving closer to joining the Shanghai Cooperation Organization, and Turkey [stated their willingness to join as well](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-17/turkey-seeks-china-led-bloc-membership-in-threat-to-nato-allies). The organization is working towards doing more trade in non-USD terms.

![Tanchum Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-tanchum-tweet.jpg)

*Source: [Michael Tanchum](https://twitter.com/michaeltanchum/status/1571378502942613505)*

In a piece of good news for the West, however, Ukraine scored a significant tactical military victory against Russia in recent weeks, with Russia [withdrawing forces](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-62860774) back by the most since they pulled back from Kyiv in April.

The West’s attempt to reduce Russian exports has largely failed. The world needs Russia’s commodities, and emerging markets throughout the world (both inside and outside the Shanghai Cooperation Organization) have generally ignored Western calls to boycott Russia, and instead re-affirmed that they’ll buy commodities from whichever source is cheapest including Russia, even as some of them distance themselves from Russia’s war in Ukraine. I wrote that they would do this out of necessity back in spring 2022; countries like India with $2k USD GDP per capita risk riots if they don’t get commodities at the cheapest possible cost.

However, the West’s financial support of Ukraine, and the West’s restriction on exporting tech to Russia, has been more successful. Cutting off Russia’s relatively small import market doesn’t materially affect key global providers of semiconductors, software, and other tech, and Russia has several gaps in these areas domestically. This can negatively affect Russia both economically and militarily. It’s pretty much up to China to determine how closely it wants to align with Russia and fill some of their tech gaps. Meanwhile, China is a major commodity importer from Russia and elsewhere.

At the recent September 15-16 Shanghai Cooperation Organization Summit, Vladimiar Putin was left briefly waiting by a number of foreign heads of state in meetings. Putin has been known to be late to meetings with foreign leaders as a supposed sign of dominance, and in recent months the opposite has been occurring. Turkey’s Erdogan left Putin waiting in a July 2022 meeting (after the opposite happened to Erdogan in 2020), and a number of heads of state of former Soviet countries left Putin briefly waiting at this September SCO summit. India’s Modi [told Putin at the summit](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-16/putin-tells-modi-he-s-aware-of-constant-india-concern-on-ukraine) that now is not the time for war, and urged a path towards peace. The various SCO members and other nations continue to maintain and grow trade with Russia, but there has been some shuffling around of relationships as Russia increasingly relies on trade with non-Western countries.

## Digital Assets Note- The Merge

Ethereum successfully performed what they call the Merge last week, which in this context means a transition from a proof-of-work system to a proof-of-stake system.

This occasion warrants a bigger-than-usual digital assets note, in contrast to my prior report where I had a smaller-than-usual digital assets note.

Blockchains serve as global distributed ledgers that are regularly being updated with new transactions, with the full history recorded on all full nodes around the world. In proof-of-work systems, energy is used by miners to update the global ledger state. In proof-of-stake systems, a subset of coinholders serve as validators to update the global ledger state.

The first piece of good news is that the successful Merge reduces technical risk. The Ethereum developers have been planning this since the network’s inception in 2014, and this was always a big question hanging over it. While many proof-of-stake systems exist now, converting an active proof-of-work system to a proof-of-stake system was very hard in the technical sense and was basically unprecedented. It was originally supposed to take less than a year in 2015, but ended up taking seven years. The moment of the Merge itself, however, was smooth with no major bugs or major forks.

The second piece of good news is that this will reduce supply issuance, and during periods of heavy volume, it could result in outright deflationary supply of ether. The issuance rate depends on how many validators are active, while the burn rate depends on how heavy network usage volume is (which can be thought of kind of like share buybacks, where the more profits that are generated, the faster a company can buy back its shares and reduce its share count). So far since the Merge, the supply is increasing but at a much slower rate than before.

The bad news is that proof-of-stake is far more complex, relies more on governance, requires centralized checkpoints and manual coordination to recover if the blockchain ever goes offline (people running Solana validators for example manually meet in a Discord chat to figure out how to restart the network every time it goes offline), and tends to gradually centralize power over time rather than gradually distribute power like proof-of-work does (due to large capex expenditures). I’ve discussed some of the centralization issues with proof-of-stake in a few different areas, with my longest piece being [here](https://www.lynalden.com/proof-of-stake/).

My view is that proof-of-stake systems can be thought of as federated computing environments, and can be quite efficient for things like crypto games, NFTs, and crypto asset trading, but do not offer the same hardness, decentralization, and trust-minimization as a proof-of-work system. They reduce their energy usage, but then rely more on human governance instead. They reduce their division of powers, and rely more on circular logic, where the coinholders determine the state of the ledger, and the state of the ledger is the record of who the coinholders are, which is a perpetual motion machine that only works while the system is constantly online and ceases to make sense the second it goes offline. If such a system ever goes offline (as Solana has done quite often), it is nearly costless to create a countless number of alternative ledgers, and there is no absolute determination for what the correct ledger is other than oligopolistic human governance and/or centralized developer checkpoints.

Proof-of-work systems don’t have that problem; the heaviest proof-of-work chain speaks for itself. There’s no ability to costlessly create alternative ledger states that compete with the real one. It would take years of computation to make a heavier chain. Even if the whole Internet somehow goes offline and then eventually comes back online, the Bitcoin network can restart itself where it left off with no manual coordination between entities. Simple user-run nodes would be able to find the heaviest chain and continue from there.

These trade-offs often get obfuscated or ignored by people promoting proof-of-stake systems. It’s important to be aware of the trade-offs.

Ironically, one way for proof-of-stake systems to harden themselves against that offline problem, is to regularly insert their checkpoints into the Bitcoin blockchain, and to thus piggy-back off of proof-of-work. Balaji Srinivasan made a [Twitter thread](https://twitter.com/balajis/status/1406505278766338052) about this back in 2021 (a key line: “Proof-of-work is absolute truth, proof-of-stake is relative truth.”), and last month a [paper was published](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.05408) that showed how proof-of-stake systems can use Bitcoin’s proof-of-work blockchain as a checkpoint defense against their risk of going offline:

The abstract of the paper:

> Blockchain systems based on a reusable resource, such as proof-of-stake (PoS), provide weaker security guarantees than those based on proof-of-work. Specifically, they are vulnerable to long-range attacks, where an adversary can corrupt prior participants in order to rewrite the full history of the chain. To prevent this attack on a PoS chain, we propose a protocol that checkpoints the state of the PoS chain to a proof-of-work blockchain such as Bitcoin. Our checkpointing protocol hence does not rely on any central authority. Our work uses Schnorr signatures and leverages Bitcoin recent Taproot upgrade, allowing us to create a checkpointing transaction of constant size. We argue for the security of our protocol and present an open-source implementation that was tested on the Bitcoin testnet.

That’s not to say that there aren’t some centralization pressures in proof-of-work systems as well. Historically, the limited number of semiconductor foundries and bitcoin mining hardware producers has been the tightest bottleneck, and a number of companies ranging from Square (SQ) to Blockstream (private) to Intel (INTC) are working on broadening that out.

**Price Action Analysis**

Ethereum’s unique selling proposition was that it was the main proof-of-work smart contract platform, with all the other major ones being proof-of-stake. Now, it’s one of many proof-of-stake smart contract platforms. It has much bigger branding, much bigger size/liquidity, and much more developer activity than its competitors, but at what is usually a higher transaction fee rate than competitors. As such, smaller transactions tend to move to cheaper and more centralized smart contract blockchains, while big transactions tend to remain on Ethereum.

In the very long run, Ethereum’s price will in large part depend on its usage and its ability to retain market share or not. In the short and intermediate terms, however, it’s about speculation, supply/demand, and narrative.

There are a couple main factors that support the price. The first is that its issuance rate is way down, and the second is that people staking their ether on the network to earn yield still can’t unstake. Unstaking wasn’t included in the Merge update. They are locked in as forced holders, and developers expect to take months or more to add unstaking capability.

On the other hand, the biggest negative for the price is the observation that the Merge was very hyped both in terms of what was happening on exchanges with speculative positioning, and what was happening on social media and even mainstream financial media like CNBC. So far, it has been a “buy the rumor, sell the news” event, but it’s still too early to say for sure.

The ETHBTC ratio has rolled over, and doesn’t look bullish.

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-ethbtc.jpg)

Whenever this ratio soars, Ethereum influencers tend to gloat on social media, and whenever it rolls over, Bitcoin influencers tend to gloat. Often, both sides call it too early, and this ends up being a useful contrarian sentiment indicator.

This ratio sharply rolled over this summer during the broad crypto bear market, but then the Merge date was firmly announced for September, and that contributed to a renewed period of hype and upward movement in the ratio. With that pre-Merge rally over, the ETHBTC ratio might be ready to fall again. NFT volumes are low, DeFi is mainly about trading and leverage, and competing smart contract platforms with lower fees constantly nip at Ethereum’s heels for market share.

I don’t have a firm view on what ETHBTC will do in the short/intermediate term, but at a current ratio of 0.072, I lean bearish on ETHBTC as long as it remains below the recent high of 0.085. However, a breakout over that level would be an invalidation point on the bearish ETHBTC view in a charting sense.

It doesn’t interest me personally from a consumer standpoint, but I’m not really the target market for this sort of thing. **Bitcoin Update**

With the Ethereum Merge in the news lately, we should also ask what this means for Bitcoin.

Ethereum is the only cryptocurrency that is in the same ballpark as Bitcoin in terms of market capitalization and volumes. Its market capitalization has never exceeded Bitcoin’s so far, but due to the higher-velocity nature of Ethereum (it’s primarily used for trading and leveraging), its volumes have at times eclipsed Bitcoin’s.

The big catch, however, has been that due to difficulty bombs inserted into the code, Ethereum was never truly decentralized, with developers maintaining firm control rather than individual node-runners (unlike Bitcoin, where individual node-runners have most of the power). Even without the difficulty bomb, Ethereum’s reliance on centralized stablecoins for DeFi has become another area of system centralization.

Now that Ethereum has switched over to proof-of-stake, Bitcoin has uncontested dominance in the proof-of-work category, with 94% market capitalization of all proof-of-work coins. All other proof-of-work coins combined represent the other 6%. The technical differentiation between Bitcoin and Ethereum, and more broadly Bitcoin and “everything else” is wider than ever.

Ethereum’s top five proof-of-stake smart contract competitors combined, however, do have about the same market capitalization as Ethereum. It’s a much closer race for that field, still contested, although Ethereum is the firm leader.

I expect lobbying and political attacks on Bitcoin’s proof-of-work consensus mechanism to accelerate, including from the Ethereum community and Ethereum companies, now that Ethereum has switched to proof-of-stake. Lobbying against proof-of-work has already been happening from other proof-of-stake networks.

I’ve discussed before [why Bitcon’s energy usage isn’t a problem](https://www.lynalden.com/bitcoin-energy/), and is more of a feature than a bug. It’s a small price to pay for a decentralized and open network, with better division of powers, at least for a critical system that is meant to settle trillions of dollars of value as bitcoin currently does per year. In proof-of-work systems, coinholders don’t determine the state of the ledger, and thus don’t determine who the coinholders are (circular logic, requiring a constant online state), like they do in proof-of-stake systems.

The White House recently released a report that was generally critical of proof-of-work (while acknowledging usage of stranded energy), but it had a number of shortcomings and bad sources (such as repeatedly citing a blogger with a demonstrably bad model for energy usage statistics, rather than the University of Cambridge that unsurprisingly has a much better model).

The same Nic Carter who I cited above about NFTs on Ethereum, in addition to his crypto VC work, has been a long-time analyst regarding the relationship between proof-of-work and energy/sustainability. He released a detailed annotated critique of the White House report [here](https://medium.com/@nic__carter/comments-on-the-white-house-report-on-the-climate-implications-of-crypto-mining-8d65d30ec942). Despite investing in startups that make use of both the Bitcoin and Ethereum blockchains, he has been an advocate for proof-of-work as a more secure and decentralized consensus mechanism than proof-of-stake.

I continue to view Bitcoin as a digital commodity and/or as an open protocol, whereas I view most other cryptocurrencies as equity ecosystems. They’re categorically different, but often lumped together by media, and in some ways they do compete for total market share and “moneyness”.

My short/intermediate term view on Bitcoin (and all crypto, basically) continues to be cautious, with the Federal Reserve tightening monetary policy into a period of economic deceleration. My longer-term view is that this is a good accumulation zone for Bitcoin and that it is likely to do well over a 3-5 year period, as part of a diversified portfolio. I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into it as the best way to get exposure for most people that want some.

In addition to fundamental metrics of growth and ecosystem development, I continue to view the chart of Bitcoin’s market price relative to its realized price (the aggregate on-chain cost basis for network participants) as being an ideal awareness check for sentiment and the long-term risk/reward potential.

While top-down national Bitcoin/Lightning implementations come with a lot of messiness (e.g. El Salvador’s nationwide legal tender law), there has been some success with forming small bitcoin circular economies.

The surfing town of El Zonte in El Salvador was an early example, with a successful circular bitcoin economy being set up in 2019 and becoming rather successful since then, with significant levels of community adoption. This town, now often called “Bitcoin Beach”, was the foothold that eventually led to El Salvador adopting bitcoin as legal tender alongside the dollar, nationwide.

I spoke with podcaster Peter McCormack a couple months ago about his experiences there. He has traveled to El Salvador a number of times over the years. He used to convert to dollars when he arrived for general spending, but on his most recent trip, he almost exclusively used Lightning. As liquidity and network effects have increased, the reliability of the network for payments has increased.

With the general success of Bitcoin Beach, dense little bitcoin-adopted communities have been developed in [Guatemala](https://cointelegraph.com/news/why-is-a-guatemalan-mayor-mining-bitcoin-tackling-fud-with-biogas-and-btc) and [Costa Rica](https://twitter.com/francispouliot_/status/1570816275483590664) and [Isle of Man](https://www.cityam.com/bitcoin-island-the-isle-of-man-is-turning-orange/) termed “Bitcoin Lake” and “Bitcoin Jungle” and “Bitcoin Island” respectively.

## Equities: Update on Brazil

Until I see signs that the US and global economy are turning up in rate-of-change terms, I continue to lean defensive.

For me in terms of equities, this means an overweight position in healthcare stocks, energy pipelines, and other defensive value stocks, preferably with a decent dividend yield. Combined with cash-equivalents and gold, and reasonably-priced real estate with fixed-rate mortgages, these are my defensive holdings.

I take some risk with select emerging markets (e.g. overweight positions in Brazil and India), certain bank stocks, and small positions in a handful of cyclical value stocks or growth stocks, and bitcoin. But that’s mainly because I prefer a low-turnover evergreen portfolio with defensive or aggressive tilts, rather than going all-in on a defensive or aggressive outlook as faster-money traders tend to do. My biggest cyclical overweight remains the energy sector, since I view it as structurally under-invested in and am willing to hold it through substantial volatility.

Aside from EPD and CI that I keep using as defensive stock examples in this environment, I remain optimistic on stocks like CVS and BTI. They were outperformers during the spring 2020 covid crash, and have been outperformers again in 2022. They have various risks, but the economic cycle isn’t really one of them.

However, I want to focus a bit on Brazil here. I highlighted Brazilian equities as a bullish opportunity back in my [January 23, 2022 report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2022-1-23/), and since then they have been a volatile-but-outperforming ride:

Bloomberg recently [published a good summary](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2022-09-17/the-silver-lining-in-a-tense-brazil-election-new-economy-saturday) of some of the positives in Brazil’s economy. In a world of macro doom seemingly everywhere, Brazil already has a lot of pain behind it and actually has some good things going for it. It’s worth an update.

Brazil’s upcoming elections in October are arguably the biggest wildcard here. Global investors in aggregate tend to prefer center-right governments from a sentiment standpoint, with policies that lean pro-capital rather than pro-labor. So, the fact that pro-labor Lula [is leading in the polls](https://www.usnews.com/news/world/articles/2022-09-15/lulas-lead-over-bolsonaro-edges-higher-ahead-of-brazil-election-datafolha-poll) is considered an equity risk for many investors.

However, Lula was president for eight years before, during a commodities bull market, and Brazilian equities did extraordinarily well. Although politics do matter, I think investors are placing a bit too much weight on this election. That’s not to say they should place no weight on it, but I view Brazilian equity valuations as already discounting the election. Lula’s choice of a running mates and other signals continue to [court moderates to his camp](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-09-11/lula-mulls-running-mate-for-key-cabinet-job-if-elected-in-brazil), aiming to position himself as center-left rather than to double-down on support from the far left. Of course, things could change during the administration.

Brazil’s position as a commodities exporter, and with reasonable monetary and fiscal policy at least compared to many other emerging markets, makes it a contender for a portfolio slot if indeed we have a rather inflationary and pro-commodity decade.

Perhaps most importantly, we need to look at Brazil’s inflation and interest rates. Official inflation peaked at 12% year-over-year in April 2022, and has since rolled over:

![Brazil Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-brazil-inflation.jpg)The prior two inflation peaks (circled above) aligned with significant Brazilian equity bottoms in dollar-denominated terms:

![EWZ Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-ewz-price.jpg)

Those bottoms also roughly aligned with the peak in the dollar index, and bottoms in the US ISM purchasing manager’s index. So I still expect some pain and consolidation ahead, but the point is, Brazilian equities are already in a depression-like condition and have now gotten through a double-digit period of inflation and positive real rates.

Interest rates are nearly 14% and rising, although with positive real yields, they might be towards the top of their tightening cycle. In order to maintain a stable currency, they had to get ahead of the Fed’s tightening, and so starting in early 2021 the Central Bank of Brazil began raising their rates from 2% and are now all the way up to 14%, while the Fed is still trying to combat 9% inflation with sub-3% policy rates.

![Brazil Interest Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-brazil-interest-rates.jpg)

Inflation is pretty devastating to highly-indebted developed market countries that haven’t experienced a serious bout of it in four decades. Over-financialized and highly-indebted developed market economies are based on the premise that inflation will be low forever, and that is being challenged.

Brazil, however, has dealt with multiple inflation issues historically. This isn’t the Central Bank of Brazil’s first inflation rodeo. They’re able to raise rates to double digits into positive inflation-adjusted territory if needed, and in this cycle they already have. And in really bad inflationary environments, at least their net commodity exports should generally do well.

## Final Thoughts

This has been an unusual downcycle to position for, because it has been the first stagflationary period in developed markets in four decades. Many investors rely on backtests that only go back a couple decades, whereas history books stretching back to the 1920s, 1930s, and 1940s have been far more informative for this period.

For example, usually when economic activity decelerates (as measured by the ISM purchasing manager’s index), long-duration Treasury rates go down, meaning that the bond prices go up. Usually stocks perform poorly in that environment, and bonds are the safe haven. Investors pile into them for defense, which drives their prices up and their yields down. However, in this cycle, long-duration Treasuries (as measured by the TLT ETF for example) performed *worse* than the S&P 500 in a downcycle, which took a lot of investors by surprise.

Here I show the 10-year Treasury yield with the PMI, and how this cycle broke the typical correlation:

![Rates vs PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-9-18-rates-vs-pmi.jpg)

I titled my [August 2020 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2020-newsletter/), “Treasury Market Dissonance” and focused on the early part of this trend. With the 10-year Treasury yield at 0.71% at the time but multiple signs of economic activity and eventual inflation picking up, and with a supply/demand issue for Treasuries, I wrote about higher yields materializing. In that newsletter, I also called for a potential local top in gold due to this dynamic, while re-iterating my longer-term positive view on gold.

As I described in that piece, in prior rounds of QE, Treasury yields went up rather than down when the Fed bought Treasuries, because this has corresponded with a rising PMI environment and non-Fed investors dump bonds and get into risk assets. Many investors assumed that this is how it will work in perpetuity.

However, I’ve been tracking a Treasury security supply/demand problem since the repo spike in late 2019. Sovereign bond markets tend to do rather poorly if sovereign debt is well over 100% of GDP, fiscal deficits are large and structural, there are inflationary pressures, and the central bank stops monetizing the deficit. So I was observing a deficiency in buy-and-holders of Treasury bonds, especially in the foreign sector and with the Fed slowing its rate of accumulation.

With two years of hindsight, the August 2020 thesis was mostly right, but with some issues. I was right about upward pressure on Treasury rates (the 10-year Treasury rate is now 3.45%, up from 0.71%) and right about a local top in gold. However, the gold price ended up stagnating longer than I expected (2+ years now), and the Fed became more hawkish than I thought starting here in 2022, and thus bond rates went up even more than I thought (which is also a key factor for why gold stagnated).

In the long run, my thesis remains largely unchanged, because with debt and deficits as high as they are, I view the Fed as being able to tighten monetary policy, but unable to *normalize* monetary policy (which I would define as a renewed era of structurally positive inflation-adjusted yields).

The Fed was ultra-dovish into a rising PMI environment (arguably a policy error), and now is trying to be ultra-hawkish into a declining PMI environment (arguably a second policy error). But to give the Fed credit, my thesis for the past several years has been that due to the structure of the [long-term debt cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/) and Fed decisions from decades-prior during the Greenspan era, almost any decision by the Fed is a policy error now.

A lot of analysts are armchair commentators that are constantly criticizing the Fed, whereas I view the Fed (and ECB, and BOJ) as dealing with a [Kobayashi Maru](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kobayashi_Maru), meaning a no-win scenario. The combination of high debt and high inflation simultaneously is nearly unworkable, and historically tends to result in policymakers throwing in the towel on inflation for a period of time, to inflate away part of the debt.

Thus, without necessarily assigning blame, the result becomes bouncing back and forth between policy mistakes to try to juggle conflicting mandates, while managing optics and a fiscal spiral that they have no real control over.

![Debt vs Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2022-8-debt-vs-rates.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As such, my playbook going forward is to remain diversified, and to continue leaning defensive for now, with a declining PMI environment and hawkish Fed.

Longer-term, I eventually see the Fed running into Treasury liquidity issues, and having to resort to a pause in tightening (interest rates or QT or both) or SLR adjustments to get more of that Treasury supply absorbed, and with that catalyst, I would be bullish on several risk assets again.
