## Macro Update

This latest coronavirus wave continues to be a problem for Europe and North America, and less-so for Asia.

Here are the rolling numbers of people currently hospitalized for COVID-19 in the United States:

![COVID Hospitalizations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-covid-hospitalizations.png)

The vaccines that have been announced in recent weeks should help alleviate the issue throughout 2021, but in the meantime, this wave is spreading. There are signs that it may be starting to roll over into a top, though, as public behavior has once again begun to tighten down.

Here at eight months from the starting point of job losses in late March, there are still over 700,000 initial jobless claims filed every week, which remains slightly above the peak level reached in 2009. Continued jobless claims are down to under 6.4 million, which is slightly below the 2009 peak but still extremely high by historical standards.

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Pressure continues to mount on folks who lost their jobs or businesses in this crisis, now that they no longer have aggressive stimulus replacing their incomes.

Plus, the jobless numbers are actually a bit worse than that, because at this point, some people have just dropped out of the labor force entirely, meaning they are no longer employed or looking for employment. Besides the March bottom from earlier this year, the labor participation rate is now at a more than 4-decade low:

![Labor Force Participation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-labor-force-participation.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Part of this is demographics-based, due to an aging population, but not all of it. A crazy statistic is the fact that as of this year, the United States’ all-age labor participation rate is now slightly below Japan’s labor participation rate (61.7% vs 62.2%), even though Japan’s median age is 10 years older.

Part of this structural damage to the U.S. job market is due to Triffin’s Dilemma unfolding, which is a topic I discussed in various articles including [The Global Dollar Short Squeeze](https://www.lynalden.com/global-dollar-short-squeeze/) and [The Big Tax Shift](https://www.lynalden.com/tax-shift/). Specifically, in order to maintain the global reserve currency, the United States has had to run large structural trade deficits and thus basically export large portions of its industrial base, and that trend is really starting to hit “crunch time” here in the early 2020’s.

## Stocks Are Attempting Rotation

I continue to be bullish on high-quality stocks, particularly those that don’t fall into either extreme end of the valuation spectrum.

In other words, I’m not particularly bullish on extremely highly-valued tech stocks at current valuations, and I’m also not particularly bullish on nearly-insolvent deep value businesses that may or may not go bankrupt in this cycle. However, I’m pretty bullish on a lot of the “middle market”, referring to various good-quality value stocks and growth-at-a-reasonable-price stocks that are not in the top 10% or bottom 10% of valuation profiles.

According to Wilshire data going back to the late 1970’s, an equal weight large cap index has outperformed a market weight large cap index over a 40+ year period. An equal weight index like this rebalances quarterly to put all constitutes back to equal-weighting, rather than heavily concentrating in the top few stocks.

Similarly, YCharts has S&P 500 data going back to the late 1980’s on the same topic, showing 30+ years out outperformance for equal weight large cap indices. This chart shows the equal weight total return S&P 500 index divided by the market weight total return S&P 500 index:

![Equal Weight vs Market Weight S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-equal-vs-market-longterm.png)

Whenever that line is going up, it means the equal weight index is outperforming, because the majority of stocks in the index are outperforming the top mega-cap stocks in the index. Whenever it’s going down, it means the market weight index is outperforming, because mega-caps are outperforming.

Turning points tend to happen around recessions, as market leadership rotates. Specifically, mega-cap growth stocks tend to outperform in the lead-up to a recession (so the line goes down), but once you are in or around the recession, some of the more cyclical or smaller components of the index tend to lead the way out (so the line goes up and begins the next outperformance cycle).

Here is the ratio this year, zooming on the recent activity:

![Equal Weight vs Market Weight S&P 500 1 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-equal-vs-market-1yr.png)

We can see that there was a false break-out in June, where David Portnoy and a merry gang of speculative traders drove up beaten-down stocks including literally-bankrupt stocks, which is also when stimulus-led expansion of the money supply was at its peak rate.

This second potential break-out is occurring along with announcements of successful vaccines, and seems more sustainable. The valuations of many companies within the equal-weight index certainly are more appealing than stocks such as Apple.

This is why I currently include the equal weight S&P 500 ETF, ticker RSP, in some of my portfolios as a broad play on U.S. equities.

We see similar phenomenon’s internationally as well, suggesting a turning point. Julien Bittel recently published a great chart showing the percent of MSCI World stocks beating the index year-over-year. The percentage collapsed throughout 2020 but is now turning up, and looks a lot like previous major turnings points:

![MSCI Stock Rotation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-msci-stocks.png)

*Chart Source: [Julien Bittel, Pictet Asset Management](https://twitter.com/BittelJulien/status/1329715928439918592)*

So, I continue to be a fan of stocks in the United States and internationally that have good fundamentals and are reasonably-priced. This includes some solid healthcare stocks, commodity stocks, industrial stocks, energy producers and transporters, the best-of-breed banks that are adopting well to a digital age, some reasonably-valued tech companies, and more.

I recently did a presentation for the Money Show and included three example F.A.S.T. Graphs of “overvalued”, “fairly-valued” and “undervalued” stocks to show how different the market looks depending on where you look.

As always for these F.A.S.T. Graphs, the black line is the stock price, while the blue and orange lines represent historically normal valuations based on current and projected earnings. So, when the black line totally decouples from the blue and orange lines to the upside or downside, it often represents overvaluation or undervaluation.

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-aapl-fastgraph.png)

I think 2021 could easily be a story of major sector rotation, although nothing is for certain and there could be fiscal/political curveballs to deal with along the way. I like owning a diverse basket of stocks such as AMGN and EPD in this example, and am more cautious about stocks like AAPL at this level.

As always, I prefer to emphasize a degree of quality in my stock selections; not the cheapest junk but the well-made merchandise on sale.

## Bond Yields: Informally Controlled?

I discussed my view of bond yields being coerced to artificially low levels by the Fed and other central banks in my [August Newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2020-newsletter/), and set out my longer-term view towards higher bond yields until formal yield curve control prevents further increase at some point.

My view for a while is that informal yield curve control is putting some downward pressure on Treasury bond yields currently. Indeed, the Fed bought more than half of the amount of Treasuries that the government issued over the past 12 months, while the foreign sector bought almost none. The Fed now holds more Treasuries than all foreign central banks combined.

![Federal Reserve Treasury Holdings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-bianco.jpg)

*Chart Source: Bianco Research*

However, there has indeed been some upward pressure on longer-duration Treasuries over the past few months. The Fed took a ton of extra Treasury supply off the market, but there’s still plenty for the market to absorb.

Specifically, the 10-year yield is about 12 basis points higher than it was during my August newsletter. Still, Treasury yields remain below their inflation breakeven levels (meaning, the implied forward inflation rate that the inflation-protected TIPS portion of the Treasury market is pricing for), and below where the copper-gold ratio (a long-term historical correlate to yields) suggests they should be.

**10-Year Yields vs Inflation Breakevens:**

![Treasury vs TIPS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-treasury-vs-tips.png)

**10-Year Yields vs Copper/Gold: Ratio**

![Copper vs Gold vs Yields](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-yields-copper-gold.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Jurrien Timmer, Direct of Global Macro at Fidelity](https://twitter.com/TimmerFidelity/status/1329862113108619270)*

I remain uncertain about bond yields in the intermediate term, due to the current COVID-19 wave, lack of second-round U.S. fiscal stimulus, and other factors. I wouldn’t be surprised at all to see one more big downward move in yields due to a broad insolvency issue within the next 6 months, but it’s not my base case.

Looking out a year or more ahead, however, I remain bearish on Treasury bonds in real terms, with a base case expectation that their yields will rise in this cycle unless/until the Fed increases the amount it buys per month, or performs formal yield curve control to lock their yields below a certain level like they did in the 1940’s (which is the only other time in history where U.S. federal debt to GDP reached these levels).

So far, the 10-year minus 3-month Treasury yield curve is doing exactly what would be expected at this point in the cycle. Gray areas are past recessions and the yellow area is the current recession:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-yield-curve.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As far as gold is concerned, an environment of rising real yields tends to put downward pressure on gold prices, and indeed gold has been correcting and consolidating. However, if we get to a point where the Fed steps in and institutes formal yield curve control, gold likely becomes one of the best assets to own in that scenario. Therefore, I remain long-term bullish on gold, but without much of an opinion on it for the next few months.

This chart shows gold in red on the right axis, and real interest rates (the 10-year Treasury rate minus 10-year inflation breakevens) in blue on the left axis:

I became bullish on Bitcoin within the April 12th deep dive report at $6,900/BTC, went long myself on April 20th at about the same level and discussed that within the April 26th deep dive report, wrote bullishly about the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) at $11.27/share within the June 7 deep dive report, and included GBTC in the inception of the No Limits portfolio this summer. Or, to describe it bluntly, it’s a supply-and-demand problem. Cash App, Grayscale, and now Paypal are mega-scale buyers of Bitcoin for their clients, and this is sucking up a ton of the liquid amount of coins. The combo of Cash App and Paypal alone is now buying more coins than are generated per day. A big percentage of existing coins are held by long-term holders who only part with coins at higher prices, so as these new buying avenues keep coming in against limited supply on exchanges, we get a price increase due to scarcity of supply. And along with a price increase, we get more demand and interest from people hopping on after the breakout.

So, I’m sharing the potential for a local Bitcoin correction around this level to set expectations rather than for a trading plan. I’m holding Bitcoin, not trading it. I haven’t sold any Bitcoin and don’t plan to yet. Another option is simply to rebalance quarterly. For the No Limits Portfolio, I may trim small positions from the GBTC positions here or there to prevent GBTC from becoming too large as a percentage of the portfolio.

Each person has their own unique risk tolerance and financial goals, especially with regards to a very historically volatile asset that only has an 11-year price history. I continue to view Bitcoin as a good asymmetric addition to a diversified portfolio, particularly within this bullish phase of this cycle.

## Scotiabank (BNS) Deep Dive Analysis

The Bank of Nova Scotia (BNS), operating as Scotiabank, is one of the top five banks in Canada, and among them has the broadest international banking segment. In addition to their Canadian and U.S. operations, they have a significant Latin American banking business, with an emphasis on Mexico, Chile, Peru, and Columbia.

I had Scotiabank in the Top 12 List until March 1st of this year, where I removed it when it was in the low $50’s. I didn’t want too many bank stocks on the list at that economically-sensitive time. In this environment, however, I’m quite bullish on it again.

The stock has underperformed some of its Canadian peers this year due to its Latin American exposure, which has been a more difficult part of the business. However, due to this, Scotiabank is also trading at a lower valuation than some of its peers, and pays a rather high dividend yield of over 5%, which by most metrics appears relatively safe and has a long track record of being safe.

![BNS Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-bns-price.png)

Overall, Scotiabank massively outperformed the S&P 500 during the 2000-2010 decade, but massively underperformed during the 2010-2020 decade, generally in line with the broader value-vs-growth trend shift.

From current levels, my base case would be for Scotiabank to likely outperform once again in the 2020’s and break out of this big period of consolidation, along with decent-quality value stocks in general. Overall, both in the United States and the rest of the world, value stocks are trading at a historically wide valuation gap vs growth stocks. Growth stocks are of course worth higher valuations, but there are times where they become quite overvalued vs their value peers, which along with a cyclical economic recovery, can lead to a strong period of value-factor outperformance.

Scotiabank has about 4.1% of their loan book in industries that are highly exposed to COVID-19, such as oil and gas services, office real estate, air travel, and hospitality:

![BNS Loan Portfolio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-loans.png)

*Chart Source: Scotiabank Q3 2020 Presentation*

To service this, Scotiabank built up $7.4 billion in allowance for credit losses.

Broadly speaking, Scotiabank and other banks went into this crisis with solid balance sheets, which combined with broad stimulus measures for the public, keeps their loan book relatively protected.

**Management Quality and Balance Sheet**

The Canadian banking sector is well-known for stability compared to most other global banking systems. The United States and Europe have had their fair share of banking crises, while in recent decades, Canada’s banks have been strong and resilient. Scotiabank, for example, was able to keep paying dividends straight through the global financial crisis of 2007-2009.

Scotiabank in particular has been doing well at digital adoption. They keep winning quality awards from places like JD Power over their competitors’ apps, and their digital penetration has been making great progress:

![BNS Digital Adoption](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-11-22-bns-digital-adoption.png)

As far as return on equity, Scotiabank tends to be in the upper half of large banks, especially compared to American banks. Here’s a sampling:

Some of the emerging-market banks I cover, like Sberbank of Russia and HDFC Bank of India, have higher average return on equity. However, among developed market banks, Scotiabank is one of the better ones on that metric.

**Valuation Analysis**

Scotiabank’s prospects over the next five years look solid, especially considering that they pay a dividend yield of over 5% and therefore only need a moderate amount of capital appreciation to make for a good investment.

**Company Risks**

Scotiabank has a variety of risks, in line with the rest of the global banking sector.

*Credit Risk*

The global banking system faces considerable uncertainty. A solvency crisis takes a while to play out, even as the liquidity crisis and market bottom can happen pretty early. Even as the economy likely improves in 2021, many businesses will still be defaulting on obligations. However, we’ve likely reached the peak for banks setting aside funds to absorb those defaults; they’ve front-run the next year, and came into this crisis well-capitalized.

We’ll see over the next year how bad some of the solvency problems get. Plus, it partly depends on fiscal stimulus and bailouts, which makes it inherently impossible to predict for sure. If solvency problems are not as bad as feared, then it means banks over-allocated funds for the solvency issues, and they’ll get to take those back. If solvency problems are worse than feared, then the expected uptick in earnings for banks like Scotiabank will likely need to be revised down as they set more money aside to absorb defaults.

*Interest Rate Risk*

One of the biggest risks for banks around the world is interest rate risk. Unlike credit risk which hits hard and fast, interest rate risk slowly bleeds the banks of profitability over time. Banks don’t do well in zero-rate or negative-rate environments, and tend to prefer steeper yield curves. European and Japanese banks have been absolutely crushed by low rate policy for a very long time.

Canada and the United States offer better prospects for this than their European and Japanese peers. Scotiabank further distributes their risk by having exposure to emerging markets that generally have higher inflation and higher rates, in exchange for more volatility and credit risk.

At this time, I like gold as a hedge for this. The gold price is strongly inversely correlated to real yields. Gold does better if real rates stay low. Banks do better if nominal yields go up and the yield curve has a positive slope.

*Currency Risk*

Scotiabank operates in multiple countries and has broad currency exposure. In addition, non-Canadian investors have considerable exchange rate risk between their local currency and the Canadian currency. This goes the other way as well; if the Canadian currency outperforms their local currency, it can create a positive tailwind.

Canada’s currency will in part be tied to energy prices and commodity prices more broadly, due to the Canadian economy’s heavy exposure to those industries. With low energy prices, Canada has a trade deficit, which puts downward pressure on the currency. With high energy prices, Canada could go back to having a trade surplus, which puts upward pressure on the currency.

I don’t have a strong view on the Canadian currency over the next year, but I am bullish on commodities for the 2020’s decade.

*Disruption Risk*

Investors are concerned with all sorts of disruptive risks for established bank companies. Fintech companies ranging from Paypal to smaller peers have made payments easier, and now cryptocurrencies and decentralized finance applications can further disrupt established banking channels. And then on top of that, some investors are worried about the prospect for central bank digital currencies to go around the commercial banking system and compete with them.

This is why I prefer to focus on some of the more digitally-savvy banks. I particularly like Sberbank of Russia (SBRCY), which has extremely high mobile adoption, invests in tech companies, and has already tested blockchain applications. Similarly, I like HDFC Bank of India (HDB), Discover Financial Services (DFS), American Express (AXP), J.P. Morgan Chase (JPM), Scotiabank (BNS), and the top banks of Singapore (EWS).

A simple way to hedge these disruptive risks is to have a nonzero Bitcoin position. I like that banks+Bitcoin combo at this time, within a diversified portfolio. If decentralized finance and tokenization massively disrupts the profitability of established banks quicker than almost anyone expects (rather than those banks gradually adopting to the tech as needed), then Bitcoin is an asymmetric play on that outcome. If banks continue to do reasonably well from beaten-down valuation levels, then they can continue to be solid dividend payers and participate in a potential growth-to-value equity rotation in the context of a cyclical recovery.

## Summary

While I’m concerned about this winter period, I remain rather bullish on some of the higher-quality value stocks out there compared to the broad market indices, when looking out over a 3-5 year period. The unemployment shock is still in play, and stimulus remains an unknown variable in many countries, so I’m not ruling out one more big deflationary move in yields, but I’m not heavily trading around that idea. Ultimately, it comes down to your intended time horizon.

Scotiabank, along with other high-quality banks, remains an interesting stock. Along with most other banks globally, it has been in a more than decade-long consolidation during the sluggish period of the 2010’s, but prospects for its performance could quickly shift in the 2020’s, and are showing early signs of doing so. As a dividend payer, its risk/reward analysis appears attractive as part of a diversified portfolio.

Lastly, I wouldn’t be surprised to see a local Bitcoin correction around this potential resistance level after so much localized euphoria, but within the grand scheme of things, I’m still bullish well into 2021. Most of the indicators look slightly toppy in the near-sense, but not in the big sense.
