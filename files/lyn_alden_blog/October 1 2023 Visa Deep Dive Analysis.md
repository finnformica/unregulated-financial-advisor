## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be flat as it has been since late 2022. This is because the Fed’s balance sheet is drawing down (which is negative for liquidity) while the reverse repo facility is also drawing down at a similar pace (which is positive for liquidity).

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-domestic-liquidity.png)

There’s a good argument to be made that we’ve seen the top in the S&P 500 bear market rally. With a T-bill rate of over 5.4%, and flat liquidity conditions, I think it will be hard for the S&P 500 to keep rocketing higher and to surpass its prior highs. And even if it does, can it outperform 5.4% annualized on a volatility-adjusted basis?

I view this as an environment to have a considerable cash-equivalent position (T-bills, money markets, premium savings accounts that actually pay some yield, etc) as a method of defense. Or some notes up to a couple years in length.

Adding to that argument is the fact that global liquidity is weak. This chart shows the dollar-denominated size of the combined broad money supply of the top five currencies:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-global-m2.png)

This global M2 indicator goes up when either broad money supply grows quickly, or the dollar weakens relative to other major currencies. It goes down when broad money supply grows slowly or the dollar strengthens. Right now, central banks are doing their best to tighten money supply growth, and the dollar index has gone over 106, which is bad for liquidity.

This puts a lot of pressure on emerging markets that have a lot of external debt and are running trade deficits. For example, I expect Egypt to have to do another currency devaluation by the first half of next year, if not sooner. They’re doing their best to hold off on that for now, and their government recently signed a [$1.4 billion currency swap deal with the UAE](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-09-28/egypt-signs-local-currency-swap-worth-1-4-billion-with-the-uae) which gives them some breathing room, but I think the devaluation is inevitable. And Egypt is not alone in that case- many other countries with similar dynamics (high external debt and trade deficits, among other factors) are facing similar pressures.

And arguably, that’s part of the Fed’s strategy. In the early/mid 1980s, tightening monetary policy squeezed Latin American countries and their dollar-denominated debt, which put them into a depression and greatly reduced their oil demand and fed into disinflationary conditions in the United States. In the late 1990s, the tight monetary policy put a lot of pressure on southeast Asia before it bounced back on U.S. markets via Long Term Capital Management and their subsequent blow-up. In the late 2010s tightening and the current early 2020s tightening cycle, most major BRICS nations are prepared for it this time around but a number of weaker developing countries with energy-related current account deficits such as Lebanon, Turkey, Egypt, Ghana, and others get squeezed by the higher dollar and end up facing economic problems that force them to curtail resource demand… which benefits the U.S. consumer price market for those resources.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Atlanta Fed continues to see strong growth in Q3 of this year, which is ending as of this weekend but won’t be reported for some time:

![Atlanta Fed GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-atlanta-fed.png)

Many economists are debating the “hard-landing, “soft-landing”, or “no-landing” scenarios. I continue to view it as being more useful to look at it sector by sector, since this is a somewhat unique situation given the combination of high deficits, high public debts, and attempts at tight monetary policy in the face of that fiscal looseness.

In other words, I think commercial real estate is likely to face a hard landing, whereas I don’t expect very high levels of unemployment to occur within the next couple years. I’ll repost what I wrote in my prior report for more context:

> I expect to sound like a broken record by saying this for the rest of the year, but we’re currently in a tale of two economies.
>
> -With ongoing quantitative tightening and high interest rates, the Fed is putting downward pressure on interest-rate sensitive entities such as commercial real estate owners that have to refinance short duration debt, junk-rated companies that have to refinance short duration debt, small businesses that have to refinance short duration bank loans, developing countries that need to refinance net debt balances, and so forth.
>
> -Meanwhile, entities such as households with low fixed-rate long-duration mortgages, large investment-grade corporations with low fixed-rate long-duration corporate bonds, and/or entities that are on the receiving end of the U.S. Treasury’s $2 trillion annualized fiscal deficits, are generally doing pretty well. I continue to have a constructive view toward the energy sector, the travel sector, and most businesses that cater to upper middle class senior citizens in general.
>
> Basically, the Fed’s inflation-fighting tools are geared toward squeezing the weaker segments of the economy, including low/middle income households, insecure companies, and insecure nations. I expect pain to continue in those areas for a while.
>
> -September 17th 2023 report

Consumer price inflation is having mixed results right now. Core CPI remains elevated but is coming down slowly. Headline CPI came down more quickly, but is bouncing recently due in large part to the bounce in energy prices.

![CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-cpi.png)

The main question among economists seems to be whether the Fed will be able to get it down to 2%, or whether they’ll find themselves stuck in more of that 3-4% type of range for a while. Some economic bears are calling for a period of deflation amid a hard landing.

But again, I’m looking at this somewhat differently. My interest is less in trying to call the bottom level that CPI will reach. Instead, my interest is in determining whether we are going to have a second CPI inflation cycle after whatever bottom we do reach here.

In other words, what’s the longer-term plan here? The Fed is facing high inflation from large fiscal spending, past and present, and is using its main tools (balance sheet reduction and higher interest rates) to slow down bank lending and cause enough pain in the private sector to offset that fiscal spending. Suppose that we do get some degree of broad recession, and the Fed goes back to expanding the monetary base again. If those unusually large fiscal deficits are still there (most of them are structural), and if the energy demand/supply situation remains very tight (I think it will), then what would prevent inflation from re-accelerating again? Persistent inflation reduction requires fixing the supply side, rather than merely depressing the demand side, and the demand side can only be suppressed for so long when fiscal deficits are this high anyway (because eventually there would be liquidity problems in the Treasury market, requiring the Fed to intervene even if it doesn’t want to).

So, I continue to view the economy as being in limbo right now, with no firm view on inflation or energy within say the next 3-6 months, but am positioned longer-term for higher energy prices. The good thing about high-quality oil producers right now is that regardless of how fast or slow the bullish energy thesis plays out, they’re quite profitable and inexpensive at current oil prices, so it’s a positive-carry position.

Funders of projects get perks like signed copies, early editions, and so forth, rather than things that they speculate on and hope to resell. Funders actually *like the product for its own sake*, in other words, and want to help it come into existence without any plans to re-sell their items for monetary gain.

**Orb Land: A Concept That Might Not Suck**

An example of an NFT project that at least has some thought put into it is [Orb Land](https://orb.land/). I won’t endorse anything specifically (and by design it’s not even really investable) but it’s interesting to mention for research purposes regarding the technology itself.

Mostly what the crypto industry does outside of the Bitcoin network, is try to tokenize things. The most successful tokenized thing has been the dollar: stablecoins have been a truly useful technology for many people. It basically makes offshore bank accounts or eurodollars available to people in easy digital form. There’s an argument to be made that other types of assets can and should be tokenized as well, like equities or art or whatever else, so that it can broaden access to them across jurisdictions and make them more useful (e.g. they can be moved around and traded on different exchanges, can be used as collateral at one of many different liquidity pools, and so forth).

Orbs tokenize someone’s *time*, like the time of an analyst or industry professional. Each orb is a unique customized NFT, connected to the orb creator for some contract length, which gives the orb keeper (the current buyer/owner of the NFT) the right to ask the orb creator (the analyst) questions at contractually specified intervals. However, the orb has an ongoing tax associated with it that the orb keeper has to keep paying to keep holding it.

For example, [Nic Carter’s orb](https://nic.orb.land/) can be purchased for 5 ETH (about $8,400), and charges 150% of that market price per year (so 7.5 ETH or $12,600 at the current price). While I disagree with a minority of his takes at times, Carter is a longtime crypto analyst and VC partner, with extensive industry knowledge. Whoever buys the orb can set the price that someone else can buy it from them at, but the higher they set the price, the higher they tax themselves while they hold it. And most of the tax goes to the orb creator, which is Carter in this case. The orb keeper gets to ask Carter a question every seven days, and so far he has given very detailed responses. For example, he has provided high-quality longform assessments of the solvency risks for various specific exchanges or stablecoin issuers when asked.

Basically, what this type of NFT does is put an ongoing retainer fee for some analyst or related professional onto the secondary market so that the service always has a liquid market price.

The skeptic could (*and should!*) ask why this even needs to be on a blockchain. Couldn’t some retainer services company just operate a centralized website to make a secondary market for these types of analyst retainer access services? Does this really need to trade around as a blockchain token?

The answer is that yes they could do this in a more centralized way, but the orb does make certain uses of the blockchain that are hard to replicate elsewhere. There is no enforcement mechanism on the orb other than reputation. And part of that reputation enforcement mechanism is that questions and answers are recorded in the blockchain and if the orb creator stops answering questions within the term of the smart contract, this fact gets recorded in the blockchain. Basically, the orb creator’s “default” by not answering questions would get recorded in posterity onto a semi-immutable public database. This feature might not need the most hardened decentralization around (e.g. the Bitcoin base layer), but it does need to be open and semi-immutable (e.g. Ethereum or a second layer on Bitcoin).

Because this project has an ongoing tax on the NFT, it’s purposely more of a utility than a collectible, and isn’t really useful for speculation. While I won’t be buying any orbs myself, unlike most NFTs, I think the project fits the spirit of what the industry can be when there’s actual effort to see what (if any) non-speculative use cases this technology can offer. There’s more of a fun and exploratory aspect to it rather than a low-effort speculative money grab.

**The Ramifications of Destroying Gatekeepers**

Earlier in the 20th century, most things had gatekeepers. If you wanted to publish a book, song, radio program, television show, movie, or so forth, you had to go through centralized gatekeepers. These were publishing houses, media corporations, music producers, movie producers, and so forth.

As information connectivity has improved over time, the power of those gatekeepers have been chipped away at. It’s far easier to market a peer-to-peer piece of information today, whether it’s a book, song, podcast, show, movie, or whatever. The technology is inevitable.

The most recent gatekeeper to be disrupted is the combination of the securities regulator, the centralized exchanges, and the centralized brokers. The crypto industry lets people sell peer-to-peer securities, and makes it hard for regulators to fully stamp it out, especially considering the industry’s global nature.

In some sense this is inevitable, but it also has higher stakes. The reduction of the gatekeeper in any industry empowers creators but also enables a longer tail of lower-quality items to reach the market. Consumers then have to sift through more roughage to find the diamonds. With media consumables like books and songs and shows, the stakes aren’t that high. Reviews and other quality checks can help the consumer find good items, and they can shift towards high-quality items over time. There’s not a big loss if a consumer picks a bad book or show or song; they’ve just wasted a little bit of money and time.

But with peer-to-peer financial products and securities, the stakes are higher than before. People can lose a *lot* of money by being marketed a bad NFT or crypto security token and so forth. And that perks up the securities regulators. I wrote about this in one of my crypto articles from last year:

> When founders and early venture capitalists put together a tech startup, they generally tie their fortunes to the success or failure of that idea. They invest in rather illiquid equity, and the main way to unlock that equity and get successful exit liquidity involves either going public or being acquired.
>
> To go public, they have to go through an expensive disclosures process, where they open up their books, reveal the major ownership, and discuss risks in detail. The median length of time for a startup to go public from its founding is over eight years.
>
> To be acquired, they need to build something attractive enough for another business to want to buy them out. In other words, professionals with MBAs or other business experience/education review their business and decide to buy it.
>
> Therefore, the fortunes of the founders and early investors of the startup are usually tied in a significant way to the underlying fundamentals of the business that they built and financed. The company needs some revenue, some use-case, and to go through some degree of due diligence. They have to spend years building a company that either another company wants to buy, or that gets big enough and sticks around long enough to go public, with all of the necessary disclosures.
>
> In the crypto world, it has been different. Founders and early investors can create a project, sell the coins publicly (generally to accredited investors or overseas to avoid public securities laws now, ever since there was a crackdown in domestic public initial coin offerings), work on it for a year or two or three, market it heavily, get it listed on a crypto exchange, and then dump the hyped-up coins (which likely are unregistered securities) on public retail speculators with exaggerated or outright false claims about the project’s level of decentralization and utility.
>
> In other words, the founders and early investors can separate their own profits from the actual success of the project’s fundamentals. They don’t need to spend the better part of a decade building a business that is good enough for another business to want to acquire it, or that can go through the SEC’s process for entering public markets. They can just create hype and dump their coins on the retail public, for the sake of fast exit liquidity.
>
> “Seigniorage” is the profit that a government makes by issuing its own currency, especially as it relates to the difference between production cost (near zero) and its market value. Blockchain technology has enabled private entities to benefit from seigniorage as well. They can create a crypto semi-liquid/fungible asset for very little cost, hype it up, and try to profit from it. Because very little value is being created in the process, it’s mostly a zero sum game where the creators and promoters of the coins make the money, and retail speculators lose the money.
>
> –[The Problems with DeFi & Crypto, December 2022](https://www.lynalden.com/defi-problems/)

## Visa Deep Dive Analysis

In recent months I have added some payment network stocks including Visa (V) to various portfolios, so it is worth providing more in-depth analysis on them. For this report, I’ll emphasize Visa as the leader.

Currently, I see five attractive investable areas:

1. Growth-at-a-reasonable-price network effect stocks.
2. Value stocks that are cheap but relatively future-proof.
3. Cash Equivalents (deposits, T-bills, money markets, etc).
4. Energy and commodities and/or their producers.
5. Hard bearer assets like real estate, gold, and bitcoin.

Visa falls into the first category, as a growth stock with a strong network effect that got ahead of itself in terms of valuation, consolidated in time for quite a while as fundamentals caught up, and once again has become investable as a growth-at-a-reasonable-price network effect stock.

![V FastGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-fastgraph.png)

There are four major card payment networks in the United States and somewhat globally: Visa, Mastercard (MA), American Express (AXP) and Discover Financial (DFS). Of those four, Visa and Mastercard are the biggest, and they are pure payment networks rather than credit providers. American Express and Discover are smaller, but they are combined payment networks and payment providers.

In other words, Visa and Mastercard work with banks who issue cards and take on credit risk, whereas American Express and Discover take on credit risk themselves. So we have four companies, divided into two companies of each major type. Together, they represent most of the card payment oligopoly.

Revenue, earnings, and free cash flow for Visa are all structurally upward, with a moderate drawdown during the pandemic as retail spending rapidly but temporarily contracted and shifted:

![Visa Growth Metrics](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-growth-2.png)

Importantly, Visa does not have a history of large wasteful acquisitions. They put most of their surplus profit into dividends and share buybacks. Neither the dividend yield or the share buyback yield are high, due to the stock’s high equity valuation, but this is justified based on their great growth and margins. The company has grown their dividends every year in their modern publicly-traded era. The combination of a strong growth effect and reinvesting capital back into that growth effect around the margins, is incredibly powerful for as long as the growth effect is in play. It is exactly what you would expect a company to do if it was satisfied with its business and wanted to return its profits to shareholders.

![Visa Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-shares-outstanding-2.png)

The stock is historically inexpensive based on its cyclical price/earnings ratio (lower than normal) and its dividend yield (higher than normal). However, this downward change in valuation is fully justified (and perhaps more-so) based on much higher cash-equivalent yield rates. So I wouldn’t call it undervalued; just reasonably valued.

![Visa Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-valuation.png)

In fact, if we quantitatively compare Visa’s earnings yield to the 10-year Treasury yield (e.g. the equity risk premium), it is historically unattractive:

![Visa Equity Risk Premium](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-erp.png)

This chart can be interpreted in a few ways. As an important point, most stocks that I run against this metric are currently overvalued, except for deep value stocks. T-bills and T-notes usually come up better than equities for risk/reward ratios within the past several months based on this metric.

However, I consider Visa to be less extreme in this regard than many other growth stocks. My current thesis on Visa is that it’s not undervalued, but that it’s a more attractive risk/reward than the average growth stock, and is best paired with things like T-bills and/or energy producers.

When we look at Visa’s balance sheet, we can see that both Visa’s assets and liabilities are very small relative to their market capitalization ($471 billion). Visa as a business is not capital intensive, but is instead based on its network effects. And they have a pristine AA- balance sheet credit rating.

![Visa Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-balance-sheet.png)

Specifically, Visa has about $20.5 billion in debt and $15.5 billion in cash equivalents, which leaves them with only $5 billion in net debt. They are probably earning a higher interest rate on their cash equivalents than they are paying on their debt. They earn $16.5 billion per year in profits so they could pay off their net debt in about 4 months if they directed all of their income toward doing so. Their AA- balance sheet is fully earned.

When we look at Visa’s bond debt maturities, they look great. Almost all of their debt is due between 2027 and 2050, and with an interest rate that averages roughly between 1% and 4%. They’ve locked in an average interest expense over the long run that is much lower than current interest rates, so any cash-equivalents they have in the form of T-bills or similar securities can cause the company to earn higher interest income than it pays in interest expense.

![V Debt Maturities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-v-debt-maturities.png)

**Visa Risk Analysis**

Overall, Visa is firing on all cylinders. Revenue, profits, and free cash flow have a solid upward trend. Most profits go toward buybacks and dividends, rather than destructive acquisitions. The balance sheet is remarkably strong.

So, I see three main risks to consider: valuation pressures, technological disruption, and geopolitical disruption.

*Risk 1) Valuation Pressures*

In my opinion, the overall S&P 500 is not well-positioned to provide great foreword returns. The mega-cap stocks have high valuations, and T-bills yield well over 5%, which seriously hurts the relative value of expensive equities.

But I think some stocks are in a better position to provide decent returns than others. Apple (AAPL) for example trades at a higher valuation than Visa, despite having lower annual growth numbers and more geopolitical supply chain risk. Visa, comparatively, is likely a better buy.

I consider many stocks to have valuation pressures, but I view many stocks that have consolidated for a while like Visa, Booking (BKNG), and Fiserv (FI) to be more attractive holdings. I do, however, also like to hold cash-equivalents in this current market environment.

Visa is barely any higher-priced than it was in early 2020, despite significant fundamental growth since then. The stock price got ahead of its fundamentals for a few years, but its price stagnated and fundamentals caught up. I think Visa can credibly be a $1 trillion stock one day, along with the mega-cap tech stocks that currently hold that prestige.

*2) Technological Disruption*

I’m bullish on the Bitcoin network, the Lightning network, and other newer technologies to disrupt existing payment and monetary systems over time.

However, I expect this to be a generational process. If it happens more quickly than I expect, then Visa could be disrupted faster than I expect. In that case, the mitigation is to own exposures to those assets like bitcoin, which I do own and recommend ownership of to varying degrees.

Alternatively, a risk is that central bank digital currencies could begin taking market share from payment providers. This is a serious risk to monitor, but in the United States any major change like that would likely need to go through Congress, and Congress tends to be structurally divided these days.

*3) Geopolitical Disruption*

If we continue to see more geopolitical East/West divide, then Visa and Mastercard (MA) could find themselves increasingly cut off from parts of the East. BRICS+ nations could see their own payment systems become more highly used over time.

However, the current network effect is quite strong. In my recent trip in Egypt, for example, Visa was accepted almost everywhere. People there mainly seek out black market dollars to hold as savings, not yuan or euro or other currencies. And people pay with Visa, which lets currency conversions work themselves out behind the scenes. This is really hard to change over time, and is not something I expect to be disrupted over the next 5+ years as a base case.

As a mitigation for this long term risk, I like to have exposure to Brazilian equities and Indian equities. HDFC Bank (HDB) for example is another long-term growth story that I think is worth buying and holding for the next 5+ years:.

Overall, I find the combination of stocks like Visa, Fiserv, and HDFC Bank, along with assets such as bitcoin, to be a useful combined position. My intention is to hold Visa for several years, but I would consider a sale if either 1) it becomes notably overvalued relative to its fundamentals or 2) its growth metrics begin to show signs of disruption.

## Final Thoughts: Bond Mayhem

The price action in long-duration sovereign bonds has been a train wreck lately. Yields keep rising, which means price keeps falling. Some of the longest-duration bond exposures are down 60% on nominally risk-free Treasuries.

![Bond Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-bond-performance.png)

This recent surge in bond yields has occurred despite 1) the Fed slowing down its rate hiking cycle and 2) CPI remaining relatively subdued. And bond bulls have been hammered even harder than it seems because the prices of most things have gone up over the past few years, so they’re down nominally while most things are up.

Back in July 2019 I [wrote about the sovereign bond bubble](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/), and then again in February 2020 I [re-iterated some risks](https://www.lynalden.com/treasury-bonds-risks/) about Treasuries (which I then updated in May 2020 with analysis from the initial pandemic fiscal/monetary response). A number of my articles over the past 3-4 years have been about inflation risks and bond risks.

The same trend has been true for a lot of my interviews. My first *Macro Voices* interview, back in July 2020 was titled “[The Road to Inflation](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=4IsltWA4KGg&t=885s&ab_channel=MacroVoices)” and I tended to repeat similar views on subsequent appearances. In particular, my [November 2021 appearance](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=CHaOEMpR6cg&t=904s&ab_channel=MacroVoices) focused on the bond market being wrong about the magnitude and duration of the inflation. Their prior episode featured Jeff Snider, who argued that the bond market’s low yields were correctly sniffing out upcoming disinflation. My view contrasted his, and I argued that bond markets are not a good judge of forward inflation in these types of environments.

I made similar arguments on my October 2022 *Forward Guidance* interview titled “[The Bursting of the Sovereign Bond Bubble](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=im071GMdmkw&ab_channel=BlockworksMacro)“, and discussed with the host why the bond market had been so wrong about inflation in the year prior, and was so caught off guard.

Despite being a bond bear, the results of the bond market have been even worse than I expected. I would not have guessed we would be at 5% yields today, or that bonds would keep selling off despite some degree of CPI stabilization. In large part, this is because the Fed raised interest rates higher than I would have expected, and so the interest expense of the federal government is higher than I would have expected, and so as a result total deficits and Treasury bond supply are higher than I expected.

Back in May 2022 after bonds had sold off a lot, I bought a small position in the 7-10 year Treasury ETF (IEF) for some of my portfolios as a disinflation hedge against my otherwise inflation-oriented portfolio, right when CPI was indeed near its peak (over 8% at the time). However, I stepped out of that IEF position with a -2.5% loss in March 2023 (which hit the total portfolios by only -0.15% due to the small position size) because the risk/reward was no longer attractive to me upon further assessment. I’m glad I made that move because bond prices have gone down since then. So even I, as a bond bear, had to avoid getting sucked into being long bonds at certain points. It’s been really hard for the market to navigate.

With long-duration yields approaching 5%, I find myself with a somewhat similar view again. Bonds are oversold in terms of metrics like RSI, their bad performance is being regularly cited in financial social media, and so maybe this is the time to actually become a temporary bull. However, I’m going to avoid that trade for now; I prefer the risk/reward of cash-equivalents at this time.

The U.S. Treasury continues to run large fiscal deficits despite not being in recessionary conditions (shown below), the Fed continues to sell bonds from its balance sheet by letting them mature without reinvestment, commercial banks in aggregate are reducing their Treasury exposure, and the strong dollar makes it hard for the foreign sector to accumulate Treasuries (since they’re more in “currency defense” mode right now, rather than “reserve accumulation” mode).

![Fiscal Deficits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-1-fiscal-deficits.png)

That means that something like $2+ trillion in Treasuries need to be absorbed by the domestic non-bank sector on an annualized basis. The T-bill portion is easy to absorb because it can come out of the reverse repo facility (which still has $1.5 trillion currently), but the longer-duration bonds need to be bought by households, funds, pensions, insurance companies, and so forth.

If bond prices do start to put in a low and roll back up, perhaps due to signs of economic weakness or heightened recession risk, then going long bonds with a stop out point set at the prior low might be a tradeable play, but for now with so much bond supply coming to market my risk/reward preference remains focused elsewhere.
