## Equity Sector Rotation

The US equity market and indeed the global equity market have been in the midst of a sector rotation, with growth/tech stocks selling off and cyclical/value stocks doing well. These growth/value rotations tend to happen around recessions, when big changes in fiscal and monetary policy occur with a backdrop of unusually large differences in valuation between sectors.

Whatever trend was occurring during the previous economic cycle tends to get turned upside down for the next economic cycle.

This set of charts shows the ratio of value vs growth for large-cap and mid-cap US equities. When the line is going up, it means value (as Russell defines it) is outperforming. When the line is going down, it means growth is outperforming. The second chart shows the past three years of the first 40-year chart:

![US Sector Rotation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-sector-rotation.png)

Technically since March 8th, the growth factor has once again been mildly outperforming, although those types of counter-trends are normal during a big trend change. I continue to monitor this relationship overall, and find certain stocks in various sectors appealing when they become out-of-favor and reasonably-priced.

## Dollar and Bonds

I covered the topic of the supplementary leverage ratio exemption in the previous report from two weeks ago. Due to emergency conditions in the Treasury market last year (large issuance at the same time as the foreign sector was forced-selling them for liquidity), the Federal Reserve suspended a key leverage ratio for banks in March 2020 until the end of March 2021, and the suspension of that ratio allowed banks to hold more Treasuries and bank reserves than normal.

So, as we came near the end of March 2021, the question on the market’s mind was whether the Fed would extend this exemption. Well, since my previous report, the Fed announced that they would *not* extend the exemption, and so banks must have appropriate supplementary leverage ratios by the end of this month. Most of them do, although this does tighten liquidity a bit.

In reality, the Fed took a middle-of-the-road path, because although they announced that the SLR exemption would expire as planned, they did request comments and say that they can tweak that as needed if there is an issue.

Over the course of the year, the Treasury market will be tested. The government will be issuing a big supply of Treasuries in the second half of 2021, banks can’t buy a ton, and lately there hasn’t been a lot of foreign demand for them either. With their yields having risen, however, they may begin to attract some foreign demand, particularly from Japan and a few other countries.

The 10-year Treasury rate is still pretty low relative to official inflation measures, and those inflation measures are likely to temporarily hit 3% in May due to low year-over-year base effects from the previous year.

This chart shows the 10-year Treasury yield in blue, the year-over-year inflation rate in red, and a rough measure of expected inflation through May drawn for reference. After that, it’s unclear how transitory the inflation will be; it could either settle back down or remain somewhat elevated, depending in large part on what happens in terms of ongoing fiscal spending, city rents, and energy prices.

![Yield CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-yield-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The 10-year yield reached well over 1.7%, but has since consolidated back under 1.7% for the time being. We’ll see to what extend the bond market will look forward through the base effects.

Rising yields have stabilized the dollar, which fell considerably vs other currencies in 2020 but is currently in an uptrend with higher highs and higher lows for the moment. This is a chart of the dollar index, which measures the dollar against a basket of major currencies:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-dollar-index.png)

My long-term base case remains bearish on the dollar with a 3-5 year view ever since October 2019, but for the moment we are in a clear counter-rally.

After having more deaths per capita than most other countries to the virus, the US is now ahead of many countries (besides a few like the UK and Israel) in terms of vaccination rate. The combination of large stimulus, fast vaccinations, and a Fed that has been willing to let the long end of the Treasury curve rise, has been good for the dollar recently relative to other currencies.

This consolidation helped to take some of the heat off bitcoin and give it a healthier base to continue ramping up from, in my view. So far, it’s still making higher highs and higher lows.

This next chart shows bitcoin’s price in blue, and the percent of coins held for at least 1 year in orange. During major bull runs, long-term holders tend to sell some of their coins with 5x or 10x gains to newer buyers, but that stopped last month because bitcoin stopped going up. That behavior stifles the liquid supply, which is bullish. Bitcoin would likely need to increase further in price for them to resume selling.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-hodl-wave.png)

*Chart Source: LookIntoBitcoin.com*

Many other indicators look similar; with mid-bull-cycle characteristics.

The big outlier continues to be the fact that more bitcoins are leaving exchanges than going to exchanges, which has never happened for this long before. Usually by this stage of a bull cycle, coins are going to exchanges on net, but that’s not the case now. That’s one of the more bullish indicators out there at the moment, since it doesn’t even look mid-cycle. It’s an outlier variable that may lengthen the duration of this bull run, although I don’t have a strong opinion about that yet.

The spent output profit ratio also looks good. This is a measure of whether folks are selling their coins at a loss (an indicator of a bear market; people willing to sell at a loss and get out of a position). Historically in bitcoin bull markets, when the SOPR touches about 1, indicating people are starting to sell at a loss, that tends to be the bottom of a correction, meaning that very few folks actually sold at a loss from their cost basis.

![SOPR](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-sopr.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

In terms of basic support levels, there are a few useful ones to watch. In general, bitcoin bulls don’t want to see lower lows and lower highs within this post-halving-cycle uptrend.

The recent low of about $51,000 is a moderately important support level for bitcoin to hold, and we can give that a bit of leeway by calling it $50,000, since big round numbers tend to be important psychological support. It would be ideal, but not critical, for it to stay above that level.

Below that, approximately $45,000 is the low from late February, when SOPR briefly touched 1. If it goes below $45,000, bitcoin would be backtracking two full months of progress and putting the structure of the bull market in question for the intermediate-term.

Overall, I remain constructive on bitcoin during 2021 as my base case, and continue to watch for macro adoption (corporations, insurance, large investment banks for their clients, etc) as well as these various on-chain indicators.

**GBTC at a Discount**

The Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) lately is trading at a discount to net asset value or “NAV”.

![GBTC Discount](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-gbtc-discount.png)

In other words, you can buy a portion of a trust that represents a dollar’s worth of bitcoin for about 93 cents.

Although this is unusual for GBTC, trading at a discount to NAV is normal for closed-end funds in general. For GBTC, this is a result of increased competition thanks to new bitcoin funds with lower expense ratios and due to greater ease of buying on exchanges and through accumulation platforms.

In the No Limits portfolio, I originally bought GBTC under $10/share. I sold a 20% tranche of the initial position at around $30, and another 20% tranche at about $50, and plan to hold the rest for the time being, currently at $46. The discount means that GBTC underperformed bitcoin recently, but also means that it’s now more attractive as a vehicle for getting exposure to bitcoin.

GBTC could eventually eliminate this discount if it gets converted to an ETF with the blessing of the SEC. Grayscale could also narrow the discount by reducing its annual fee. Overall, I’d be surprised if it trades at more than a 20% discount to NAV. A discount to NAV of -5% to -10% makes sense given the current fee structure relative to new competition, and if it falls below -15% it likely makes for a nice arbitrage opportunity (where folks could go long GBTC and short bitcoin to play for mean reversion.

## Franco-Nevada Analysis

For this report, I’ll touch on two similar stocks rather than one stock.

**Gold Royalty Companies Overview**

A gold royalty company is an entity that finances gold mines around the world. The royalty company provides upfront capital for the miner to develop a mine, and in exchange, the royalty company gets a certain percentage of the mine’s revenue for the life of the mine, or gets to buy a certain amount of gold each year at a defined (and very low) price, which they can then re-sell at the market price.

From the perspective of the miner, they get access to capital with a payback amount that depends on the price and volume of the product they mine, rather than a fixed amount. This reduces the risk for them but also generally increases the total amount they will pay to the financier over the course of the mine’s life.

In addition, royalty companies are specialists at financing gold mining projects, with on-staff geologists and plenty of industry experience, whereas traditional investment banks tend not to be the best financiers of natural resource projects in general. Most miners use a combination of both types of financing, with some traditional debt at the corporate level and royalties tied to specific projects.

There are a few types of properties that a gold royalty company tends to finance. The straightforward type would be a gold mine. A less-obvious but very common type would be a copper mine, since copper and gold are often found together. Gold companies (especially gold royalty companies) trade at much higher valuations than copper companies, and so it makes sense for a copper miner to sell its gold rights to a gold royalty company. As a third type, many gold royalty companies have silver royalties as well, often for mines with multiple metals.

A final point worth mentioning is that royalty companies are basically immune to rising expenses. A gold miner uses a ton of energy, so for high profits, they prefer a high gold price and low oil prices. A royalty company has rights on the gold produced from the mine, without regard for expenses, so they don’t care what the expenses are.

In an environment where gold is rising and oil is falling in terms of price, gold miners can provide more upside potential. In a general inflationary environment where precious metals and base commodities rise together, the royalty companies are good places to have capital.

**Franco-Nevada: The Reigning King**

Franco-Nevada (FNV) is the blue-chip gold royalty and streaming company in the world. It has the best performance and strongest portfolio, but trades at a higher valuation than peers.

In general, because gold royalty companies have extremely high profit margins and high revenue per employee, and in many cases zero debt (which puts equity investors at the top of the capital stack), they tend to trade at valuations similar to those of software-as-a-service “SaaS” companies.

FNV’s roots trace back to the 1980s. In its most recent form, it was spun off from the large gold miner Newmont Corporation (NEM) in 2007, and at that point already had a large collection of royalties and streams as it was consolidated during its period under Newmont.

The current chairman of the board, David Harquail, has a long and tremendously successful history with FNV. He worked in one of its earlier entities as far back as the 1980s, continued his tenure in Newmont, and then was CEO of FNV from its spinoff in 2007 until 2020, at which point he stepped back slightly to the chairman role. Since FNV’s IPO in 2007, it has outperformed the Nasdaq 100, S&P 500, and most other gold companies.

FNV owns hundreds of royalty/streaming interests. Out of its revenue, about 70% comes from gold, about 20% comes from other precious metals, and about 10% comes from energy. Its willingness to finance energy projects makes it somewhat different than the majority of other gold royalty companies. Energy is a nice side-diversifier that the company has been willing to engage in.

The company’s gold-equivalent ounces “GEOS” have moderated in recent years, but their revenue and income continues to grow due to higher commodity prices. They have a large exploration portfolio that, over time, should contribute to more GEOs as well, and they do continue to make new finance deals.

In terms of performance, FNV outperformed gold and the gold miner ETF (GDX) during the bull market after its IPO, the bear market from 2013 through 2015, and the bull market since 2016.

## Sandstorm Gold Analysis

If Franco-Nevada is the reigning king, Sandstorm Gold (SAND) is an ambitious prince. Newer, smaller, and faster-growing, and at a cheaper valuation relative to expected forward production.

Sandstorm’s co-founder and CEO, Nolan Watson, used to work at Wheaton Precious Metals (WPM), which is the other top-two royalty company alongside Franco-Nevada. At Wheaton in his twenties, Watson was the youngest CFO of any company traded on the New York Stock Exchange at the time. Since his late twenties and now into his early forties, he has run Sandstorm.

Sandstorm emphasizes exploration upside, so they tend to invest in projects with big tracks of land. If new gold deposits are found by the miners on that land (as is often the case next to established gold mines), Sandstorm’s royalty rights extend to those properties as well. Over the past several years, their properties indeed produced a lot of discovery upside.

Over the past decade, Sandstorm went from owning 5 royalty rights to 200 royalty rights.

![Sandstorm Diversification](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-sand-diversification.png)

*Chart Source: March 2021 Presentation*

In the early phase, because Sandstorm started from nothing, they had to issue a ton of equity in order to finance their projects, as is common among royalty companies. Now that they are very established, they no longer have to issue shares to finance projects, and can instead finance new projects with existing cashflows from currently-producing projects.

Indeed since 2018, Sandstorm has been a net buyer of its own shares, rather than a net issuer. In 2020, however, a lot of their old warrants were exercised into more shares, which resulted in a one-time increase in the number of its shares. As of this writing, the company has a much cleaner share structure, and should likely reduce its share count over time.

![Sandstorm Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-sandstorm-shares.png)

Sandstorm has a lot of expected growth from now into the mid-2020s, but a lot of it rests on one big project: the massive Hod Maden mine in Turkey that is currently under development. Sandstorm bought partial rights to it a few years ago and it takes many years to bring a huge mine into production.

As the chart below shows, Sandstorm is trading at a mild valuation discount vs similar royalty company peers, and notably below Franco-Nevada. If we look out to 2024, assuming the Hod Maden project comes online as expected and substantially boosts Sandstorm’s EBITDA, either their price should increase by a lot, or they’ll be trading far below the valuation of any other royalty company, which is unlikely.

![Sandstorm Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-sand-valuation.png)

*Chart Source: March 2021 Presentation*

The market, basically, is unsure about the success of the [Hod Maden project](https://www.sandstormgold.com/our-royalties/hod-maden/), so until it’s complete, the stock price is somewhat in limbo. This is a rather binary outcome; if it’s completed on time, the upside potential for Sandstorm stock from current levels is 2x or more in a 3-year period. If it’s greatly delayed or disrupted, Sandstorm stock is likely to languish.

**Company Risks**

Franco-Nevada and Sandstorm have similar risks, so I will combine their risk analysis sections here.

*Project Concentration*

One of the benefits of gold royalty companies is that since they have interests in dozens or hundreds of projects, they have less concentration risk than a typical gold miner. However, they still have quite a bit of concentration.

Franco-Nevada’s top four projects collectively make up about half of their revenue. If any of those get disrupted, each one could impair over 10% of the company’s value. Each of them has a three decade or more expected mine life from the current time.

In terms of existing revenue streams, Sandstorm really isn’t any less diversified than Franco-Nevada. However, when taking into account forward projects, Sandstorm’s growth and valuation is heavily tied to the success of its Hod Maden project in Turkey. The vast majority of their 2021-2024 growth above the current baseline depends on it succeeding on schedule.

Turkey is historically a good mining jurisdiction that respects foreign capital, and Sandstorm’s leading partner is a local company with a history of getting projects done. However, Turkey is dealing with a set of currency and economic problems, so there is the possibility of a tail risk that the jurisdiction could become a problem.

This concentration risk can be eliminated by investing in multiple streaming companies. I personally invest in Franco-Nevada (FNV), Sandstorm SAND), Wheaton Precious Metals (WPM), Royal Gold (RGLD), and EMX Royalty Corp (EMX). I also like Metalla (MTA). There is no single project, or top 2-3 projects, that would impact my overall portfolio to a significant degree.

*Lack of Deals*

The gold royalty model has been tremendously successful. As a result, many new entrants have come into the space. There is a lot of competition to finance mines.

Sandstorm, for example, was unable to complete major deals in 2020 despite coming close to doing many deals and having a large number of talks with various miners. As a result, Sandstorm has more capital as a percentage of market capitalization than usual; it just needs a place to spend it productively. This was one of the reasons why Sandstorm was lackluster in 2020 compared to FNV, although this is an issue that all royalty companies face to some degree in this environment.

*Gold Price*

Gold miners and royalty companies are both tied to the price of gold. A typical gold miner might get it out of the ground for $1,200 and sell it for $1,700. A royalty company, however, might get it basically for a cost basis of $500 or so, and capture that bigger spread. Still, the gold price really matters for royalty companies too in terms of growth and valuation, as we’ve seen since the correction that began in August 2020.

Based on most of my analysis in the charts above, gold is reasonably-valued. Its price will heavily depend on real interest rates, especially for US Treasuries, as well as on global money supply growth and in particular US money supply growth.

Fortunately, both Franco-Nevada and Sandstorm are debt-free and with plenty of cash, and so although they face volatility, their solvency risks are extremely low. Sometimes they briefly issue a bit of debt in order to finance a big deal, but then pay it off quickly.

*Gold Demonetization*

Many people in the digital asset community expect that bitcoin and/or other crypto assets could totally replace gold as a store of value, and that gold may end up being demonetized after a long run. The argument goes along the lines that gold spent about 5,000 years as the best technology to store value, until 2009 when Satoshi Nakamoto solved the double-spend problem and the Byzantine General’s problem, and therefore successfully created bitcoin and by extension the concept of digital scarcity.

There is over $500 trillion worth of assets in the world. Of that, gold’s market capitalization is estimated to be about $10 trillion, and of that, less than $5 trillion is considered to be used directly or indirectly for the purposes of private investment, with the rest being for industrial, ornamentation, or central bank usage.

Bitcoin, now worth over $1 trillion, does somewhat compete with gold. However, both gold and bitcoin compete with the global bond sovereign bond and cash market that together consist of well over $100 trillion.

Do I expect gold to be demonetized this decade? No.

As a defense against being wrong about that, however, I also own a sizable chunk of bitcoin. If gold were to be demonetized due to bitcoin, it would require a bitcoin price several times higher than today to do so. And as described by their relative sizes, bitcoin rising several times its current size doesn’t necessarily de-monetize gold; both compete with much larger pools of capital.

I don’t really play the “bitcoin vs gold” competition as some do. I like gold, silver, platinum, copper, bitcoin, oil, gas, uranium, and more for my commodity portfolio, and sometimes one asset is a bit more appealing than another one due to being oversold or otherwise with attractive forward fundamentals. Out of the group, I would expect the best returns to be from bitcoin if the bitcoin project continues to be successful, but the others give us nice diversification and a large selection between commodities, their producers, and their financiers.

As my base case, I think the market capitalization of both gold and bitcoin will grow over the next 3-5 year timeframe from current levels.

## Summary Thoughts: A Period of Uncertainty

In early 2020, there was a massive deflationary shock across the world as economies shut themselves down in the face of the pandemic. Defensive/disinflationary assets like tech stocks and gold greatly outperformed many other assets.

This was followed by the largest fiscal and monetary policy response from governments around the world since World War II. Cyclical/inflationary assets bounced back and took the lead from there. The announcement of multiple viable vaccines in autumn 2020 then gave that trend another leg up.

Going forward, it’ll be harder. The first two big moves are now out of the way, like a control system trying to re-establish its equilibrium.

For anyone who has vague familiarity with electrical engineering or control system design, the global asset market (inclusive of policy response) is basically behaving like an underdamped stable system. For the uninitiated in terms of what the heck that means, here is a chart of an arbitrary electrical signal (in blue) trying to hit a target level (red dotted line), with a series of big overshoots in either direction until it starts to balance around its target, with some economic annotations added to make the point:

![Control System](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-28-control-system.png)

*Chart Source: [Dynamics and Control](https://apmonitor.com/pdc/index.php/Main/SecondOrderApplications)*

We had a tech bubble (deflationary shock from economic shutdown, with everyone piling into tech), then we had a big surge in commodities and re-opening beneficiaries (inflationary bounce-back from policy response, everyone playing the re-opening trade), and the question is, what’s next?

My long-run base-case is that we’ll see more outperformance from the value/commodity side of things, although I’m willing to play both sides in terms of individual stock selection. However, it’s natural to have counter-rallies in either direction as the system (including policymakers in Washington DC and other country capitols) tries to balance the equation.

If the system becomes unstable in either direction rather than stabilizing as it normally does, it’s more likely to happen on the inflationary side, because policymakers have more tools to control deflation (print and spend more money) than to control inflation in this environment of very high debt levels. For a contextual analysis on the long-term debt, fiscal, and monetary cycle, [see my article on the subject](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/). However, the natural forces of deflation (technology, demographics, and debt) are quite strong.

If you isolate the S&P high beta cyclicals, you can see a big 3-year consolidation (and thus underperformance) from 2018 through most of 2020, and then a massive gap up after the vaccines were announced in November 2020, followed by a near-vertical line straight up and then recent correction:

Well, that seems to be somewhat overdone for the moment.

Do I think copper prices will be higher 5-10 years from now compared to today? Probably yes.

However, do I think copper stocks got a bit stretched recently? Probably yes. Or at least, at current levels, I don’t currently have clarity regarding their forward risk-adjusted return prospects for the next few years, after having bought them aggressively through 2019 and 2020 when they were cheap. I’m willing to take big profits and re-assess.

Mining stocks tend not to be long-term compounders. It makes sense to trim them when they get overheated, whereas I’m more willing to let wide-moat durable stocks run longer.

I’d love to see a correction or consolidation in certain commodity/value names to buy into. Many of them I still think look fine at current levels with a long-term view, like the Japanese trading companies I’ve covered over the past several months. They are built more like long-term compounders. Some of the pure-plays, especially in hot areas like copper, however, became rather stretched.

With the dollar rising, sovereign bond yields in an uptrend, and big overshoots in both directions over the past few months, I view this current period mostly as a moment of observation rather than conviction. As certain things settle, we can get more conviction from those levels.

I have a specific things to watch in the months ahead (like high inflation prints from low baseline levels and the associated bond market response), and am picking through various stocks and assets classes to identify oversold or overbought stocks, as well as to keep holding the majority of the long-term compounders that I own.
