## U.S. Business Cycle Update

The past two weeks have been another mixed bag of good and bad news, depending on where you live and what metrics you care about. In the macro portion of this report, we have updates about COVID-19, the U.S. unemployment situation, and central bank swap lines between the U.S. Federal Reserve and various other central banks.

**The COVID-19 Disparity**

While daily new COVID-19 cases are very low in Europe and Asia, they continue to go up substantially in the United States, to record heights.

Early deaths in the United States and Europe came significantly from the virus getting into nursing homes, which were particularly hard-hit due to the age and morbidity issues that exist in that demographic. Now, those sorts of locations are being better-protected from the virus. The overall virus continues to spread very slowly in Europe and Asia, and is accelerating in the United States, but among younger demographics on average.

Germany has almost three times the population of the U.S. state of Texas, for example, and yet Texas now dwarfs Germany in daily new cases these days:

![Texas vs Germany Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-texas-vs-germany.png)

This isn’t just due to differences in testing. Texas’ hospitalizations for COVID-19 have similarly ramped up over the past month. Fortunately, despite the rise in cases and hospitalizations, the death rate has continued to decline for now because some of the most vulnerable populations are better-protected, as previously described.

However, deaths happen with a lag after reported cases, so by perhaps mid/late July, we’ll know if this surge in cases in June leads to an increase in the number of deaths or not. Either way, due to capacity constraints in hospitals, the rapid rise in cases gave several governors pause for fully re-opening their states.

The Texas Medical Center, which is located in Houston and is by several metrics arguably the largest medical complex in the world, has reported that a number of hospitals in Texas have reached normal capacity limits, and have been [forced to use surge capacity](https://www.houstonchronicle.com/news/health/article/TMC-hospitals-go-into-Phase-2-surge-plans-as-ICU-15380224.php), which they had set up in advance. At any given time, intensive care units are perhaps 75% occupied, with 25% free capacity, and COVID-19 has taken over that normal spare capacity, and has now spilled into temporary surge capacity accommodations.

From the [Texas Tribune, July 1st](https://www.texastribune.org/2020/07/01/coronavirus-texas-houston-hospitals-cases-surge/):

> At Lyndon B. Johnson Hospital on Sunday, the medical staff ran out of both space for new coronavirus patients and a key drug needed to treat them. With no open beds at the public hospital, a dozen COVID-19 patients who were in need of intensive care were stuck in the emergency room, awaiting transfers to other Houston area hospitals, according to a note sent to the staff and shared with reporters.
>
> A day later, the top physician executive at the Houston Methodist hospital system wrote to staff members warning that its coronavirus caseload was surging: “It has become necessary to consider delaying more surgical services to create further capacity for COVID-19 patients,” Dr. Robert Phillips said in the note, an abrupt turn from three days earlier, when the hospital system sent a note to thousands of patients, inviting them to keep their surgical appointments.
>
> And at The University of Texas MD Anderson Cancer Center, staff members were alerted recently that the hospital would soon begin taking in cancer patients with COVID-19 from the city’s overburdened public hospital system, a highly unusual move for the specialty hospital.

Because they planned ahead with surge capacity accommodations, it’s not a major issue at the moment, but it gives public officials, even ones that want badly to fully re-open, a degree of caution about letting the virus continue to spread. Many officials in those hospitals are worried about what capacity could look like a week or two from now with the current growth rate. In response to the heightened number of cases lately, Texas re-closed its bars and limited restaurant capacity to 50%. Similar re-opening reversals came in California, Arizona, and other recently hard-hit states.

Different people and different countries have different views politically about what to do regarding the virus. Some countries, particularly in East Asia, controlled the virus without major government-mandated lockdowns, for example, and simply emphasized masks, hygiene, and contract tracing, which they had experience dealing with during previous pandemics.

But the main constraint by public officials is hospital capacity, so if it begins to spread exponentially for one reason or another as it has recently in the United States, that in part dictates decisions that public officials make about what sorts of venues can be open, and to what degree. If a hospital system becomes overwhelmed, the fatality rates not just of COVID-19, but all-cause hospitalizations in the area, can increase due to inadequate treatment.

In other words, different rates of virus spreading will likely play a role in how hard-hit the service sector is this summer among various regions. Right now, the service sector in these hard-hit U.S. states is under threat again, with a set of re-closures and pauses in re-opening for many states, rather than further re-opening throughout the country.

I continue to view the “[inverse square root sign recovery](https://twitter.com/LynAldenContact/status/1253335717717000195)” as my base case, meaning a quick partial rebound in jobs as the service sector re-opens, followed by a longer multi-year slower grind to get back to the full previous peak in total employment as the credit cycle plays itself out.

**Dollar Shortage Swap Line Drama, or Lack Thereof**

During March of this year, as I covered in previous reports and public articles, there was a shortage of U.S. dollars in offshore markets, which often happens during big liquidity crunches.

Countries and their nonfinancial corporations outside of the United States have over $12 trillion in U.S. dollar-denominated debt, and therefore need dollars from global trade to service that debt. If global trade becomes diminished and/or banks stop lending (like what happened in March) , it can create an abrupt squeeze and scramble for dollars, resulting in a dollar spike and a vicious cycle of foreign institutions needing dollars that become increasingly expensive to get, since everyone needs them at once.

This can lead foreign institutions and central banks to sell Treasuries and other U.S. assets to get dollars, which can then cause liquidity problems in U.S. markets, as foreigners basically pull money out. In mid-March, the U.S. Treasury market “ceased to function effectively” as the Federal Reserved described it, in significant part because the foreign sector abruptly sold $300 billion in Treasuries to get dollars. The Treasury market basically became broken and illiquid, with very wide bid/ask spreads and briefly spiking interest rates.

To address these dollar liquidity crunches, in addition to monetizing newly-issued Treasury securities, the Federal Reserve loans dollars to other central banks, and takes some of their currency as collateral. So, for example, they can loan some dollars to the European Central Bank and the Bank of Japan, and hold euros and yen as collateral to make sure they get paid back. These foreign central banks can then use the dollars as they see fit to address the dollar shortage in their regions, and then give the dollars back when things return to normal and there isn’t an acute scramble for dollars. This is an alternative to those countries having to sell Treasuries and other U.S. assets (stocks and corporate bonds) to get dollars.

During this crisis, the Federal Reserve opened 14 swap lines with other central banks. However, almost all of the lending went to the Bank of Japan and the European Central Bank. Four of the swap lines were never used, and several others were barely used. The last column shows the peak amounts that were used in 2020 so far:

![Swap Line Table](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-swap-line-usage.png)

*Table Source: [BIS June 20 Annual Economic Report](https://www.bis.org/publ/arpdf/ar2020e.pdf)*

Peak swap line usage reached about $450 billion in May, but central banks began rapidly paying them down in June, and as of last week at the beginning of July, total swap line levels are down to half of that peak figure, at just $225 billion.

![Swap Line Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-swap-lines-chart.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

In addition, the Treasury/Eurodollar spread (“TED Spread”) is back to low levels. This spread measures the difference between the interbank lending interest rate and the T-bill interest rate. When it is low, it suggests that offshore dollar liquidity is fine, but when it is high, it suggests that offshore dollar liquidity is problematic (dollar shortage) and/or that institutions don’t trust each other (solvency/liquidity concerns).

During periods of acute dollar shortage, such as in 2008 and back in March 2020, the TED spread spikes, but swap lines have been effective in bringing it back down each time, including in 2020 so far, as shown by this chart:

![TED Spread and Swap Lines](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-swap-lines-ted-spread.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

I had a discussion with hedge fund manager Brent Johnson on Real Vision last month, and we focused a lot on swap lines in our chat. His view, and those that agree with him, is that swap lines are likely to be weaponized, meaning that the U.S. will give or take swap lines to countries based on certain conditions, whereas I’ve argued against that outcome, due to the Fed’s concerns about the U.S. Treasury market if foreigners need to sell Treasuries to get dollars. It’s in the United States’ interest to ensure that there isn’t an offshore dollar shortage, in other words.

So far, the latter story is playing out. Swap lines were given to 14 countries, 4 of which were not even used, several others were used in trivial amounts well below their limits, and half of the total amount lent has already been paid back between May and June. As with previous crises, the TED spread abruptly went down when these swap lines were issued, indicating that they were effective at alleviating the acute offshore dollar shortage.

And these swap lines are quickly being paid back with the TED spread remaining low and the dollar index remaining range-bound, indicating that the the offshore dollar shortage is not a big issue at this moment anymore, and that the swap lines were effective for the brief period that they were needed.

I’ll continue to monitor global funding markets because the dollar remains a critical component for equity prices and other asset class performance. At the moment, offshore dollar markets are looking reasonably healthy, but it’s always possible to have a double-dip or reversal into more global liquidity issues later this year or next year.

## ETF Spotlight: EFA

The iShares MSCI EAFE ETF (EFA), which tracks developed markets in Japan, Europe, Australia, and a bit in Singapore and Hong Kong, looks pretty interesting at current levels. The average P/E ratio is under 16, although earnings throughout the world are messed up in 2020 and I wouldn’t rely on that metric too heavily, but price-to-book is only 1.52x, and the dividend yield is higher than the S&P 500. Cyclically-adjusted price-to-earnings ratios (CAPE ratios) tend to be moderately low in those countries as well.

This is in part due to sector differences. EFA has less tech exposure than the S&P 500. However, even adjusting for sector differences, EFA still is cheaper than SPY. EFA has 14% healthcare, 14% industrials, 12% consumer staples, 11% consumer discretionary, and 8% information technology exposure; so it’s a moderately high-value sector mix, but the regions have been out-of-favor for the past decade.

Each decade tends to have regions of outperformance. In the 1980’s, it was Japan. In the 1990’s, it was the United States and Europe. In the 2000’s, it was emerging markets. In the 2010’s, it was the United States again. In the 2020’s, my base case is it will be some subset of Japan, Europe, and emerging markets over the United States, mainly due to currency differentials, valuation differentials, and potential U.S. corporate tax changes. EFA significantly underperformed SPY in the 2010’s decade, and I expect it to do similar or better in the 2020’s decade from current levels.

The United States is entering this decade with higher unemployment levels than Europe and Japan, larger budget deficits, and more aggressive money-printing. Europe and Japan both have trade surpluses with the United States, while the United States has trade deficits with them. This is in part because the dollar is overvalued; held up by its global reserve status and offshore dollar demand to service dollar-denominated debts. Depending on who wins the U.S. elections in November, the U.S. corporate sector could very well face higher corporate tax rates in the years ahead, after many decades in a row of tax reductions compared to their developed peers. At the very least, U.S. corporate tax rates are unlikely to go down anymore in the 2020’s, since they’re already at historically low levels, so that multi-decade tailwind of lower and lower corporate taxes is most likely gone, and could very well become a headwind.

Therefore, I do like to have some exposure to foreign developed markets in my portfolios. The regions are surprisingly healthy compared to U.S. markets at the moment, when looking out over the next 3-5 years. There are, of course, severe risks everywhere in the world during this period.

The Vanguard Developed Markets ETF (VEA) is similar to EFA, except it also includes South Korea, which in my view is in a solid position as well. EFA has more liquidity for traders (particularly the options market), while VEA has lower expense ratios for buy-and-holders.

## Sberbank (SBRCY) Deep-Dive Analysis

I’ve had Sberbank in my Top Twelve stock list for a while, and in my Schwab portfolio. It’s time for a deep-dive analysis, especially because I think it’s very undervalued at the moment for long-term investors.

This is the largest bank in Russia. There’s an ADR with the ticker SBRCY available over-the-counter for American investors in dollar terms.

Sberbank has a massive market share of the Russian banking industry, ranging from 30-50% or more of market share in various areas:

![Sberbank Market Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-sberbank-marketshare.png)

*Chart Source: [Sberbank June 2020 Investor Presentation](https://www.sberbank.com/common/img/uploaded/files/info/ir_presentation_as_of_1_june_2020.pdf)*

The degree to which Sberbank has dominant market share in Russia cannot be overstated. For American readers, it’s like J.P. Morgan Chase and Wells Fargo and a bunch of fintech firms combined into one bank, for that country. Sberbank’s economic moat in Russia is exceptionally wide.

Sberbank has 96.3 million active retail clients, 2.6 million active corporate clients, 14.2 thousand branches, and 279 thousand employees. They have more employees than J.P. Morgan Chase, (JPM), the largest bank in the United States, even though Sberbank’s market capitalization is less than 1/4th of JPM’s value, with Sberbank’s market cap at $64 billion and JPM’s at $282 billion. This is significantly due to lower valuation and currency value for Sberbank.

Sberbank also invests in, acquires, and/or partners with a variety of fintech firms in Russia, and some global partnerships as well. This prevents those firms from taking Sberbank market share, and diversifies and extends Sberbank’s reach. These investments range from health records to restaurant delivery apps to ride-sharing apps to mobile payment processors to various types of insurance, and so forth. In other words, rather than being a stodgy old bank in a declining industry, Sberbank has above-average adoption of digital technologies and new markets compared to most global peer banks around the world.

For example, [in March 2020](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-russia-sberbank-huawei-tech/russias-sberbank-partners-with-chinas-huawei-on-cloud-services-idUSKBN20Q2HX), Sberbank partnered with Huawei of China to launch SberCloud; a B2B service for cloud applications.

From Reuters:

> Sberbank and Chinese technology giant Huawei said on Tuesday they had formed a strategic partnership to provide cloud services for Russian businesses, extending the reach of Russia’s largest bank into the country’s digital economy.
>
> SberCloud.Advanced clients, which Sberbank hopes will range from large businesses to small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) and start-ups, will have access to 37 new cloud services, and will represent a first on the Russian market because all services will be fully integrated into one system.

> State-owned Sberbank, which has investments in Russian internet giants Yandex and Mail.Ru, has been steadily transforming itself from banking to online services.

And back [in September 2019](https://www.ledgerinsights.com/sberbank-blockchain-trade-finance-oil/), Sberbank tested blockchain technologies with a firm in Singapore. Sberbank bought some accounts receivable from the firm via a blockchain-based transaction. Blockchains can potentially reduce overhead costs for banks, and rather than fighting this trend, Sberbank tested it with small-but-real money.

And in terms of simple digital banking, Sberbank has higher mobile adoption than most major banks worldwide. 60 million of their clients use their mobile app for banking.

Given the dominant market share and the broad reach of partnerships that the bank has, my view is that an investment in Sberank expresses a broadly bullish macro view on the Russian economy over the long term from current cheap levels, rather than just a bet on a specific finance stock.

**Management and Performance**

Sberbank has outperformed the broader Russian equity market over the past 9 years, although returns in general have been poor during that timeframe:

![Sberbank Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-sberbank-performance.png)

Sberbank used to be majority-owned by the Central Bank of Russia, and as of this year, that stake was transferred to Russia’s Ministry of Finance. They hold just over half of the company, which technically gives the Russian government control over the bank. Most of the rest of the shareholders are international investors, and a small stake is held by local Russian retail and institutional investors.

![Sberbank Shareholders](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-sberbank-shareholders.png)

*Chart Source: Sberbank June 2020 Investor Presentation*

All else being equal, I’d rather have a company that is wholly-owned by private investors, without nationalized influence. However, the Russian government has historically had reasonable terms for its state-controlled enterprises. In particular, Russia wants their major state-controlled companies to pay a 50% dividend payout ratio when possible, so that the Russian government can earn those hefty dividends, which of course would also benefit smaller shareholders.

Sberbank has historically been very well-managed, with high returns on equity, conservative leverage ratios, and a diverse spectrum of investments in fintech opportunities.

**Russian Macro Situation**

An analysis of a Russian bank would be incomplete without understanding the Russian economic situation, and in particular their currency situation.

Russia is a slow-growth country, with relatively flat population levels, and an emphasis among its large corporations on energy and materials production, which are historically “low value” industries. Russia is classified by MSCI as an emerging market, but really it’s more of a hybrid between a developed market and emerging market.

On one hand, Russia has per-capita GDP metrics that are in line with the high-end of emerging market countries, and is broadly considered to have high levels of corruption and wealth concentration similar to and in many cases exceeding other emerging markets.

On the other hand, Russia is a leading military, economic, and aerospace technology power. For the past nine years, for example, ever since the United States retired their space shuttle program, The United States has been reliant on paying Russia to launch American astronauts into space to reach the international space station. This was finally resolved this year by SpaceX demonstrating its ability to start launching American astronauts into orbit, ending what has been a somewhat embarrassing era for American space prowess. Russia has maintained a very advanced space and aeronautical program for decades, including more advanced than the majority of other developed countries, and they regularly sell military equipment to other countries as an export.

Russia entered 2020 with a government fiscal surplus and a positive trade surplus. The decline in oil prices in 2020 will impact that and cause deficits this year, but Russia is financially-prepared for this environment, including more prepared than they were during the rough 2014-2016 period.

Russia maintains among the lowest debt-to-GDP levels among nations in the world. These figures are as of the end of 2019:

![Global Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-global-debt.png)

*Data Source: BIS Statistics Warehouse*

Russia has higher foreign-exchange reserves than the total amount of external debt for the country. In addition to below-average debt levels, they have above-average foreign-exchange reserves as a percentage of GDP, and especially as a percentage of broad money supply.

In particular, Russia has been buying gold aggressively for the past decade, to stock their foreign-exchange reserves and de-dollarize their currency exposure. Out of all major countries, Russia has the highest gold reserve value as a percentage of broad money supply.

As I explained in my [recent gold article](https://www.lynalden.com/reasons-to-buy-gold/), for example, the United States’ official gold reserves equal about 2.5% of U.S. broad money supply, while Russia’s official gold reserves equal about 19% of Russia’s broad money supply, which is the highest “gold-backing” in the world among major countries even though of course the currency is not actually pegged to gold.

In my opinion, Elvira Nabiullina, the Chairwoman of the Central Bank of Russia, has been one of the most prudent central bankers in the world in modern times, with a focus on tight monetary policy and an emphasis on building reserves and making Russia as financially strong as possible. This may have seemed overly-cautious for a while, even potentially having stunted Russia’s growth potential, until of course a crisis like COVID-19 happens, and oil prices crashed to record low levels, suddenly making Russia’s high level of gold reserves a key backstop, with high interest rates and thus plenty of policy space to cut rates and provide fiscal and monetary policy support to the economy.

As the world deals with COVID-19, and Russia, Saudi Arabia, and the United States battle each other for oil production market share, Russia has a lot of resources to rely on, thanks to its period of building surpluses and storing resources during easier times.

Russia is of course impacted by the pandemic and global recession, but Sberbank is well-capitalized to handle this. Sberbank identified 4.7% of their loan portfolio to be “industries of special attention”, referring to businesses in the tourism, sports, hotel, catering, education, and conferences industries, and other industries that have been deeply impacted by COVID-19. And many of their retail clients are of course under pressure as well, but there is a rather wide-reaching fiscal support program by the Russian government.

So far, things are bouncing back in Russia as they are in many other places, but at a very uneven rate for different industries:

![Russia Spending](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-russia-spending.png)

*Chart Source: Sberbank June 2020 Investor Presentation*

Because Russia is the one of the largest producers of energy, their currency tends to take a hit when oil prices take a hit. However, this gives them a release valve, unlike Saudi Arabia that has a dollar-pegged currency. When Russia’s currency declines in value (despite strong underlying currency fundamentals), it makes their exports more competitive, and makes their labor cheaper in terms of global purchasing power, which gives them a lot of flexibility to weather tough times until there is a rebound in oil prices and global economic activity, when their currency can bounce back.

**Valuation Analysis**

Sberbank is a bank, and thus is highly-cyclical in regards to the Russian economy. They face loan losses in bust times, and higher profitability in boom times.

Russia had a recession during the 2008 global financial crisis, and then again in the 2015/2016 global slowdown and oil price crash, and then again during the 2020 COVID-19 pandemic. And yet, in all of these times, although Sberbank’s profits went down, they remained profitable rather than experiencing outright losses.

As I said, Sberbank has been conservatively managed and very prudent, and the fact that Russia has low overall debt levels certainly helps, even though Russian consumers themselves are still financially vulnerable and were showing signs of stress from late 2018 through 2019.

Over the course of these business cycles, Sberbank keeps making higher lows and higher highs in terms of earnings per share, even as the share price itself has languished due to lower valuations:

![Sberbank FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-sberbank-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Sberbank is currently trading for a blended price/earnings ratio of about 6x.

Specifically, the stock is trading at a P/E ratio of below 5 times their high annual earnings in boom times from 2017-2019, a little more than 8 times their expected trough earnings this year, according to analyst 2020 EPS estimates, and about 6 times their expected 2021 earnings, although those estimates are highly uncertain.

Sberbank’s valuation is so low at the moment, that the 2019 dividend offered more than a 9% yield compared to the current price, despite having a moderately low payout ratio. They will likely reduce their next dividend (it’s currently under review), like they did during 2014-2016, but this dividend should grow back in the years ahead. Remember, the Russian government has a majority stake in the company, and they want a 50% dividend payout ratio when possible.

With Sberbank, patient investors get a high dividend yield, a very low valuation, and a company that has consistently grown over the past two decades from one business cycle to the next. Right now, it’s at a trough in that business cycle in recession times, and thus very cheap.

Sberbank is currently trading below book value, despite being profitable from year to year. Even if 2020 ends up being their first unprofitable year in the past 15 years (which analysts don’t currently expect to be the case, but let’s say it happens), the bank is well-positioned to take that hit and be profitable in 2021, 2022, etc.

Russia had some high inflation problems during the 2015-2016 crisis, which pushed Sberbank’s valuation levels to depths even lower than they are today. However, since then, in the boom times that followed, Russia strengthened its financial position, and is so far not experiencing that problem in 2020.

Russia currently has a 4.5% benchmark interest rate and a 3% inflation rate. Compared to many advanced markets that are currently stuck at zero or even negative yields (which is terrible for the banking sector), Russia still has positive yields, including positive inflation-adjusted yields. This is good for bank profitability.

Overall, the Russian stock market has one of the lowest cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratios (CAPE ratios) in the world:

![Star Capital Valuations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-7-5-star-capital-valuations.png)

*Chart Source: [Star Capital](https://www.starcapital.de/en/research/stock-market-valuation/)*

Research by Meb Faber and others has shown that, over the past 25 years, investing in a diverse set of the bottom 25% country equity markets in terms of the CAPE ratio, has produced significantly outsized returns compared to investing in, say, the S&P 500. In other words, buying into the beaten-down parts of the world as a value investor, has historically outperformed investing in expensive markets, or sticking with just one market, for investors with long time horizons.

Past results are no guarantee for future results, but for a variety of reasons, I am bullish on various high-quality Russian stocks at these low prices. Prior to the 2008 crisis, Russia’s stock market had much higher broad equity valuations, but has trended lower in recent years, and their currency and equity markets particularly became out-of-favor globally after the annexation of Crimea. These valuation cycles tend to change from decade to decade, and right now, Russia is simply very cheap as a whole, which includes Sberbank as one of the top companies.

I wouldn’t put my whole portfolio into beaten-down areas, but I’m certainly happy to put a small portion of capital to work in stocks from Russia and similar regions. For me, it’s all about position sizing. Small investments in various cheap places, means that even if some of those investments run into tail risks that result in poor performance, it doesn’t tank the whole portfolio. Statistically, a collection of these well-placed bets in cheap markets should pay off over the long run, and historically has done so.

This model starts from 2019, prior to the COVID-19 crisis. Sberbank will take an EPS hit in 2020 and 2021, but analysts expect the bank to regain 2019 levels by 2023, and the bank has a good history of growth.

In this model, the bank grows by an average of just 2% per year from 2019 through 2029, and can be sold for a P/E ratio of 10x by the end of the period in ten years, which is fairly average for this stock. I use an aggressive 12% target rate of return in this model (which is higher than my standard 10% target), because for a Russian bank I want some extra risk premium, and that aggressive discounting pushes down the fair value. In addition, I assume the payout ratio stays static at 2019 figures, about 45%, although in reality it will likely increase to 50% after the bank gets past this COVID-19 period.

Even with all of this, the calculated fair value is $16-$17/share, compared to the actual price of under $12 today. It’s undervalued even compared to a further margin of safety price of, say, $14-$15.

That 2% growth estimate doesn’t even keep pace with inflation; in reality I expect higher growth. I also think valuations could increase substantially during the 2020’s decade, like why not a P/E of 12 if things go well for the bank in the 2020’s? So, I’m using fairly bearish assumptions here with my growth estimate and expected valuation at sale.

Investors must also consider currency risks. My base case over the next 5 years is actually for the ruble to stay about flat or even strengthen against the dollar as the price of oil eventually rebounds, and as Russia’s fiscal situation is stronger than the U.S. fiscal situation in terms of government deficits as a percentage of GDP, and that could add another tailwind to performance. However, even if we say that’s wrong, and allow for mild continued ruble devaluation against the dollar or euro, investors still have plenty of margin of safety here, given how low the valuation is despite numerous foundations of strength for the company as Russia’s economy likely rebounds in the years ahead.

**Company Risks**

*Macro Risk*

Like most financial institutions, Sberbank is a cyclical company, and its fortunes are significantly impacted by Russian economic expansions and recessions. This is mitigated by low debt levels and strong reserves for the country, but the Russian consumer has been under financial pressure for the past two years or so, and Russia in general has high levels of wealth concentration and expensive property prices in major cities.

A prolonged low oil price, or other severe long-term impact to the Russian economy, would harm Sberbank’s profitability for more than just a year. Investors should ideally have a neutral-to-positive view of long-term Russian economic prospects before deciding to invest in Sberbank. Low valuations can offset this risk, but it remains something to be aware of.

At these valuations and a propensity to pay hefty dividends, Sberbank’s stock doesn’t really need growth or a good outcome to do well over the long run from these levels. It just needs a non-terrible outcome, which seems likely. Investors that expect a terrible economic scenario for Russia in the years ahead should probably avoid Sberbank stock.

*Sanction Risk*

Sberbank has a big “Russian discount” at the moment, along with the rest of the Russian equity market. In addition to having high levels of corruption, Russia has faced sanctions from various countries, particularly from the 2014 annexation of Crimea by Russia.

A handful of countries prevent citizens from owning shares of Russian financiers, including Sberbank. Canada, for example, [has rules](https://laws.justice.gc.ca/eng/regulations/SOR-2014-58/FullText.html) against citizens transacting in new securities including equity stakes of Russian banks, including Sberbank. My interpretation is that this would apply to buying into a new equity offering, rather than affecting the purchase of existing shares. However, I’m not a lawyer, so Canadian investors will have to interpret the meaning of that for themselves or preferably consult with their brokerage or legal counsel.

In general, investors should check with their country’s rules and regulations for owning shares of Sberbank. It’s one of Russia’s largest companies, it’s included in most major global index funds, and it can be bought easily via its ADR, with J.P. Morgan Depository Receipts as the transfer agent for the ADR, and yet in this period of heightened inter-country instability, there may be rules against citizens in some countries owning shares.

In this era of de-globalization and increasing inter-country tensions, it’s always possible that some political event could happen and result in forced divestment of Russian stocks by citizens in certain countries at an inopportune time, and this should factor into a valuation discount.

## Conclusion

Russia has among the world’s lowest debt levels, highest real interest rates, highest FX reserves relative to money supply, highest gold reserves relative to money supply, and went into 2020 with a fiscal surplus and trade surplus, which gives them a significant degree of flexibility to deal with the economic impact that is happening in 2020.

Sberbank in particular has historically performed well, generally maintains profitability in tough times, gets stronger after each business cycle, typically earns high returns on equity, benefits from high real interest rates, has adequate reserves to deal with the problems that many of their borrowers are experiencing when it comes to potential loan defaults, and is very cheap with a good dividend in most years.

For investors that don’t want to invest in a Russian bank, this report also covered the iShares EFA ETF, with a relatively optimistic view of foreign developed markets as a segment within a portfolio, and I provided some new portfolios for members to peruse at their leisure.

Focusing back on the broad macro outlook, one of the catalysts to look for in the weeks and months ahead is another round of fiscal stimulus in the United States, likely in mid-summer or late-summer prior to the elections in the autumn. This could be a boon for risk assets and result in another leg lower in the dollar, but we’ll cross that bridge when we get to it, and in the meantime, it remains a challenging time for markets with not a lot of upside catalysts to look forward to in the near term.
