## Macro View

In the long run, assets are priced based on fundamentals. In the short run, assets are priced primarily on sentiment and liquidity.

To make things complicated, liquidity can impact fundamentals. For example, liquidity conditions affect whether emerging markets are getting squeezed by higher financing costs with a stronger dollar or not. This impacts their aggregate economic growth rates, which then ripples through the various fundamentals of the global economy.

Domestic liquidity in the United States has been rather sideways since the start of Q4 2022. This metric (blue line below) represents the Fed’s balance sheet minus the Treasury General Account and minus reverse repos, with the S&P 500 (red line below) shown for comparison purposes:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Fed has been the primary reducer of liquidity as it lets bonds mature off of its balance sheet each month, which is a form of quantitative tightening. On the other hand, as the Treasury Department has drawn down its Treasury General Account since mid-2022, this has *added* some liquidity back into the market. The net result has been rather sideways liquidity lately.

Going forward, this sideways liquidity situation is likely set to continue for the next few months. The Fed is still reducing its balance sheet (negative for liquidity) while the debt ceiling issue is likely to result in lower Treasury General Account balances (ironically positive for liquidity) until the debt ceiling is resolved, as described in the next section.

When the debt ceiling is raised and the Treasury Department begins refilling its Treasury General Account with more bond issuance later this year, then that would likely be very negative for liquidity, assuming the Fed is still reducing their balance sheet at that point. That would be a negative double-whammy for liquidity.

Meanwhile, the dollar index peaked at around the start of Q4 2022, right when domestic liquidity bounced and began trending more sideways than down. This September/October timeframe is also when it became pretty clear that the consumer price index was rolling over on a year-over-year basis, which slowed down the market’s assessment of future Fed rate hikes. This has been good for emerging markets and precious metals.

**US Debt Ceiling Note**

The United States government has a “debt ceiling” that it reaches every once in a while, resulting in debates by politicians, until it is eventually raised. It generally gives the out-of-power party an opportunity to apply pressure to the in-power party (in terms of the presidency), make a spectacle of the existing debt, and potentially extract some policy concessions. Then, when the out-of-power party gets in power, they downplay the debt ceiling until they are out of power again.

The reason that the debt ceiling has always historically been raised, is because it’s about whether the government will honor *past* debts or not. They need to raise more debt to roll over existing debts and pay out previously-made promises. I covered the problematic U.S. fiscal situation in my [November 2022 public newsletter issue](https://www.lynalden.com/november-2022-newsletter/) and on a recent [Macro Voices podcast episode](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=206rKb3LoS0&ab_channel=MacroVoices).

People often ask the obvious question, “if they always raise the debt ceiling, then why do they even have a debt ceiling?” The answer is that the reason for its existence is out of convenience, for lack of a better method.

Prior to 1917, Congress had to approve each individual debt issuance, and what form that debt would exist in (e.g. the specific durations and other nuances of how the debt would be structured). However, starting with World War I, the scope of the federal government became so large that this process became unworkable. So, Congress passed a law that allowed the U.S. Treasury Department to figure out the nuances of how to structure the debt associated with financing the expenditures that Congress passed, with Congress maintaining control via an overall debt ceiling.

Congress still passes specific spending bills and debt ceilings, but does not micromanage individual Treasury debt issuance. Congress basically tells the Treasury Department, “You can issue the debt securities using whatever details make sense, since you’re the professional accountants after all. However, you can’t go over this amount.” It’s like how a board of directors gives the Chief Financial Officer of a corporation a lot of leeway to figure out the financing details, as long as he or she acts in line with overall guidance from the board.

For most of its history, the debt ceiling was not really used as a political tool. But over the past decade and a half, the country has become more politically polarized, and so it has come up more often. Both parties have used it to some degree when not in control of the presidency, but the most significant instances happened during Obama’s presidency when his administration was strongly opposed by the Republican Party.

In 2011, the debate became so fierce and reached so close to the deadline that S&P downgraded the credit rating of the federal government from AAA to AA+, and hasn’t raised it again. Microsoft (MSFT) and Johnson and Johnson (JNJ) both technically have a higher S&P credit rating than the United States federal government. Australia, Canada, Denmark, Germany, the Netherlands, Singapore, Sweden, Switzerland, and a few other countries also still have AAA ratings.

I wrote a [public article in October 2021](https://www.lynalden.com/sovereign-default/) that detailed some of the things that could happen if the U.S. actually defaults due to political gridlock, with different scenarios depending on how long that default lasts.

Technically, the U.S. hit its debt ceiling this week. However, hitting the debt ceiling is not when the potential default crisis happens. Once the government hits the debt ceiling, the Treasury Department can begin taking “extraordinary measures” as they call them in order to avoid defaulting. Recently, Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen estimated that these extraordinary measures can keep the government financed into June, although it’s impossible to precisely predict the date ahead of time.

A key extraordinary measure that the government turns to quickly is that they temporarily stop reinvesting the special type of Treasuries that are held in the retirement accounts (the “G Fund”) of federal employees. This happens behind the scenes and gets resolved once the debt ceiling impasse has ended. It creates a couple hundred billion dollars of headroom in the meantime, and they will likely be using this approach starting around now.

Another major component of these extraordinary measures is that they draw down the Treasury General Account, which is the Treasury’s cash balance that they hold at the Federal Reserve. Currently, the Treasury aims to maintain a balance of approximately $500 billion, but as of this writing it is only $339 billion. During debt ceiling gridlock events, this balance historically gets drawn down to nearly zero. This drawdown temporarily adds liquidity to the financial system.

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-tga.png)

One of the later extraordinary measures that they turn to is to shut down parts of the government, and thereby temporarily reduce government expenditures. This does impact the economy to some degree depending on how long it lasts, because a significant number of people stop getting paid. They aren’t at this point yet but it would become increasingly likely to occur as the “default date” nears.

Once the debt ceiling is raised one way or another, and the Treasury begins to refill its Treasury General Account while the Federal Reserve is also performing quantitative tightening, it could be quite negative for liquidity. As of this writing, there is not yet a lot of clarity for precisely when that will happen, whether the debt ceiling default situation will go down to the final hour (or even past the final hour into technical default), and so forth.

As this situation develops I plan to continue reporting on it, as part of my overall market liquidity analysis. At the moment, unless or until some of the primary variables change, the scenario looks like overall domestic liquidity will be sideways for a few months until the debt ceiling is raised, and then likely will head down again from that point.

Labor shortages continue to persist, and hold up the labor overall market. Jobless claims remain low, the unemployment rate remains low, and total nonfarm payrolls continue to rise. However, leading indicators of employment, such as temporary help services (blue line below) and overtime hours (red line below), have both rolled over:

![Temporary Work and Overtime Hours](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-temp-and-overtime.png)

On the other hand, the copper-to-gold ratio, which is generally quite correlated with macro indicators such as the US purchasing manager’s index, has been bucking the trend since the second half of 2022:

![PMI and Copper/Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-pmi-copper-gold.png)

I have been viewing the recent move in the copper/gold ratio to likely be a bear market rally that will eventually correct down to align more with what the purchasing manager’s index is doing, but the longer this divergence persists, the higher the likelihood that this may not be the case. A stagflationary supply-side problem may be materializing.

Copper inventories are historically low. The commodity trading company Trafigura has been warning for months about this.

>
> Kostas Bintas, Trafigura’s cohead of metals and minerals trading, said at the FT Mining Summit on Thursday that inventories can currently cover 4.9 days of global consumption and could finish the year at 2.9 days. For comparison, copper inventories are typically counted in weeks.

Bloomberg [reported on January 11, 2023](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-01-11/metals-prices-could-spike-with-markets-so-tight-says-trafigura):

> Geopolitical shocks could trigger a surge in metals prices because the market has never been tighter, according to Trafigura Group.

>
> His comments come as mining executives warn that much more investment is needed to ensure the world has enough supplies of energy-transition metals in the next decade.

And then on [January 17th it was reported](https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/trafigura-plans-take-large-amounts-lme-copper-sources-2023-01-17/) by Reuters and others that Trafigura would take a considerable amount of copper from the LME:

> LONDON, Jan 17 (Reuters) – Commodity trader Trafigura is planning to take large amounts of copper from London Metal Exchange registered warehouses, two people familiar with the matter said, adding that the metal was likely to remain in Europe.

So, it’s possible that the copper/gold ratio will fail to correlate with most economic indicators in this cycle, if there is a specific supply-side problem. I don’t have a strong view on that at this time, but I am noting the divergence.

**Chinese Re-Opening**

As China re-opens, it should put a bid under the price of oil (all else being equal), and should be beneficial for tourism-dependent countries that Chinese citizens previously tended to visit.

Chinese flights continue to increase this year (red line below), and I plan to monitor this statistic over time. The timing of the Chinese new year affects these current few days.

![China Flights](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-china-flights.png)

*Chart Source: [Airportia](https://www.airportia.com/flights-monitor/?country=CN)*

The green line shows the pre-COVID baseline, which was typically 13,000 to 14,000 flights per day.

The light blue line shows what happened in 2020, where flights began the year strong but quickly collapsed during the initial lockdowns. Flights then gradually recovered throughout the rest of the year.

The dark blue line shows the rolling lockdowns that China imposed throughout 2021, and shows how they spent the second half of 2021 below even the subdued level of flights that occurred in the second half of 2020.

The purple line of 2022 shows where China completely decoupled from the rest of the world. While much of the rest of the world opened up and reduced their travel restrictions, China locked down even harder, and there were much fewer flights in 2022 than in 2020 or 2021, a far fewer than the pre-COVID trend in 2019.

However, over the past month, after mounting protests and an inability to contain the latest and most-transmissible variants, the Chinese government has been cutting back on travel restrictions, letting the population develop herd immunity, and broadly re-opening. Most of the efforts to contain the virus in 2022 were likely wasted.

Going forward, Chinese monetary policymakers intend to strike a balance between stimulus and price stability, with an ongoing stimulated re-opening, but not an impulsive spike of liquidity, as [Bloomberg reports](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-01-13/china-s-pboc-says-it-will-avoid-flooding-economy-with-stimulus):

> China will avoid unleashing massive monetary stimulus into the economy this year as it seeks to strike a balance between boosting growth, creating jobs and maintaining price stability, a central bank deputy governor said Friday.
>
> “We will ensure overall social demand gets powerful support. Meanwhile, the magnitude will be reasonable and appropriate and we will avoid flooding the economy with liquidity,” said Deputy Governor Xuan Changneng at a press conference. He repeated the People’s Bank of China’s long-standing stance that cash supply will stay “reasonably sufficient.”

China’s reduction in flights over the past couple years removed some marginal oil demand from the world, and that oil demand seems to be returning now.

Chinese stocks are also bouncing off their lows, although they remain below pre-pandemic average valuation levels (price/book, price/earnings, etc). There is considerable value in many Chinese companies, but investors have to weigh the geopolitical risks of owning Chinese-based companies in a world that has increasing tensions between major sovereign powers.

[Patrick Zweifel](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel), the chief economist at Pictet Asset Management, has been publishing an ongoing series of charts that track various Chinese economic data.

During the past three years of lockdowns, their [industrial production](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel/status/1615362187756974080) (supplying much of the rest of the world with goods) remained on a strong growth trend:

![China Industrial Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-china-industrial-production.jpg)

Meanwhile, their [retail sales](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel/status/1615362191460564998) and [real estate construction](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel/status/1615362199593312258) collapsed. This chart on the left shows retail sales compared to the prior trend:

A lot of on-chain bitcoin metrics look pretty much how we would expect them to look if bitcoin was forming major cyclical bottom.

The ratio of bitcoin that hasn’t moved in over a year seems to be stabilizing and rolling over from high levels:

The big caveat is that a lot of unprofitable growth/meme equities are bouncing sharply, too. For example, ARKK is up 20% year-to-date. That’s fine, but from there, CVNA is up 38%, Peloton (PTON) is up 35%, and Bed Bath and Beyond (BBBY) is up 46%. Some growth stocks were overdone to the downside and due for a bounce, but to see such a broad rally in stocks of this nature is not great, and indicates that once this short-covering bounce is over, prices could stagnate for a while again.

I expect the bitcoin price bottom to be a process. The bottom could very well already be in, or it may be retested later this year if the macro liquidity situation gets bad enough. Either way, most indicators suggest that the asset is in a deep value zone and likely to provide good 3-5 year returns from these price levels, assuming the fundamentals of the network continue to strengthen on a structural basis. For most people who want some bitcoin as part of their asset mix, I think [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) or rebalancing a slice of a portfolio into it is the optimal way to do it rather than trying to trade it too much.

As an advisor to [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/blog), a venture firm that invests in bitcoin-related startups, I’m in the fortunate position to be able to talk to a lot of startup founders in the ecosystem, and have a good view of the ongoing pipeline of development for the network. Although the Bitcoin network itself is an open source decentralized protocol with no leadership team, there are many small companies building on top of it, making the protocol easier and more useful to interact with.

Meanwhile, the Lightning network (open source network of channels that runs on top of the Bitcoin network) continued to see strong growth in 2022, despite the bear market. This is because the Lightning network is primarily used for utility (quick payments) and hardly at all for speculation or leverage.

“Wallet of Satoshi” has been a particularly popular mobile Lightning app because it’s very user friendly. The downside is that it’s a custodial wallet, but it has seen rather explosive growth:

## Value vs Growth Equity Analysis

During disinflationary decades, growth-oriented equities tend to outperform. The 1930s, the 1960s, the 1990s, and the 2010s were examples of this phenomenon.

With low wage growth, over-supplied commodities, and low price inflation, the cost of capital tends to be very cheap. Cheap costs of capital allow investors to place very high valuations on growth companies.

On the other hand, inflationary decades tend to favor value equities. The 1940s, 1970s, and 2000s were examples of this phenomenon. Commodity/energy producers tend to be the big winners, but more broadly, value-oriented equities in general tend to do better than their growth-oriented counterparts due to being less sensitive to interest rates.

In a January 2021 public articled called “[Interest Rate Effects on Equities: Valuation Impacts](https://www.lynalden.com/interest-rate-effects/)” I used three examples to show how fast-growing stock valuations are more sensitive to changes in discount rates compared to slow-growing inexpensive value stocks. For that article, I took three different example companies, and applied discounted cash flow analysis to each of them twice. The first instance applied a 12% discount rate, and the second instance applied an 8% discount rate. The results showed that by changing the discount rate affects the fair value estimate of growth stocks way more than it affects the fair value estimate for value stocks.

Here is the relevant excerpt:

> **Value Stock DCF Example**
>
> Let’s say a company earns $100,000 in free cash flow per year. It’s a steady-state business, so we expect it to grow its cash flow by 5% per year for the next ten years, and then level out at 3% annual perpetually after that. That growth is just keeping up with population and currency inflation; its market share and volume per capita are rather static.
>
> If we discount those future expected cash flows by a 12% rate, then the value of the next 25 years of cash flows would be $1.096 million:
>
> ![Interest Rate Valuations: Value 12 DCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/value-12-dcf.png)
>
> In other words, we would be willing to pay a price/FCF ratio of roughly 11x, if we want 12% annualized returns on these cash flows.
>
> If we were willing to look out further, and discount a couple more decades of the company running in perpetuity, we’d get to a little bit over $1.2 million for the valuation, or a bit over a 12x price/earnings. However, for this article I’ll stick with the 25 year forecast.
>
> Now, suppose we’re analyzing the same exact company, but interest rates are 4% lower. So, due to lack of viable alternatives, we’re also willing to drop our discount rate by 4%, and so we use an 8% discount rate. Here’s the updated chart:
>
> ![Interest Rate Valuations: Value 8 DCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/value-8-dcf.png)
>
> The 25 years worth of discounted cash flows for this updated model would be $1.586 million. In other words, we’d pay a price/FCF ratio of nearly 16x.
>
> That valuation is 44.7% higher than our previous $1.096 million valuation assessment where we were using 12% as our discount rate for the same company.
>
> It’s the same company, but with a lower discount rate and thus a higher fair valuation. Keep that 44.7% number in mind.
>
> **Growth Stock DCF Example**
>
> Now, let’s look at a second company, with a higher growth rate.
>
> It starts with $100,000 in free cash flow per year, but it’ll grow that by 20% per year for the first 5 years, then 15% for the next 5 years, then 10% for the next 5 years, then 5% for the next 5 years, and then 3% thereafter once it’s fully matured.
>
> If we discount those future expected cash flows by a 12% rate, then the value of the next 25 years of cash flows would be $2.860 million:
>
> ![Interest Rate Valuations: Growth 12 DCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/growth-12-dcf.png)
>
> In other words, we would be willing to pay a price/FCF ratio of nearly 29x, if we want 12% annualized returns on these cash flows.
>
> Now, if we analyze the same company using a lower 8% discount rate, here’s what we get:
>
> ![Interest Rate Valuations: Growth 8 DCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/growth-8-dcf.png)
>
> The 25 years worth of discounted cash flows for this updated model would be $4.628 million. In other words, we’d pay a price/FCF ratio of over 46x.
>
> That valuation is 61.8% higher than our previous $2.860 million valuation assessment where we were using 12% as our discount rate for the same company.
>
> Notice that, during the same reduction in the discount rate from 12% to 8%, the growth stock had a notably larger percent increase in its fair value than the value stock (61.8% vs 44.7%). This is because the growth stock has a larger percentage of its expected cumulative cash flows occur far into the future compared to the value stock that is more front-loaded, so a larger portion of the growth stock’s expected cumulative cash flows are subject to the multi-year compounding effect of the discount rate.
>
> The growth stock is more rate sensitive, in other words. Whenever we have a big shift to lower interest rates, and the market expects those low rates to persist for a while, then it’s not surprising to see a surge in valuations of growth stocks during that transition from higher to lower rates.
>
> **Hypergrowth Stock DCF Example**
>
> Lastly, let’s look at a third company, with an even higher growth rate.
>
> For the next five years, it won’t be profitable at all. Once it establishes positive free cash flow on the sixth year, it grows that by 30% for the next 5 years, then 20% for the next 5 years, then 15% for 5 years, then 10% for 5 years, and then 10% for the next 5 years, and so on.
>
> If we discount those future expected cash flows by a 12% rate, then the value of the next 25 years of cash flows would be $2.589 million:
>
> ![Interest Rate Valuations: Hypergrowth 12 DCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/hypergrowth-12-dcf.png)
>
> Now, if we analyze the same company using an 8% discount rate, here’s what we get:
>
> ![Interest Rate Valuations: Hypergrowth 8 DCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/hypergrowth-8-dcf.png)
>
> The 25 years worth of discounted cash flows for this updated model would be $4.917 million.
>
> That valuation is 89.9% higher than our previous $2.589 million valuation assessment where we were using 12% as our discount rate for the same company.
>
> So, the hypergrowth stock is even more rate sensitive than the growth stock (61.8%), let alone the value stock (44.7%).
>
> -Lyn Alden, January 2021

However, structurally, I think value equities in general look better than growth equities overall.

## Final Thoughts: US vs ex-US Equities

Ever since the end of September 2022, the S&P 500 has been underperforming the MSCI EAFE index (which consists mainly of European, Japanese, and Australian equities). The top chart below shows the ratio of the S&P 500 index to the MSCI EAFE index (higher means S&P 500 outperformance and lower means MSCI EAFE outperformance) and the bottom chart shows the dollar index:

![S&P 500 vs MSCI EAFE vs DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-spx-eafe-dxy.png)

The same is generally true for the S&P 500 vs emerging markets:

![S&P 500 vs Emerging Markets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-1-22-spx-em-dxy.png)

This past decade, much like the 1990s, was a massive period of outperformance for U.S. equities. Fund managers around the world overweighted U.S. equities, and the combination of the strong dollar and the rise of the big tech and social media companies propelled significant U.S. equity outperformance.

Decades where growth equities outperform value equities have historically benefited U.S. equities above foreign equities, in large part because the U.S. stock market is more growth-oriented. On the other hand, decades where value equities outperform growth equities have historically benefited foreign equities over U.S. equities.

The value/growth ratio pivoted for U.S. equities back in mid-2020, with value outperforming since that time. However, the Chinese government crackdown on its own tech giants in 2021, the European energy crisis in 2021/2022, and the U.S. Federal Reserve being more hawkish than the European Central Bank and the Bank of Japan in 2022, contributed to a strong dollar and U.S. equity outperformance even in the face of value equities doing better than growth, at least until Q4 2022.

Going forward, there seems to be considerable opportunity in equity markets outside of the United States. However, in the second half of this year when the debt ceiling is presumably resolved and the U.S. Treasury begins refilling its cash account (which is negative for liquidity), that could cause another dollar surge. My base expectation is pointing towards a shift in outperformance from U.S. to foreign equities, but I think that roll-over could be more of a process, potentially with some retests, as we move through this year.
