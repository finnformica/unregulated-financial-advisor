## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Reverse repo facilities have been flat for the past two weeks as the Treasury General Account continued to build, and so the net result was a mild drop in liquidity. It continues to be rangebound overall.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity has also been middling. My preferred metric for this is global broad money supply denominated in dollars, which is affected by both the rate of money supply growth and the strength or weakness of the dollar relative to other currencies:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-global-m2.png)

The S&P 500 has been down into correction territory (>10%) since its second top in late July 2023. Since my prior report on October 15th where I warned that neither of these liquidity charts are particularly bullish for risk assets, the S&P 500 has been straight down almost 5%.

![S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-spx.png)

My expectation is that the S&P 500 will not reach its late 2021 highs in *real* terms for a very long time. More specifically, I expect the 2020s decade to be a choppy lost decade for the S&P 500 in real terms. It’s looking short-term oversold at current levels, and there are certainly individual stocks in the index that are downright cheap.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The U.S. business cycle continues to be in limbo, where various indicators like PMIs, temporary workers, overtime hours, and other metrics are soft, and most year-over-year metrics that I follow are mildly negative in real terms (see the October 15th report’s economic heatmap, for example). However, some of these indicators have bounced from lows that they set earlier this year.

For example, [Conference Board leading indicators](https://www.conference-board.org/topics/us-leading-indicators) have been stabilizing, albeit at low levels:

![LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-lei.png)

Moreover, the first estimate for what GDP growth was in Q3 2023 came out and it was the biggest one in almost two years at 4.9% annualized:

![US GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-gdp.png)

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow model’s initial prediction for Q4 is 2.3% annualized, which would still be decent.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-gdpnow.png)

As I’ve been saying, when trying to analyze these various competing metrics, it turns out that it’s a tale of two markets. Interest rate sensitive sectors are in a lot of trouble, while on the other side of that, the U.S. federal government itself is pouring more deficits into the economy largely due to those higher interest rates (and various people and entities are on the receiving side of those, which is stimulatory for them). So the travel industry, the restaurant industry, cash-rich companies, and other parts of the economy are booming.

Here are restaurant sales, for example. The blue line on the left axis is the nominal figure, and the red line on the right axis is CPI-adjusted. Either way it’s up:

![Restaurant Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-restaurant-sales.png)

As can be seen, restaurant sales have an annual seasonal pattern, but are otherwise not very cyclical. Restaurants were largely un-phased by the 2001 recession, but stagnated for a few years in the aftermath of the much larger 2008 recession. They had an unprecedented collapse during 2020 due to the pandemic-related lockdowns, which were unique. My expectation for them is to continue to be moderately upward, or at worst to stagnate for a couple years.

Overall, I continue to have a very mixed outlook for the economy, with very loose fiscal policy stimulating large swaths of it, and tight monetary policy suppressing other swaths of it.

**Middle East Risk**

Israel has begun their ground invasion of Gaza. The broad concern for some weeks has been that when Israel begins this action, Iran and their proxies might attack Israel to pressure Israel on multiple fronts or otherwise threaten their security.

The United States has spent the prior weeks moving assets into the region, including two carrier groups, a marine naval group, and a variety of anti-missile defenses. The United States has also sent a number of direct warnings including from President Biden himself. The idea is that, if Iran and its proxies were to launch something big against Israel, the United States has capacity to help Israel defend against that attack and might outright counter-attack. It is reportedly one of the most concentrated projections of power by the U.S. Navy in the region in decades, and so tensions are high.

Russia reportedly has hypersonic capabilities, and so does Iran, China, and some other countries. It’s unclear how they would work in the field compared to the narrative that surrounds them, with potential for attacker malfunction or defender success, but they are considered to be potential anti-carrier attack methods. Carriers have been around for a century and aside from nukes are the key aspect of U.S. military power projection, and so a number of countries have tried to develop measures against them, either novel (e.g. hypersonic and thus potentially able to bypass missile defenses) or asymmetric and regional (e.g. build and launch a very large number of inexpensive attacks at a carrier and some percentage of them will get through the defenses and hit, which has a capacity to work if a country pre-emptively builds that capacity around a specific region, such as the Persian Gulf or South China Sea).

Focusing on my lane as a financial analyst, any escalation of military action that directly involves Iran and their oil production facilities would be a gamechanger for macroeconomic conditions globally, due to the spike in energy prices that it could lead to. The ground invasion by Israel was a key catalyst for this potentially happening. Thus far, despite the initiation of a ground invasion, there has been no major sign of it from Iran, and I continue to hope that no such escalation occurs.

I have been comparing the macroeconomic environment of the 2020s to the 1940s for several years, while stating that I hope that the 2020s are “less kinetic” than the 1940s. The unfortunate fact is that wars lead to sovereign debt crises, but sovereign debt crises also lead to wars. Going to war builds up debt, but also, enemies attack when they perceive the gates to be down, financially or otherwise. How many wars can the United States contribute to financially and logistically while it has massive fiscal debts and deficits at home? This is one of the leading questions of our day, and something that is hard to invest around.

I continue to view physical gold, energy commodities, and energy producers as being useful positions to overweight in an otherwise diverse portfolio this decade, due to ongoing U.S. fiscal issues, insufficient energy capex in recent years, and low valuations and allocations by capital providers into those areas. Any escalation in the Middle East can only add to this thesis, but the thesis is not dependent on it. Within the context of a diversified portfolio, these types of energy-related assets help to protect other assets that generally prefer disinflation and order, such as broad equity positions.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin recently broke out to new cycle highs that haven’t been seen since May 2022. It had been rangebound for a while with about $31k serving as resistance, but it broke out as high as $35k and has since settled at $34k.

![Bitcoin Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-bitcoin-chart.png)

Importantly, this move was driven by the spot market, and happened on high volume. It also did this at a time when stocks were dropping, which makes it more impressive.

As a final sign of a healthy rally, bitcoin mostly rallied alone, rather than bring the whole crypto market with it. Late stage bull markets are associated with a lot of low quality coins surging in price, whereas early stage bull markets tend to be driven by bitcoin in particular.

The ETHBTC ratio dropped to its lowest level in quite a while, and slightly below the June 2022 dip that it had in the aftermath of the [Terra LUNA fiasco](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/).

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-ethbtc.png)

That was not because ether dropped, but rather just because ether remained rangebound as bitcoin broke out to new cycle highs. And this was despite the fact that with the Ethereum network’s new proof-of-stake consensus model, it has had less supply issuance than the Bitcoin network. In other words, there’s been a big gap in demand between bitcoin and ether. And Ethereum continues to face diverse competition from other programmable blockchains.

A likely force behind bitcoin’s recent rally is the increasing likelihood that a bitcoin spot ETF will hit the market by Q1 2024. The SEC has lost key legal battles against them, and institutions are increasingly signaling higher odds that approval is on the table. Moreover, there are big and durable network effect advantages that go to whichever ETF provider attracts the most assets under management from the starting gate, and so some institutions will likely have significant marketing budgets to their clients and ecosystems for the asset.

If bitcoin remains above $31k or so within this new breakout range, then that looks pretty healthy. If it falls back into the sub-$31k range, then that’s not great. That’s a pretty useful line in the sand for a trader at the moment.

In general, however, I don’t recommend trying to trade bitcoin. Instead, within the context of a portfolio, I recommend risk-managing it and having reasonable expectations. With the No Limits portfolio, for example, I added bitcoin exposure  in 2020 (back then through GBTC, now through Paxos), I trimmed 20% of the position after it was up 3x in price, and I trimmed another 20% of the position after it was up 5x, and then added a bit back when when it crashed in 2022. This was not out of attempting to time it, but rather just out of managing the position size relative to the rest of the portfolio.

For my cold storage bitcoin, I don’t really plan to touch that for many years, unless my thesis on the asset structurally changes. And I [dollar-cost average](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into it.

## Global Debt Deep Dive Analysis

Global debt levels are an important topic, as some countries have entered fiscal dominance, including Japan most thoroughly, and the United States most recently.

Here is a chart of global debt levels across government, household, and corporate sectors:

![Global Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-global-debt.png)

It should be noted that Japan also has significant foreign exchange reserves and a large sovereign wealth (pension) fund, which makes the government debt ratio a bit less extreme than on the surface, but still very high. Japan has a tremendous amount of financial ammo to prevent their currency from devaluing in a disorderly manner.

More impactfully, Singapore has a sovereign wealth fund that exceeds their total sovereign debt, and so they actually have negative net debt at the state level. Their high levels of gross debt basically represent low-cost financing to acquire those foreign assets and keep their currency from strengthening.

And Norway has a tremendous sovereign wealth fund, which drives their net debt to deeply negative levels.

Other nations have some sovereign funds here and there that are moderate relative to their GDP, but aside from those major outliers, the chart is largely reflective of the reality at the sovereign level. Some countries like the Netherlands also have significant foreign holdings at the private level.

Most countries have actually *reduced* their debt/GDP ratios over the past year. That’s because bondholders got killed nominally in market terms and also in real terms even if they hold to maturity. Countries printed a lot of money and inflated a small portion of their debt away, resulting in significant inflation and nominal GDP growth, with bond bulls taking the brunt of the pain. This is why, despite various macroeconomic concerns, I have been more in the pro-equity camp, at least until rates started reaching pretty significant levels.

The United States is one of the most indebted nations at the sovereign level after one nets out sovereign wealth funds and reserve assets. But then perhaps more importantly, they are also running among the largest fiscal deficits in the world as a percentage of GDP, while also running a structural trade deficit. The fact that our deficit looks like this during high real GDP growth and low unemployment should be sending more alarm bells than it currently is:

![Fiscal Deficit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-fiscal-deficit.png)

While Europe and Japan are slower-growing overall, the weakness of the United States is that it is the most financialized. Our tax base is reliant on ever-higher asset prices, unlike theirs. And our financial system is built around constant trade deficits, unlike theirs. This has been coming back to hit us over the past year:

![U.S. Interest Expense](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/october-2023-newsletter-interest-expense.png)

The United States also has a very unusual relationship with regard to foreign holdings of its sovereign debt, since it is the reserve currency issuer. Many countries have significant dollar-denominated reserves and significant dollar-denominated debts (and most of the debts are not owed to U.S. entities). The stronger the dollar gets relative to other currencies, the more that other countries are prone to sell their Treasuries so that they can get dollars and either service their debts or defend their currencies. And so as long as the dollar remains this strong, the foreign sector isn’t buying Treasuries on net:

![Treasury vs Dollar](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-treasury-vs-dollar.png)

This mechanism plays an eventual role in why the Federal Reserve sometimes has to loosen monetary policy even when they weren’t planning to. The foreign sector owns such a large share of U.S. assets including U.S. Treasuries, that if the dollar gets too strong too quickly, it can result in a liquidation of U.S. debt by the foreign sector in order to get dollars, and the need for the Federal Reserve to step in and support it, which loosens monetary policy and the dollar index.

We are not at that point yet, but it’s important to keep that in mind going forward.

**Emerging Market Debt vs Reserves**

Using data from the Bank for International Settlements, we can zoom in on some of the major emerging markets. This shows foreign exchange reserves as a % of GDP in orange, and debts that are denominated in either dollars or euros as a % of GDP in blue and gray respectively:

![Debt and Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-em-debt-and-reserves.png)

These big EMs are net creditor nations at the sovereign level in aggregate, but not across the board.

Countries like Saudi Arabia and Taiwan have run trade surpluses for a while, and thus have built up a lot of reserves relative to debts. In contrast, many Latin American countries have more debts than reserves, and some other countries like Turkey and Indonesia are in that position as well.

As long as a country is doing fine economically and moving in the right direction, having more debt than reserves doesn’t necessarily represent a crisis, although it does represent a more vulnerable position. So for example, Mexico and Indonesia are in decent shape overall, despite some concerns with these metrics, whereas Turkey and Argentina are in outright currency crisis because their fiscal and/or monetary situations are uncontrolled.

To add a couple caveats to that chart, Saudi Arabia has a huge sovereign wealth fund that if included, would push their total reserves to well over 100% of GDP. China also has a sovereign wealth fund that would bump their numbers up moderately higher. On the other hand, Russia has a meaningful percentage of its reserves frozen, and thus they are inaccessible, although even taking that into account they still have considerable reserves (mainly their gold and yuan holdings) relative to their debt. And it’s worth reminding that the chart is adjusted for GDP, and so for example China would dwarf the other nations in absolute terms, but what tends to matter for a currency is the balance of these factors relative to the size of the economy.

**Case Study: Egypt’s Debt Situation**

If we zoom in on one country having a particularly difficult time, a net debtor nation rather than a net creditor nation, we can use Egypt as an example.

The country has 105 million people, which makes it the most populous Arab country by a large margin. They have one of the larger militaries in the Middle East, and they control the Suez Canal, but they are still a very impoverished country on a per-capita basis. They have beautiful beaches on both the Mediterranean Sea and Red Sea, and of course are known for their historical sites of all types.

After its independence from the United Kingdom in the 1950s, Egypt went through a period of deep socialism with a lot of corruption for the next two decades or so, which substantially impaired its economic growth rate. It then transitioned out of that model, and reinvigorated its growth rate with more market-oriented reforms, but still under the context of a military dictatorship that lasted for three decades and was eventually overthrown in 2011. Since the revolution, they have however returned to de-facto military rule.

Egypt used to have constantly rising tourist arrivals for decades, until their 2011 revolution. Since then, their tourism numbers have been rangebound. Tourism, along with their Suez Canal, are among the primary ways for them to bring in foreign capital. The country as a whole now runs a large and persistent trade deficit:

![Egypt Balance of Trade](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-egypt-balance-of-trade.png)

The current president, el-Sisi, has been the country’s president since 2014. Under his rein, the country has embarked on a number of government-run infrastructure mega-projects, including major new highways and railways, and the construction of an entire new capital city outside of Cairo that they plan to bring most of the government and military headquarters to, along with some percentage of the civilian population that choose to buy property there. This city includes the [Iconic Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iconic_Tower_(Egypt)), which is now the tallest building in Africa. However, these mega-projects rely on a lot of external financing, much of which is in U.S. dollars. Here is their external debt, which is over $160 billion:

![Egypt External Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-egypt-external-debt.png)

This debt growth was already a problem, but with the Fed aggressively tightening up their monetary policy in these past two years to strengthen the dollar vs foreign currencies, it serves as a catalyst that threatens to break any country that has gotten over its skis. And Egypt has gotten over its skis.

The country’s local currency broad money supply (the Egyptian pound) grows at a 20% annualized rate. So, everyone who earns wages in this currency has to try to get a 20% raise each year to avoid being diluted. The same is true for landlords and small businesses- if they are not aggressive at raising prices, they get diluted, and basically get poorer in international purchasing power (dollar) terms constantly. The same is true for their savings, and many of the working class operate primarily in cash markets, meaning they don’t have interest-bearing deposits like the higher classes do.

![Egypt M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-egypt-m2.png)

The country’s foreign exchange reserves are low relative to their external debt, at only $35 billion:

![Egypt FX Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-egypt-reserves.png)

During the 2011 revolution, they aggressively used their reserves to defend their currency. Starting in 2016, they engineered a currency devaluation by increasing their reserves. In the past few years, they have been undergoing another currency devaluation, but this time with no reserve accumulation.

Here’s the official exchange rate:

![EGPUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-egpusd.png)

Currently, Egypt’s central bank is holding a 31 EGP-to-USD peg, which is unsustainable. They are relying on their reserves and external financial support from Gulf nations to support it. And one of the conditions from the IMF in order to refinance part of their external debt, is for them to stop pegging their currency and to devalue it. On the street market, it trades for 47-to-1, which is up dramatically in recent months and heavily implies an upcoming devaluation.

However, when Egypt does let the peg go, it’ll likely create another round of inflation, which will be unpopular among the people. And so, the government is likely trying to keep the peg in place through December 2023, which is when they hold their elections. In Q1 2024 is when we should probably expect the next official devaluation.

The challenge here is that what Egypt is primarily using the debt for (infrastructure and new cities) doesn’t immediately generate much dollar cash flow or improve the trade deficit. Instead, these projects can relieve city congestion and boost economic productivity, but only if they encourage the development of, or are paired with, things that directly bring in cash or that reduce the need for imports (more tourists or more manufacturing, basically). And that second part is not really materializing. Tourism is rebounding decently but insufficiently, and manufacturing remains weak.

And from the perspective of an everyday Egyptian, they are stuck in a constant trap of currency dilution. At the end of the day, this external debt and the resulting rounds of currency devaluation come out of their savings and future earnings. In U.S. dollar terms, many Egyptians are going backwards in recent years, and thus anything they need to import is getting more expensive, and their ability to travel abroad is diminishing. This is somewhat (but insufficiently) offset by better local infrastructure development.

And this is true for a lot of the weaker emerging markets; the ones that have structural trade deficits, and high external debt. I focused a lot on this topic in my book, [*Broken Money*](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG83QBJ6).

Egypt is among the most troubled among developing countries at the moment, and they have a high risk of entering a more sustained period of runaway inflation, much like what Turkey has been facing in recent years. However, Egypt has only 40% of the GDP per capita as Turkey, so it’s more of a hardship for most people to go through that scenario than Turkey, since it’s from a position of less wealth and development to begin with. I am very bearish on the Egyptian pound relative to its current 31-to-1 official peg.

**Overall Takeaways**

I plan to cover some additional international metrics in the next premium report as well, but overall I have a constructive long-term investment outlook on a number of emerging market countries including India, Brazil, most net energy producers, and a number of other southeast Asian countries (e.g. Malaysia, Thailand, Indonesia).

For countries going through crisis, there are still ways to invest, although not as passively. For example, it’s possible to buy real estate in Egypt with a 7-year payment term, and without too big of a price/interest difference compared to just buying the property in cash. Especially for someone that makes their income in harder external currencies, this basically represents a 7-year short of the Egyptian pound, assuming that one is indeed interested in owning Egyptian property with all of the benefits and risks that can come with that.

Mainly, I study these metrics for a macro context. The fact that the majority of the big BRICS countries have more reserves than hard currency debt, helps them withstand a strong dollar environment. Brazil, Russia, and China are all running trade surpluses at the moment, and have more reserves than debt. India tends to run trade deficits, but has very rapid economic growth and has more reserves than debt. South Africa is the weakest one financially of the original BRICS nations. And then when we consider their various oil-producing allies in the Middle East, most of those are net trade surplus nations with reserves that significantly exceed their debts too.

Overall, it’s a barbell situation. The United States itself, which is the source of a significant percentage of the world’s total trade deficit and fiscal deficit, is entering what I would argue is a quiet fiscal crisis, although they are doing their best to sustain it for now. And then many net producer nations in the middle with either net creditor status or net trade surplus status are muddling through, doing decently. And then it’s generally the weaker net debtor developing countries of the world that are breaking under the recent dollar strength as the Fed attempts to sustain their own unsustainable U.S. fiscal situation.

## Final Thoughts: Keep an Eye on Gold

For the defensive portion of a portfolio, I continue to like short-term Treasuries, medium-duration inflation-protected Treasuries, and gold.

Gold touched over $2k/oz on Friday. If it can break out to new highs of $2,070 or above, then I think it can run higher for several hundred dollars with a rather sustained move. Gold historically does well more often than not at the top of a Federal Reserve tightening cycle. It has a cup-and-handle price pattern that keeps pushing for a breakout.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-gold-chart.png)

Gold has already broken out to new highs in most other currencies, including the euro, yen, UK pound sterling, Canadian dollar, Chinese yuan, and most emerging market currencies. In fact it kind of looks like you’d expect it to look in those currencies, given all that’s happened in recent years:

![Gold International](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-gold-international.png)

The U.S. dollar and Swiss franc are among the last holdouts to hold the line against a gold price breakout, due to how strong those currencies have been relative to others. My base case is to see gold break out to new highs over the next year, but of course this is a very challenging macroeconomic situation to navigate, and so I prefer pairing gold with T-bills and TIPS for diversification in the defensive portion of my holdings.

Both stocks and copper peaked relative to gold in late 2021, and have been on a downward path relative to gold in the two years since, with no clear sign of reversal yet. They both still look heavy:

![Gold vs Stocks and Copper Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-gold-vs-stocks-copper-annotated.png)
