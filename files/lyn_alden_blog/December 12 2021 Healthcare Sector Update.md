## Macro View

**Inflation Update**

A new consumer price index reading came out on Friday, showing a 6.8% year-over-year official inflation print.

![CPI vs Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-cpi-vs-rates.png)

This is the highest official inflation reading in 39 years. And if CPI was measured similarly today to how it was decades ago (i.e. directly including house prices and treating hedonic adjustments in the same way), it would be in the double digits similar to the 1970s.

Perhaps more interestingly, the spread between official CPI and the 10-year Treasury rate is at the lowest level since 1951, at well below -5%. Traders can make a lot of money if they get in and out of long-duration Treasuries at the right times, but the underlying asset is losing purchasing power in this environment. The same is true for short-duration Treasuries and bank cash. They still have their uses of course (liquidity and volatility reduction), but it’s a rough environment for investors who have been calling for disinflation and have been overweight those types of paper assets.

The rent and owner’s equivalent rent portions of the CPI basket are still in an upswing, on a sizable lag to home prices:

![Rent CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-rent-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Apartment rents from major apartment marketplaces are currently showing approximately 18% year-over-year rent increases, but with a local topping pattern currently in place for the moment.

![Apartment Rent](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-apartment-rent.png)

*Chart Source: [Apartment List](https://www.apartmentlist.com/research/national-rent-data)*

From base effects in the CPI calculation, there’s a decent chance that we’ll see a local top in official CPI sometime in Q1 2022 within the 7-9% year-over-year range. As we look later into 2022 beyond that point, the future is less clear and depends on certain variables.

For one, fiscal policy is a key variable. I’m assuming that some version of the Build Back Better plan will pass either this year or next year prior to 2022 mid-term elections, although it’s not certain because the US Senate has a tight political split. That bill has significant implications for both nominal GDP growth and deficits over a multi-year period, but it’s not as “fast-acting” as the stimulus checks and other aspects of recent stimulus. It does have some fast-acting components though, like the continuation of child tax credits.

Secondly, the likelihood and magnitude of a potential wage-price spiral remains unclear. A wage-price spiral describes a scenario where prices go up for some reason (let’s say stimulus or some resource constraint), and so employees demand higher wages, which leads employers to charge even higher prices to consumers to avoid margin compression, which leads employees to demand even higher wages to keep up, and so forth in a rather self-sustaining loop for a while.

According to the Atlanta Fed, US wage growth is now at a 14-year high. Wage growth did not collapse in this cycle like they did after the prior two recessions:

![Wage Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-wage-growth.png)

*Chart Source: [Atlanta Fed](https://www.atlantafed.org/chcs/wage-growth-tracker)*

A 4.3% average year-over-year wage increase seems like a lot, until one remembers that year-over-year official price inflation was 6.8% and year-over-year monetary inflation was 13%.

So, just going from the official CPI numbers, the average worker got a 2.5% *pay cut* this year rather than a raise, in terms of purchasing power.

The median price of a house in the US went up 20% year-over-year. So, a typical worker got a 4.8% raise, and the typical house went up 20%. Mortgage rates also went up a bit over the past year. As a result, the typical wage buys a lot less house than it did a year ago. Meanwhile, ground beef is up 17% year-over-year, and bacon is up 26%. Used car prices have skyrocketed 25-50% or more over the past year as many new vehicle models are facing lengthy delays.

No wonder consumer sentiment is not particularly high at the moment, and no wonder the labor market is so tight. Employers on average are trying to attract workers to fill and retain positions with… pay cuts in inflation-adjusted terms.

This suggests that pressures for wage growth are likely to be substantial for a while. If they want a less acute truck driver shortage, for example, they’re probably going to have to pay more. The trucking industry [has no shortage of people willing to get into the job](https://time.com/6116853/truck-driver-shortage-supply-chain/), but retention is extremely low as the compensation for many subsets of the industry is inadequate relative to the working conditions.

> Turnover was unchanged at large truckload fleets to close 2020. The annualized turnover rate for over-the-road truckload drivers held steady the last three months of 2020, according to the American Trucking Associations’ (ATA) Quarterly Employment Report.
>
> In the fourth quarter, the turnover rate for truckload fleets with more than $30 million in annual revenue was unchanged at a 92% annualized rate. The churn rate for smaller truckload carriers dipped two percentage points to 72%.
>
> –[CCJ, March 2021](https://www.ccjdigital.com/economic-trends/article/15064753/driver-turnover-rate-holding-steady)

The truck driver shortage has been a problem in the US and Europe for years now, and remains an ongoing issue. Here’s a sampling of recent news:

![Truck Driver Shortage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-truck-driver-shortage.png)

And yet, paying higher wages for truck drivers or personnel in other industries means that businesses either take a hit to their profit margins, or pass their costs onto consumers, which can necessitate more wage increases among market participants to keep up. That’s why it is called a wage-price spiral.

The trends of globalization and automation have suppressed wage growth for quite a while, but that strategy is currently running into headwinds with such an efficient-but-not-resilient global supply chain system. Automation is obviously advancing still, but globalization may have reached a peak for a while, with renewed political interest in re-shoring or near-shoring supply chains to some extent. If that trend of globalization indeed stabilizes or reverses rather than keeps increasing, that’s an inflationary variable.

**Federal Reserve Policy Update**

The Federal Reserve began a monetary tightening cycle in November 2021 and recently indicated plans to accelerate it. We’ll know more about their plans after their next meeting on December 15th.

There are some analysts who say that the Fed will be able to normalize policy (which would mean persistently positive real rates), while others believe the Fed can never raise rates at all. I think both of those extremes are likely incorrect.

The Fed can indeed tighten policy for a period of time, but their chances of normalizing policy within the next several years is very low.

With consumer price inflation currently at 6.8%, it would take roughly 7% short-term interest rates to achieve positive real rates. The Taylor Rule, which is a model that suggests what the Fed’s short-term interest rate should be based on inflation and GDP growth, currently says the Fed’s rate should be at 7.5%. It’s currently at 0%.

![Taylor Rule](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-taylor-rule.png)

*Chart Source: [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2021-12-09/markets-overestimate-a-powell-pivot-on-inflation-to-prop-up-growth-assets)*

The reason the Fed can’t normalize policy towards persistent real rates is that with the US about to reach $30 trillion in federal debt (about 125% of GDP), reaching the Taylor Rule interest rate level and holding that for a period of time would result in the US federal government’s interest expense approaching $2 trillion per year as more and more bonds mature and get refinanced at those higher rates. That’s about half as much as the federal government brings in with tax revenue in a given year.

At $30 trillion in federal debt, for every 100 basis points the weighted-average interest rate is on US government debt, total interest expense increases by about $300 billion. So if weighted-average rates reach a bit over 3% (a more moderate example), then that’s $1 trillion per year in interest expense.

In a positive real rate scenario, corporate bond rates and mortgage rates would go a lot higher as well. That would damage the stock market and housing market, which would reduce US federal tax receipts and therefore probably widen the federal deficit further. The junk bond market would probably freeze like it did in Q4 2018.

That’s part of what makes the 2020s less like the 1970s and more like the 1940s; debt was too high in the 1940s, and is too high in the 2020s, to support persistently positive real rates. Policymakers painted themselves into a corner over the course of decades by this point. In the 1970s, debt as a percentage of GDP was very low, so they could boost interest rates to double digits if need be in order to quell inflation.

But when analysts say “the Fed can’t raise rates at all due to high debt levels”, I don’t necessarily agree with that. A few 0.25% rate hikes don’t meaningfully impact US interest expense. Only if rates start getting above 2% would that start to be the limiting factor. Instead, there are other things to watch for.

I do think the Fed is going to try to tighten policy here until either 1) price inflation cools off in rate-of-change terms or 2) the junk credit market breaks like it did in Q4 2018 or 3) the Treasury market breaks like it did in Q1 2020 or 4) some similar level of market dysfunction. And they already have new standing repo facilities in place for if/when that third option happens.

Ever since the global financial crisis, repo activity has been a sign than there are not enough bank reserves in the system and that the Fed is about to do QE, while reverse repo activity has been a sign that there are excess bank reserves in the system that the Fed is about to halt QE for a period of time. The sample size is not big but the logic makes sense; it has to do with how much liquidity is in the system relative to T-bill collateral. And right now, there is a lot of reverse repo activity.

This chart shows the percentage of large bank assets that consist of cash in blue, and reverse repo activity in red:

![Cash vs Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-cash-vs-reverse-repo.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This second chart makes it more clear. It’s the Fed’s balance sheet in blue and reverse repo activity in red:

![Balance Sheet vs Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-balance-sheet-vs-reverse-repo.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

With high inflation, labor shortages, and record reverse repo activity, the Fed’s mandate here is clearly to try to tighten policy. How far they get remains uncertain, and it’s something I’ll be monitoring signs for as they progress.

They’ll be tightening from a starting point of being well behind the curve. Historically, their short-term rate is normally higher than inflation but ever since the global financial crisis, their short term rate has spent most of its time below the inflation rate, and is currently the furthest below inflation that it has been in modern history:

![CPI vs FFR](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-cpi-vs-ffr.png)

So, they will indeed likely be tightening a bit until something leads them to stop, but in absolute terms they remain in a financial repression scenario with interest rates held well below the prevailing inflation rate, due to so much debt being in the system. The only comparable environment we have for that to learn lessons from in the United States is the 1940s war era.

**The Stock Market and Stimulus Correlation**

Ever since the global financial crisis, US stocks have been very reliant on persistent stimulus, either monetary or fiscal, in order to keep going up.

This chart is cluttered but quite important in my opinion. It shows the Wilshire 5000 price index (the closest proxy for the broad US stock market) and the Fed’s balance sheet. I’ll walk through it in the following paragraphs:

![Stimulus Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-stimulus-annotated.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed, annotated by Lyn Alden*

In 2010 (first green box), the stock market had partially rebounded from its 2009 depths. The Fed had performed emergency quantitative easing (QE1) during the crisis, but had not been actively stimulating recently. The Fed’s balance sheet went sideways, and the stock market chopped along sideways as well. Then in late 2010 (the end of the first green box), the Fed announced QE2 and both the Fed balance sheet and the stock market began going up together.

From late-2011 to 2012 (second green box), the Fed held their balance sheet flat and the stock market chopped along sideways again. Then after that green box, they did QE3 from 2013 to 2014 to expand their balance sheet a lot, which coincided with another bull market period.

From 2015 to 2016 (third green box), the Fed once again held their balance sheet flat and the stock market chopped along sideways again. This was a bigger deal because the dollar strengthened, and several emerging markets including Brazil in particular entered very serious recessions. The price of oil crashed and remained down for years as global growth slowed. The US experienced an industrial recession (which was not quite big enough to cause a full recession), in large part because of how weak the energy sector was, which cascaded into weakness in manufacturing to supply the energy sector.

The year 2017 (purple circle) was an exception. The Fed continued to hold their balance sheet flat and began gradually raising interest rates, but Donald Trump was elected to be president in November 2016 and was sworn in to office in early 2017, with a Republican majority in both houses of Congress. The stock market quickly began pricing in tax cuts including corporate tax cuts in particular, which indeed materialized a year later. This was a form of fiscal stimulus; tax cuts without associated spending cuts, resulting in a bigger fiscal deficit, which was good for the stock market. Corporations were able to repatriate hundreds of billions of dollars from overseas and use the majority of it for share repurchases, which was great for stock prices but didn’t meaningfully impact GDP growth or employment.

From 2018 through much of 2019 (fourth green box), there wasn’t any more fiscal stimulus coming down the path. The Fed continued raising rates but also began performing quantitative tightening, which means shrinking their balance sheet. The stock market once again entered a very choppy sideways period. By 2019 it was clear that the global economy was slowing, the US yield curve inverted, and the Fed slightly reduced interest rates.

From late 2019 onward, the Fed has been performing massive monetary stimulus. This was initially triggered by the repo rate spike in September 2019, but then was kicked into high gear by the COVID-19 global lockdowns to address the illiquid Treasury market. The largest fiscal stimulus and monetary stimulus in modern history occurred together, and this was massively beneficial to the stock market.

With fiscal stimulus winding down, and the Fed now tightening, the base case would be for a volatile market again as we enter another probable “green box” in 2022. Equities have been very reliant on these ongoing stimulus efforts. Plus, equity valuations are much higher now, and 2021 already had record inflows into equity at a scale that exceeded the prior twenty years combined. Everyone piled into equities. It *could* be different this time, but that’s not the assumption I would go with. The caveat is that fiscal stimulus from a Build Back Better bill might serve as another purple circle, but that remains to be seen, and much of that money is not very fast-acting like stimulus checks or corporate tax cuts are.

So, I am looking for a defensive posture around the margins.

**China Policy and Stock Tactics**

Chinese stocks have performed very poorly this year; much worse than my base case expectation was. This is despite the fact that the CNY strengthened vs the USD in 2021.

My thesis from 2019-2020 as I entered the positions was that I was relatively bullish on Chinese equities, but wanted to avoid the bubbly Chinese real estate sector which is where the bulk of their debt is. Therefore, the Chinese internet companies were my preference, with cautious position sizes. It was a way to have exposure to the non-real-estate part of the Chinese economy.

This thesis made sense, but reality doesn’t always match expectations. What the thesis didn’t allow for was that the CCP would have a record crackdown in 2021 specifically on those internet companies. They busted anti-trust behavior, aggressively went after excess video game usage, stopped IPOs, and made the big companies commit to paying billions of dollars into the rest of the economy, which can be thought of as massive shadow fines. The stocks subsequently fell to record-low valuations as many western investors put them into the “uninvestable” pile.

I remain rather bullish on Chinese internet stocks from these beaten-down levels with cautious position sizes (risks are elevated, but negative sentiment and low valuations are more in their favor). However, here in December, they do make good tax-loss harvesting opportunities to counter some taxable gains elsewhere. When life gives lemons, one should at least make lemonade.

Plus, some of these stocks now have increased de-listing risk from US exchanges. That itself shouldn’t be a major issue in the long run (Tencent for example only trades over-the-counter in the US anyway, and that has never been a valuation problem for it relative to Alibaba or JD which do trade on major US exchanges), but it can be inconvenient if/when delisting happens.

Therefore, there is a good case to sell some of the individual Chinese securities and replace them with a Chinese ETF such as MCHI, or a broad emerging market ETF (which have 30-40% exposure to China). The ETFs tend to hold Chinese stocks on the Hong Kong exchange, rather than the American ADRs. This switch would lock in the capital gains losses which is beneficial to do before the calendar year ends for tax season, while avoiding a wash sale, and while maintaining the overall theme.

Julien Bittel of Stouff Capital shared a useful chart the other day that quickly summarizes the current bull case, which looks similar to the 2018-2019 bull case:

I would add that China’s 20th National Congress, which occurs every five years, is scheduled for late 2022. This is where President Xi Jinping is expected to cement his third term with no cap on how long he may rule, which is unprecedented in modern Chinese political history and shifts the country more firmly towards authoritarianism.

Xi’s crackdowns on the country’s internet stocks throughout 2021, along with crackdowns on the country’s overleveraged property sector (China’s longest-running well-known financial problem) stabilized some things but also reduced competing sources of power in the country. However, it seems unlikely that Xi would want a decelerating economy in 2022 during the lead-up to his congress. To the extent that he can control it (which is quite a bit), he would want the hard parts to be long over with by that point, and for the economy to be improving in rate-of-change terms.

Plus, the Winter Olympics will be held in China in February 2022, and the year will likely be a big one for the launch of China’s central bank digital currency (“digital renminbi” or “e-CNY”), which they have been testing for years.

My base case is that 2022 and perhaps the following years will likely be decent years for Chinese equity performance. If 2021 was the CCP’s “bad cop” year, then 2022 is more likely to be their “good cop” year, to put it roughly.

The big looming risk if we look out into 2023 and beyond is a possible Chinese invasion of Taiwan. That’s a situation I’ll be monitoring. And if that occurs, it’s not just a risk for Chinese stocks; it’s a risk for global supply chains and any international business that interfaces with China.

For example, it was [recently reported on](https://arstechnica.com/gadgets/2021/12/report-apple-ceo-tim-cook-engineered-a-secret-275-billion-deal-with-china/) that Apple made a $275 billion “secret” deal with China in 2016, where Apple apparently managed to avoid the full brunt of China’s regulatory heat for the subsequent years. Tim Cook reportedly made repeated visits to China during that period as part of the negotiations.

I think a lot of investors underestimate how intertwined S&P 500 performance is with China, both in terms of selling products to Chinese consumers, and of course having many of their products made by Chinese technicians. A lot of people say they don’t invest in Chinese stocks for ethical reasons or regulatory risk reasons, but then they invest in Apple stock where the CEO personally makes deals with CCP leadership and the company had phones made in Chinese facilities that were surrounded by suicide nets due to harsh working conditions.

Apple is the second largest holding in the Vanguard ESG US Stock ETF, which means it screened well for their environmental, social, and governance criteria. Many American stocks are in that situation besides just Apple, where they are extremely reliant on China and yet investors happily go maximum long them and consider them to be ESG-friendly.

As part of a diversified portfolio, I am rather bullish on Chinese equities in 2022. However, it is by no means assured of course, and the thesis may not work out. There is a lot of risk in equity markets around the world including US equities for 2022.

China seemingly got a lot of their economic pain out of the way in 2021 whereas for the US, 2021 was a big pro-stimulatory year. For 2022, things are looking a bit tighter for the US and a bit looser for China. I still wouldn’t get into Chinese real estate though.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin had a big leverage liquidation event and has been in a substantial local downtrend.

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-12-12-btc-chart.png)

The good news is that offshore trading leverage was greatly reduced due to this bearish liquidation, and overall sentiment is now very poor which tends to be a decent contrarian indicator. Coins continue to move away from exchanges and into cold storage, which tightens available supply.

The bad news is that until the price starts putting in some higher-highs, and until the spent output profit ratio “SOPR” goes firmly back above 1 (i.e. people stop selling for a loss), it’s still quite possible that this correction has deeper to go. There have been no particularly compelling signs that a bottom is in place yet. It certainly might be, and some signs are starting to imply it, but that would still be guessing at this point.

![Bitcoin Adjusted SOPR](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-12-bitcoin-asopr.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

Most on-chain indicators still look decent for bitcoin’s price in the intermediate term. However, they haven’t shown strong support yet, and combined with the Fed monetary tightening, there is certainly a tactical bear case to be made against the bull case. I’m constructive overall.

At these price levels, long-term holders appear unwilling to sell their coins. This chart shows the bitcoin price in blue vs the percentage of on-chain coins that haven’t moved in over a year in yellow:

![Bitcoin HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-12-bitcoin-hodl-wave-annotated.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/1-year-hodl-wave/)*

During raging bull markets when longer-term holders are up 5x or 10x or 20x on their cost basis and thus have life-changing money, some of them do start selling into strength as new buyers enter the market. However, during consolidations and bear markets, those longer-term holders stop selling in aggregate, and indeed start accumulating again, which brings coins back into stronger hands and reduces the liquid supply of coins.

We can see that the 2013 bull market was a “double pump”. There was a false top, a long consolidation, and then a much higher top. I put a deep red dot on the chart showing that long-term holders stopped selling in aggregate during that 2013 consolidation (which basically tightened the liquid supply in a finite asset), and then continued selling down to the deep green dot into the second leg of the bull run and after.

In contrast, the 2017 bull market was a smoother run, with shorter corrections. There was no pause in long-term holders selling, i.e. no deep red dot on the HODL wave. They just kept selling into strength until the deep green dot when the cycle finally exhausted itself.

The question here in late 2021 is whether this is a pause for a higher price point, or if that was the top of the cycle and that bitcoin is now firmly in a consolidation phase. The overall distribution cycle was muted so far; the price gains have not been enough to convince many long-term holders to sell much. My base case overall is that up-and-down cycles will likely be smaller in the future because the 4-year supply halving is less impactful and it’s a more institutional asset, but it’s unclear whether this particular cycle still has higher to go or not. I lean towards that it does.

Long-term holders are generally not selling, many North American miners are choosing to hold the coins they create rather than sell them to the market, 90% of the 21 million total bitcoins that will eventually exist have already been mined, and bitcoins are structurally leaving exchanges (which never happened in prior cycles). So there is a certain degree of tightness to this market that remains for now, and potentially sets up the next leg up in price.

## Smart Contract Note

When bitcoin sharply corrected over the past few weeks, it was a bit surprising because altcoins initially held up about equal or better than bitcoin. Usually in down moves, bitcoin holds up better than other coins. There were various arguments as to why that might be the case, with probably the most compelling reason being that bitcoin’s 2021 performance was largely driven by institutions and large investors while other coins were mostly driven by retail investors, and those institutional investors have been more prone to risk-off behavior from Omicron and Fed tightening in this environment.

However, in the subsequent week or so, many of the altcoins have been weaker than bitcoin, almost like a delayed risk-off reaction. It’s still unclear how this correction will shake out.

In other news, BadgerDAO, a DeFi application that runs on Ethereum, had $120 million in funds stolen from an exploit, adding to a long list of DeFi exploits. Smart contracts in general are basically making experimental alpha code in public, which is of course prone to attack. In addition, Solana which is also still technically in development, was hit by [another DDoS attack](https://www.coindesk.com/tech/2021/12/10/solana-validators-engineers-grapple-with-blockchain-slowdown-on-public-call/).

Ethereum held up better than most other coins in this particular correction, despite having a variety of technical issues related to scaling, transaction ordering, bugs, DeFi exploits, and other issues that I’ve been covering over time. Plus, there are ongoing delays of the Ethereum 2.0 merge.

The reason I have been tactically bullish despite these issues is because of the one-way Ethereum 2.0 smart contract lockup. Ether tokens are flowing out of exchanges faster than bitcoins, in part because they are going into a lock-up contract that will only be released when the merger with the Ethereum 2.0 chain is complete. As I’ve been describing it, it’s a well-engineered supply squeeze. There are now 8.6 million ether locked up:

![Ethereum Staked](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-12-ethereum-staked.png)

*Chart Source: Glassnode*

The flip side of that is that I’d be quite concerned about ether’s price when those 8.6 million locked-up coins are released for trading. It could be 10 million or more coins by then, coming back to the market around the same time from a very illiquid state and with big unrealized profits. It’s unclear if that merge will happen in 2022 or not.

Basically, there are five potential catalysts for the next Ethereum bear market. The first could be a “sell the news” event on the Ethereum 2.0 merge where those coins are unlocked, ending the supply squeeze and bringing a lot of illiquid coins back to a state of liquidity. The second could be some sort of admission that the Ethereum 2.0 merge is not going well and that those coins end up getting released back onto Ethereum 1.0 instead. The third could be a big crackdown by the SEC which could delist many blockchains, including Ethereum or many coins that run on Ethereum, from US crypto exchanges due to being labeled unregistered securities. The fourth could be some other technical issue that messes up Ethereum’s blockchain. The fifth could be a broad and persistent cryptocurrency bear market, which we might or might not be in now. NFTs would likely be particularly vulnerable in that sort of downtrend due to their lack of liquidity.

For those reasons, I consider Ethereum to be more of a speculation, and quite possibly an unregistered security. It was clearly an unregistered security when it was issued but the question is whether the SEC still considers it a security or not (the previous SEC chairman did not while the current SEC chairman is more deliberately unclear on that point). Ethereum has a large network effect and a lot of experimentation, but so far the ecosystem has been a rolling series of speculative bubbles in DeFi, then NFTs, then DAOs, along with many technical challenges while more centralized competitors nip at its heels.

I think all of those categories (DeFi, NFTs, and DAOs) are interesting over the long run, but not particularly in their current speculative forms. I tend to agree with Michael Ippolito, the co-founder of Blockworks, who proposed that speculative projects in a given crypto cycle tend to be crappy for their first iteration but give us insights about what may actually be useful in following cycles.

Unlike for Bitcoin, I have no particular long-term view on Ethereum other than making sure investors are aware of the risks and technical issues, while also sharing the factors that seem to affect price like this one-way Ethereum 2.0 lockup contract.

**Stablecoin and Crypto Regulatory Changes**

It seems that a theme shaping up for 2022 and/or 2023 is one about more US regulatory enforcement of securities laws as it pertains to the crypto ecosystem. There have been shots across the bow in 2021 but nothing particularly big.

The US Treasury and other groups in the US government released a [report on stablecoins](https://home.treasury.gov/system/files/136/StableCoinReport_Nov1_508.pdf) last month which made the recommendation that stablecoins should be issued by actual banks and should be regulated in line with KYC AML standards. In other words, the government wants to make sure stablecoins have appropriate collateral and that their transactions can be censored or blocked as desired.

An issue with stablecoins since their creation is that their collateral is often inappropriate. They have assets that are subject to credit risk but are not necessarily managing their asset-to-liability ratio with that credit risk in mind.

Take a bank for example. Their liabilities are primarily customer deposits. Their assets include nominally risk-free things like Treasuries and cash, but then also things that have various risks such as corporate bonds and bank loans. Those riskier assets pay higher yields but have some percentage of credit loss each year, and during recessions tend to have extra losses. So, the bank needs to ensure that it doesn’t just have a 100:100 assets-to-liabilities ratio. Instead, it needs more like a 110:100 assets-to-liabilities ratio.

If a bank had a 100:100 assets-to-liabilities ratio, then any loss at all would immediately render them insolvent, meaning their liabilities exceed their assets. So, a bank needs to be able to lose maybe 5-10% of its assets in a bad recession and still have assets higher than its liabilities.

Stablecoin providers often attest to having about the same amount of assets as their token liabilities, rather than something like 110% assets to liabilities. They wouldn’t need a capital buffer if 100% of their assets were in nominally risk-free Treasuries and bank deposits, but when you look at the independent accounting firms’ attestations of stablecoin providers like Tether, they often consist of a mix of assets including Treasuries, cash, corporate bonds, loans, and a little bit of precious metals and crypto tokens. In other words they have an asset risk profile that looks kind of like a bank with a range of risk, but without the big capital buffer that banks use, and without access to a central bank.

The second-biggest stablecoin USDC has been moving towards an all cash and Treasury model over the past few months, because they see that the writing is on the wall. The recent Treasury report stated that stablecoin issuers should be banks. The details of the regulation could take a few forms by the time the dust settles but the general direction of policy is that USD stablecoins used by most entities in the US will need to be regulated like banks, and either back their assets with the right type of nominally risk-free collateral or alternatively the right type of capital buffer for collateral that has credit risk.

In addition, Gary Gensler, chairman of the SEC, has said numerous times including recently that many digital assets are securities, because they had an ICO and pass the Howey Test. This doesn’t include Bitcoin, and most likely doesn’t include things like Litecoin or Bitcoin Cash or Dogecoin and so forth (despite the other issues with those coins). But it does include many tokens that performed initial coin offerings, such as many of the smart contract blockchains and many of the protocol tokens that run on those blockchains.

To the extent that investors do speculate with cryptocurrencies outside of Bitcoin, I would recommend prudent position sizing to minimize these sorts of regulatory risks.

## Health Care Stocks Update

The healthcare sector is a relatively attractive place in this environment. The valuations are lower than normal, and the sector is rather resistant to economic cycles.

Instead, the big risk to the performance of the healthcare sector is regulation, since government/taxpayer money is responsible for a large percentage of the purchases from the industry, and that has been a hot topic during the second half of 2021. House Democrats have been interested in passing legislation that would reduce drug producers’ ability to raise prices, and to enhance Medicare’s ability to negotiate for lower prices. This might be included in the Build Back Better bill that is being prioritized by the Biden Administration.

However, given the realities of the Senate (a 50-50 split of Democrats vs Republicans, including some Democrats who identify as moderates and generally oppose sweeping changes), and given current polls and approval ratings that point towards Republican strength in 2022 elections, and given the amount of lobbying done by the healthcare industry, it appears increasingly less likely that sweeping healthcare reform will pass anytime in the next couple election cycles. The version of the bill that can get through the Senate makes some changes to the government’s relationship with the industry but is more mild in terms of changes than the House version.

Mainly, certain long-established drugs may be unable to raise prices faster than CPI, and Medicare beneficiary out-of-pocket expenses towards prescription drugs would have caps.

Beyond that, over the long arc of time it’s a valuation issue. Over any given 1-year or even 3-year period stock prices can wildly fluctuate from company fundamentals, but eventually fundamentals drag prices along the way. It’s like a person walking a dog on a leash; the dog might take a more erratic approach to the destination as it goes back and forth compared to the person, but ultimately the dog winds up at the same destination.

Cigna (CI) for example trades at a price/earnings/growth “PEG” ratio of less than 1x. Currently it’s in an undervalued “buy zone” at a time when most people are commenting about how expensive the broader S&P 500 is:

## **Final Thoughts**

I have been covering the long-term debt cycle for a few years now. My two most comprehensive articles on it are [here](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/) and [here](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/).

The main point of that theme is that because sovereign debt levels are so high (similar to the 194os in the US or the 1920s-1930s in Europe), we are in a lengthy period of financial repression where interest rates will spend most of their time below the prevailing inflation rate, much like the 1940s. Cash holders and bond holders will likely continue to get devalued in terms of purchasing power going forward.

![Long-Term Debt Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-longterm-debt-cycle.png)

![Policy Response](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-policy-response.png)*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Some people mistake that to mean that the Fed can never tighten their policy at all, but that’s not really the case. It’s all a matter of degree. If year-over-year CPI growth is 6.8% and nominal GDP is growing quickly, they can indeed tighten a bit and maybe get off zero on interest rates, while still remaining far behind what would otherwise be appropriate monetary policy.

The Fed has two official mandates and then basically a third shadow mandate that takes precedence over those. The first two mandates are to maximize employment and to maintain stable prices. The third shadow mandate is to maintain financial stability (e.g. if the Treasury market goes illiquid like in March 2020, or the repo market blows up overnight like in September 2019, or the junk bond market freezes for six weeks like in Q4 2018, that type of problem quickly becomes their #1 priority to address).

Right now, their mandates are not directly conflicting, which is why they can tighten to some extent. The labor market is tight despite the fact that labor participation remains low, and there’s really nothing the Fed can do about low labor participation (that’s more of a structural offshoring, automation, demographics, skills mismatch, fiscal policy, and wage issue). Meanwhile, year-over-year CPI is running at 39-year highs. Financial markets are stable enough, and the Fed has standing facilities in place in case the Treasury market goes illiquid again. Their mandate here is to try to address the high inflation.

It’s likely going to happen at some point that the Fed’s mandates directly conflict with each other. This would take the form of either high unemployment or financial instability occurring during a time of high inflation. All of their recent moments of financial instability that forced them to quickly shift dovish (March 2020, September 2019, and Q4 2018) occurred during disinflationary periods. If they were to get a moment like that during an inflationary period where the market expects them to tighten and they have to take emergency dovish action instead to quell an acute financial stability problem, that would likely be an absolutely major shock for the dollar and a variety of assets.

But today is not that day, and the base case for now is a period of relative tightening. They will be far behind the tightening curve of where traditional metrics suggest they should be (because those metrics don’t take into account the debt levels and thus the need for financial repression), but they’ll be less dovish than the past two years, until something forces them to do otherwise.
