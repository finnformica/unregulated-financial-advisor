# Lukoil Deep-Dive Analysis

*Published January 5, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This issue takes a look at the status of the U.S. business cycle, and then explores one of the cheapest and best-performing oil stocks in the world.

## U.S. Business Cycle Status

One of my themes over the past few months is that the U.S. economy is at an inflection point, with high uncertainty for either a potential rebound or continued deceleration.

After a period of economic deceleration through late 2018 and into 2019, followed by a big dovish pivot in the U.S. Federal Reserve’s monetary policy late in 2019, there have been some signs of a potential reversal and economic re-acceleration, but an equal or greater number of signs of continued deceleration and heightened recession risk over the next year.

**Initial Jobless Claims: Breakout or Fakeout?**

The 4-week moving average of weekly initial jobless claims has reached nearly two-year highs. This is the first time during the decade-long economic expansion in the United States that jobless claims have stopped declining for this long, which may represent the start of a bottoming process in unemployment. Here is a 5-year chart:

![Initial Claims Breakout](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-initial-claims-breakout.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Federal Reserve*

This potential employment-bottoming process is supported by fundamental research into S&P 500 companies. Corporate earnings growth in the United States had flat-lined for a few years during the 2013-2017 period, but the big corporate tax cuts that took effect in the beginning of 2018 added a large boost to net profit margins, and corporations used that extra cash (including repatriated cash from overseas) to buy back a record amount of shares and boost earnings per share as much as possible.

The full year 2019 was about stock valuation expansion on top of flat earnings. Corporate earnings have been totally flat for the year, and buybacks have slowed from 2018 highs.

With corporations already largely maxed out on record leverage, and facing very sluggish U.S. GDP growth, the last big lever for them to pull to enhance short-term profitability is labor force reductions. This is a classic end-of-cycle phenomenon.

It’s important to keep watching jobless claims every two weeks or so, which I will do with this research service for now, because it could potentially fall and resume its previous trend. If it doesn’t, we probably have some interesting times ahead.

**Decade Lows in the ISM PMI**

The Institute for Supply Chain Management published their monthly purchasing manger’s index (PMI) survey on Friday, and the results were worse than consensus expectations.

In particular, their manufacturing PMI reached new cycle lows, after a brief bounce:

![January 2020 ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-ism-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Anything under 50 represents a contraction for the manufacturing sector. And it indirectly extends beyond the manufacturing sector; this level of 47.2 for the ISM manufacturing PMI represents an implied overall real U.S. GDP year-over-year growth rate of just 1.3%.

Consensus expectations were that it would continue to bounce back up a bit, to 49 or so.

Most evidence indicates that the 2018/2019 economic slowdown started before the trade dispute between the United States and China began, and that the trade dispute merely fueled it by adding to corporate capital investment uncertainty and higher raw product costs onto an already decelerating economy.

Most financial media, however, focused on the trade dispute as though it was causal, like the only key variable causing the U.S. economy to decelerate, and many analysts expected that a cessation in the trade dispute would result in a quick re-acceleration of growth. So far, this isn’t happening. Stock prices have gone up substantially, but raw U.S. economic data remains sluggish and, in some cases, reaching new cycle lows.

Unless we see a confirmed bounce in U.S. economic data, U.S. equity investment risks remain elevated.

## PJSC Lukoil ADR (LUKOY) Deep Dive Analysis

During the 2000-2010 decade, the S&P 500 energy sector dramatically outperformed the broader S&P 500 index:

![Energy Stocks Outperform](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-energy-outperformance-decade.png)

However, during the 2010-2020 decade, the situation reversed, and energy stocks remained nearly flat with a “lost decade”, particularly during the second half, while the broader S&P 500 soared:

![Energy Sector Underperformance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lost-decade-energy.png)

It has gotten to a point where a single company, Apple, has a larger market capitalization than all energy companies in the S&P 500 combined, which has never happened before.

Until this current decade, the supply and demand balance with oil was usually aligned more with scarcity than abundance. Analysts were worried about “peak oil”, referring to the eventual period where annual oil production would peak and start to decline, even as world oil demand continued to increase, creating a severe shortfall.

New technologies and nearly endless amounts of cheap capital helped solve that for many years, with unprofitable shale oil that was previously inaccessible. With interest rates near zero for the past decade, banks and private equity funds (fueled with pension fund money) have been happy to fund shale growth projects. This resulted in a renaissance of American oil production, and a sudden global oversupply of oil that even OPEC couldn’t mitigate.

For years, however, most of these shale projects have not been free cash flow positive, and shale oil production rates have been lower than initially advertised. They drilled and drilled, but the capex costs consumed most of the cash. It has been great for jobs, but a lousy deal for shareholders, the suppliers of the capital.

Now that liquidity is less abundant, and banks and private equity funds have learned the hard way that shale oil has challenges generating consistent free cash flow at these oil price levels, capital for new drilling has become a bit more scarce.

Shale producer investor presentations from years ago focused heavily on growth rates. These days, they instead advertise the size of their expected capex cuts, and emphasize their various abilities to hopefully generate free cash flow even at low oil prices. Previously it was about how much oil they will produce, and now it is about how little they will produce and instead how much free cash flow they will generate to pay dividends and reduce debt levels. All else being equal, that would help the global supply/demand balance for oil.

However, while U.S. shale oil regions have been the swing producers of the past decade, it is instead some of the international energy majors that interest me the most at current price levels, with fresh capital drying up for new U.S. shale production except for the well-run producers with the best reserves.

**Cheap and Efficient**

Lukoil (ADR ticker: LUKOY) is a Russian oil and gas major that operates more in line with U.S. corporate standards than many of its international counterparts, meaning that they have a very strong focus on maximizing shareholder returns with tight capital allocation policies. Their ADR trades over the counter (OTC), but despite that lack of major U.S. stock exchange representation, the company is very broadly covered by analysts.

Dr. Mark Mobius, the economist and fund manager that pioneered emerging markets investing for the past three decades, had a role in assisting Lukoil’s shareholder-friendly governance policies as a board member. The company focuses more-so on quality metrics than many of its peers, like generating consistently high returns on invested capital (ROIC):

![Lukoil ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoil-roic.png)

As such, Lukoil is one of the best-performing major energy companies over the past 15 years, with dividends reinvested:

![Lukoil Decade Outperformance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-decade-outperformance.png)

You might assume, due to significant outperformance, that Lukoil is overvalued now. However, the opposite is the case. Part of the reason that Lukoil outperforms is that it is consistently undervalued, and investors can reinvest their dividends into more shares at an accelerated rate.

Here is the price-to-sales ratio for various energy majors over the past decade:

![Lukoil Price to Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-ps-ratio.png)

And price-to-book:

![Lukoil Price to Book Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-pb-ratio.png)

And EV/EBITDA, with BP removed due to anomalies related to their Deepwater Horizon disaster that would make the chart unreadable:

![Lukoil EV/EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-ev-ebitda.png)

So the best-performing and consistently cheapest major energy company are one and the same: Lukoil.

**Financial Strength**

Lukoil has a very strong balance sheet, with considerable cash, relatively little debt relative to their larger scale, a smooth debt maturation profile, and investment-grade credit ratings.

![Lukoil Debt Slides](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-debt.png)

*Source: Lukoil 3Q 2019 Earnings Call Slides*

Based on debt/fundamental metrics alone, their credit ratings would be higher if they were not a Russian firm. Their balance sheet strength can go head-to-head with any other energy major, based on the numbers.

For example, they maintain among the lowest debt-to-equity ratios compared to their peers:

![Lukoil Debt to Equity Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-debt-to-equity.png)

And they have the highest ratio of free cash flow relative to debt:

![Lukoil FCF to Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-fcf-to-debt.png)

With over 25 billion boe in reserves, Lukoil has enough oil to last for two decades at current production rates, which is longer than the average energy major. Much of it is conventional low-cost oil. Their oil/gas breakdown is roughly 3/4 oil and 1/4 gas.

**Dividend Policy**

Lukoil has generally maintained one of the higher dividend yields among energy majors, despite maintaining the lowest payout ratio most of the time, due to how cheap the stock is.

![Lukoil Dividend Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-dividend-yield.png)

Here were the payout ratios over the past few years:

![Lukoil Payout Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-payout-ratio-3yr.png)

Despite paying a high dividend yield, Lukoil has maintained a low payout ratio in most years. That is one of the benefits of a cheap stock; they can pay high yields even with a low payout ratio.

Within the past decade, which was very difficult for energy stocks, Lukoil reduced its share count more than the other energy majors as well, which is good for each remaining share:

![Lukoil Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-lukoy-shares-outstanding.png)

Share buybacks can be good or bad depending on how they are done, and for what purpose. A very cheap stock with limited growth prospects gets a major benefit from buying back shares, because each share becomes worth a considerably larger portion of the company, and earnings are distributed over an ever-shrinking number of shares, which boosts all per-share metrics (earnings per share, book value per share, dividends per share, etc), and they can buy their shares for a very attractive price and generate a strong return from doing so. Lukoil has historically managed its capital allocation very well, allocating into capex, dividends, and buybacks, in the right proportions.

During the fourth quarter of 2019, Lukoil announced a new dividend policy. They will now distribute 100% of adjusted free cash flow to shareholders as dividends. Here is their policy:

> The total amount of dividends on the Company’s issued shares, excluding the shares held by LUKOIL Group entities, equals at least 100% of the Company’s adjusted free cash flow;
>
> The adjusted free cash flow is calculated on the basis of PJSC “LUKOIL” consolidated financial statements prepared in accordance with International Financial Reporting Standards (IFRS) and is determined as net cash provided by operating activities less capital expenditures, interest paid, repayment of lease obligations, and expenses for purchase of Company’s stock;
>
> Dividend per share is rounded to a whole Russian rouble;
>
> Dividends are paid twice a year with the amount of interim dividends calculated based on the consolidated financial statements for the six-month period.

This increased yield should attract more shareholders and may be beneficial to the share price over time. Consensus analyst expectations are that, based on the current share price, the dividend yield under the new policy could be 10% or higher for the full year 2020 and beyond. The white line on the following chart shows the massive boost that the new dividend policy is expected by analysts to have:

**Risks**

*Oil and Gas Price Volatility*

As an oil and gas producer, Lukoil’s fate is of course tied to oil and gas prices, which are volatile and economically-sensitive.

On one hand, there is less capital available for U.S. shale oil producers, which have been the disrupters over the past decade for global energy markets. With shale producers starting to be forced into profitability rather than being allowed to continue self-destructive drilling for growth at all costs, the global energy market could normalize. Many low-quality U.S. energy producers are reliant on junk bond issuance, and in any sort of significant economic downturn, that could dry up and force some of them to cease operations.

On the other hand, as I continue to update every 2 weeks, the U.S. economy is continuing to slow. Since the U.S. is the largest consumer of oil and the second largest importer of oil, it serves as an important source of demand for the global energy market. Even mild demand reductions from a sluggish economy can cause lower oil prices for a time, until those low prices force the least-efficient drillers to cease production.

I don’t know how the energy sector will perform within the next quarter or two. However, from a multi-year perspective, I am reasonably bullish on high-quality oil producers like Lukoil, with fortress balance sheets, low costs of production, and strong capital allocation policies. My base case over the next few years is for a weaker dollar, a potential increase in U.S. inflation, and possible disruption in the credit markets that fund low-quality shale oil producers, which could benefit Lukoil via sustained or higher oil prices.

Therefore, I like adding to high-quality energy companies at current price levels for a portion of my diversified portfolios, and will rebalance into them during periods of weakness. Lukoil is one of those stocks that I wouldn’t be surprised if it was 25% higher or lower within a few months from now, but I believe there is a high probability that it should do well over the long run.

*Electric Vehicles and Renewable Energy*

The longest-term risk for major energy companies like Lukoil is the technological advancement of electric vehicles and renewable energy. As someone with an electrical engineering background, I’ve always been interested in renewable energy and watching its development.

Electric vehicles by themselves are not really a huge disruption to fossil fuels, because electricity itself is not an energy source; it’s just a transmission medium for energy. Coal, natural gas, oil, nuclear reactors, hydroelectric dams, wind farms, solar utilities, and other forms of energy like geothermal or tidal mechanisms need to produce that usable energy so that it can be sent over electrical transmission lines and into your Tesla. With coal being phased out, that puts more emphasis on natural gas for core electrical generation.

However, as costs for solar and wind energy continue to decrease, these sources of energy may become an increasing competitor to oil and gas. Right now, solar and wind market share of electric generation is low, but with a substantial growth rate. But if they become a significant enough contributor, and electric vehicles take off, then the electrical grids in most countries will need to be substantially modified and expanded, which is a huge capital expenditure.

Right now, most of us get household energy from electricity, and vehicle energy from gasoline. If gasoline-powered cars are largely replaced by electric vehicles, then it means both our household and vehicle energy would come over the electrical grid instead of through those two separate mediums, and the current electrical grid in most places is not prepared for that higher level of electrical throughput.

Oil and gas are likely to remain a significant part of the global energy mix for a while, especially in emerging markets where price per unit of energy is very important to their standard of living. And as long as oil stocks stay cheap with high shareholder yields like Lukoil, they can be quite lucrative for shareholders.

In fact, it may take a significant oil supply/demand balance shortfall and much higher prices to help boost renewable energy and electric vehicle adoption.  Electric vehicles are still highly reliant on government incentives to be cost effective, but if oil and gas were more expensive (which would be great for Lukoil), it would increase the cost/benefit analysis more in favor of electric vehicles.

Investors often over-state how quickly something will be disrupted, or assume that government opposition necessarily leads to poor investment returns. That’s not always the case.

For example, when the U.S. government started cracking down on smoking decades ago, many investors thought it was bad news for Philip Morris, currently known as Altria (ticker: MO). The stock was cheap, but for investors that held on and continued reinvesting dividends, it became one of the best-performing stocks in the United States over the next few decades.

Imagine that you had invested $10,000 in Altria and $10,000 in the S&P 500 back in January 1995 when smoking rates were on a downtrend, and reinvested all dividends.

The S&P 500 investment would be worth more than **11 times** as much today, while the Altria investment would be worth more than **326 times** as much, or over $3 million, and makes the massive eleven-fold S&P 500 investment growth look flat in comparison:

![Altria Value Stock Example](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-altria-example.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

While this was an exceptional case, cheap value stocks with high ROIC metrics that pay big dividends can create very strong returns even when faced with growth pressures, government disincentives, and so forth. Growth isn’t everything; it’s all about the mix of growth, value, and how skillfully management uses their capital.

In Altria’s case, heightened regulation against smoking effectively banned tobacco advertising in the United States, which prevented newcomers from entering the market. This “locked in” all of the established tobacco companies and allowed them to be exceptionally profitable with limited marketing costs. Altria was the best-performer because they skillfully deployed their cash into dividends and other investments while remaining very cheap as a stock.

Despite all of this, if you think oil and gas will become irrelevant within a decade for a combination of reasons, and that energy prices will remain perpetually cheap, and that stocks of high-quality oil majors are not worth buying even when they are historically inexpensive, then I suggest buying into the Indian equity market to express your conviction and potentially profit from it.

One of India’s biggest problems economically is that they are still impoverished in terms of GDP-per-capita, and yet they have to import most of their oil. As a percentage of GDP, India’s energy import problem is bigger than China’s. It seriously hurts India’s trade balance and current account balance, and therefore weakens their currency. If high energy prices were removed from India’s list of economic risks, its future of growth and stability would be a lot more comfortable.

For that reason, I like to own both Russian and Indian equities for diversification, because they compliment each other well. Russia is very cheap, is slow-growing, has a great balance of payments, has low debt and huge foreign currency reserves, and is a major energy/commodity exporter. India is somewhat expensive, is fast-growing, has a challenging balance of payments situation, also has low debt, has only modest foreign currency reserves, and is a major energy/commodity importer.

If, however, you feel particularly bearish on energy prices for the long term, then India (via funds like INDA or IFN, or individual stocks) is worth considering as an overweight position. Otherwise, check out companies like Lukoil.

*The “Russian Discount”*

Russia is currently and historically one of the cheapest equity markets, and is still classified as an emerging market despite being one of the world’s top military and economic forces.

The country ranks near the top of international corruption rankings, the population levels are stagnant, GDP growth is slow, and there are more limits to freedom of expression than most developed countries have. Ever since the annexation of Crimea, Russia has been hit with sanctions that have hurt its currency strength and economic growth, although with time this has generally subsided. In fact, a weaker currency makes several Russian commodity exports (energy, metals, and agriculture products) more competitive.

There is always the possibility that certain countries could ban their citizens from owning Russian stocks, which would likely hurt the equity prices of those stocks and may force investors to exit at an inopportune time. This is a fairly low risk in my opinion, but worth noting. Canada already dabbled in doing this for its citizens; Canada has limited some of Canadian citizens’ investment options into Russia since 2014, although it hasn’t affected Lukoil specifically.

**Conclusion**

Lukoil, and oil prices in general, have done very well over the past quarter. However, zooming out and looking back over the past several years, it has been extremely rough for energy companies.

While volatility is to be expected for a Russian energy stock like Lukoil, I’m interested to begin dollar-cost averaging into it.

I’ve had Total (TOT) as part of my newsletter portfolio for several months, and recently added Canadian National Resources (CNQ).

In my view, many investors under-appreciate jurisdictional risk. They think, for example, that if they own the S&P 500, which gets over 40% of its revenue internationally, then they are fully diversified. However, all of those companies share the same jurisdiction; the same legal authority to tax and regulate them.

For example, just like how Congress and the President cut taxes for U.S. companies a couple years ago, which boosted net income by somewhere between 10% and 30% for many companies, a future Congress and President could just as easily raise taxes on all 500 companies in the S&P 500. They could also tax certain types of energy production by companies based in the United States.

For that reason, it is good to have jurisdictional diversification within a portfolio, meaning to have companies or indices of companies that answer to different governments worldwide. This may be especially true for oil companies during a time when many governments want to incentivize renewable energy. Having U.S. energy firm exposure, Canadian energy firm exposure, European energy firm exposure, and Russian energy firm exposure gives you broad access to different types of conventional and unconventional oil and gas production, while minimizing risk to any single jurisdiction.
