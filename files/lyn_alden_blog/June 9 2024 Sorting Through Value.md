# Sorting Through Value

*June 9, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report focuses on recent global interest rate cuts, various liquidity indicators, and the health of the U.S. banking system.

The digital asset section discusses the recent movement of Mt Gox coins, recent U.S. political action regarding banks’ ability to custody cryptocurrencies, and tech takeaways from the Oslo Freedom Forum.

The investment analysis section looks at a handful of underperforming stocks to see if they are more likely to be value opportunities or value traps, and then finishes with a note on emerging markets.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic U.S. liquidity indicators continue to be rangebound, which seems to be what policymakers are targeting between the Treasury and the Fed, and it is likely to continue approximately this way for at least the next several months:

![Dual Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-dual-liquidity.png)

Last week, both the Bank of Canada and the European Central Bank cut interest rates by 25 basis points (0.25%). The general approach for both of them was to note that while official measures of inflation remain moderately above target, they have fallen enough that slight cuts to interest rates are warranted. In other words, their descriptions were that they consider these new interest rate levels to still be restrictive and disinflationary, but just slightly less so than their prior levels.

Perhaps the most interesting aspect was that the European Central Bank revised their forward inflation forecasts *slightly up*, while they simultaneously *slightly reduced* their interest rates. While that’s not a direct contradiction (due to their prior argument about these rates still being restrictive), it’s nonetheless noteworthy.

The dollar index spent the prior two months softening a bit, which has benefited global liquidity a little bit. However, these interest rate cuts by the Bank of Canada and European Central Bank pushed back on that trend, and kept the dollar index elevated. As a result, global liquidity continues to be rangebound by most metrics.

I think this approach by the Bank of Canada and the European Central Bank sets the stage for how the U.S. Federal Reserve may act later this year. They can make slightly dovish moves while still making the case that their approach is restrictive. However, I have no firm view at this time for the timing of the Fed’s interest rate changes, due to mixed economic data.

**U.S. Bank Unrealized Losses**

In late May, the FDIC published their [quarterly banking profile for Q1 2024](https://www.fdic.gov/news/speeches/fdic-quarterly-banking-profile-first-quarter-2024). They concluded that U.S. banks in general continue to show resiliency, but with a handful of problem areas that need continued monitoring.

The number of “problem banks” that the FDIC considers to have significant risks increased from 52 in the prior quarter to 63 this quarter, although that’s only 1.4% of banks, which is within the normal range of 1-2% of banks. And since most of these are smaller banks, total assets of problem banks are $82.1 billion, which represents less than 0.3% of total bank assets.

The total unrealized losses for banks’ securities has been rangebound lately, and currently sit at $517 billion:

![Unrealized Bank Losses](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-unrealized-bank-losses.jpg)

However, for context U.S. banks collectively have over $2.3 trillion in equity capital, so less than a fourth of their capital is impaired by unrealized securities losses. And banks collectively earn about $60 billion per quarter in net income, which after optionally paying out dividends, they can retain and use it to build more equity capital.

So I would describe the current period as a *malaise* for banks. Their net interest margins are pressured from higher rates and holding a lot of low-yielding long-duration securities, and many of them will be retaining income each quarter by trimming their dividend growth rates and share buyback rates, as they slowly retain more net income to offset those losses and build more capital. Details vary significantly from bank to bank, based on what types of lending they do and what types of securities they hold.

This ongoing bank malaise keeps somewhat of a soft ceiling on Fed interest rate hikes and domestic liquidity reductions, although it’s only one factor in their mix of policy variables.

Overall, total bank loan growth remains slow but positive:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-bank-loans.png)

**U.S. Economic Indicators**

Economic indicators in the United States continue to be mixed.

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator continues to suggest positive real growth here in Q2, but with some mixed signals:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-gdp-now.png)

Much of that recent decline is due to a weak trade balance, rather than due to weak domestic activity, as shown by the negative light blue bars in the bar chart above. In the past couple of months, the United States continues to have a widening trade deficit, which factors negatively into the GDP calculation:

![Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-trade-balance-annotated.png)

ISM PMI manufacturing activity has been weak lately. It is up off its lows, but has had trouble getting and remaining above 50, which is the level that signals the divide between contraction and expansion. It missed consensus expectations in May by coming in at 48.7, relative to the expectations of 49.8:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-ism-pmi.png)

Unemployment readings continue to be relatively strong, but are off their peak levels. The official unemployment rate is currently 4.0% which is historically low, but is up from this cycle’s low point of 3.4% that it set back in early 2023. Normally when there is that much of a sustained uptrend from a low base, a recession (marked in gray on the chart below) follows:

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-unemployment-rate.png)

Job openings came in weaker than expected last week, although in absolute terms they are similar to pre-pandemic levels adjusted for mild population growth:

![Job Openings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-jolts.png)

There is currently conflicting data between the household survey and the payroll survey for overall U.S. employment trends, which is a data quality issue that complicates analysis. The payroll survey continues to show strong numbers, while the household survey has been notably weaker, with a widening gap. The payroll survey counts people with multiple jobs, whereas the household survey does not, and there are a variety of other differences as well, which post-pandemic remote work and gig work might be amplifying to some degree.

The years 2022 and 2023 were marked by weakening economic data across most metrics, held up by strong employment and wage data and large ongoing fiscal deficits which have some nominally stimulatory effects. It’s possible that we’re seeing employment finally start to soften, while some of those other metrics continue to stabilize.

In this sense, we may continue to have a wider-than-normal gap between the performance of different sectors (mirroring the wider than normal gap between tight monetary policy and loose fiscal policy), and may continue to have economic cycles that are different from the 1980s-through-2010s period. Employment weakness tends to feed on itself, so that’s a key area to focus on in the coming months.

Overall, this series of mixed data gives the Fed some flexibility regarding interest rates this year, with the bias probably leaning toward holding rates steady for now and gearing up for mild cuts later this year. Official measures of inflation are still above their target of 2%, but if the unemployment rate continues to increase over 4%, then the Fed would be in a position to consider both sides of their mandate: stable prices and low unemployment, as well as financial stability.

However, as I described my prior report, I think moderate interest rate cuts could be rather uneventful for the U.S. economy. As long as mortgage rates don’t reach a lower-low compared to past cycles, there would likely not be much of a refinancing cycle, which means that not a lot of consumer income would be unlocked.

The main beneficiaries of a U.S. rate cut would likely be foreign markets with substantial amounts of dollar-denominated debt, as that kind of interest rate move could soften the dollar quite a bit relative to emerging market currencies, even if such an interest rate move doesn’t move the needle much on the U.S. economy. And liquidity proxies like gold and bitcoin would likely benefit.

**Mt. Gox Coins on the Move**

A little over a decade ago, Mt. Gox was the biggest bitcoin exchange around, and its trading volume was as high as 70% of total bitcoin trading volume across exchanges at the time.

Based in Japan, Mt. Gox was originally a *Magic: The Gathering* trading card exchange. That’s where the name comes from: **M**agic **T**he **G**athering **O**nline e**X**change.

They began including bitcoin trading in 2010, since bitcoins were basically digital collectibles at the time, and they quickly rose to become by far the biggest exchange of that era. However, in early 2014 the exchange halted withdraws, and it turned out that hundreds of thousands of clients’ coins were missing. It was such a big incident at the time relative to the small size of the Bitcoin network back then, that many people understandably thought Bitcoin as a network would die as a result of this.

However, the anti-fragile nature of the Bitcoin network asserted itself in response. This massive loss of coins accelerated the development and adoption of hardware wallets; Trezor introduced the first one later in 2014, and then other companies like Coinkite and Ledger launched devices as well. Now there’s a big ecosystem of various hardware wallets to safely hold coins offline, and they’ve sold millions of devices over the years. Andreas Antonopoulos popularized the phrase “Not your keys, not your coins” to teach people the risks about relying on third-party custodians.

Of course, custody services are a bit different now. Reputable firms like NYDIG, Fidelity and and publicly-traded corporations like Block (SQ) custody coins for many clients here in 2024, rather than small trading card exchanges as was the case back in 2014. Still, understanding counterparty risk is important, and the fraudulent collapse of the offshore FTX exchange was a recent example.

Some of the coins from the Mt. Gox loss were salvaged, and literally for a decade they’ve been going through bankruptcy proceedings to get back to some of the original owners. Specifically, there are about 140,000 coins left to redistribute, which is way lower than the 850,000 coins they originally lost, but is way higher in terms of dollar value due to how much price appreciation that the bitcoins have enjoyed over the past decade.

And now, those coins are on the verge of actually being delivered. They’re expected to be distributed to their creditors later this year, and they’ve recently moved on-chain, seemingly to consolidate before being paid out. This shows up on some of the charts that track movement of old coins.

This has sparked some concerns about price, since some of the recipients will be quite wealthy and will finally have the liquidity available to them. However, most analysts I’ve talked to expect that probably less than 20-30% of the 140,000+ coins will be sold by those that receive them, implying 42,000 or less, which is not much in the grand scheme of things. The Mt. Gox creditors fought hard to get paid back in bitcoin rather than dollars, and these remaining ones could have sold their bitcoin IOUs for liquidity a long time ago.

In comparison, well over 260,000 net bitcoins have been absorbed by ETFs since their launch in January 2024, which dwarfs the number of Mt. Gox coins that might be sold. The price of bitcoin has likely already reflected much of the Mt. Gox selling pressure risk, and indeed tends to move around whenever there is news on the Mt. Gox front. It’s a potential negative catalyst to watch for later this year, but not very large, and I am mostly viewing this as intermediate-term noise.

**Conflicting Views of Crypto in the United States**

In my prior report, I covered the recent Congressional action on a number of bills, including a bipartisan one that would overturn a SEC ruling that effectively prevents banks from serving as a custodian for crypto assets:

> Since 2022, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission has had a staff accounting bulletin (“[SAB 121](https://www.sec.gov/oca/staff-accounting-bulletin-121)“) that effectively prevents banks from serving as a custodian for crypto assets.
>
> However, a little over a week ago, both the U.S. House and the U.S. Senate comfortably [passed legislation](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/legal/senate-passes-bill-to-overturn-sec-rule-on-bitcoin-and-crypto-custody-biden-threatens-veto) to overturn SAB 121. It was somewhat bipartisan in nature, with the majority of Republicans and a significant minority of Democrats voting for it, notably including Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer.
>
> Before the vote, the Biden Administration issued a statement that they would veto it if passed. They have about a week to do this, and it’s unclear if the bigger-than-expected margin of passing it will change that decision to veto it or not.
>
> -May 26 premium report

As an update for this report, President Biden indeed vetoed the bill as he said he would. The veto message indicated that the reason for the veto was mainly about not undermining the SEC, and stated that he was eager to work with Congress on legislation for this industry.

I don’t view this as particularly impactful for price, since Coinbase, BitGo, NYDIG, Fidelity, and other entities have strong custodian businesses. However, it’s more of a signpost for government acceptance. The bipartisan nature of the bill is, in my view, a bigger signal than the the narrower veto of the bill. This continues to be an area to watch going forward.

Meanwhile, in recent months cryptocurrency industry executives have raised large amounts of donor capital for former President Trump’s campaign and have spoken with him. Two weeks ago, when Trump attended the Libertarian National Convention, he said he would pardon Ross Ulbricht (the early bitcoin proponent and creator of Silk Road) on the first day of his presidency if he wins, and would protect the right for Americans to self-custody cryptocurrencies and bitcoin. The specific nature and phrasing of these statements indicate direct involvement from the cryptocurrency industry donors.

It remains unclear how the election will go, but we are at the point where the cryptocurrency industry as a whole is at a scale big enough to impact national elections to some degree, including candidate positions and potential voters.

**Bitcoin and Nostr for Human Rights**

I spent this past week in Norway for the annual Oslo Freedom Forum, and I’m finalizing this report while flying out from it.

Run by the Human Rights Foundation (chaired by Gary Kasparov), the Oslo Freedom Forum is an event that gives democracy advocates and human rights advocates from around the world a chance to tell their stories to the world, and helps donors connect with activists on how to best help people in authoritarian regimes worldwide achieve higher levels of freedom.

More broadly, the Human Rights Foundation does a variety of work around the world to support activists as they defend peoples’ basic freedoms. They use the UN’s [International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Covenant_on_Civil_and_Political_Rights) for their definition of human rights, which was ratified by 173 countries and includes:

* freedom of speech and expression
* the right to equal treatment and due process under law
* freedom from slavery and torture
* freedom of association
* the right to leave and enter their countries
* freedom from interference and coercion in matters of conscience
* the right to acquire and dispose of property
* freedom from arbitrary detainment or exile
* the right to worship in the manner of their choosing
* the right to participate in the government of their country

I joined on two panels for the forum back in 2022. This year, I did a fireside interview with Jack Dorsey, the co-founder and former CEO of Twitter and the founder and current CEO of Block, about the open-sourcing of communication protocols and AI, and I gave a keynote talk about the subject matter of my book, *Broken Money*.

The most [newsworthy](https://www.entrepreneur.com/business-news/jack-dorsey-elon-musk-social-media-algorithms-a-problem/475324) [takeaway](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/twitter-founder-jack-dorsey-warns-141244705.html?guccounter=1&guce_referrer=aHR0cHM6Ly93d3cuZ29vZ2xlLmNvbS8&guce_referrer_sig=AQAAABVcMTyOzKorV3HFbFiPBBwafpEy2I4AMb1T-kmNnxurR91T-Eku-iDI6DtmrOFSdcFNBxxbsR8uXyEW9vriHfB7fePFceB2juwnB_s8Pp1btstW9nHtbmMUha_gcvUi22bcBuruJeHIiQ1kWOP9BAuJsWgeHsHDkPXrphFrP54J) from the interview with Dorsey was that he is a big proponent of choice of algorithms. That’s the segment that many news outlets highlighted, and is the segment for which Elon Musk tweeted his agreement with. I was familiar with Dorsey’s view on this and find it interesting, so I made sure to ask him this question specifically and provide a context for him to talk about it.

Content-sorting algorithms affect what types of content people see on social networks. Rather than subjecting users to specific algorithms, there can be a market of algorithms that users can choose from while still using the same social network. Dorsey described algorithms as having an impact on free will, since while government and social media moderation policies can affect users’ ability to share their views, algorithms can subconsciously affect how people interpret and understand the world in the first place, and thus what views they end up having.

A social media company’s algorithm can intentionally give more reach to positive content directed toward one country and give more reach to negative content directed toward another country. Or, an algorithm can intentionally highlight more negative or divisive types of content in general, which tends to keep users engaged on the network (which is good for business), but possibly at the cost of social harm.

There are all sorts of big and small effects, intentional and unintentional, that content-ranking algorithms can have on billions of people. A handful of people at a social media company can tweak the algorithm that countless people use, in a similar way that a handful of people at a central bank can tweak the interest rates for money that countless people use, and without necessarily knowing all of the downstream effects from each of their decisions.

A solution that Dorsey likes quite a bit for this is called Nostr, which I wrote about early last year [here](https://www.lynalden.com/open-networks/). Nostr is an open-source protocol that multiple different companies build client apps for. These client apps can affect how the user experience works and can provide different algorithms, or can allow the user to pick their own algorithms from a marketplace, and yet users of different clients and/or different algorithms are still accessing the same underlying protocol and pool of content.

In other words, I can post about something from the [Primal](https://primal.net/) Nostr client, and someone can read the same post on either their Primal Nostr client, or their [Damus](https://damus.io/) Nostr client, or the [Amethyst](https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.vitorpamplona.amethyst&hl=en&pli=1) Nostr client, which were made by separate groups and yet are interoperable with each other because they rely on the same underlying protocol. Individuals do control clients that they build, but nobody controls the underlying Nostr protocol itself.

A centralized social media company is vertically integrated, whereas the Nostr approach separates the underlying content from the client that allows the user to access that content. The best analogy for this is to reference how email works. There’s the underlying open-source SMTP protocol which serves as the foundation for email as we know it, and different interoperable webmail providers like Gmail, Yahoo, and so forth, provide specific user experiences for sending and receiving emails over the SMTP protocol. Nobody can controls SMTP and nobody can get banned from SMTP, but different webmail providers can have different spam filters for how they sort emails that they receive over SMTP, can ban individuals or regions from using their specific webmail servers, and so forth.

Dorsey initially took efforts in 2019 to open-source Twitter’s underlying protocol and separate it from the Twitter client, but for a variety of corporate reasons he wasn’t able to complete that, and thus had had a complicated history when it comes to content moderation decisions and his various corporate incentives and power structures. Therefore, he now focuses on highlighting the Nostr ecosystem, which he describes as being precisely what he was looking to build. He now donates to various Nostr developers to help expand and accelerate it as an ecosystem.

In addition to finding Nostr interesting in its own right (for over a year and a half now), the reason I highlight it is that it’s relevant for bitcoin. This is because Nostr natively includes bitcoin tipping on posts and can serve as the social graph for many different bitcoin wallets. In addition to “liking” or “reposting” someone’s post, you can tip them using the Bitcoin Lightning network. Or if you just want to send money to someone, you can look them up on Nostr and do so, including across borders. And since it’s open-source, you can connect whichever Bitcoin Lightning wallet you prefer, and it’s all interoperable.

For several years, the Human Rights Foundation has been actively supporting the Bitcoin ecosystem. This is because they recognized early on that many pro-human rights groups and anti-corruption groups routinely get their bank accounts shut down in authoritarian jurisdictions. In order to receive donations and perform their business, they need to be able to receive and move money. Bitcoin and stablecoins, therefore, are tools that many aid organizations, human rights organizations, and so forth have turned to. Here in 2024, the HRF has become increasingly interested in Nostr as well.

I’ll have more content about this topic regarding specific tech developments in future reports and articles, but that’s my primary takeaway from the conference.

## Sorting Through the Losers

Many of our investment themes have been doing well lately. In particular, being on the right side of the fiscal dominance trend has been important in terms of asset class decisions.

Most of the time when we rotate stocks out of one or more of the portfolios, it’s because they’ve become too expensive. And that’s a good problem to have. For example, I reduced the position sizing in MicroStrategy (MSTR) and removed it from the top-twelve stock list back in March after it had a monstrous run-up in price and was trading at 2x its underlying net asset value.

However, occasionally we have to rotate some stocks out for less fun reasons: fundamental underperformance. If a stock’s growth thesis is not playing out as expected, then it risks becoming a value trap (rather than a value opportunity), and needs to be culled from the herd. Position sizes on individual stocks are kept small so that a handful of losers aren’t a problem. They’re inevitable.

The challenging part is knowing when to either 1) double-down or 2) cut a loser. And ultimately that comes down to answering a single (hard) question: is it mostly short-term issues that are driving the problems for a given company, or more structural long-term issues? If the former is the case, then it’s more likely a value opportunity. If the latter is the case, then it’s more likely a value trap.

So, the investment section of this report is dedicated to going through some of the potential losers that we have: previous stock picks that have been weak here in 2024, to see if they are still worth holding, or if they should be trimmed. Rather than sweep them under the rug, we must shine a light on them and see why they aren’t doing well, so that we may know what to do with them.

**CVS Health**

![CVS FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-cvs-fastgraph.png)

CVS Health (CVS) has performed a number of acquisitions to become a major healthcare giant. In addition to their core pharmacy and pharmacy benefits management businesses, they acquired Aetna in 2018, which is one of the United States’ largest health insurers. In 2022 and 2023, they spent a combined sum of over $18 billion to acquire Signify Health and Oak Street Health, which greatly expanded their primary care and home care capabilities.

CVS now has a pretty straightforward path to focus on: shareholder returns. The business has a lot of moving parts, and can use incoming cash flows to pay down debts, buy back inexpensive shares, and pay rising dividends, while it streamlines and consolidates its operations and gets the most out of its various subsidiary synergies. Their network of pharmacies and retail stores can shift more floorspace to healthcare services, so that people who use CVS services have multiple ways to get treatment, and so that CVS can retain a competitive advantage against ecommerce-based pharmacies.

![CVS Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-cvs-balance-sheet.png)

However, they’ve run into turbulence in 2024. Specifically, a number of people who delayed getting certain types of healthcare services during the pandemic years are getting those services now, and that’s eating into the profitability of insurance companies. And because of this, CVS missed analysts’ estimates last quarter. We have a 1.08% allocation of the Newsletter portfolio to CVS.

Their overall revenue numbers continue to be solid, especially as pharmacy revenue keeps doing well, but it’s the insurance profitability that’s been the issue on the bottom line. There are also some one-time issues related to acquisitions.

Overall, I view this likely to be a temporary problem, rather than a structural problem, and so I view CVS as worth holding and watching for now to see if the thesis goes back to playing out. The stock has signs of a potential bottom and upturn in at this point, but we’ll see if that holds.

**Bristol Myers Squibb**

![BMY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-bmy-fastgraph.png)

Bristol Myers Squibb (BMY) has arguably been the most disappointing stock pick in a while. In my view, this is the one that is showing the most signs of being a value trap. We have an 0.81% allocation of the Newsletter portfolio to BMY.

It was a big outperformer back in 2022: when most things were going down, some of our energy and healthcare names were rising, and BMY was in that pack. But in 2023 and 2024, BMY has been a persistent dud, and not just in terms of price action.

BMY has several major patented drugs that are losing their patent protection in the intermediate term. To combat this, they routinely develop new drugs, and acquire companies that have many drugs in production or in pipeline, so that they can replace older drugs with newer drugs. However, that relies on them developing and buying the right drugs at the right price, and investors have had concerns regarding BMY’s ability to navigate its pipeline switchover.

My analysis over the past couple of weeks is that the stock price has likely fallen far more than the underlying value of the company. I think a price to free cash flow ratio of under 7x is rather pessimistic, although until their pipeline starts to show better results relative to their expirations there’s not a good case for it to be over 10x. Overall, I am quite mixed on the company, and will be watching it rather closely.

The company has an “A” credit rating, and pays a well-covered 5.8% dividend yield. In late 2023, the company came under management of a new CEO.

For now I am keeping BMY in the Newsletter portfolio, but am shifting its location in the Top 12 stock list with Visa (V), which is a stock that we have in a variety of portfolios. Visa will be moved to the conservative section and BMY will be moved to the aggressive section.

The analyst that downgraded the stock still expects gradual EPS *growth* in 2024 and 2025 for the bank, and the stock is trading at an earnings multiple of under 7x relative to his forward EPS estimates and other analyst’ estimates. So, even the bear isn’t all that bearish, in the grand scheme of things.

Given all this, my view of Bank OZK remains unchanged. I think it’s a long-term value opportunity, however it must be managed with proper position sizing because a bank of this size does have risk.

## Final Thoughts: Emerging Market Rotation

Patrick Zweifel, the chief economist at Pictet Asset Management, published a [great set of charts on social media](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel/status/1796496439239184754) regarding emerging markets. Pictet always has some of the best charts in the business.

Here’s the performance of emerging markets over the past 36 years, since the time that emerging markets became recognized as a true “asset class”:

![DM vs EM Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-dm-vs-em-performance.jpg)

As can be seen in the chart on the right above, relative performance of emerging market equities vs developed market equities varies dramatically over time, and is generally tied to a strong or weak dollar index. During periods where the dollar is coming down from a high, such as the late 1980s and early 2000s, emerging markets can generate tremendous outperformance. But during periods where the dollar is strengthening, such as the late 1990s and over the past decade, emerging markets have gone through long periods of stark underperformance.

Zweifel makes the case that growth signals are suggesting a bottom in emerging vs developed markets, and relative stock prices are potentially set to follow:

![DM vs EM GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-dm-vs-em-gdp.jpg)

He also highlights that on a purchasing power basis, the dollar is near the high end of its historical valuation range, and emerging market currencies are near the low end.

![DM vs EM Currency](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-9-dm-vs-em-currency.jpg)

I continue to view emerging markets as broadly worth watching, and I’ve covered them in a number of reports recently. Chinese stocks have shown evidence of a bottom, and Latin American equities have kept pace with U.S. equities on a total return basis (i.e. inclusive of price and dividends) since late 2021, even as they’ve underperformed on price alone.

I don’t necessarily expect fireworks at the moment for emerging markets, but whenever the U.S. economy does soften and the Fed eases from its current stance, perhaps later in 2024 or within 2025, a positive catalyst of a softer dollar could be in place for undervalued emerging market equities built upon undervalued currencies.
