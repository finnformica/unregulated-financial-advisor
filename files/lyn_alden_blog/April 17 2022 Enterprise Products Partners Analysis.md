# Enterprise Products Partners Deep Dive Analysis

*April 17, 2022*

## Macro View

This continues to be an unusual macroeconomic environment.

Demand-side inflation is beginning to subside in the US and elsewhere, with the removal of fiscal stimulus. However, supply-side inflation continues to be a significant problem, exacerbated by the war in Ukraine and China’s unusually strict lockdowns.

**Chinese Lockdowns**

China went through several weeks of doubling down on its zero-COVID policy, which has been quite a headscratcher for many analysts, myself included. The Omicron variant is extremely contagious but relatively mild, and yet they seem to be treating it like a zombie apocalypse disease from a horror movie.

Large portions of Shanghai and dozens of other cities have been locked down over the past three weeks even more thoroughly than they locked down in spring of 2020. For example, overall subway traffic in Shanghai slowed to basically zero during these weeks, whereas there was still some minimal activity in spring 2020.

Most notably, the number of cargo ships waiting outside of ports in Shanghai spiked to a record high, far surpassing the disruption that occurred two years ago in spring 2020. This has been confirmed with satellite ship tracking:

![Shanghai Ships Waiting](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-shanghai-ships-waiting.jpg)

*Chart Source: [Rodrigo Zeidan](https://twitter.com/RodZeidan/status/1515275175528308744)*

Documenting things in China is always difficult. The Chinese government is restrictive with data and speech, but then on the other hand, some western sources outside of the China sometimes try to sensationalize things to a greater degree than what is happening, and come off as xenophobic or unreliable. Filtering through sources to find what’s really happening is tricky. However, the available data clearly show that what’s happening is massive, and it’s confirmed in various ways by on-the-ground reporting.

Approximately 40% of the Chinese population is estimated to be in strict lockdowns, with Shanghai being among the strictest.

[Jared Nelson](https://twitter.com/JaredTNelson/status/1512713451587710977), an American data compliance officer living in Shanghai has documented this situation in depth. People in his community were unable to leave apartments. Some volunteers for each building were allowed to exit and get supplies for the building but they had to wear a full-body hazmat-style suit to do so.

[Yaxue Cao](https://twitter.com/YaxueCao/status/1512798825768964110) pointed towards 100+ videos and images of rather extreme lockdown behavior throughout Shanghai. The government workers are often appearing as large groups of people all dressed in hazmat-style suits enforcing compliance on groups of people that are getting frustrated with their limited mobility and lack of resources.

[Patrick Madrid](https://twitter.com/patrickmadrid/status/1512600420610363394) shared a video from a Chinese friend that showed tons of people screaming from their high-rise apartment windows. Similar videos have been shot and shared elsewhere in Shanghai. The issue is that food production, food delivery, medicine delivery systems are all strained. Many people have had trouble getting basic supplies while forced to remain in their apartments.

[Anthony Fu](https://twitter.com/antfu7/status/1514412132502159361) shared that he has been pretty lucky due to having some food stockpiles during his lockdown, but that the situation is harsh for his neighborhood. People have trouble getting food, and they get turned away from hospitals for many serious conditions.

[Qin Chen](https://twitter.com/QinchenCQ/status/1513845959200976897), who is locked down, shared a timeline of the lockdowns throughout Shanghai.

Major media have reported on this as well. CNN [reports](https://www.cnn.com/2022/04/11/asia/china-shanghai-lockdown-culver-intl-hnk-dst/index.html) of the difficulties getting food in the locked-down city of 25 million people, since the delivery system is not designed to handle 100% of food going through it. Reuters [reports](https://www.reuters.com/world/china/chinas-xi-says-sticking-tough-covid-curbs-will-bring-victory-2022-04-14/) of the rising stress and difficulties that many residents are sharing on social media. The South China Morning Post had a very critical piece against the lockdowns, [reporting](https://www.scmp.com/news/people-culture/social-welfare/article/3174313/no-end-shanghais-covid-lockdown-sight-anger) that residents are frustrated with clunky lockdowns, lack of food and medicine, forced culling of their pets suspected to have the virus by health workers in hazmat suits, etc.

In addition to having negative implications for Chinese asset prices and domestic politics, this breakdown in logistics and economic output has negative implications for global supply chains and global economic growth in the quarters ahead. Shanghai and other Chinese cities are massive manufacturing and logistics hubs, and all of that is now more disrupted in 2022 than in 2020.

**Inflation**

I’ve had an inflationary view for the past few years, which has played out even faster than I thought. This is due to both monetary inflation on the demand side *and* real resources constraints on the supply side.

However, over the past several months I’ve been tracking a possible local top in year-over-year headline inflation occurring by the end of Q1 of this year. I described this back in December:

> From base effects in the CPI calculation, there’s a decent chance that we’ll see a local top in official CPI sometime in Q1 2022 within the 7-9% year-over-year range. As we look later into 2022 beyond that point, the future is less clear and depends on certain variables.
>
> -December 12, 2021

Two months ago, I re-iterated that year-over-year headline inflation would likely top by the end of Q1 or the early part of Q2. The caveat, however, was that if something caused energy prices to spike; in that scenario inflation could overshoot my base case. Here was my description, since it’s still mostly relevant:

> The producer price index is showing signs of having reached a local top in year-over-year terms. I think we’ll probably see the same for headline CPI by late Q1 or early Q2.
>
> ![Inflation Year-over-Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-20-inflation-yoy.png)
>
> The reason has to do with base effects. Monthly inflation has been rather steady for about a year now, so now high inflation prints are about to start lapping year-ago similarly-high inflation prints, and thus the year-over-year figures are likely to stop going up. Monthly inflation would need to *break out to a higher level* in order to get yearly inflation up at this point.
>
> ![Inflation Month-over-Month](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-2-20-inflation-mom.png)
>
> However, if inflation remains relatively high (4-8%), even if it stops going higher on a year-over-year basis, that’s still a significant problem.
>
> Investor views about what inflation is going to do next tend to be very polarized. There is one camp that argues that all of this inflation is transitory, just caused by supply chain disruptions, and that it’ll go back to normal soon. There is another camp that thinks this is all just starting and that we’re entering a period of secular inflation.
>
> My view falls near the middle but towards the secular inflation view. For the past couple years I’ve been in the “inflation camp” overall, which has been correct so far. Basically, I think there is a good chance that year-over-year inflation will reach a local top in a couple months, and that we’ll have a period of flat or lower year-over-year inflation prints for a period of time, while still being above-target. And I think this is within the context of an overall inflationary decade; the 2020s decade will likely have higher average inflation than the 2010s decade due to tighter commodity supply and tighter labor markets. In other words, I think inflation will be a persistent problem, but not necessarily in a straight line that just keeps going up.
>
> The right-tail risk to that view is that if oil prices spike higher this summer due to lack of supply and solid emerging market demand, it can result in inflation (or more precisely, stagflation) that is even higher than my base case. There is a reasonable probability of that happening, and I’ve written a number of times about energy shortages, which is part of why I remain in the inflationary camp overall. I do think we’ll probably have oil price spikes to new highs this decade; I just don’t know if they will spike this summer or not. I continue to have a structurally bullish view on oil and gas equities in general, and am willing to hold them through periods of volatility.
>
> -February 20th, 2022

Well, the Russian war on Ukraine certainly added some fuel to the inflationary fire by disrupting more of the supply side. The unusually-strict Chinese lockdowns are a second important factor contributing to that. So that’s a one-two punch to the supply side, pointing towards more inflation and/or shortages.

These events weren’t part of my base case, so they activated the right-tail risk of even higher inflation prints in March than originally expected. Energy and food prices spiked in March due to war-related supply chain disruptions, above and beyond what was already happening from monetary inflation. We’re still within the 7-9% headline CPI level that I mentioned back in December, but we’ll see if that upper estimate gets breached.

Core inflation (which excludes food and energy) is indeed starting to look toppy as originally expected, but headline inflation which includes food and energy got an extra kick to it in March mainly related to the war, up to 8.5%:

![YOY CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-cpi-yoy.png)

We can see the divergence more clearly on the month-over-month figures, and it highlights how inflation is not really just one number, but instead a series of attempts to measure a basket of prices:

![MOM CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-cpi-mom.png)

We won’t know until the April print (which comes out after the month is over) whether headline inflation indeed peaked in Q1 (March) or not. It partly depends on geopolitics at this point, including Russian energy trade, Chinese lockdowns, Europe/US recession risks, and so forth.

Basically, Americans are no longer receiving stimulus checks, economic growth is slowing, and oil prices are currently off of their March highs. But the Chinese logistics situation is the worst ever, and there are plenty of headlines we could wake up to that would add more fuel to the supply-side inflationary fire.

Aside from energy and commodity supply-side disruptions, the chart for retail sales describes the demand-side inflation picture well. Monthly retail sales are on the right axis in blue, and the percent change in retail sales from last year is on the left axis in red:

![Retail Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-retail-sales.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Back in 2009, retail sales dropped during the recession, and there was no massive fiscal stimulatory response. The retail sales trend simply continued on from that lower base, and so it took a few years to surpass the pre-recession high.

In 2020, retail sales dropped briefly, but then as lockdowns ended and stimulus went out to people, retail sales exploded higher. The amount of broad money in the system increased by almost 40% in two years. Here in 2022, the rate of change is getting back to normal levels, as direct fiscal stimulus is no longer occurring. But with more money permanently in the system, most prices are permanently higher.

Here’s a way to think about the current price inflation. Back in 2020 and 2021, we had demand-side monetary inflation from massive stimulus, as well as supply-side limitations, which resulted in both price inflation and shortages/delays, but more-so towards price inflation. In 2022, the demand-side monetary inflation is heading back to normal, but the supply-side limitations are still bad and arguably getting worse. When the demand side is strong and the supply side is limited, we get inflation and shortages/delays. If the demand side is weak and the supply side is limited, it’ll have a tendency to lean more towards shortages/delays, since the public can’t pay higher prices for many things outside of the essentials, since they simply don’t have the money. Eventually that results in recessionary conditions, and probably more stimulus, but that’s a big valley to go over.

![Inflation and Shortages](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-inflation-and-shortages.png)

*Chart Source: Lyn Alden*

I continue to have a stagflationary view overall, which is basically what that bottom-left quadrant on the chart is. It might or not become an actual recession, but at minimum it’s a slowdown in growth, while the supply side is tight.

I expect inflation to be a significant problem throughout the years ahead, but not necessarily in a straight line, and sometimes it will manifest in shortages rather than pure price inflation. There will likely be economic slowdowns, periods of central bank pushback, withdrawn stimulus, and other efforts to control inflation.

And I think we’re in one of these counter-inflation environments now, as policymakers try to push back. Even after we get past a local top in inflation, it doesn’t mean that inflation is gone forever; there continue to be structural issues with the monetary system and with real-world resource constraints (commodity capex and supply chains) that are persistent.

So, I remain firmly in the “inflation camp” for the long run (both monetary inflation and supply-side real resource constraints) but am also monitoring some disinflationary countertrends that will likely occur along the way.

**Fed Policy and Treasury Supply/Demand Issues**

The Fed is expected to increase their short-term rate by 50 basis points in May and to continue increasing it from there in upcoming meetings every six weeks. Additionally, they have signaled plans to begin quantitative tightening; letting maturing bonds roll off of their balance sheet.

My expectation is that they will tighten policy until something breaks, such as credit markets or the Treasury market going illiquid, or with outright recession indicators beginning to manifest. At that point they’ll likely have to reverse course and go dovish again, even if inflation is still above their target level.

Meanwhile, this has already been an unusually large drawdown for the Treasury market:

![Treasury Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-bloomberg-treasury.png)

A stagflationary environment (low inflation-adjusted growth, poor consumer sentiment, but real-world supply constraints and persistent price inflation) is the hardest environment for central banks to deal with. This commonly occurs for emerging markets, but hasn’t been an issue for the developed world since the 1970s, and debt-to-GDP is currently much higher than in the 1970s which limits the degree of hawkishness they can apply.

The United States continues to run large structural fiscal deficits, but not in the form of direct stimulus. Additionally, the US economy consists of two-thirds consumer spending, which is tightly correlated with asset prices being high (wealth effect). If the US economy and/or US asset prices sag, it leads to sagging of tax revenue as well, which needs to be made up with additional Treasury debt issuance. As the Fed tries quantitative tightening, it will mean a lot of Treasury supply is coming to market, with potentially not enough buyers to absorb it all.

Commercial banks have been the biggest buyers of Treasuries aside from the Fed, but they are getting rather stretched here. This chart shows the total Treasury securities held by banks in blue on the left axis and the year-over-year absolute change in their holdings in red on the right axis:

![Bank Treasuries](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-bank-treasuries.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Another way to visualize it is to look at the percentage of commercial bank assets that consist of Treasuries. Currently this figure is over 20%, which is already the highest ratio in modern history:

![Treasuries % of Bank Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-bank-treasuries-percent.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The percentage of bank assets allocated to Treasuries got way higher back in the 1940s/1950s era (along with the Fed, it was primarily commercial banks that funded the war), but there was a different set of regulations back then.

Unless hedge funds, pensions, or foreigners pick up their rate of Treasury purchases by a lot, this could get pretty awkward. In a risk-off environment, it’s certainly possible that some of them probably will increase Treasury purchases. The question is whether it will be enough.

The “endgame” for this long-term debt cycle will probably involve the Fed being forced to resume asset purchases even during above-target headline inflation, in order to stabilize the Treasury market, and this could involve yield curve control. When it comes to that, they have methods to kind of hide it or keep it at arms length, such as removing the SLR requirement for the banking system (which would allow commercial banks to absorb more Treasuries rather than the Fed) or by using their standing repo facility and jawboning that it’s some technical issue, but ultimately it would look a lot like emerging market style debt monetization, and should be good for hard assets if/when it occurs.

Until such a pivot point occurs, this continues to be a difficult environment for broad asset prices and warrants some amount of cash on hand as defense, along with defensive assets like healthcare stocks, pipeline stocks, and gold. Economic growth is sluggish, while central banks around the world are tightening monetary policy.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin continues to struggle and chop along with no clear direction, which is in line with what it typically has done during declining-PMI environments.

Meanwhile, the percentage of coins that haven’t moved on-chain for over a year has hit an all-time high:

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-btc-hodl-wave.png)

*Chart Source: Look Into Bitcoin*

During strong bull markets, long-term on-chain holders tend to sell into that strength, since they are up 5x, 10x, or more on their positions. New capital comes in and buys up coins on-chain. As such, the percentage of coins held for 1+ year decreases. During sideways or downward markets, long-term holders tend to maintain their coins or keep buying, and so the percentage of coins held for 1+ year increases.

In other words, during bear markets many coins move from liquid fast-money hands to illiquid strong hands. When fresh demand later comes into the space (often during a rising PMI environment), it doesn’t take much capital inflows to move the price up, since the existing coins are so tightly-held by that point.

A similar metric is to check the supply of illiquid coins, which are defined as coins held on-chain by entities that are measured to have a high propensity to accumulate and a low propensity to sell.

![Bitcoin Illiquid Supply](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-btc-illiquid-supply.jpg)

*Chart Source: [@TheRealPlanC](https://twitter.com/TheRealPlanC), via Glassnode*

This is one area where the current market looks different than the 2018 bear market. In this market, there is a lack of new demand coming in from new entities which makes it rather bearish, but existing high-conviction entities continue to accumulate coins. In contrast to this, during the 2018 bear market, there was a lack of new demand from new entities, *and* existing high-conviction entities weren’t buying much either, which is why it capitulated so far down in price back then.

Basically, there is a lot of dry tinder building up here (an increase in illiquid supply and a decrease in liquid supply), but no spark (new demand) yet. Fast money has no interest in bitcoin at the moment until something changes, whereas the long-term accumulators have very high conviction and are aggressively stacking coins. I continue to have an uncertain 6-month view but a bullish long-term view.

I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the best way to get exposure to bitcoin for most people that want some as part of their asset mix.

**Bitcoin Conference 2022 and Lightning Announcements**

The annual Bitcoin Conference (run by Bitcoin Magazine) was held in Miami from April 6-9. The first version of this particular conference was held in 2019 with 1,900 attendees, the second was in 2021 with 12,000 attendees. This 2022 event was the third version with 35,000 attendees, with a reported $30 million production cost. There are a number of other bitcoin events around the world throughout each year, but this is by far the biggest.

In addition to the conference itself, there are many side parties and networking events hosted throughout the city during the week. I had the opportunity to talk to a number of VCs and institutional capital allocators, including many of the ones that are funding the Lightning network in various capacities, to get their updated thoughts on what is going on in the ecosystem. It’s useful to get industry experts’ views on the best things that are happening, the biggest risks that are happening, and so forth.

The conference is also frequently used to make announcements. There were a few items in particular that were noteworthy.

Lightning Labs announced a $70 million Series B capital raise, along with a new specification called Taro which if successful will allow multiple asset types to be issued on the Bitcoin/Lightning network. It makes use of the November 2021 Taproot soft fork for the Bitcoin network, and the most notable feature will probably be for stablecoins; users will be able to send stablecoins instantly and nearly for free. It could also technically be used for NFTs or wrapped versions of other blockchain tokens or any particular allowable security, if users want to do so.

The way this would work in practice is that a user with a mobile Lightning wallet will have a bitcoin balance as well as a balance for USD stablecoins or whatever other assets they might be holding, and can send those assets around easily. The assets will be able to be sent on-chain or over Lightning.

Here’s an excerpt from Lightning Labs about Taro:

> **Overview:**
>
> Taro is a new Taproot-powered protocol for issuing assets on the bitcoin blockchain that can be transferred over the Lightning Network for instant, high volume, low fee transactions. At its core, Taro taps into the security and stability of the bitcoin network and the speed, scalability, and low fees of Lightning.
>
> Taro relies on Taproot, bitcoin’s most recent upgrade, for a new tree structure that allows developers to embed arbitrary asset metadata within an existing output. It uses Schnorr signatures for improved simplicity and scalability, and, importantly, works with multi-hop transactions over Lightning.
>
> Taro is conceptually similar to colored coins, but makes use of Taproot transactions to radically improve privacy and scalability. Assets issued on Taro can be deposited into Lightning Network channels, where nodes can offer atomic conversions from Bitcoin to Taro assets. This allows Taro assets to be interoperable with the broader Lightning Network, benefiting from its reach and strengthening its network effects.
>
> Taro uses a Sparse-Merkle Tree to enable fast, efficient, private retrieval and updates to the witness/transaction data and a Merkle-Sum Tree to prove valid conservation/non-inflation. Assets can be transferred through on-chain transactions, or over the Lightning Network when deposited into a channel.
>
> Participants in Taro transfer bear the costs of verification and storage by storing Taro witness data off-chain in local data stores or with information repositories termed “Universes” (akin to a git repository). To check a Taro asset’s validity, its’ lineage since its’ genesis output is verified. This is achieved by receiving a verification file of transaction data through the Taro gossip layer. Clients can cross-check with their copy of the blockchain and amend with their own proofs as they pass on the asset.
>
> **Summary:**
>
> -Allows assets to be issued on the bitcoin blockchain
>
> -Leverages taproot for privacy and scalability
>
> -Assets can be deposited into Lightning channels
>
> -Assets can be transferred over the existing Lightning Network
>
> **Features and Limitations:**
>
> Taro allows for a long list of features that make the protocol scalable, robust and friendly for low-powered mobile devices in situations of limited bandwidth.
>
> -Taro is light client friendly: has low verification costs and needs only access to untrusted bitcoin transactions. Taro does not require knowledge of the entire blockchain.
>
> -Taro allows for atomic swaps between Taro assets and BTC
>
> -Taro can handle both unique and non-unique assets as well as collections.
>
> -Taro allows for creative multi-signature and co-signatory arrangements.
>
> -Taro allows for flexible settlement times that can range from immediately (when introduced into a Lightning channel) to hourly or daily.
>
> -Taro channels can contain both BTC and multiple Taro assets, allowing Taro to exist in the Lightning Network without additional overhead
>
> -Future features may include confidential transactions and zero-knowledge proofs as part of Taro transfers.
>
> -Source: [Lightning Labs: Taro Builder’s Guide](https://docs.lightning.engineering/the-lightning-network/taro)

Some layer-2 platforms on top of bitcoin already have stablecoins and NFTs. Tether originally started on a layer above bitcoin, for example, before it primarily moved over to Ethereum and Tron. More relevant for today, the Liquid network has stablecoins, NFTs, and other assets on it, and there are some other layer-2 networks as well. What makes Taro particularly interesting is that its assets can run directly over the Lightning network, which makes stablecoin payments nearly instant and nearly free.

Jack Mallers of Strike announced a set of partnerships with NCR Corporation (NYSE: NCR), Blackhawk Network, and Shopify regarding integration with the lightning network. Users will be able to use lightning to buy from places like McDonald’s (MCD) and Walmart (WMT) and all sorts of other places since they generally use these payment handlers. Separately, BitPay also announced integration with the lightning network.

The combination of this news is significant in my view. I’ve been bullish on Bitcoin and Lightning for a while, and have been separately bullish on stablecoins as well (which normally run on smart contract blockchains). Having stablecoins running over Lightning channels is a powerful combination, because payments using Lightning are instant and cheap, and if users stick to stablecoins they can avoid the capital gains tax problem of paying with bitcoins. A bunch of point-of-sale companies integrating with Lightning is separately quite good as well.

If all of this is deployed at scale, it improves the value proposition of the Bitcoin network, and makes the network more useful.

CNBC [published a good piece on April 13](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/04/13/watch-as-we-send-bitcoin-from-miami-to-a-ukrainian-in-poland-.html) focusing on the Lightning network:

> Vorobiova and CNBC decided to put Lightning payments to the test — with the expertise and translation skills of bitcoin developer Gleb Naumenko, who is currently hiding out in the western part of Ukraine as the war rages on.
>
> The bottom line? It really does work as well as bitcoin boosters say it does.
>
> The process of downloading a crypto wallet onto Vorobiova’s phone, transferring bitcoin over the Lightning Network from the U.S. to Poland, and withdrawing the equivalent in Polish currency from a bitcoin ATM from the southwest city of Wrocław took less than three minutes.

Taro and some of these point-of-sale integrations should continue making the Bitcoin network better and cheaper to use in practice, and less reliant on converting bitcoins back into fiat cash at expensive ATMs.

Aside from Lightning Labs, a number of startups that build on Lightning announced capital raises. OpenNode raised $20 million, Voltage raised $6 million, IBEX Mercado raised $4 million, and a number of others including Impervious also raised capital.

When the iPhone came out in 2007, few people thought, “wow this could really disrupt the taxi industry a decade from now”. Basically, multiple types of technology had reached the point where everyone could have an internet-connected computer in their pocket, and that represented a set of building blocks that led to things few people imagined, including applications like Uber.

Having a network of instant and nearly-free transactions across multiple asset types, including self-custodied bearer assets, that can be programmed with all sorts of multi-signature use-cases, represents another set of building blocks that really won’t matter on the grand scale for years, but that in the long run looks extremely interesting.

Overall, the Lightning network looks today a lot like the Bitcoin network looked back in 2014. It’s tiny and not on most peoples’ radar, but is hitting a critical mass of liquidity and usability.

## Smart Contract Note

I had a rather large smart contract news update in my prior report, so I’ll keep this section short with a few significant news items.

Ethereum developers announced plans to delay the Merge until autumn 2022. The Merge is the planned switch from proof-of-work to proof-of-stake. The switch has been delayed for a number of years (back in 2015 it was said to be “9-12 months away”), and then most recently was supposed to happen in 2021, was delayed to spring or summer 2022, and is now being moved to autumn 2022. I don’t have a strong conviction on when the Merge will occur.

Solana-based NFTs are now available on OpenSea, and there has been somewhat of an uptick in Solana’s NFT market share relative to Ethereum. It remains to be seen how durable that gain will be. Ethereum still retains the largest NFT market share, and has so far been able to hold that market share better than it has been able to hold DeFi market share.

Jack Dorsey sold an NFT of his first tweet last year on Ethereum for $2.9 million, and donated the proceeds to charity. The buyer then tried to sell the NFT for $48 million within the past week, but he only received a handful of bids with the highest being for around $14,000. This shows how fickle and faddish NFTs can be, with the potential for losing 99%+ compared to your purchase price. I think NFTs have longer-term use cases for supporting artists and for ticket sales and so forth, but as giant high-value speculations in their current forms, I don’t think they will age very well and this was an example of why. I wrote skeptically about NFT prices a number of times over the past year:

> The most expensive trading cards in the world are worth a few million dollars, so I guess I’m not one to judge what art is. ## Enterprise Products Partners Update

I’ve been bullish on US energy pipeline businesses for a while, and the case for that continues to get stronger.

Europe has an energy crisis, wants/needs to reduce its reliance on Russian oil and gas, but doesn’t have a fast alternative. US liquefied natural gas exports have helped fill some of the demand, and there are increasing deals to get more oil and gas from north-African countries. But there is still a big gap to fill. Meanwhile, Russia plans to build infrastructure to divert more of its exports to Asia.

In recent years, policymakers in many places including the US were heavily focusing on the idea of an energy transition, but now they are increasingly emphasizing energy security. Changes in price tend to cause a change in pollical priorities.

**Overview**

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) is one of the largest, most diversified, and best-run pipeline businesses in the US. It issues a K-1 form, pays high distributions, and it is only indirectly affected by energy price changes, so it’s not a place where fast-money traders ago. Approximately half of their shares have been held by investors for 5+ years.

![EPD Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-map.png)

*Chart Source: [EPD Analyst Conference, April 2022](https://www.enterpriseproducts.com/investors/presentations)*

Natural gas liquids are their biggest line of business. This is followed by crude oil, petrochemicals, and natural gas:

![EPD Breakdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-breakdown.png)

*Chart Source: EPD Analyst Conference, April 2022*

Globally, the demand for liquified petroleum gas (which is within EPD’s natural gas liquids segment) is on the rise. Liquified petroleum gas can be used for heating, cooking, refrigeration, motor fuel, and other things in areas that are not connected to a natural gas pipeline grid, which makes it particularly suitable for exporting to developing countries.

![LPG Demand](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-lpg-demand.png)

*Chart Source: EPD Analyst Conference, April 2022*

Petrochemical demand also continues to rise, and with low volatility:

![Petrochemical Demand](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-petrochemical-demand.png)

*Chart Source: EPD Analyst Conference, April 2022*

In Q1 2022, EPD acquired Navitas Midstream Partners for $3.25 billion. This is a useful tuck-in acquisition focused on natural gas gathering in the Permian Basin. It gives EPD 1,750 more miles of pipelines, and exposure to over 10,000 drilling locations with no exposure to federal lands. A combination of mild organic growth, small acquisitions, unit buybacks, and large cash distributions, are all accretive to per-unit growth for EPD unitholders going forward.

In the long run, EPD management is interested in getting involved with hydrogen storage and transportation. Electricity can be used in a process called hydrolysis to split hydrogen from water, which can serve as a vehicle fuel, as opposed to battery-powered electric vehicles. If the hydrogen industry takes off in the decades ahead for a number of use-cases, EPD is pretty well-positioned to be a big player in it.

I don’t think many people realize how much of various metals are needed for battery storage technology on the scale that many proponents envision the industry to eventually get to. It’ll be challenging to find and dig up all of those necessary metals, and ironically the amount of petroleum needed to dig up those metals will be huge. Hydrogen, to the extent that it takes off, represents basically a diversification of energy storage technologies.

**Balance Sheet Analysis**

EPD is tied with a few others as having the top credit rating in the midstream industry, with a BBB+ credit rating. They have 4.4% average cost of debt with about two decades of average duration.

For the past fifteen years, their debt/EBITDA ratio has been extremely consistent:

![Debt to EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-debt-to-ebitda.png)

The business continues to grow in terms of absolute assets and absolute debt levels:

![EPD Asset and Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-assets-debt.png)

**Distribution Analysis**

EPD has raised its distribution for 23 consecutive years. Most MLPs had to cut their distributions within the past six years, first from the 2015/2016 energy price crash, and then from the COVID-19 crash. EPD has been among the partnerships that, due to relatively low leverage, has had the flexibility to get through slowdowns without cutting its distribution.

Their distribution growth has slowed over time as they became self-financing. They eliminated the general partner holding company and its associated incentive distribution rights, and then they shifted towards much higher average distributable cash flow coverage (aka a lower payout ratio). They now earn 1.7x as much distributable cash flow as they actually distribute, and that difference goes towards self-financed growth, unit reduction, and so forth. That way, they don’t have to issue new units anymore and are starting to reduce their unit count.

![EPD History](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-history.png)

*Chart Source: EPD Analyst Conference, April 2022*

The 2014/2015/2016 period slowdown and the 2019/2020/2021 period slowdown were both due to energy price crashes and a reduction in US energy production. EPD temporarily stagnated during those periods, but didn’t have to cut its distribution, unlike many other pipeline businesses.

EPD’s shift towards self-financing (i.e. no longer being reliant on issuing equity) can be seen in the chart below. Their number of units went from quickly-rising, to mildly-rising, to flat, and now it’s starting to mildly decrease.

![EPD Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-shares.png)

EPD used to raise each quarterly distribution by a tiny amount, so that the full year growth was pretty significant. Going through an environment of weak energy demand and a shift towards self-financing led them to purposely slow down their distribution growth to create that gap between cash flow and distributions. During that time, they shifted towards a more typical model of raising the distribution once per year instead of every quarter.

Going forward, they can grow distributions as quickly as cash flow, now that they have that big coverage gap and a more favorable energy price and demand environment. The 2020/2021 period was tough for distribution growth, but 2022 has been better and I think this trend will continue in 2023.

![EPD Distributions](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-distribution.png)

Overall, EPD currently pays a distribution yield of approximately 7%, and they raised the distribution by over 3% in 2022. I think the business can offer low double-digit annual returns going forward, which is better than the bond market and many other equities can provide in this stagflationary type of environment.

**Valuation Analysis**

EPD continues to trade below its historical average valuation. It looks a lot like how it looked back in 2010.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

When looking at price to cash flow or enterprise value to EBITDA, it appears to be a reasonable bargain with an anticipation of approximately 10% forward annualized returns.

![EPD Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-valuation.png)

EPS has outperformed the S&P 500 since inception, but underperformed over the past 3-year, 5-year, and 10-year periods in what was a very hard environment for energy-related assets. Year-to-date, they’ve been outperforming again, and I think that will probably be the case for the 2020s decade as a whole.

![EPD vs SPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-epd-vs-spy-ytd.png)

During inflationary periods, asset-heavy businesses tend to outperform. I don’t think the market has fully realized yet how much higher the cost to build a new pipeline is today vs 3 years ago, due to so much monetary inflation and supply chain issues.

The replacement and new-construction costs of all of EPD’s assets are quite high, and I think they will continue to increase.

**Risk Analysis**

I view EPD as having been significantly de-risked in recent years. As previously described, energy security is becoming more important to people and policymakers, and it’s clear that EPD’s assets will be in demand for a long time.

Political and activist opposition still does exist for pipelines, especially in certain areas. However, with energy security becoming more important for the public and policymakers, I think this risk is reducing somewhat. Plus, EPD’s primary jurisdictions have this problem less than a company like Enbridge (ENB) does with its jurisdictions, for example.

Inflation is both a good thing and a bad thing for EPD. On one hand, they have long-duration fixed-rate debt that is getting inflated away, while they hold hard pipeline assets that are basically indexed to inflation from a potential acquisition point of view. Many of their contracts adjust cashflow upwards with inflation, but not all of them, and not always at the right speed to truly keep up with inflation. Rising labor costs and materials costs can limit their overall benefit from an inflationary environment.

Like any asset-heavy business, EPD can face risks in the form of damaged pipelines, operational mistakes, natural disasters, or other things that disrupt cashflow, reputation, or both. While I consider EPD to be quite safe, the safety still should be combined with other assets in a diverse portfolio to minimize industry-specific and partnership-specific risks.

Oil and gas producers are potentially subject to windfall taxes. When prices are low, they can and do suffer losses and go bankrupt, but when prices are high, policymakers sometimes decide that their profits are excessive and that they should be subject to extra taxation. I view pipeline assets as having significantly less risk for this than producers, but it’s still a nonzero risk to be aware of if energy-related assets in general are among the top performers in the 2020s decade, as I suspect they might be.

## Final Thoughts

With US economic growth slowing, Europe facing energy security problems, and central banks tightening monetary policies despite the economic sluggishness that is occurring, this remains a risk-off period.

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-4-17-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

Demand for goods and services is moderating as fiscal stimulus subsidies, but the supply side remains heavily disrupted. Every time it starts to resolve a bit, it gets kicked back down by something new.

I continue to find good opportunities in defensive equities, such as some of the higher-quality healthcare stocks and pipeline stocks. I also like holding some cash-equivalents and gold as part of a diversified mix, especially in this specific macro environment.

There are a number of volatile assets, like bitcoin and certain growth equities, that I strongly like for the long run, but investors have to manage position sizes so that their expected level of volatility is appropriate for their financial goals, especially during a period of slowing economic growth and pressure on many financial asset prices.
