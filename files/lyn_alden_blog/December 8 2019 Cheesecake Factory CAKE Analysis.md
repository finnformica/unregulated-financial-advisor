# Cheesecake Factory Deep-Dive Analysis

*Published December 8, 2019*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This issue takes a look at the status of the U.S. business cycle, and then explores a well-managed small-cap restaurant stock with a hefty dividend that I think has significant long-term potential from current levels. A recent acquisition by the company serves as a potential catalyst for improvement.

## U.S. Business Cycle Status

As a recap, in my [free newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/november-2019-newsletter/) from three weeks ago, I discussed that we were at an unclear U.S. economic inflection point, and then two weeks ago in the [previous premium report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2019-11-24/), I gave some updated data and re-iterated this inflection point that we’re in.

Unfortunately, we still have an unclear rate-of-change direction for this inflection point and are thus still basically within a “Schrodinger’s zone” of potential divergence in both directions, but we do have some more data points to work with.

This past week, we had November ISM manufacturing and non-manufacturing purchasing manger’s indices (PMIs) come in below expectations. Analysts and economists in aggregate were expecting a bounce higher in these PMI surveys, but instead the results drifted down. The manufacturing PMI is in contraction (below 50%) with a reading of 48.1%. The non-manufacturing PMI was in slow-expansion mode at 53.9%.

![November PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-ism-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

On the other hand, we also had a better-than-expected non-farm payroll report on Friday, with 266K net jobs added in November and a dip in the official unemployment rate from 3.6% to just 3.5%. This number included the end of the GM strike, but even considering that, it was stronger than expected.

This strong jobs report was supported by two weeks in a row of relatively tame initial jobless claims reports as well. In my premium report from two weeks ago, I said we need to watch these initial jobless claims numbers. During the first two weeks of November, initial jobless claims were ticking up, indicating a potential bottom in employment, which would be very bearish. However, we’ve had two weeks in a row since then of declining initial jobless claims, which means we don’t really have a confirmed unemployment bottom yet, which is therefore neutral or bullish for now.

The Atlanta Fed GDPNow estimate is for 2.0% GDP growth in Q4, and their estimate was below 0.5% just a few weeks ago in early/mid November. New data have significantly boosted their estimate from almost-recessionary levels to moderate levels.

On the other hand, the NY Fed’s Nowcast estimate stands at 0.6% GDP growth for Q4, and 0.7% growth for Q1 of next year, which is very bearish. As they describe it, *“Negative surprises from the ISM manufacturing survey were only partially offset by positive surprises from employment data.”*

So, even different Federal Reserve bank models have conflicting views based on current data.

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy for the past five years to break it down further, measured in year-over-year percent change terms:

![December 2019 Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years (20 quarters, plus recent months where data exist), and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

The interesting pattern in the chart is that the hardest-hit deep-red indicators during the 2016 slowdown (industrial production, capital goods orders, exports, imports, CPI, and overall GDP) have been holding up better during this 2019 slowdown (just orange or yellow at the moment), while the indicators that remained strong in 2016 (construction spending, payroll growth, and yield curve steepness) are the ones deeper in the red this time around in 2019. Basically, these sets of metrics have been taking turns for their periods of strength and weakness rather than all going up or down together.

Overall, we’re still at a difficult phase in the economic cycle that doesn’t have a clear up or down trend. The stock market may have gotten a bit ahead of itself with bullishness throughout November, but overall there is no obvious deterioration in economic strength during this past month. On the other hand, there is no confirmed bounce towards a new cycle of growth just yet either.

My strategy continues to be to maintain some defensive elements in my portfolio, along with ETFs for diversification, while hunting for attractively-priced high-quality stocks domestically and abroad.

## Cheesecake Factory (CAKE) Deep Dive Analysis

Cheesecake Factory Inc. (ticker: CAKE) is a small-cap company that owns and operates a number of restaurant brands including *The Cheesecake Factory*, *North Italia*, *Social Monk Asian Kitchen*, *Grand Lux Cafe*, and a variety of small brands under the Fox Restaurant Concepts umbrella. With a market capitalization of just under $2 billion, they are roughly on the line between small-cap and mid-cap companies.

They have mildly outperformed the S&P 500 since inception, but with significant variance, including periods of dramatic outperformance during economic expansions and then reverting closer to the mean around recessionary times:

![CAKE Historical Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-cake-performance.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

Over the past 1-year, 3-year, and 5-year periods, they have underperformed despite doing quite well fundamentally, mainly due to declining valuations. This has drawn my attention over the past couple months.

This year, the company made a strategically important acquisition which was initially punished by the market, but that I think provides significant long-term growth potential to the restaurant chain. I’ll discuss this acquisition in more detail later in this report.

**Company Overview**

As the company describes in their latest 10-K filing:

> The Cheesecake Factory restaurants strive to provide a distinctive, high-quality dining experience at moderate prices by offering an extensive, innovative and evolving menu in an upscale casual, high-energy setting with attentive, efficient and friendly service. As a result, The Cheesecake Factory restaurants appeal to a diverse customer base across a broad demographic range. Our extensive menu and strategic selection of locations enable us to compete for substantially all dining preferences and occasions, from the key lunch and dinner day parts to the mid-afternoon and late-night day parts, which are traditionally weaker times for most casual dining restaurants, as well as special occasion dining. The Cheesecake Factory restaurants are generally open seven days a week for lunch and dinner, and we offer additional menu items for weekend brunch.

Most of Cheesecake Factory Inc’s restaurants, including their namesake *The Cheesecake Factory* chain, target the upscale casual market, meaning fancier than casual but not as fancy as fine dining. For example, here’s what their *The Cheesecake Factory* interiors look like:

![CAKE Interior](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-cake-interior.jpg)

*Source: Cheesecake Factory October 2019 Presentation*

*The Cheesecake Factory* locations tend to be large and very busy, especially during the late night when many other restaurants are winding down.

They have extremely broad menus, more than most restaurants, including steak, chicken, burgers, seafood, pizza, pasta, sandwiches, salads, and so forth. I personally don’t like that; I prefer restaurants that have a narrow menu that they specialize in, and for example I think *The Cheesecake Factory’s* steaks are rather poor compared to a proper steakhouse. However, a broad menu seems to have been working well for *The Cheesecake Factory*, because a whole family or dining group can find something they like on the menu.

Naturally, *The Cheesecake Factory* specializes in dessert, and gets 16% of sales from dessert, which is much higher than most restaurants. While much of their menu is not particularly special in my view, their desserts are broadly considered to be exceptional. I don’t even particularly like cheesecake but I still like theirs.

Over the past several years, third-party app-based delivery providers have expanded the restaurant’s reach. In 2009 they received 9% of sales from delivery orders, but in 2018 it was up to 14%. This is also a competitive threat, though, because delivery food via third-party apps has lower margins than dine-in eating.

The company has 202 *The Cheesecake Factory* locations in the United States and one in Canada that are company-operated. The management team believes that they can eventually expand to 300 locations in the United States and 10 in Canada. The closest location to my home is approximately 1 hour away, for example. The only location I have visited is in Florida whenever I visit family there (they love the restaurant and brought my attention to it). I have close friends that have visited multiple locations throughout the northeast USA.

The company also has 24 locations that operate in Mexico, the Middle East, and China that are licensed to third-party operators, with the first one having opened in 2012. There are currently three licensee operators of those 24 locations.

The biggest licensee by far is based in Kuwait and has 16 locations throughout multiple Middle East countries and rights/options to expand into eastern Europe. They also can expand into Egypt, which is a huge 100 million population market with a lot of potential. I was in Egypt during much of October visiting family, and malls and restaurants there (often developed operators from Gulf states like Kuwait) are absolutely booming, and many of them have American restaurant brands like *Chili’s*. I could easily see a bunch of *The Cheesecake Factory* locations sprinkled into the mix; they would fit right into the higher-end development complexes.

Another licensee is based in Mexico, currently has four locations in Mexico, and has rights/options to expand elsewhere in Latin America. The third licensee is based in Hong Kong, with four locations in China, and has rights/options for multi-country Asian expansion.

With company-operated restaurants, the company pays the upfront costs and receives an average 20-25% cash-on-cash return from those locations. For licensed locations, the company doesn’t require any capital expenditure. Instead, they receive development fees and ongoing royalty fees, and they have a team dedicated to helping the licensed operators create and operate the locations in line with the company’s standards. The company reports that each international licensed location adds about $0.01 to the stock’s overall EPS.

The company has internally developed and deployed a small number of other restaurant chains, including *Grand Lux Cafe*, *Social Monk Asian Kitchen*, and *RockSugar Southeast Asian Kitchen*.

Lastly, they now also have over 21 *North Italia* restaurant locations, and 48 diverse restaurants within their recently-acquired FRC brand that I’ll get into later in this report.

**Management and Company Metrics**

The company traces its roots back to Evelyn Overton, who opened a few initial bakery shops to supply cheesecakes to restaurants. Starting in the late 1970’s, her son David Overton started opening small *The Cheesecake Factory* restaurants, and incorporated the chain into a company in 1992.

David Overton remains the CEO to this day, and has successfully led the company with a long-term focus since its inception. Many other top executives at the company have long tenures as well. This isn’t a company that focuses on short-term quarterly results, and instead clearly focuses on long-term profitable performance and retaining their management hierarchy for long tenures.

Multiple studies show that mature/profitable companies that focus on earning high returns on invested capital (ROIC) statistically do quite well, and Overton’s company specifically looks to earn an average ROIC of over 15% per year, which they often achieve. This is evidence of a well-run operation, but with natural periods of weakness that align with recessions.

![CAKE Metrics](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-cake-roic-roe-roa.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

*The Cheesecake Factory* has a 3.7 out of 5 rating on Glassdoor.com, which puts it higher than most other large restaurant chains for employee satisfaction. *P.F. Chang’s* has a 3.3 rating, *Chili’s* has 3.4, *Olive Garden* has 3.5, and *Outback Steakhouse* has 3.6.

A few companies have higher ratings, like *Longhorn Steakhouse* has 3.9, and privately-owned *In-N-Out Burger* has an astoundingly high 4.3. Fox Restaurant Concepts, which is now a subsidiary of the company, also has an unusually high rating of 4.1.

Cheesecake Factory Inc has also been rated as one of Fortune’s Top 100 employers for the past six years in a row.

The company has historically maintained little or no long term debt, which gives them exceptional balance sheet strength for a small company. During the most recent quarter, however, they borrowed approximately $300 million in debt in order to finance the acquisition of FRC, and they plan to gradually pay this down. Even with this increased leverage, they only have a debt/income ratio of about 2-3x.

In comparison, Bloomin Brands (the parent company of Outback Steakhouse) has a debt/income ratio of over 8x, which is very high. They trade at lower valuations than Cheesecake Factory Inc but have a lot less balance sheet flexibility.

**Growth Plans and FRC Acquisition**

There was a small company called Fox Restaurant Concepts (“FRC” for short), founded by Sam Fox in 1998, that serves as an incubator/developer for new restaurant concepts. Sam Fox has developed a highly successful record of building out restaurant ideas and selling some of the mature ones to other funds or businesses.

In 2016, Cheesecake Factory Inc invested into two of Fox’s mature restaurant concepts, *North Italia* and *Flower Child*. Recently, after this multi-year period of successful co-investment, the company was in talks to purchase those mature restaurant chains from Fox. However, they decided to go a step further, and in July 2019, they announced an agreement to acquire all of Fox Restaurant Concepts. They now own *North Italia*, *Flower Child*, and a variety of smaller concepts, as well as the incubator structure itself.

Sam Fox will continue to lead FRC as an independent subsidiary of Cheesecake Factory Inc, operating as a restaurant concept incubator. *North Italia* operations will move from FRC’s HQ in Arizona to the Cheesecake Factory’s HQ in California, so that the company can begin scaling that chain up alongside *The Cheesecake Factory*. The rest of the smaller concepts will continue to be led by FRC in Arizona, with the option to scale the mature ones up over time as they become mature and proven enough.

*North Italia* currently has 21 locations, and Cheesecake Factory Inc expects that they can scale the chain to about 200 in the United States. They have a streamlined Italian upscale casual menu with an extensive wine and cocktail selection, and alcohol sales account for over 30% of total sales (compared to only 12% for *The Cheesecake Factory* restaurants). Private dining is a more significant portion of their sales than most restaurant chains. These locations are very profitable, with a 35% cash-on-cash return according to the company.

The company expects to increase the location count of *The Cheesecake Factory* by about 3% per year, while *North Italia* and various FRC locations are expected to increase by 20% per year or more. The company’s namesake brand is the mature cash-generator, while these other concepts are the growth drivers.

I think this acquisition has great synergy. FRC and their *North Italia* brand bring diversification and significant growth potential. Cheesecake Factory Inc brings plenty of capital for more rapid expansion of Fox’s mature restaurant concepts, and provides logistics and other operational infrastructure to scale them up as well.

It has a better balance sheet and stronger performance than many other restaurant chains, which affords it a higher valuation by the market. However, it is currently at-or-below its historical average valuation levels based on most time periods due to a recent bout of weakness.

The stock currently has a dividend yield of over 3.3%, and has grown the dividend each year since they started paying it in 2012.

I’m going to go ahead and just downgrade their revenue growth estimate from 8% to 6% right from the start for valuation purposes. The company grew revenue at a 3.7% annualized rate over the past five years and an 4.0% annualized rate over the past ten years.

Granted, the company now has more aggressive growth drivers post-acquisition, and they expect *The Cheesecake Factory* to drive about half of the growth in line with their historical figures (3-4%) while *North Italia* and FRC are expected to drive the other half of growth with a faster pace from their much smaller base, which gives a credible reason to believe in upcoming growth acceleration.

However, I’ll believe 8% when I see it. That’s double their 5-year and 10-year growth rates. For now, let’s conservatively say about 6% growth going forward based on significant contribution from these new opportunities. I’d rather be happily surprised to the upside rather than disappointed to the downside.

Next, the company factors in a 2.5% dividend yield, but due to lower valuations currently, it’s more like 3.3%. That’s not bad, compared to the S&P 500 average yield of around 1.8% these days.

Lastly, the company expects to reduce its share count by 1.5% per year, and to pay down acquisition debt over time even though its balance sheet remains in solid shape. Total capital returns from dividends, buybacks, and debt reduction are expected to average 5.5% per year.

At the current price of $43.22, this stock is reasonably valued for 10% expected long-term returns without factoring in valuation expansion, which I believe is better than the S&P 500 is prepared to deliver from current valuation levels.

In addition, with a strong balance sheet, good management, and a proven business model, it’s not very risky, as long as it is part of a diversified portfolio.

With a more bullish scenario of 8% growth and eventually selling the stock for a higher earnings multiple of 18, 13-14% long-term returns are indeed possible from today’s price point, but I would not use that as my base case. That’s the optimistic case. Margin improvement from *North Italia*‘s growth could also give a performance boost.

The base case is pretty good, the downside risks are real but well-managed in my view, and the upside potential is very significant.

Basically, this is an example of a stock that transitioned from a high-valuation growth stock into a more mature dividend-paying quality/value stock with a more modest growth rate. However, with the acquisition of FRC and the company’s plan to scale *North Italia* nationwide, the company has a realistic path to increase their growth rate again.

**Risks**

*Industry Competition- Moderate Risk*

The primary risk for any restaurant stock is that it ceases to have the most relevant food and environment for changing consumer preferences. The restaurant industry is one of the most challenging, and it requires good management to keep a tight ship and to continually update the menu and environment in order to maintain strong customer traffic.

The company updates their menu twice per year, including pricing changes, and in response to criticism about their large portion sizes, *The Cheesecake Factory* has introduced what they refer to as a *SkinnyLicious* menu subset that focuses on lower-calorie portions.

With Cheesecake Factory Inc’s strong management team, and with the added diversification of *North Italia* and other brands within Fox Restaurant Concepts, I believe that the company is well-protected from some of the more negative scenarios that restaurants can face. However, it’s always a risk worth monitoring.

*Margin Pressure- Major Risk*

Cheesecake Factory’s international licensed locations offer high margins, but their company-operated locations, which represent the bulk of their revenue, have been under margin pressure. Labor costs have been increasing but the company has been slow to raise menu prices. Operating margins reached a peak of 8.8% in 2016 but are currently as low as 5.9%.

The growth of *North Italia*, with its significant alcohol mix leading to 35% cash-on-cash return potential per location, may help to offset these margin pressures as that chain becomes a larger portion of the overall company.

*Executive Retention- Moderate Risk*

As previously described, Cheesecake Factory Inc has been well-managed by its co-founder, David Overton, since 1992. He is currently 73, which means he could potentially have a decade or more leading the company ahead of him, or could retire at any time. The company has a strong executive team with long tenures at the company, but losing a co-founder as its long-time CEO would bring about transition risk. Specifically, there’s always a risk with new non-founder management that the company could lose its long-term focus and become more “corporate” with an increased focus on quarterly results and executive compensation, rather than focusing on the long-term process of building a successful company as they have been doing.

On the flip side, fresh management at the top might help the company boost margins. For example, they might decide to streamline *The Cheesecake Factory’s* massive menu, which it seems to me as a customer/investor is something that is at least worth exploring.

In addition, Cheesecake Factory Inc recently acquired Fox Restaurant Concepts, which is reliant on Sam Fox’s expertise. Fox is now leading this segment as an independent subsidiary of Cheesecake Factory Inc, and he has a proven track record of successful restaurant concepts. If he were to leave the company, it would bring about significant transition risk for this smaller but promising segment of the overall company.

*Economic Cycle- Moderate Risk*

Restaurant sales are moderately affected by the economic cycle. People eat out less, and may choose less expensive options, during recessionary periods.

The U.S. restaurant industry is healthy and growing, with about $55 billion in revenue per month:

![Restaurant Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-restaurant-sales.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Industry-wide revenue grows by about 5% per year on average, but during the severe recession of 2008/2009, the year-over-year industry growth briefly dropped to about -2%:

![Restaurant Sales YoY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-restaurant-sales-yoy.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This doesn’t seem like much, but it can have a substantial impact on operating margins, which are only 5-8% for a company like Cheesecake Factory Inc. Their earnings per share dropped by over 20% during the 2008/2009 recession. For the full year 2018, the company’s earnings per share were already down 14% from their 2016 highs, and is what led to a significant part of the stock’s recent decline.

This is a risk worth factoring into growth rates, and is one reason why I instantly knocked down the company’s 8% target revenue growth to a baseline estimate of 6%. Growing by 8% represents an ideal potential year, where the industry is great and the company executes its new growth strategy well. Any long-term growth period, however, needs to assume that a recession occurs somewhere in there, severely throwing off expected growth numbers for a year or two.

*Stock Market Selloff- Moderate Risk*

Regardless of economic and earnings performance, Cheesecake’s stock could easily be subject to a sell-off should the S&P 500 face a severe correction.

This particular stock was less volatile than the S&P 500 during the 2001 recession, but far more volatile than the S&P 500 during the 2008/2009 recession, with an 80% draw down from its high point. As of today, the stock is already in a 35% draw down from its 2017 all-time high of over $64/share.

![CAKE Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-cake-drawdown.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

It’s not hard to model a pretty severe scenario. Earnings drop by 20-25%, the valuation on those earnings falls by 20-25%, and you temporarily have share prices cut in half from their previous highs.

This is the kind of stock that I approach as though I want to own the company for its future cash flows and mostly ignore fluctuating stock prices. However, those fluctuating stock prices can give us attractive entry prices to increase our position, and if the stock price ever gets way ahead of fundamentals, we can trim the position.

*Tax Changes- Moderate Risk*

U.S. corporations are enjoying the lowest tax rates they’ve had in modern history. Due to the U.S. corporate tax cuts that were passed in 2017 and came into effect in 2018, Cheesecake Factory has sharply reduced tax rates.

These tax cuts were not paid for by reduced government spending; they were merely additive to the U.S. deficit, which now stands at 5% of GDP and growing. It’s quite possible that future administrations will reverse these corporate tax cuts, which would negatively impact the net income of most U.S. companies including Cheesecake Factory Inc.

Many U.S. investors believe that they are “globally diversified” if they own an index like the S&P 500 or other broad U.S. stock index, since this collection of 500 U.S. companies gets over 40% of total revenues from outside of the United States. However, all companies in the U.S. are subject to the same jurisdictional risk- that the federal government could raise taxes on them or perform other acts to the detriment of their shareholder returns. For that reason, it’s useful to have a truly global portfolio that includes stocks headquartered outside of the United States as well.

**Conclusion**

As a high-quality stock that is 35% down from its 2017 highs due to margin pressure, acquisition risks, and overall investor appetite in growth stocks more-so than value stocks for the past several years, this stock is becoming interesting at current valuation levels.

I believe that the acquisition of Fox Restaurant Concepts is exactly what Cheesecake Factory Inc needs to boost growth and to improve margins. Sam Fox’s proven ability to create and grow new restaurant concepts, combined with *North Italia*‘s high potential to be scaled up nationwide with Cheesecake Factory Inc’s ability to invest heavily into the chain, gives the combined company a large runway for profitable growth going forward if they execute well.

Nothing is guaranteed, but with proven management, a strong plan, and reasonable valuations, the stock represents a solid risk/reward investment opportunity and robust dividend payer in my view, when held as part of a diverse portfolio.

For patient investors that want a bit more of a margin of safety than the current $43.22 share price and are willing to give up some near-term upside potential, this is also a decent stock for selling put options on as well.

For example as of this writing, investors can sell March 20th 2020 cash-secured put options at a strike price of $40 for $1.40 each. If the stock stays over $40 by the time this option expires in about 3.5 months, the investor would make a 3.6% return from the premium alone, which is higher than the full year’s worth of the stock’s dividends and represents a 13% annualized rate if repeated. On the other hand, if the stock drops below $40 before the expiration date, the options will be exercised, and the investor would acquire the stock at a cost basis of $38.60/share (based on $40 minus the $1.40 premium), which is more than a 10% discount from today’s share price and may represent a great long-term entry point.
