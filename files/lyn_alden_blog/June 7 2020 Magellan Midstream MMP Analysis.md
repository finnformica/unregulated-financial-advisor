## U.S. Business Cycle Update

This has been an unusual recession by many measures. The magnitude and speed of job losses dwarfs any recession in modern history, while the fiscal and monetary response to flood the economy and markets with liquidity (“printing money”) has also never been larger.

We find ourselves in a situation where the United States has 20-30 million job losses and high unemployment levels not seen since the Great Depression, and yet the Nasdaq 100 recently hit all time highs, and the S&P 500 is nearly positive for the year.

While this can be interpreted as a disconnect between the stock market and the real economy, if we take into account the government and central bank stimulus, the disconnect is not so large as it seems, although I think many of the mega-cap stocks are overvalued.

I highlighted this in my [May 2020 Newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2020-newsletter/), which showed how cyclical stocks and basically most stocks outside of the top 5 mega-caps in the S&P 500 were struggling year-to-date, similar to the rest of the economy. Since then, we’ve seen a bit more of a rebound in the rest of the S&P 500 as well, including cyclicals, small caps, and value stocks.

![YTD Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-ytd-performance.png)

In the [previous premium report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-5-24/) from two weeks ago, I highlighted Berkshire Hathaway and the broader XLF financial sector ETF as bullish ideas, and they took off faster from that point than I would have guessed, including a dramatic performance gap over the S&P 500 within this growth-to-value rotation that has been occurring for the moment:

I think these financial stocks have a lot of positive runway left for the long run and are still well off their highs, but this was an unusually explosive move up, so a correction at some point wouldn’t be surprising.

**Income Up, Trading Up**

About a week ago, another fascinating data point came out. National personal income for the United States in April was reported (it comes out with a lag, so this was a late May report), and it was UP rather than down. Yes, after many people lost their jobs in March and April, overall nationwide income went up. And specifically, it went up at the highest rate in year-over-year percent terms in four decades.

Nationwide personal income was $19.1 trillion (annualized rate) in February, before the Pandemic shutdown. It then dipped in March to $18.7 trillion. But in April, it had a huge jump up to $20.6 trillion. Usually, personal income goes down in recessions, but it’s up this time, which makes this period unique. The next chart shows total personal income (blue, left axis) and the year-over-year percent change of total personal income (red, right axis), and this period is truly unprecedented with both having gone straight up in April:

![Personal Income April 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-personal-income.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This is occurring due to federal stimulus. The government passed $2.7 trillion worth of stimulus programs to offset the damage from the pandemic shutdown, including sending out one-time $1,200 checks to most Americans, providing an additional $600/week in unemployment benefits on top of the normal unemployment benefits through July, and providing small business loans that can be used to pay rent and employees, and can turn into grants if employees are retained into the summer.

This fiscal spending was financed by the Treasury selling Treasury securities through the primary dealers to the Federal Reserve, and the Federal Reserve creating new dollars to buy those Treasury securities. Thus, it represents a sizable increase in the monetary base and the broad money supply.

Or as the BEA described it:

> The increase in personal income in April primarily reflected an increase in government social benefits to persons as payments were made to individuals from federal economic recovery programs in response to the COVID-19 pandemic.

Many people are making a higher income with these extra unemployment benefits than they did when they were employed. Add on the one-time $1,200 checks that most employed and unemployed folks received, and April was a great month for many people financially, rather than a bad month due to job losses. May will be less-good when the data come out 3-4 weeks from now, because it will have the extra unemployment benefits but not the one-time checks, but overall it should still be quite substantial rather than a big gap down.

Consumer spending was down in April according to the report, which we knew ahead of time due to so many businesses being shut down. So, for many people, their overall income is up and their spending levels are down because they’re stuck at home and getting big checks in the mail.

And what did they do with the difference? Especially without sports betting or casino gambling? Well, a lot of them bought stocks and options. Small retail trading activity is way up by about 5x in 2020 compared to 2019. Millions of people opened new trading accounts at Robinhood, E-Trade, TD Ameritrade, Fidelity, Schwab, and so forth, and began trading.

They’ve been particularly heavy into bullish call options. This trend was already happening in 2018 and 2019 due to zero-commission trading, but the pandemic shutdown added another huge run up in call option activity:

![Small Retail Call Options](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-small-retail-calls.jpg)

*Chart Source: [SentimenTrader](https://sentimentrader.com/)*

In front of his big audience (1.4 million Twitter followers, plus his website and other platforms), he has turned to day-trading in an era of no sports, betting a large portfolio on some of the most speculative stocks like budget airlines and such.

**The Jobs Surprise**

The most timely way to track job data is by looking at weekly initial jobless claims and continued jobless claims. At this point, continued jobless claims is the key metric to watch, and it has been in a sideways range throughout May at 20-25 million for a few weeks now:

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

There are also monthly reports on the unemployment rate and total number of non-farm employees in the U.S. workforce.

The jobs report for May came out on Friday (early June), and it was a surprise to many people. After the April report (which came out in early May) showed a loss of -20 million jobs, consensus economists were expecting a further loss of -7.5 million more jobs in May. Instead, the number surprised to the upside with a gain of +2.5 million jobs, or about 10 million jobs higher than the consensus expectation of -7.5 million. Many market participants cited this for the huge risk-on market rally on Friday.

The jobs situation, however, remains disastrous by historical standards, so the enthusiasm is centered around the idea that this is the start of a V-shaped recovery.

![Nonfarm Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/PAYEMS)*

If that employment chart above was a value stock, I’d buy it. I just don’t think it’s going straight up.

The unemployment rate was 14.7% in April, and economists were expecting it to go as high as 19% in May with this report, but instead it dipped to 13.3%, which is still higher than all recessions since the Great Depression, but better than what many were pricing into their expectations.

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-unemployment.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/UNRATE)*

A key fact, though, is that the [BLS noted an error](https://www.bls.gov/news.release/empsit.nr0.htm) in the report:

> However, there was also a large number of workers who were classified as employed but absent from work. As was the case in March and April, household survey interviewers were instructed to classify employed persons absent from work due to coronavirus-related business closures as unemployed on temporary layoff. However, it is apparent that not all such workers were so classified. BLS and the Census Bureau are investigating why this misclassification error continues to occur and are taking additional steps to address the issue.
>
> If the workers who were recorded as employed but absent from work due to “other reasons” (over and above the number absent for other reasons in a typical May) had been classified as unemployed on temporary layoff, the overall unemployment rate would have been about 3 percentage points higher than reported (on a not seasonally adjusted basis). However, according to usual practice, the data from the household survey are accepted as recorded.

Remember, small business loans were given out, and they can turn into grants if they keep employees on the payrolls through the end of June. This was structured by policymakers to incentivize small businesses to keep people employed through the pandemic shutdown. Some of those people are actually at work, while others were sent home from shutdown businesses but are still receiving paychecks through June thanks to the program. Other workers are on temporary leave; furloughs.

Many of these absent workers are supposed to be counted as effectively unemployed for statistical purposes, but as the BLS noted, many were counted as employed. The unemployment rate would be about 3% higher, or over 16% if these were counted properly. Non-farm payroll numbers would also be down by several million.

So, May’s numbers came in better than consensus expectations, but it’s still not as rosy as the headline numbers suggest. Many people are looking for a quick V-shaped recovery, meaning that we get back to the levels of February employment within a few months, effectively by the end of the year. Others have been looking for a U-shaped recovery (a more gradual recovery, likely taking years), or an L-shaped recovery (a semi-permanent loss in jobs taking an even longer time to recover from).

My base case since April has been what I’ve called, for lack of a better description, a “backward square root sign” recovery. This combines a fast V-shaped partial recovery when the pandemic shutdown ends and businesses are allowed to open, followed by a longer grind to get back up to full pre-pandemic employment levels.

![Square Root Recovery](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-square-root-recovery.png)

And this scenario, if it plays out, will be uneven by industry. Some will recover far faster than others, but that’s my base case for nationwide jobs overall.

By July, employees that would otherwise have been laid off but are still getting paychecks due to the the small business lending program loans/grants, will likely be laid off if the business hasn’t recovered enough demand to support their positions naturally, and we’ll find out about some of that in the August report. Plus, by the end of July, the government’s extra $600/week in unemployment benefits on top of the normal unemployment benefits expire.

That is, unless Congress and the President decide to pass another round of stimulus to extend some of these programs. They are already talking about the next stimulus spending round, but the question is what form will it take. Democrats want a more aggressive package, while Republicans have been focusing on a smaller package, and President Trump appears somewhat in the middle. In my view, the outcome of this is particularly important to watch given the nationwide civil unrest that has occurred over the past two weeks.

Back in September 2019, I [wrote about](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4292918-american-consumer-is-fragile-people-realize) how fragile the American consumer is. Average statistics tend to hide the median statistics, which are more relevant for most of the population. The median American has an incredibly fragile financial state, and it has worsened over the past couple decades. So it’s no wonder that we’ve seen an unprecedented bipartisan fiscal stimulus; policymakers chose that route over what would have otherwise been a very politically unpopular deflationary and solvency crisis, and that game is likely still in the early innings.

## Trust Spotlight: GBTC

In my April premium reports, I highlighted Bitcoin as a bullish small position speculation and then went long myself with a time horizon of a couple years or so. Reasons included the upcoming Bitcoin new-supply halving, excess global liquidity, and Bitcoin’s ongoing sturdy network effect after more than a decade in operation.

Although it is very risky/volatile on its own, I consider something like a 1-2% position in Bitcoin to be a hedge against massive liquidity injections by governments and central banks.

We’ve had a nice run so far as it went from under $7k per bitcoin at the time of purchase to briefly over $10k, and now hanging out in the mid-$9k+ region. But Bitcoin can be fickle with exceptionally high volatility, so a big pullback or big move up at any time is possible.

Since writing about it, I have received a number of questions regarding what the best place to hold Bitcoin is. The answer comes down in part to how hands-on you want to be.

If you are hands-on with it, the safest way is to store your own private keys. If you want to be hands-off with it, then probably the best way is to have exposure with a few different platforms so that you are not very concentrated in any one platform.

Coinbase was one of the first-movers when it comes to making Bitcoin easy to buy for folks. A lot of people in the Bitcoin community don’t like Coinbase for a variety of valid reasons, but it is very popular among folks that aren’t sure how to buy Bitcoin, and just pick the name they’ve heard of and that’s very large. So, it’s a common go-to solution. I happened to buy some there in April because I had a very old unused Coinbase account already lying around from years ago.

Kraken is another big one, and I have an account there as well.

Swan Bitcoin is a new one and has great reviews so far. They focus on dollar-cost averaging, so for folks that like to accumulate their holding over time, that’s a great place to look.

An option that I don’t particularly like in principle, but has its use, is the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC). It is publicly-traded over-the-counter, but not on major exchanges (so it’s not available in M1 Finance, but is available in, say, Fidelity or Schwab and so forth). It’s not an ETF; it’s a trust that holds bitcoins, and has a market capitalization of over $4 billion, which is very significant considering that Bitcoin’s total market capitalization is $177 billion as of this writing.

Buying shares of GBTC is a way to get exposure to Bitcoin within a brokerage account, including in a tax-advantaged account like an IRA. That’s what makes it unique and useful. Despite its usefulness, however, there are a few issues with it.

One issue is that they charge a 2% annual fee. This should ideally be lower, but they simplify don’t have much competition.

A second issue is that the trust uses Coinbase as its custodian, so there is a lot of centralization there.

A third issue is that the trust routinely trades at a premium to its net asset value. Their NAV per share is $9.36 as of this writing (basically, that’s a measure of how much their holding of Bitcoin is worth per share based on current prices), while the share price of the trust is $11.25. So, the share price has more than a 20% premium over the net asset value of actual Bitcoin held.

The premium over NAV varies significantly over time:

![GBTC Premium to NAV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-gbtc-premium-to-nav.png)

This premium over NAV is partly for convenience (holding through an existing brokerage account rather than people having to google “how to buy Bitcoin” and then looking up the various exchanges and creating an account at one), but also for tax reasons. This is a very volatile asset class, so gains can be big and thus capital gains taxes can be big. For example, let’s say you buy Bitcoin at $7k and it doubles to $14k, and you sell it. That’s going to be a big capital gains tax. But GBTC lets you hold it in something like a Roth IRA, which is tax free.

So if you want that little speculative 1% position to hold for the next couple of years for example, it can be stashed away in a tax-advantaged account with GBTC. The tax savings help to justify the premium over NAV, as long as the premium is near the bottom end of its historical range.

Whenever Bitcoin has a huge run-up in price, GBTC tends to get a bigger-than-normal premium to NAV as investors flood into it. This means that during big upward moves, GBTC’s share price often outperforms Bitcoin itself. Here’s a snapshot of 2017, for example:

![GBTC vs BTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-gbtc-vs-bitcoin.png)

In June 2017, Bitcoin was up over 150% for the year while GBTC was up over 300%. Then again in August 2017, there was a period where Bitcoin was up nearly 400% while GBTC was up over 700%. During that end-of-year 2017 melt-up, Bitcoin hit a high of over 1,800% gains for the year, while GBTC was up over 2,700%.

So, if there is another bullish run in Bitcoin in the next couple years, GBTC could briefly outperform. If there is a Bitcoin spike and the GBTC premium over NAV blows out to huge levels again, it would be a good idea to sell GBTC and lock in that gift of a gain.

On the other hand, if a straight-up Bitcoin ETF ever becomes available, GBTC’s premium to NAV could go away. Investors in the trust could have 20% downside losses just to get back to NAV at current levels.

So, I don’t particularly like GBTC in principle, but I’m highlighting it as a long idea, and I am bullish on GBTC as a small, volatile, speculative position with a 2-year timeframe.

## Magellan Midstream Partners LP (MMP) Deep-Dive Analysis

Magellan Midstream (MMP) is a pipeline company that transports oil and refined products to several states in the United States. They are the largest refined products transporter in the country, and that accounts for nearly two thirds of their business. The remainder of their business is mostly crude oil transport.

They are a master limited partnership rather than a corporation, meaning they issue a K-1 during tax season. Turbo Tax and other providers handle those forms quite easily, but it does represent an additional form during tax season to deal with.

The main selling point for the business is that they currently pay a 8.5% annual distribution yield, and they’ve been able to maintain and grow that distribution through all volatile periods for the energy sector for twenty consecutive years since inception. That doesn’t guarantee future payouts, but it shows the historical resiliency of those payouts, which cannot be said for the majority of the midstream industry.

Like many stocks, Magellan had a brutal sell-off in March, but is recovering strongly and has a positive MACD crossover on the weekly chart from low levels.

![Magellan Midstream Technical Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-technical.png)

Here’s how Magellan describes their operations in their most recent quarterly report:

> We are principally engaged in the transportation, storage and distribution of refined petroleum products and  
> crude oil. As of March 31, 2020, our asset portfolio consisted of:

* > our refined products segment, comprised of our approximately 9,800-mile refined products pipeline system with 53 connected terminals, as well as 25 independent terminals not connected to our pipeline system and two marine storage terminals (one of which is owned through a joint venture); and
* > our crude oil segment, comprised of approximately 2,200 miles of crude oil pipelines, a condensate splitter and 37 million barrels of aggregate storage capacity, of which approximately 25 million barrels are used for contract storage. Approximately 1,000 miles of these pipelines, the condensate splitter and 30 million barrels of this storage capacity (including 22 million barrels used for contract storage) are wholly-owned,with the remainder owned through joint ventures.

“Refined products” is a broad category. As Magellan explains it:

> Refined products are the output from crude oil refineries that are primarily used as fuels by consumers. Refined products include gasoline, diesel fuel, aviation fuel, kerosene and heating oil.

Magellan previously had a separate marine storage/terminal business as well, but they recently sold a significant portion of that to another pipeline business, and used the proceeds to buy back some Magellan units, meaning they streamlined the business a bit. The remaining marine assets were consolidated into the other two segments: refined and crude.

Here is Magellan’s asset map as of March 2020:

![Magellan Midstream Asset Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/2020-6-7-mmp-asset-map.jpg)

*Image Source: [Magellan Midstream](https://www.magellanlp.com/WhatWeDo/AssetMap.aspx)*

Historically, Magellan is very insulated from changes in commodity prices. They mostly transport crude oil and refined products around the country, and collect fees based on volume, and much of that volume is contracted. Oil supply is more volatile than refined product demand, and Magellan’s refined business is about refined product demand.

The pandemic shutdown that stopped people from driving or flying to many places for months, and shut down a lot of manufacturing that uses refined products as ingredients, significantly reduced gasoline and other refined product demand, so that was a rare setback for Magellan’s business. However, Magellan has fundamentally held up well thanks to having among the least debt in the whole midstream industry, so a few months of disruption is not a huge deal for them. The main risk is about what refined product demand does over the next 5-10 years.

**Management and Performance**

Since inception in the early 2000’s, Magellan has trounced the broad market in terms of total performance, as they went from small-cap to mid-cap while paying out high distribution yields:

![Magellan Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-performance.png)

However, their price performance has stagnated and turned downward in the past five years. As will be described in the next section on valuation, this is more a function of valuation, and not really a management or fundamental performance issue.

The business model is simple. They used to grow fast and pay smaller distributions. Currently, they grow more slowly, but at lower valuation levels they pay a very high distribution yield of 8.5%, which is covered by distributable cash flow. Therefore, they don’t need much growth to provide good returns at these levels; they just need to maintain their current business and grow their pricing line with inflation.

Magellan used to have a separate GP and LP structure, and LPs paid incentive distribution rights to the GP. Magellan was an early mover in the industry to consolidate this framework and simply the company structure, so they no longer deal with incentive distribution rights. This reduces the cost of capital. Magellan was also one of the first midstream businesses to stop relying on external funding; for many years they have not been reliant on issuing units to fund growth, and instead can fund it internally by paying out smaller but more secure distributions than many of their midstream peers, and using leftover funds to pay for growth projects. So, Magellan management has been consistently ahead of the curve when it comes to trends in the midstream industry.

Lastly, Magellan maintains among the least leverage in the industry. Their target is to maintain debt levels of less than 4x their distributable cash flow, and they are currently under that threshold with 3.4x-3.7x levels according to 2020 executive estimates.

In terms of debt-to-EBITDA, they are lower than other pipeline businesses such as Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) and Kinder Morgan (KMI). It doesn’t really get any less leveraged than this in the midstream sector:

![MMP Debt to EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-midstream-debt-ebitda.png)

This is why Magellan is one of the three popular “blue chips” in the midstream industry, meaning they are tied as having the highest credit rating among large midstream operators. Magellan, Enbridge (ENB), and Enterprise (EPD) are the three popular midstream businesses that have a BBB+ credit rating, which is at the high end of the spectrum for this very asset-heavy industry. And among those three blue chips, Magellan has the least leverage relative to its EBITDA and operating profits, although they are also the smallest and most concentrated of the three, with only a $10 billion market capitalization compared to the other two that are worth tens of billions.

Whenever there is a crash in energy prices, many midstream companies have to cut their distributions, but a few like Magellan just keep paying and growing distributions through the crisis. For example, in the chart above when Kinder Morgan (blue line) went above 7x debt-to-EBITDA as a result of the 2015 oil price crash, they ended up having to cut their distribution and still haven’t recovered years later. But Enterprise and Magellan just kept chugging along thanks in great part to their low leverage ratios. Magellan has raised its distribution for 20 years in a row.

**Valuation Analysis**

After the sector performed extraordinary well from 2000-2015, many midstream operators became overvalued during the 2013-2015 timeframe, including Magellan. After the price of oil crashed in 2015 and it put pressure on the industry, many weaker players went bust or cut their distributions, while the strongest players in the industry like Magellan just kept growing and paying distributions, but at a lower growth rate.

![MMP Adjusted Earnings FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

![MMP EBITDA FAST Graphs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-ebitda.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

I’ve been bullish on some of these stronger midstream players over the past few years at various times during this lower valuation period, and they’ve only gotten cheaper, with higher yields. After this pandemic crash, Magellan is extremely cheap in my view.

Their distribution yield reached an all-time high in March, and even after a partial recovery, it is as high as it was in 2009, which was a great buying opportunity.

![MMP Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-yield.png)

Magellan management estimates that they can comfortably cover their distribution in 2020, and from there it should improve further.

Here is their historical EV/EBITDA ratio, which is the lowest it has been in a decade:

![MMP EV to EBITDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-ev-ebitda.png)

In May 2020, during this crisis, Magellan was easily able to sell bonds that mature in 2030 for a yield of just 3.25%, which gives them an excellent cost of capital thanks to their low leverage levels. So they borrow at low yields, maintain low leverage, and earn much higher rates of return on their operations.

For a dividend discount model with [StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/stockdelver-book/), I’ll use a bearish scenario.

Let’s say, hypothetically, that U.S. refined products demand peaks at recent levels, and just goes sideways. So, Magellan has no growth and just raises its prices in line with inflation in this scenario, and I’ll use the Federal Reserve’s 2% average inflation target as a baseline. Magellan’s debt is fixed-rate, so a higher inflation environment would probably benefit them due to their capital structure.

I’m not saying they won’t have growth; I’m saying let’s imagine they don’t have growth, and see what its value should be in that scenario.

Currently, Magellan trades for under $48, but the fair price for 10% long-term returns according to this model is over $52:

![MMP StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-7-mmp-stockdelver.png)

With 2% inflation, that would be 8% real returns per year over the long run, without really growing, just by not shrinking and by paying a high distribution each quarter. Frankly, I don’t expect the S&P 500 to produce 8% annualized real returns from 2020 through 2030.

Even if Magellan were to start shrinking volumes by 1% per year, that would still result in perhaps 6-7% real annualized returns, assuming they can keep pricing levels in line with inflation.

Overall, it doesn’t take a very bullish assumption for this stock at current levels to likely provide good returns throughout the 2020’s decade.

**Company Risks**

*Demand Risk*

Historically, gasoline demand, diesel demand, and jet fuel demand didn’t fluctuate too much from year to year. Even though oil prices can be very volatile, demand for energy tends to be pretty consistent. The pandemic shutdown stopped people from driving or flying or in many cases working, which caused a rare massive decline in energy demand. Magellan’s cash flows are primarily tied to demand for these products.

Electric vehicles present a long-term threat to Magellan’s gasoline business (but not really their diesel, jet fuel, or other refined products businesses). However, electric car adoption is slower than many headlines suggest, and still accounts for a very small percentage of cars on the road. Low gasoline prices also reduce incentives for consumers to switch to electric vehicles.

Plus, electricity itself is not a form of energy production. It is a transmission of energy from the electrical grid, which primarily gets its energy production from natural gas, coal, nuclear, hydro, and a bit of oil and solar/wind. In addition, electric vehicles require much larger amounts of semi-rare metals, such as copper, silver, cobalt, nickel, and others. One way to hedge Magellan’s risk against electric vehicles is to own producers of those types of metals that electric vehicles heavily use, which I’m also bullish on.

Therefore, as part of a diversified infrastructure/commodities exposure within a portfolio, I like Magellan at current valuation levels.

*Commodity Exposure*

Many pipeline companies claim to be like toll booths, just collecting fees from volumes without any commodity exposure. However, every time there is an oil price crash, many of them get exposed as not being very resilient to oil price declines, and end up cutting their distributions.

A few MLPs like Magellan, however, truly have acted as toll booths, maintaining and growing distributions for years and decades.

The partnership has historically sought to keep 85% or more of their operating margin in fee-based, low-risk projects.

Most of their modest commodity risk is due to butane blending, which generates $100+ million or more for Magellan per year, but is highly variable. They make more money from this activity when there is a wide price difference between butane and gasoline, which generally occurs when gasoline is at higher prices.

The way it works is pretty simple in principle, at least before RINs and hedging are taken into account. Butane is cheaper than gasoline, but has a high Reid vapor pressure, or RVP. The more butane you add to gasoline, the higher the RVP of the mixture is, which makes it more prone to boiling off into the atmosphere and contributing to smog during the summer. The Environmental Protection Agency therefore historically [sets reduced limits](https://www.epa.gov/gasoline-standards/gasoline-reid-vapor-pressure) on RVP for gasoline during the summer, which means businesses can’t put as much butane in, and that contributes to generally higher gasoline prices in summer months.

When Magellan receives product to transport, it checks the current RVP of the mixture, and if it’s below the allowed threshold, they can inject some inexpensive butane to increase the overall volume of gasoline they can sell at the end of their portion of transportation chain, which means more profits for them. And the bigger the cost difference between gasoline and butane, the more profitable it is per gallon to do this.

In any given year, these commodity-related profits contribute $100-$200+ billion in operating margin to Magellan, so they can be significant but non-critical fluctuations in Magellan’s overall results.

*Pandemic Second-Wave*

There is much concern among investors about what will happen to the economy if there is a second wave of COVID-19. Many countries have significantly flattened the transmission curve, but there are concerns that it could come back in the autumn and winter when the economy is trying to get back to normal.

If this occurs, some countries may perform another economic lock-down. If the United States does this, it would sharply reduce the demand for refined product again due to lack of flying and driving and manufacturing. However, civil unrest has made this a lot more complicated, and further government-mandated lock-downs seem less likely given the severity of the economic damage and the fact that people are now accustomed to social distancing, wearing masks, and so forth. For as long as COVID-19 remains with us, it seems more likely that it will be up to people to manage their own exposure based on their vulnerability to it.

If one of these worst-case scenarios plays out, such as another economic lock-down or a big deflationary/solvency crisis that goes unaddressed, a distribution cut for Magellan is not impossible. However, unlike Kinder Morgan cutting their distribution several years ago, it would not be due to an insolvent capital structure, and instead would be due to extreme fluctuations in product demand despite having a solid capital structure. That means, in theory, any unlikely-but-possible distribution cuts from Magellan should be a temporary measure to maintain liquidity, rather than as a result of permanent losses of capital. In the event that they do cut their distribution due to a second wave or other severe demand shock in the year ahead, I would expect it to be restored somewhat quickly when that period resolves.

Unlike driving, flying in general is likely to be muted for quite a while. It took several years for flight volumes in the United States to get back to highs after the 9/11 terrorist attacks, and it will probably take years to recover this time as well. That will be a headwind against jet fuel transport volumes. This is factored into my bearish “no growth” scenario for Magellan.

**Conclusion**

A big question in the coming months is to what degree the U.S. government and central bank will attempt to withdraw stimulus and try to let the weak economy stand on its own two feet (thus risking another deflationary period, more consumer and business insolvency, and a likely market sell-off), or attempt to continue supporting the economy and the market to fend off civil unrest and mitigate widespread business collapse (thus risking inflation and ongoing mal-investment with so much intervention).

Overall, the many tech growth stocks and speculative deep-value stocks are showing a lot of enthusiasm in their prices lately, partially thanks to unprecedented bullishness from small retail traders.

However, retail traders are not speculating in boring things like Magellan, which remains relatively cheap compared to its expected fundamental performance, even in a no-growth scenario for the 2020’s. Magellan is neither a high-flying tech stock or a nearly-insolvent speculative value play (both of which are currently popular among the Robinhood App demographic), and instead stands somewhere in the middle as a solvent business that has been having a relatively rough time lately. This makes it more suitable as a high-yielding longer-term investment within a diversified portfolio.

Magellan does have risks related to refined product demand and electric vehicles, but these are offset by an extraordinary strong balance sheet and smart management, and can be further hedged by pairing it with exposure to the commodity producers that make the metals required for electric vehicles. I’m also bullish on many of those metals producers and include them in my portfolios.

As a reminder, after such volatile market activity over the past few months, investors may wish to check their portfolios to see how their actual allocations may have deviated from target allocations, and to consider rebalancing as appropriate in line with their personal risk profiles.
