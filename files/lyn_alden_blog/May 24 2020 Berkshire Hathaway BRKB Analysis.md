# Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B) Deep Dive Analysis

*Published May 24, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report focuses on some macro updates, provides portfolio updates, and then dives into one of the most macro-vulnerable sectors: the financial sector.

## U.S. Business Cycle Update

The U.S. continues to experience job losses, with weekly initial jobless claims coming in at seven figures per week, but at a slower pace than in March and April. Meanwhile, the total number of lost jobs (represented weekly with a bit of a lag by continued jobless claims) continues to accumulate, and is now up to over 25 million.

This chart shows weekly initial jobless claims (blue, left axis) and weekly continued jobless claims (red, right axis) in one chart:

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The previous two recessions had a peak of fewer than 4 million continued jobless claims (2003) and fewer than 7 million continued jobless claims (2009), so those figures are dwarfed by the current number of 25+ million continued jobless claims and rising:

![Historical Continued Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-historical-continued-claims.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/CCSA)*

It is claimed that a lot of these are temporary job losses, and are ready to bounce right back as quarantines lift. We’ll see in time how correct that is, but consider a hypothetical outcome:

Initial claims are still coming in at over 2 million per week, and the 25+ million in continued claims lags that figure by a week and doesn’t include all job losses. Suppose that this figure eventually tops out at 30 million, as a nice round number.

If half of those job losses are temporary and magically snap right back into the workforce this summer when quarantines lift and people try to resume more normal lifestyles, that would still leave 15 million continued jobless claims, or far more than twice the number that there was in 2009. If 75% snap right back magically, that would still leave 7.5 million, which is more than the 2009 peak.

Whatever the ratio is, it’s not going to be a 100% jobs snap-back. Restaurants, airlines, casinos, cruises, theme parks, and hotels are not going to all come back to 100% capacity at once, and some of the weaker ones won’t come back at all. Many physical retail chains have already announced permanent store closings or in a few cases, total liquidations, in 2020.

Suppliers to those various businesses have been impacted and have reduced their operations, ranging from food suppliers to accountants. Other companies that haven’t been directly impacted from COVID-19 have laid off workers as well, ranging from Cisco to IBM, as they seek to streamline and maximize profitability.

The sheer magnitude of the job losses was driven by virus-related shutdowns, but this surely has triggered a credit cycle event (business cycle recession) as well, which means that employers don’t hand out jobs easily anymore. Times are tough and they’re trying to cut costs and figure out what they really need at the moment.

The Federal CARES Act (the massive $2.2 trillion stimulus passed by Congress and signed into law by the President) is providing an extra $600/week in unemployment benefits to these folks with lost jobs, on top of the normal unemployment benefits and in addition to the one-time $1,200 checks that went out to most Americans. This $600/week federal unemployment bonus expires at the end of July 2020 (a little over 2 months from now), and Senate leader McConnell has indicated that he is opposed to continuing it. This might be a bargaining chip to get something he wants from Democrats in favor of agreeing to it, but time will tell. One of the issues with the program is that a decent percentage of people in the program get more income from unemployment than they would if they were working in their old jobs, which is a poor incentive structure. Maybe they can tweak the numbers going forward.

It costs $15 billion per week by the federal government to pay 25 million people $600/week on top of their normal benefits. That is $65 billion per month or $780 billion per year.

So, towards the end of this summer, unless we get a sudden 90%+ bounce-back in jobs, we’ll likely either see the prospect for a deflationary crunch as millions of unemployed people start to get a lot less money, or we’ll see further deficit spending by the federal government, which the Federal Reserve will likely monetize with a re-acceleration of QE. Lawmakers are already working on the next stage of fiscal stimulus; it’s just a question of what that stimulus will spend money on and how big that stimulus will be.

**Retail Traders**

As people sit at home but still receive large checks, there has been an explosion in day-trading activity by small non-professional investors.

The number of trades at major online brokerages saw a sizable jump in 2018 and 2019 due to the rise of zero-commission trading, but even that upward move has been dwarfed by what happened in 2020. We’ve literally had a 4x rise in trades:

![Number of Trades](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-number-of-trades.jpg)

*Chart Source: [SentimenTrader](https://sentimentrader.com/)*

And they’re largely bullish, including record levels of call option purchases, especially after this stock rally from the March lows went up pretty far:

![Small Trader Call Options](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-small-trader-calls.jpg)

*Chart Source: [SentimenTrader](https://sentimentrader.com/)*

Most of what they’re buying are American stocks. This is more-so an American cultural phenomenon than a total-world phenomenon. They’re buying major American ETFs like SPY/QQQ/IWM, and they’re buying the big tech names as well as some beaten-down value names, but either way it’s mainly American names.

Meanwhile, investment legends with decades of outperformance history, like Warren Buffett and Stanley Druckenmiller, have recently come out as cautious after this market run-up.

In fact, Buffett sold his company’s billions of dollars worth of airline stocks over the past quarter, despite his capital loss on the position, while small Robinhood investors were buying airline stocks in record amounts. American Airlines is the 4th most-owned stock on Robinhood, and Delta Airlines is the 5th. Here’s American Airlines on Robinhood:

![American Airlines Robinhood](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-robinhood-aal.png)

*Chart Source: [Robintrack](https://robintrack.net/symbol/AAL)*

So we have small retail “new account” investor sentiment as extremely bullish, while larger money is skeptical. And we have Warren Buffett selling his airline stocks to (mostly young) small account Robinhood buyers.

Robinhood traders have also bought a huge amount of car rental company Hertz stock since March thinking it was a value, and then Hertz filed for bankruptcy this weekend.

Buffett has been criticized for buying high and selling low during this 2020 crash (against what his usual long-term approach is), but the vast majority of his positions are unchanged, and he specifically sold stocks that are the most-harmed by COVID-19 and that likely have a long road ahead for recovery. In other words, he sold stocks for which his core thesis changed.

I sympathize with him on that. I analyzed cruise ship company Carnival in December 2019 after it had fallen by quite a bit, and I became bullish at what were relatively low price levels at the time. After peaking in the $70’s, Carnival stock fell into the $40’s and was starting to experience a rebound, and that is when I bought, especially because it had the strongest balance sheet in the industry. Even with a probable recession scenario in the cards, I liked Carnival fundamentally and in terms of valuation at that level and was willing to hold it through most types of events.

However, a once-in-a-century global pandemic and government/cultural response that literally shuts all cruises down worldwide, changed the thesis. That was the worst-of-the-worst tail risk for a stock like that. I sold my Carnival position on March 10th at $24/share for a loss, after announcing in the March 9th premium report that I would do so. It was a small 1.4% position as part of a diversified portfolio, but I cut losses when it was clear that the solvency of the business was in question. Even that sale was frankly later than it should have been sold, but after I sold, the stock fell all the way to below $8, and has since rebounded to $14. It would need a 70% gain from where it is now just to break even with the $24 price point at which I sold it.

And funnily enough, Carnival is the 8th most popular stock on Robinhood. They bought what I was selling.

I’m all for buying value at the current time. I like certain banks and certain industrials and certain emerging market companies, for example, as I explained in my [First Liquidity, Then Solvency](https://seekingalpha.com/article/4348831-first-liquidity-solvency) article. But in my opinion it needs to be smart value, cautious value, rather than “knee-jerk buy low” value. What is cheap, can get cheaper, if the fundamentals simply aren’t there.

## ETF Spotlight: XLF

For investors that want some risk and value in their portfolio, I think there is a much better risk/reward scenario in bank stocks than in airlines at the moment. The SPDR XLF S&P 500 Financials Sector ETF (XLF) offers an easy low-cost way to get exposure.

The financial sector has seriously underperformed the broad S&P 500 year-to-date:

![XLF YTD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-xlf-ytd.png)

During the 2008 recession, banks were at the heart of the crisis. They had record amounts of leverage and had gambled on speculative housing loans, and all of that collapsed. Many weaker banks failed, and a combination of bailouts and quantitative easing recapitalized the rest of the banking sector. From then, large banks had more strict regulations about leverage and maintaining high levels of reserves, and were stress-tested each year to ensure they could handle a crisis as bad as 2008.

In this 2020 crisis, banks themselves are not at the heart of the problem. The rest of the economy is where the heart of the problem is, with tons of people out of work and with tons of businesses shut down, but with high debt levels still on their books. Banks are exposed indirectly (but very seriously) due to loans they provide consumers and companies with, but this time, banks do have a lot of reserves and have used more strict lending standards. So, banks have been beaten-down due to their cyclical exposure to the economy that is experiencing unprecedented job losses and economic shutdowns, but it’s a very different core issue than in 2008 when banks were at the epicenter.

This next chart captures it well. The blue line shows cash as a percentage of total assets among all large U.S. banks. The red line is Treasury and securities as a percentage of their total assets:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-bank-cash.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

During the run-up to the 2008 crisis, banks (which were rather unregulated at the time) feasted on low interest rates and speculated on the housing market. They went into the crisis with only 3% of their assets as cash, and about 11% of their assets as Treasuries. Much of their assets were in loans, particularly in the housing market.

Since the crisis ended, regulations have mandated higher liquidity levels. Large banks have stayed at around 8% cash as a percentage of assets or above over the past decade. They went into this crisis with more than twice as much cash and Treasuries as a percentage of assets as they had in 2008. In other words, a banking collapse is not the heart of this crisis like it was in 2008.

The initial quantitative easing by the Federal Reserve during the 2008 crisis pushed banks from 3% up to 8% cash levels, and a late round of QE in 2014 pushed them further up to 16% cash levels, which they used over time, mainly to buy Treasuries.

Recently, this current round of QE by the Federal Reserve re-stocked banks up to a whopping 15% cash levels, which is near their all-time peak that they reached in 2014. Their combined cash-and-Treasury position is currently at all-time-highs of nearly 35%; higher than in 2014 and any other time in modern history.

So yes, banks will have terrible 2020 earnings numbers and plenty of losses, but they are stocked up with record cash and Treasury reserves to take the hit, and their stock prices have already been slammed to low levels. Meanwhile, the government is bailing out consumers with record amounts of unemployment benefits and helicopter money checks to prevent mass consumer bankruptcy. There have been and will be plenty of bankruptcies, but the question is how widespread they will become.

If we chart bank cash levels alongside price action of the XLF ETF, we can see that by the time the Federal Reserve re-capitalized banks during the previous crisis, the low point in stock prices for the financial sector was basically in, and it was a good long-term buying opportunity from that point, despite low interest rates and ongoing economic problems.

![XLF vs Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-bank-cash-vs-xlf.png)

Everything moved faster this time and banks have already been filled up with cash, and at almost twice as high on a percentage-of-assets basis (15% in 2020 vs 8% in 2009, plus higher Treasury security allocations this time as well).

Gameblazer06, an anonymous expert on the financial sector who I’ve conversed with over the past year and who has really nailed a bunch of trades recently, posted a great chart the other day on FinTwit showing that banks, in terms of price to tangible book value ratios, are at more than 25-year lows:

![Banks Price to Tangible Book](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-bank-tangible-book.jpg)

The ratio of XLF to the broader SPY S&P 500 ETF is also at its all-time low since inception in 1998, matched only by the low point in 2009:

![XLF-to-SPY Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-xlf-ratio.png)

I think the financial sector will fundamentally have a rough time over the next few years, but that current low prices already reflect that. Meanwhile, mega-cap tech stocks are trading at near record valuations with high investor sentiment.

I’m pretty bullish for the long run on the beaten-down large cap U.S. banking sector from these low levels, as they’re stocked full of record cash and Treasury levels, and their customers (consumers and businesses) are receiving record amounts of government financial support.

Nobody likes them, nobody wants to buy them, they’re cyclical, they’re not pretty, and it’s easy to come up with valid reasons to dislike the financial sector at the current time and wait until everything looks great. But in my view, there’s a good probability that large bank stocks will climb the wall of worry over the next several years from these low levels.

The most likely threat to the financial sector in my opinion is not mass insolvency. Congress and the Federal Reserve will do enough to mitigate that for consumers and companies broadly as a matter of national security. Unlike airline stocks that can go down by themselves as a sector, bank stocks at this huge level of capitalization would only go down as a sector if the broader economy goes down for the count.

Instead, likely the biggest threat for the financial sector is low or negative interest rates, which have caused long-term harm to the banking sectors of Japan and Europe. However, with the amount of fiscal response to the COVID-19 crisis in 2020, the sheer amount of unemployment and ongoing fiscal spending that is likely to come, I think the risk of low interest rates for banks is overblown in the United States after taking into account these low bank stock price levels, especially for large banks that have plenty of fee-based income, which gives them a buffer against perpetually low rates.

Those long periods of bank stagnation in Japan and Europe occurred during a period of tight fiscal policy and ultra-loose monetary policy. Going forward, ultra-loose fiscal policy is likely to occur in the U.S. and the rest of the developed world, which is a different type of environment.

Yes, nominal rates will be low, and the Federal Reserve will likely lock Treasury yields at low levels with yield curve control. However, I do not think rates will be quite as low for as long in the United States as they have been in Japan and Europe, and that the expectation for low rates is already reflected in current low U.S. bank share prices.

## Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.B) Deep-Dive Analysis

Warren Buffett’s company Berkshire Hathaway is a pretty good deal at the moment, in my view.

This isn’t surprising, given that Berkshire is the largest component of the aforementioned XLF Financial Sector ETF, and [Berkshire’s portfolio](https://www.cnbc.com/berkshire-hathaway-portfolio/) of public stocks is filled with bank stocks including Wells Fargo, American Express, J.P. Morgan Chase, PNC Financial Services, U.S. Bancorp, Bank of America, and Bank of New York Mellon. Buying BRK.B instead of XLF is another way of getting broad U.S. financial sector exposure.

Berkshire is a conglomerate. At the core of their business is their insurance operations, including Geico and Berkshire’s other insurance brands. Warren Buffett invests the insurance float into stocks, T-bills, and some wholly-owned subsidiaries. The subsidiaries are numerous, but include large “hard asset” companies like the Burlington Northern Santa Fe Railway (one of the top-two freight rail systems in the United States), Precision Castparts (engineered components), Lubrizol (specialty chemicals), and Clayton Homes (the largest builder of manufactured homes in the country) as well as utilities and a variety of specialized retail businesses.

The company currently has $137 billion in cash, cash-equivalents, and short-term investments (T-bills) on its balance sheet, which along with the likes of Alphabet (GOOGL) and Microsoft (MSFT), makes Berkshire a contender for one of the top 5 fortress balance sheets out of all companies in the world.

**Management and Performance**

Berkshire Hathaway has been one of the best-performing investments in history, as Buffett has used a counter-cyclical investment approach to buy assets cheaply, and he maintains a fortress balance sheet through all market conditions. Quantitative evidence from various research papers has shown that it’s not necessarily Buffett’s stock-picking that has done so well, it’s the fact that he rarely takes massive losses in book value, and he uses low-cost leverage from his insurance float to invest in stable businesses with high returns on capital.

Since the late 1990’s (and even more so in the decades before that), Berkshire has outperformed the S&P 500 and the financial sector (of which his company is the biggest component):

![Berkshire Long-Term Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-brkb-performance.png)

However, the 2010’s decades was a rough one for banks in relative terms. The 2000 decade was tough for overvalued tech stocks after the Dotcom Bubble, whereas the 2010’s was great for tech stocks, since they started from much cheaper levels. As such, Berkshire has significantly underperformed the tech-heavy S&P 500 over the past decade, although it did slightly beat the broader financial sector:

![Berkshire 10-Year Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-berkshire-performance-10yr.png)

I think the 2020’s could be a better decade for the financial sector on a relative basis. Technology stocks are already quite expensive, and a shift from disinflation to reflation could hurt expensive tech and help cheap financials on a relative basis.

**Valuation Analysis**

Berkshire, by many metrics, is reasonably-valued relative to its fundamentals. In simple terms, “buying whenever the black line goes well below the blue line” has historically worked out well for the long run:

![Berkshire Hathaway F.A.S.T. Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-brkb-fg.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Because Berkshire is a conglomerate and has almost $200 billion worth of public equities, it’s not a great candidate for a traditional StockDelver valuation analysis. After 2019 was a negative year for earnings, analysts expect another down year in 2020, bringing the adjusted EPS to $9.00. From this low point, analysts expect a 14% annual growth rate over the next two years to $11.74/share in adjusted EPS in 2022.

Will that come to pass? I don’t know. Too much depends on what fiscal stimulus ends up happening in 2020 and 2021. This is a very macro-driven environment so I won’t try to estimate specific growth rates for a conglomerate such as Berkshire. I like it more as a relatively cheap macro play that blends Buffett-selected financials with hard-asset cyclicals.

The cyclically-adjusted price-to-earnings ratio is a useful way to value stocks over the course of a business cycle. Any one year can be particularly good or bad. The “PE 5” divides the current price by the average of the past 5 years of real earnings. The “PE 10” divides the current price by the average of the past 10 years of real earnings. Both measures for Berkshire are well below their recent averages, at valuations not seen since 2009:

![Berkshire PE 5 and PE 10](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-berkshire-pe5-pe10.png)

Berkshire’s price to tangible book value is also low for this cycle:

![BRKB Price to Tangible Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-5-24-brkb-p-tbv.png)

**Company Risks**

*Leadership Ages*

Berkshire Hathaway Chairman and CEO Warren Buffett is about to turn 90 years old this summer, and his investment performance hasn’t been as strong in recent decades as it was in prior decades, mainly because he under-invested in technology companies. Berkshire’s Vice Chairman, Charlie Munger, is 96 years old. Hopefully they have many years ahead of them, but in terms of risk mitigation, their ages are important now. Buffett has acknowledged this repeatedly, including in the 2019 company annual report that was released earlier this year.

The company has a succession plan in place, with Ajit Jain and Greg Abel currently running a lot of the day-to-day operations of the company. Jain runs the insurance portion of the business while Abel runs the non-insurance portions.

Whenever the day comes for which it is announced that Buffett is no longer leading the company, Berkshire stock could take a price hit. However, after that point, there is a lot of value to unlock, and I wouldn’t be surprised if that day turns out to be a “buy the bad news” sort of event. There would probably be a lot of changes from Jain and Abel over the following years, including either the beginning of a dividend payout, or an eventual spinning off of several of Berkshire’s subsidiaries to streamline the sprawling business empire that it has become.

*Interest Rates*

The biggest long-term risk for the financial sector is low interest rates, and Berkshire has a large insurance and bank stock portfolio. Banks make a lot of their money on the difference between long-term rates and short-term rates, and insurance companies makes a lot of money on Treasuries and other safe bonds.

We’re in an extremely low interest rate environment, and this sort of environment has hurt the profitability of Japan’s banks and insurers, and Europe’s banks and insurers, for decades.

Many investors have cited this as a reason to avoid the U.S. financial sector, due to the oncoming deflationary “Japanification” stagnation scenario that they see as likely to come to the United States. This is a real risk and should be considered, but fortunately some of Berkshire’s non-financial exposures (manufacturing companies, railways, utilities, non-bank stocks, and so forth), can mitigate this risk a bit.

However, the United States has a very different financial situation than Japan and Europe. Japan and a significant portion of Europe are creditor regions with current account surpluses, whereas the United States is a debtor region with a currency account deficit, and thus could experience more inflation in a post-virus environment with a lot of fiscal stimulus and money-printing as policymakers try to get the unemployment rate back down to non-crisis levels.

*Insurance Operations*

As one of the largest insurers in the world, Berkshire has a significant amount of tail risk. That is the main reason that Buffett has cited for why he likes to keep a twelve-figure sum of cash and T-bills on Berkshire’s balance sheet.

In fact, Berkshire’s insurance operation is so large and complex that it recently led to a comical outcome. As Buffett explained in the most recent report:

> I must add one final item that underscores the wide scope of Berkshire’s operations. Since 2011, we have owned Lubrizol, an Ohio-based company that produces and markets oil additives throughout the world.
>
> On September 26, 2019, a fire originating at a small next-door operation spread to a large French plant owned by Lubrizol. The result was significant property damage and a major disruption in Lubrizol’s business. Even so, both the company’s property loss and business-interruption loss will be mitigated by substantial insurance recoveries that Lubrizol will receive.
>
> But, as the late Paul Harvey was given to saying in his famed radio broadcasts, “Here’s the rest of the story.” One of the largest insurers of Lubrizol was a company owned by . . . uh, Berkshire.
>
> In Matthew 6:3, the Bible instructs us to “Let not the left hand know what the right hand doeth.” Your chairman has clearly behaved as ordered.

That particular event is small for Berkshire as a whole, but the point is, Berkshire has broad worldwide insurance exposure to a variety of unexpected outcomes, and Berkshire insures some of its own companies. Even a company as financially strong as Berkshire should only exist as part of a diversified portfolio in most cases.

**Conclusion**

The U.S. financial sector, including Berkshire, currently have a lot of characteristics similar to 2009. They have fallen in price and have become very cheap, but they are filled with more cash and Treasuries than ever. I like them at current price levels for the long run, even though the risks are considerable. And I particularly like to pair them with hard asset companies, ranging from gold miners to infrastructure operators to industrial manufacturers.

On the other hand, in 2009, everything was cheap, including many tech stocks that went on to rule the world over the subsequent decade. Tech stocks did great over that decade, but now, even in the midst of this crisis, are quite expensive. Only a handful of sectors including financials are quantitatively cheap on a historical basis.

Time will tell, but as the economy continues to chug along into the 2020’s decade, the relative performance of beaten-down financials could do quite well compared to the expensive broad S&P 500, and we could see a period of value sectors such as financials and materials outperforming growth sectors, similarly to what occurred in the 2000’s decade.
