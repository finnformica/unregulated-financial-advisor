## Macro View

**The Strait of Hormuz**

It has been about six weeks since the attack by the US and Israel on Iran, and the Strait of Hormuz has remained primarily closed with limited exceptions.

A ceasefire late this past week led to a sharp drop in the price of oil and raised hopes about an end to the crisis, but that ceasefire was broken within the first day. Iran and the United States have since publicly disagreed about the list of items they had partially agreed upon, leading to widening doubt about the durability of the peace. Talks between the two sides within Pakistan then failed to reach an agreement over the weekend.

Electronic interference, along with the intentional deactivation of transponders by some ships to hide their location adds a degree of uncertainty to the data, but by most [credible reports](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-10/only-iran-favored-ships-pass-hormuz-as-trump-demands-reopening), the volume of ships headed through the Strait of Hormuz has been extremely low during the ceasefire. The handful that have been getting through were for the most part Iran-aligned.

Today, President Trump announced on Truth Social that the US Navy would begin blocking the Strait of Hormuz as well. Iran began collecting tolls going through the strait, which they had not been doing prior to the war, and Trump has said that the US Navy will stop ships that have paid the toll to get through. The stated intent is to eventually get the strait back to how it was prior to the war, with toll-less crossings and no danger from mines.

![Truth Social](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-truth-social.png)

This sharp reduction in the flows of energy and raw materials continues to weigh heavily on the global economy. Nations with less per-capita wealth are likely to take the biggest hit in terms of outright shortages, as they get outbid by wealthier countries for the remaining available energy and materials. However, high prices and rationing continue to be rising issues in many countries.

Within the United States, gasoline prices have surged, and the consumer price index for March came in hot, which simultaneously squeezes consumer finances and constrains the Fed from easing interest rates.

![CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-cpi.png)

![Gasoline](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-gasoline.png)

Relatedly, the Fed has been [putting out research](https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/detecting-tariff-effects-on-consumer-prices-in-real-time-part-II-20260408.html) to assess how much tariffs have contributed to price increases in manufactured goods versus other factors in the United States. There was an unusual rise in manufactured goods prices during the past year (despite below-trend money supply growth, and despite balance sheet reduction during most of the year), and they attribute all of that above-baseline rise in prices to the tariffs:

> We estimate that the tariffs implemented through November of 2025 have raised core goods PCE prices by 3.1 percent through February 2026, explaining the entirety of excess inflation in the core goods category relative to pre-pandemic inflation rates and contributing to a 0.8 percent boost in core PCE prices as a whole.

![Goods Inflation from Tariffs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-goods-tariffs.png)

I continue to view this as a time primarily for building watchlists rather than rushing to deploy capital into markets. However, there are a few investments that I’ll propose later in this report, as a first tranche of capital I’m putting to work after having accumulated some cash during this time.

**Liquidity Check-Up**

The domestic and global liquidity situation remains decent, despite the war-related economic disruption.

Neither the Fed’s standing repo facility nor the swap line facility are being materially used:

![Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-repo.png)

![Swap Lines](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-swap-lines.png)

The secured overnight financing rate remains elevated relative to the interest rate that the Fed pays on bank reserves, indicating mild liquidity constraints, but the situation is not as rough as it was in late 2025 when the Fed was effectively forced to end their balance sheet reduction. In fact, during the past couple of days it cooled off pretty rapidly:

![SOFR IORB](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-sofr-iorb.png)

The dollar index has similarly cooled off since its local peak in late March. There are no signs of an acute global dollar shortage, although certain developing nations (e.g. Egypt, which is reportedly seeking a $3 billion emergency loan from the IMF) are likely facing some constraints in that area.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-dxy.png)

The Fed continues to perform what I’ve called “the gradual print” in line with expectations:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-fed-balance-sheet-longterm.png)

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-fed-balance-sheet-shortterm.png)

Senior loan officer surveys and weekly bank lending numbers still look decent, meaning commercial banks are still circulating credit reasonably well:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-bank-loans.png)

Of course, the major credit-related headlines are around the $3 trillion private credit industry. A larger than normal ratio of investors are requesting withdrawals from private credit funds, which will likely have a dampening effect on the availability of riskier types of loans (smaller businesses, consumer financing, and so forth) for those that borrow them.

Higher bond yields continue to be a sizable drag on liquidity conditions as well, although in the US these numbers took a rest in April. Fresh new multi-decade high yields on Japanese bonds are among the more concerning pockets of the global market as of this writing:

![US 10-Year Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-us-10yr.png)

![JPY 10-Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-jpy-10yr.png)

**US Business Cycle**

The US economy continues to show signs of stagnation.

US consumer sentiment in March dipped to a record low, which is rather shocking considering that there are seven decades of data since the surveys began. This is lower than the bottom in the stagflationary 1970s, lower than the 2008/2009 global financial crisis, lower than peak Covid lockdowns in 2020, and lower than the inflationary war-related energy crisis in 2022.

![Consumer Sentiment](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-consumer-sentiment.png)

*Source: University of Michigan*

While the economy is not shedding jobs, it’s not adding them either. Some of this is attributable to aging demographics and sharp reductions in net migration, but another component of it is AI. Meanwhile, real disposable income grew only 1% year-over-year.

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow tool estimates only 1.3% annualized growth for Q1 2026, which has ended but has not yet been tallied and reported:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-gdpnow.png)

People are facing a rather harsh combination of weak income growth, a weak job market, high cost of living, a sudden surge in energy inflation, and broad concerns that rapid advancements in AI will devalue the income potential of many college degrees for debt-laden graduates.

There are pockets of the economy I continue to be cautiously bullish on, but for the most part I’m still taking a rather defensive view toward markets until there’s more clarity on the Strait of Hormuz.

* 4/13: Sell June 18 $380 cash-secured put options on MU.

Micron (MU) is one of the three companies that collectively make almost all of the world’s random access memory, and the only one that’s based in the United States. As AI usage has shifted from information-based chatbots toward activity-based agents (and more broadly, toward a greater need for persistence of memory), the demand for memory has soared alongside graphics processors.

The prices of memory stocks similarly surged throughout 2025, although they’ve taken a breather here in 2026.

![MU YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-mu-ychart.png)

![MU FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-mu-fastgraph.png)

-Bears point out that memory is a highly cyclical business. As the three main memory makers perform a lot of capex to increase supply and meet demand, they will eventually oversupply the market and crash memory prices just like they have in past cycles.

-Bulls argue that we’re still in early innings for how much memory the world will need over the next 3-5 years. Additionally, there are supply constraints that limit how quickly new supply can reach the market, and thus prices are likely to remain elevated for quite a while. With the boom in AI agents, this isn’t a normal cycle.

While there are risks, I generally side with the bulls here, and view memory stocks as more likely to break to the upside out of their current consolidation than to the downside. Additionally, the high volatility is interesting in and of itself for rebalancing.

As such, I’ll be selling Micron June 18 cash-secured puts at a strike price of $380. This’ll pay a decent premium during the two months until expiry, and if the stock price drops it means I’ll be entering at a cost basis in the ballpark of $350 (the current price is $420).

However, I do recommend conservative position sizing with a stock that moves this sharply.

## Visa and HDFC Bank Updates

While I remain cautious on a number of fronts, I’m currently looking to deploy my first tranche of capital since the start of this war on Iran six weeks ago. Selling options on Micron as noted above is one such move, and the following two additions round out this first tranche.

**Visa**

In my prior report, I mentioned that the sell-off in Visa (V) stock was healthy and that it was on my watch-list to add capital to. I’ve been long Visa for a while.

At the moment, it looks like it’s trying to put in a price bottom. Whether that bottom will hold or not is another story, but in general Visa benefits greatly from the nominal expansion of everything.

Their strong network effects and broad regulatory shields make them difficult to disrupt by competitors or to “vibe code” away with AI. And while I’m bullish on the growth of stablecoins, I don’t view them as a direct competitor to the major card networks per se. Consumers want reversible transactions, buyer protection services, and rewards points. Stablecoins, which are typically irreversible transactions, are less attractive for daily purchases even as they are great for certain contexts.

In mid-2021, Visa was briefly overvalued with a peak price/earnings ratio of 42x. That valuation figure has since fallen to around 25x earnings, which is about the lowest they’ve reached within the past decade and is more reasonable.

![V FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-v-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (an 20.12x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Their balance sheet is a fortress, with an AA- credit rating and no net debt. They don’t take on any lending risk directly, and instead leave that to participating banks in the network. Annual revenue growth is strong in the low double-digits, which I expect to gradually fall into the high single digits in the years ahead.

![Visa Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-v-revenue.png)

The company has incredibly high returns on capital, and thus it is able to pay rising dividends and continually buy back its shares while simultaneously investing in capex to grow its business.

![Visa Dividends and Buybacks](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-v-dividends-buybacks.png)

**HDFC Bank**

When I first invested in India’s biggest bank years ago during a sell-off, it started off promising. However, since then it has stagnated. By adding primarily on dips I minimize the issue, but overall it hasn’t been great.

The biggest challenge was that in mid-2023, they merged with their parent company, and that slowed them down. However, as they reach the third year of that consolidation effort, I expect an acceleration. The core thesis for the investment is that India’s economy is one of the fastest-growing ones globally, and overall household debt remains relatively low, and so the growth of the financial sector is likely to be as-fast-or-faster than the broad economy.

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has put pressure on the Indian economy, as they are a large energy importer and not a wealthy one on a per-capita basis. However, India’s government and corporate sector have been quite pragmatic on energy sourcing, so I have high confidence that despite some economic pain, they’ll take the steps necessary to ward off some of the worst risks of an energy crisis.

HDFC’s stock price fell sharply during this war, both in local currency terms (as this next FAST Graph shows) and in dollar terms, but within the past week it started to put in an attempt at a bottom. Still, there’s certainly a risk the price could head lower.

![HDFC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-hdfc-fastgraph-annotated.png)

The valuation is currently around the lowest on record, relative to both earnings and book value, although that’s from the relative starting point of high valuations (Indian companies are rarely cheap, given the strong structural growth prospects).

In my view, this sell-off gives another entry point into a company that should (regardless of its recent struggles) have a solid growth runway over the next decade.

## Final Thoughts: Gold and Bitcoin

Gold, silver, and platinum have all put in temporary price bottoms since mid-to-late March.

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-gold.png)

The sharp sell-off that they went through was healthy in my view, after such a rapid and euphoric rise. I continue to hold the base case that while such sharp price rises are concerning for potential downward corrections, I don’t view the metals as fundamentally overvalued or in a bubble. Instead, I view them as having sharply revalued from being undervalued to fairly-valued.

As such, I’m less bullish on them than I used to be since they don’t quite have the same asymmetrical payoff potential as when I began recommending them in 2018, but I continue to have a long-term constructive outlook on them and view them as fitting well within a diversified portfolio.

Meanwhile, Bitcoin has held up well during this war, since most of the fast money was already out of it prior to the start of the hostilities. It has mostly been flat since early February, meaning it’s over two months into stabilization. Weekly technical indicators are looking good.

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-4-12-btc.png)

While there are further risks to the downside, for those who are long-term bullish on bitcoin’s technical robustness and self-reinforcing network effects (as I am), I view this as a time to layer more capital in.
