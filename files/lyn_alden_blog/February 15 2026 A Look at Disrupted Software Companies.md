*February 15, 2026*

## Macro View

**Domestic Liquidity**

The Fed continues to expand their balance sheet, which has eased the various interbank liquidity issues that were popping up in Q4 of last year.

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-fed-balance-sheet.png)

Meanwhile, banks are making loans. It’s not explosive loan growth by any means, but the numbers are headed in a positive direction:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-bank-loans.png)

Overall, most liquidity metrics in the US are in decent shape. And I remain moderately bullish on US regional bank stocks.

**Global Liquidity**

The flat-to-down dollar recently has been beneficial for global liquidity. This is because the dollar is the primary unit of account of cross-border debts, so when the dollar weakens it tends to ease liabilities relative to company cash flows, especially in emerging markets which is where most marginal growth exists in the world.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-dxy.png)

However, Japan’s rising short-term rates and long-term rates have put the breaks on global liquidity from the east:

![Japan 10 Year Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-japan-10year.png)

Likewise, China is no longer playing the game of, “pump up real estate prices” and has instead redirected most of its liquidity toward industrial policy.

![China Credit Impulse](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-china-credit-impulse.png)

It remains an underreported fact that China has leapfrogged Japan, Germany, South Korea, and the United States to become the world’s largest auto exporter during the past five years.

![China Auto Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-china-auto-exports.png)

Many of us in North America or Europe wouldn’t be first in line to buy Chinese automobiles. But they’ve gone up the quality spectrum dramatically in recent years, resulting in being low-priced mid-quality cars (with a broad spectrum that stretches into luxury cars as well).

It’s hard to overstate how much Chinese auto exports have been helpful to various emerging market consumers during this time, particularly within the middle class.

Using Egypt as an example, they had massive inflation in the aftermath of the combo of global Covid/lockdown disruptions and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine (which impacted energy supplies as well as grain production for a while). Thanks to wages not keeping up with the country’s rapid currency debasement, the number of wage-hours it takes for an average Egyptian to buy the same Volkswagen or Hyundai has gone up quite a bit in recent years. But the introduction of inexpensive Chinese cars have largely offset that problem. Egyptians in many cases switched from buying those types of cars to buying Chinese brands, which often come at a major price discount without too much of a sacrifice in quality. It would have been worse for them economically had there not been this huge new influx of inexpensive vehicles to turn to.

Altogether, global liquidity is in middling shape. There aren’t really any liquidity “booms” among the major economies. A weaker dollar, along with a lack of oil supply disruptions and plenty of industrial output from China, have eased financial conditions and logistical bottlenecks for many countries, but not to the point of creating the conditions for a true flourishing. Global liquidity is “decent” all things considered.

**US Economy**

The US economy continues to operate at two speeds, with wealthy asset-holders doing rather well while younger and lower-income consumers in general are struggling. Those on the right side of AI capex and fiscal spending are generally doing fine, while those on the wrong side of moderately high interest rates (e.g. those reliant on high turnover of housing inventory) are still having a rough time.

The total job market is roughly at stall speed:

![Payems](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-payems.png)

However, for the first time in while, there are green shoots in the US manufacturing sector. The manufacturing purchasing manager’s index has reached its highest level since the Fed’s 2022 tightening campaign, and the new orders segment in particular has been giving good signs:

![PMIs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-ism-pmi.png)

## Digital Assets

Since bottoming on February 6th, bitcoin has been trending sideways.

So far, the price action is similar to the prior bottom that formed on November 22, which held for about two months before rolling lower.

I’ve been covering bitcoin as an asset since 2017 at $6,800/coin, and this is probably the worst sentiment I’ve seen in the space. During the depths of the 2022 bear market, amid the implosion of FTX, the broad crypto industry was in shambles but enthusiasm in the bitcoin-specific ecosystem was still high. But here in 2026, it’s incredibly weak from what I’ve seen.

I continue to be structurally bearish on the broad crypto space outside of bitcoin and stablecoins, as I explained [in this article](https://www.lynalden.com/why-most-cryptocurrencies-wont-accrue-value/).

For bitcoin, I remain long-term bullish, but price bottoms in bitcoin tend to take a while. Outside of the hyper-stimulus that occurred in spring 2020, bitcoin usually doesn’t put in V-shaped bottoms. Unlike a company, there’s no quarterly earnings release or company pivot that can rapidly change perception on it. Instead, it tends to hit a low level, trade in a choppy range for a long time, as weakly-held coins rotate out toward stronger hands and put in an increasingly firm price floor.

Bitcoin’s price action has been strongly correlating with software stocks lately, which have sold off rapidly. Both the 5-year chart and the 6-month chart show an unusually high degree of correlation:

## A Look at Disrupted Software Stocks

As I mentioned in the digital asset section above, software stocks have been trading poorly, and bitcoin has been correlating with them:

![IGV vs BTC 5 Years](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-igv-btc-5years.png)

The iShares Expanded Tech-Software Sector ETF (IGV) has over 100 companies, but the top couple dozen account for most of its value:

![IGV Top Holdings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-igv-top-holdings.png)

Microsoft (MSFT) and Oracle (ORCL) are interesting, because they’re not pure software companies. They’re also AI hyperscalers.

Back in my [November 23, 2025 premium report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2025-11-23/), I focused on the hyperscalers, with a neutral view toward Alphabet (GOOGL) and a more cautious view on Microsoft, Oracle, and Meta (META). Since then, three out of four have underperformed the S&P 500, with only Meta outpacing it:

![Hyperscaler Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-hyperscaler-returns.png)

The main issue is that the hyperscalers have so much competition and capex to do going forward, which limits their free cash flow generation potential. And that came at a time when they were trading at very high valuations. Here’s Microsoft, for example:

![MSFT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-msft-fastgraph.png)

I was bullish on Alphabet from 2022 through 2025 as my preferred hyperscaler, because: 1) I viewed the AI threat to its search business as being overblown on an investable time horizon, 2) I viewed it as a major AI player in its own right, and 3) I viewed YouTube as a continued source of growth for it. It was trading at a discount to its peers despite being a quiet powerhouse. However, after its major price surge to $300/share, I shifted from bullish to neutral in late 2025.

Further down the list in IGV, we see more typical software-as-a-service “SaaS” companies. The bearish thesis for many of these stocks is that AI generative tools and agents are increasingly able to do what these software companies enable people to do.

Fortunately, our model portfolios are not very SaaS-heavy at all. The only positions I have that are in this ETF are ADBE and MSTR.

**Adobe Inc.**

While I think software companies will become oversold and interesting in the future, I still view many of them as being expensive. Considerable risk for disruption has been presented to them, so their sell-off is warranted.

The one I am currently willing to dabble in is Adobe (ADBE).

I initially added Adobe to the newsletter portfolio in January 2019, after many software stocks had a big sell-off. It has been incredibly volatile since then. It soared from around $230 at that entry point up to over $650, then fell all the way back to $250, then soared again up to $630, and has since fallen to nearly $260.

Along the way, I tended to pound the table when it was cheap, and warn when it became richly priced, although the subsequent moves were usually bigger than I had expected, as the market is basically bipolar about this company:

> In my May 28, 2023 report, I sorted through AI concepts. Nvidia (NVDA) had already run up in price at that point, so my approach was to find things that would benefit from the trend but that had not yet been priced that way. Adobe (ADBE) was my pick, and it’s up 44% since then, nearly doubling the performance of Nvidia and tripling the performance of the S&P 500.
>
> However, I think Adobe is getting over its skis now, with a PEG ratio closer to 3x than 2x.
>
> ![ADBE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-adbe-fastgraph.png)
>
> They do have a fortress balance sheet and dominant market position, and thus are worth the top end of a typical valuation range.
>
> I expect the stock to be higher five years from now than it is today, but I wouldn’t be surprised to see a year or two of correction along the way to let off some steam. I view Adobe as a hold but not currently a buy. It’s worth trimming some, or just holding off on new purchases for a bit.
>
> -December 12, 2023 premium report

After it fell again, I started to get more bullish in 2025, but the stock has generally refused to put in a price floor. It has now fallen below its 2022 lows:

![ADBE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-adbe-fastgraph.png)

Adobe makes most of its money from its Creative Cloud offering- a suite of professional design products. It has historically had massive industry dominance which gave it monopoly-like pricing power, but cheaper and easier-to-use web-based services with strong collaborative features have been breaking into their moat lately.

Canva is strongly competing with Adobe on the photoshop/illustrator front. It’s easy to use, and many new designers start with Canva now. However, Adobe still dominates the professional market, especially in video-related content generation.

Meanwhile, Figma hit Adobe on the UI/UX design front. Adobe tried to buy Figma but was blocked by regulators. After that, Figma went public (ticker: FIG), but its stock price has been cut in half since then. It’s currently trading at a much lower market capitalization than Adobe was willing to pay years ago.

All of these companies, including Adobe and its competitors, are integrating generative AI. The question remains: to what extent will AI commoditize these platforms or fail to do so?

Adobe is still growing its top and bottom line. Here’s their five-year revenue, income, and free cash flow changes:

![Adobe Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-adbe-growth.png)

Adobe has about as much cash as it has debt, which gives it a fortress balance sheet. And they plow a lot of their free cash flow into reducing their share count:

![Adobe Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-adbe-balance-sheet.png)

![Adobe Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-adbe-shares.png)

At ~12x blended earnings (~15x trailing), Adobe is now priced for negligible growth. It has firmly transitioned from being a growth stock to being a value stock.

So, is Adobe a value play, or a value trap?

The bear case is basically as follows. Canva and other tools are strongly competing with Adobe on the entry-level design front, to the point where Adobe’s ecosystem isn’t necessarily the first place that new designers begin. Thus, Adobe gets banished to the pro market, rather than controlling the full entry-to-pro design stack. That gives companies with big entry-level market share, like Canva, time to potentially eat into the pro market over time. Adobe might still serve as the “last mile” for professional designs for a long time, but a company needs fewer Adobe subscriptions going forward, because they can do more with simpler tools and only need Adobe’s heavier-hitting tools in some cases.

The bull case would point out that Adobe’s growth hasn’t taken much of a hit yet, three years into the AI boom. The overall design market is growing (thanks to AI making it way more accessible to people), and Adobe’s strong balance sheet and low valuation puts them in a decent position to thrive even as their growth and margins likely do come under pressure from competition in the years ahead. They’re still the industry standard for professional design, and competitors will have to invest heavily to compete with them at that top level.

While my conviction is not as high for Adobe as it was for Alphabet from 2022-2025, I err toward Adobe being a value play rather than a value trap. At this valuation level, and with this secure of a balance sheet, I view the company’s professional products as being able to sustain a solid business.

But I’ll be watching that thesis closely to see if it continues to hold up from a fundamental standpoint or not.

## Final Thoughts: Equal-Weight S&P 500

Over the multi-decade long run, an equal-weight version of the S&P 500 has historically outperformed the market-weight version. The equal-weight method is a bit trickier to do (i.e. higher turnover), is a bit more value-oriented, and is a bit more mid-cap oriented, even though it consists of the same 500 companies.

However, starting around 2015 and extending for the next decade, the market-weight version dramatically outperformed, catching up with decades of previous equal-weight outperformance. This is because the mega-cap companies hugely outperformed virtually everything else domestically and globally.

The mega-caps were moderately asset-light businesses with strong self-reinforcing network effects and economies of scale. This led them to experience high-ROI growth, and yet with only moderate capex, leaving them with plenty of free cash flow to plow into share buybacks.

The S&P 500’s corporate margins reached record levels, led by these tech mega-caps. Basically, most of the value accrual went to the top. Users got a free or low-cost product, but basically gave up all their data while also ending up with higher rates of depression (i.e. the negative effects of social media).

So far, it appears that AI will push things in the opposite direction. Large company network effects are weaker when it comes to AI, meaning they need massive capex spending to compete with each other. Users can pretty easily switch AI products when one becomes better than another. “Ecosystems” are weaker, less sticky.

Instead of the mega-caps being the primary winners, it seems as though consumers will be the primary beneficiary of this “race to the bottom” of capex spending. Or at least, consumers who are early adopters of AI to expand their productivity on an individual or business level.

If that is true, then it’s a potential catalyst for the equal-weight S&P 500 relative to the market-weight one. And indeed, since the start of November 2025, the equal-weight version has sharply outperformed.

![S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-15-spx.png)

That’s roughly around the time of my report on hyperscalers channeling their free cash flow toward capex spending. And since then, the capex spending announcements from them have only grown larger. Meanwhile, AI tools are getting rapidly better at coding.

While this current rate of equal-weight outperformance is unsustainable, I think we could see a zig-zag move up over time in this ratio. It’s something I’ll be monitoring going forward, as the concentration of power potentially shifts away from asset-light network effect mega-caps and towards harder asset types of businesses.
