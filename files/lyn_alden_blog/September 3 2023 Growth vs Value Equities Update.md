## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic U.S. liquidity has been flat for over a year now.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-domestic-liquidity.png)

There are a few drivers of liquidity, with the U.S. Federal Reserve and U.S. Treasury Department being the largest.

-The Fed can currently affect liquidity negatively by deciding to keep doing quantitative tightening until it breaks something. This is somewhat offset by its bank lending programs (in response to having broken something back in March 2023) which are adding liquidity back into the financial system and offsetting some of its quantitative tightening.

-The U.S. Treasury Department can affect liquidity by altering the size of its cash account at the Fed, and by determining whether to fund its new debt issuance primarily with T-bills or longer duration securities. If they issue a lot of T-bills (which they did this summer), they can be purchased by money market funds, and they can draw liquidity out of reverse repos to buy them (which is neutral for overall liquidity). If they issue a lot of longer duration T-notes and T-bonds, then money markets cannot purchase them, which is worse overall for liquidity especially if combined with a rising cash account balance.

In a topic that is adjacent to liquidity, the U.S. broad money supply decreased from April 2022 through April 2023, but since May 2023 has been flat:

![M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-m2.jpg)

When we look globally, China, the Eurozone, and Japan are some of the other major liquidity drivers. In particular, China’s fiscal stimulus efforts or lack thereof, and Japan’s ongoing quantitative easing and yield curve control (monetary stimulus), remain some of the major liquidity drivers.

This is a chart of global M2 in dollar-denominated terms. Back in 2022, I was chatting with Preston Pysh about this indicator and he has conveniently made a version of it in Trading View:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-global-m2.png)

An ideal version with unlimited data would capture the 15 or more largest currency blocs, but the top five or so capture the bulk of it. This is a monthly chart, and so if we were to look at it on a more granular daily level, we would see a brief spike down during March 2020 as well.

In general, risk assets do better in rising liquidity environments, while cash and defensive assets do better in falling liquidity environments. Right now, the financial system is in limbo, with large U.S. fiscal deficits (stimulatory), offset by Fed quantitative tightening (contractionary), with some bank liquidity programs (stimulatory), ongoing fiscal tightness out of China (contractionary), and ongoing monetary looseness out of Japan (stimulatory). The liquidity situation is not as bad as it was in late 2022 and early 2023, but it’s not very strong either.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Atlanta Fed continues to have a very high estimate for Q3 real GDP growth, although some of the data are questionable:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-gdpnow.png)

The U.S. continues to experience a tale of two economies, in large part because fiscal policy is so loose ($2 trillion annualized deficits) while monetary policy is increasingly tight (5%+ interest rates and quantitative tightening). And tight monetary policy ironically makes fiscal policy looser, due to causing much higher interest expense.

Overall U.S. payroll growth continues to be positive, albeit with deceleration in rate-of-change terms. Throughout 2023, nonfarm payrolls have tended to be announced and then revised down later. Temporary help services, which are a more cyclical part of the labor market, have been in firm contraction territory. Overtime hours, which are another cyclical labor market measure, have also been very weak lately but are showing early signs of stabilization.

![Cyclical Labor Indicators](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-cyclical-labor.jpg)

Other measures of the labor market suggest similar weakness under the surface:

![Modified Sahm Rule](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-sahm-rule.png)

The ISM Manufacturing PMI continues to be weak, although there are early signs of an *upturn* in rate-of-change terms:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-ism-pmi.png)

It continues to be a very unusual economic environment, which makes sense given the unusual fiscal environment. Large fiscal deficits can support parts of the economy (particularly those receiving Social Security, Medicare, Defense Spending, or Interest Expense) while other parts of the economy soften.

I think the way to maintain exposure to it is by being industry-specific. Maintaining exposure to strong areas, particularly those that are deficit-supported or otherwise doing well, makes sense for the offensive side of a portfolio. Avoiding troubled areas, and maintaining a decent amount of cash equivalents is useful for playing some defense with other parts of the portfolio.

**2023 BRICS Summit News**

The [BRICS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BRICS) nations (Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa) recently met at their 15th annual summit. This summit was more hyped than usual because ever since Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent freezing of Russia’s reserves by the West, the BRICS nations have accelerated their plans on de-dollarization.

Currently, the dollar is involved on one side of 90% of global FX trading volumes. There are about 160 different currencies, which are basically little bubbles tied to their own borders, and if they want to trade globally they are stuck with either 1) barter between unsalable currencies or 2) using the most globally salable currency, which is the U.S. dollar.

In other words, each country’s central bank runs its own local ledger, but then to varying degrees most of them tie into the U.S. Federal Reserve’s ledger by a combination of 1) dollar-denominated international pricing contracts, 2) holding dollar-denominated reserve assets, and 3) using dollar-denominated financing (debts). This gives the U.S. the ability to freeze their funds, sanction them from using the ledger, etc. And it’s mainly based on the post-WWII environment where the U.S. was the sole hyperpower.

Now that China has grown considerably, and the world economy is more distributed overall, there are natural calls for a more balanced or distributed global payment system as well. For example, should Saudi Arabia continue to only sell oil in dollars, even though China is by far its biggest customer now, rather than the United States?

But talk is easier than action; monies and financial systems have significant network effects and high switching costs. China and its partners have serious scale to create an alternative system, but it’s a *massive* undertaking. China and India aren’t even on great geopolitical terms, for starters.

There were some analysts, generally from the gold enthusiast community, expecting the announcement of a shared currency backed by gold, which didn’t materialize. Instead, what we should expect out of BRICS is a gradual piecemeal coalition to align their payment systems. To the extent that they de-dollarize, it will be due to various currency swaps, commodity contracts, and so forth to use the major currencies of the bloc, especially the Chinese yuan. And with that, we will probably see continued gradual diversification of reserve holdings.

In addition to network effects around payment rails and currency liquidity, impediments to the yuan meaningfully displacing the dollar are 1) capital controls and 2) a lack of attractive securities to hold relative to what’s available in U.S. capital markets. The latter has been a consistent problem for the Eurozone as well, since multiple different smaller semi-liquid capital markets are less attractive than one giant liquid capital market.

I expect some meaningful degree of de-dollarization and global currency diversification in the decade ahead, but it will become overhyped for periods of time, and analysts will likely keep mistaking words for actions. Actions are what matter.

The news that *did* come out of the BRICS summit is that six new members will join on January 1, 2024. These nations include Argentina, Egypt, Ethiopia, Saudi Arabia, Iran, and the UAE. They are in light blue on Wikipedia’s useful map below:

![BRICS Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-brics-map.jpg)

-Saudi Arabia, Iran, and the UAE are very important entrants, because they are major oil producers. In addition, Saudi Arabia and Iran have a history of geopolitical animosity, which has been a main catalyst for the close relationship between the U.S. and Saudi Arabia. Over the past couple of years, China has served as the peacemaker between the two countries, and their formal inclusion into BRICS helps further cement that pragmatic peace.

-Egypt is another major inclusion, with a population of nearly 110 million people, control over the extremely strategically important Suez Canal, and a military ranking that is considered around #14 globally. I’m currently in Egypt, and people I know here who do not normally focus on financial matters have been highly interested in Egypt’s BRICS inclusion. They see it as a way to escape from the yoke of U.S. financial and geopolitical imperialism, and to align with a broader coalition. (I do, however, expect another round of devaluation for the Egyptian currency over the next year or so.)

-Argentina’s inclusion is at least somewhat in question at this time, because while their establishment leaders want to be included and the formal plan is to join, their rising populist leaders generally do not favor BRICS. In particular, Javier Milei came out ahead in Argentina’s August 2023 primary election, and he has a less favorable view of BRICS than his political peers. Should he win the autumn presidential 2023 election, it’s unclear if this would affect Argentina’s BRICS inclusion.

Overall, the BRICS alignment represents a major alternative to the US/Europe/Australia/Japan alignment, and whether in this name or another name (e.g. the Shanghai Cooperation Organization) will likely represent a defining geopolitical bipolar power competition in the years ahead. However, major global financial changes take time, due to serious network effects and complexities.

## Digital Assets Note

Last week, Grayscale (the company that runs the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust GBTC) won their lawsuit against the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission. The SEC had blocked their proposed conversion to a spot bitcoin ETF, along with everyone else who wanted to list a spot bitcoin ETF, and the appeals court ruled that the SEC was “arbitrary and capricious” with its decision and that given the 99.9% correlation between the futures market and spot market, the SEC failed to show that the spot market is manipulated relative to the futures market.

This is relevant because the SEC’s main reason for repeated denials has been that spot bitcoin ETF proposals fail to protect against price manipulation, even though other countries have spot bitcoin ETFs with no problem, and even though there is no meaningful price difference between spot and futures, while they allow futures bitcoin ETFs and even *leveraged* futures bitcoin ETFs.

However, the court ruling does not immediately clear the way for a spot bitcoin ETF, and indeed the SEC has once again delayed its decision on all spot bitcoin ETFs. Rather, the court decision basically means that if the SEC wants to keep denying them, it has to come up with different arguments or evidence rather than repeating the same reasons that have now been struck down, since their arguments thus far have been insufficient and in the court’s words, “arbitrary and capricious”.

The bitcoin price jumped on the court ruling news but then quickly gave up its gains. As I wrote in my prior report, the main factor that is likely to meaningfully move the bitcoin price in the months or years ahead is global liquidity. The supply side is tight (coins have migrated from fast-money weak hands to long-term strong hands as they normally do in bear markets), so it’s mainly about the external demand side, and that is highly correlated with liquidity conditions:

> Picking up a bit of exposure during liquidations tends to be a good move in the long run. I don’t have a firm view on the extent or duration of the current down move in the price of bitcoin because in the intermediate term I think it’ll be largely tied to global liquidity, the U.S. Treasury market, and Chinese stimulus or lack thereof that I discussed in the initial macro section of this report.

## Growth vs Value Updates

As I discussed (and quantified) in a [2021 article](https://www.lynalden.com/interest-rate-effects/), high-valuation growth equities are generally more sensitive to interest rates than value-oriented equities.

Throughout 2022, as the Federal Reserve aggressively raised interest rates, unprofitable hypergrowth equities were basically killed, and profitable growth equities took a major valuation haircut. As a result, growth equities underperformed value equities in aggregate.

During 2023, especially after the March banking crisis, the market has increasingly priced in a top in interest rates, and along with the AI craze, has bid up growth equities relative to value equities.

This chart shows the ratio of value equities to growth equities. When it’s rising it means value is outperforming in aggregate, and when it’s falling it means growth is outperforming in aggregate:

![Value vs Growth Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-value-vs-growth-chart.png)

Value outperformed in the strong growth era of the 1980s. Growth outperformed in the tech boom of the 1990s. Value outperformed during the commodity bull market and the rise of BRICS in the 2000s. Growth outperformed in the 2010s decade of relative economic stagnation, and accelerated during the pandemic. Value began outperforming from the depths of the pandemic during the recovery, although 2023 is challenging that.

If we take Alphabet (GOOGL) as an example (one of the growth stocks I have in the Newsletter portfolio), it has returned to its average decade valuation (the blue line):

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-googl-fastgraph.png)

Arguably, it should be at a lower valuation, because interest rates are higher than average. If we look at the spread between Alphabet’s earnings yield and the 10-year Treasury rate or 3-month Treasury rate, it doesn’t look great:

![GOOGL YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-googl-ychart.png)

During the 2000s decade, Alphabet had *tremendous* growth rate, and so a wide valuation spread vs interest rates made sense. Now as a $1.7 trillion company, its growth is slowing and maturing, and so the fact that its earnings yield is less than the 10-year Treasury rate and significantly less than the 3-month Treasury rate, is potentially more of a concern.

However, Alphabet is by no means one of the worst offenders. Apple (AAPL) has slower growth rate and a higher earnings multiple than Alphabet, a balance sheet that is not as strong as Alphabet, and more geopolitical China-exposure risk than Alphabet due to where the bulk of its hardware supply chain is located.

Similarly, Microsoft (MSFT) is trading at over 30x earnings despite being a mature company worth over $2.4 trillion. Consensus analysts expect 11% forward adjusted earnings growth next year after 7% trailing adjusted earnings growth last year, and yet the earnings multiple shot up from 25x to 32x within 2023 thus far.

In general, I continue to like the growth stocks that are in the Newsletter portfolio, although some of them may be getting a little bit stretched up here. Market participants seem heavily focused on the Nvidia’s (NVDA) of the world and aren’t really focusing on the Visa’s (V) or the HDFC Bank’s (HDB) of the world. So I’ll take some of those latter types of growth stocks with a 5-year view.

For value, I continue to have a bullish view on energy producers, healthcare companies, and select financials like American Express (AXP). In aggregate they are cheap, they pay decent dividends, and they can continue to buy back cheap shares or strengthen their balance sheets.

I have previously been using a concept of a three-pillar portfolio, referring to profitable equities, cash equivalents, and hard assets as the three pillars. I think it’s worth separating the equity side into two separate concepts: profitable growth-oriented network effect stocks, and income-producing value stocks.

For growth equities, what I look for is a dominant market position and a major entrenched economic moat along with a good balance sheet, like Visa. And then I look for a price/earnings ratio that is less than 2x its typical annual earnings growth rate. This style of investing is often called “growth at a reasonable price” or “GARP”.

For value equities, I look for companies that are spinning off tons of free cash flow, with higher earning yields than I can get with Treasuries, and with some degree of pricing power that can protect against inflation (unlike Treasuries) in order to compensate for higher volatility than Treasuries. And I try as best as I can to filter out value traps that just don’t have good business models anymore (e.g. fax machine makers in an internet era, or the like).

Along the way, I monitor price and fundamental data, and look for problems. If a stock’s fundamentals deteriorate, it might be worth cutting loose. Similarly, if a good stock becomes greatly overpriced, it might be worth trimming. If a stock’s fundamentals continue to be great, and its price continues to be reasonable, then it might be worth leaning into and adding more.

Using F.A.S.T. Graphs data, for example, we can compare Alphabet and Exxon Mobil.

-The past decade has been very kind to tech companies. Major innovations have occurred, industries have been disrupted, and high valuations have been awarded. The company averaged just over 20% annual earnings growth during the five-year stretch from 2018 to 2023, and the balance sheet is stellar. Forward growth is expected to be in the teens for a while. The current earnings multiple for expected 2023 earnings is about 24x, which is under 2x earnings growth (as long as earnings growth remains above 12% per year). We should expect some valuation contraction as the company reaches market saturation, and interest rates are rather high, so ideally I’d like to get it for under 20x earnings, and consider it a “hold” at this time at 24x earnings.

-The past decade has not been kind to oil companies. Exxon Mobil is trading at 12.5x analyst-estimated 2023 earnings. That’s rather cheap, and it’s not as though these are peak earnings years; oil has been in a bear market for a decade and a half. The company has a great balance sheet, and I think the worst of the oil bear market is over, so I expect the stock to chop along while paying gradually growing dividends, with potentially major price upside should a structural oil bull market materialize over the next five years as I expect it to. Deeper value names like Shell (SHEL) or BP (BP) can be bought for under 8x forward earnings, although they have a bit more baggage overall.

## Final Thoughts: Energy Price Update

Crude oil has broken out to higher highs for the calendar year. And in terms of momentum, oil is back above major moving averages, oil’s major moving averages are turning up, and oil has a constructive MACD on the weekly chart.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-wti-crude.jpg)

I continue to have a long-term bullish outlook on oil given the tightness on the supply side, although I don’t necessarily expect bullish “fireworks” until there is either a better liquidity environment and some degree of economic re-acceleration, or major geopolitical escalation.

And as I mentioned in the equity section above, large oil and gas producers are in pretty good shape right now. I like the long-term risk reward of owning them at low valuations as they pay dividends, buy back shares, and strengthen their balance sheets. Some of them are earning more interest on their cash and cash-equivalents than they are paying on their long-term fixed rate debt, which is remarkable.

Energy producers are generally in the “value” category, which is relevant for the earlier report section on the topic. The top-tier integrated oil majors can be purchased for a low double-digit earnings multiple, and the middle-tier integrated oil majors can be purchased for a single-digit earnings multiple. With good balance sheets and without oil sitting at a middling price level.

Additionally, uranium has been continuing to break out. It had a brief spike in the months after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine because that added some geopolitical sourcing risk. The price eventually retraced from that spike, but so far 2023 has been a more natural bull market. It’s up to over $58 as of late last week:

![Uranium Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-3-uranium.png)

Today, one of the world’s largest uranium producers, Cameco (CCJ) [announced](https://www.cameco.com/media/news/cameco-provides-production-and-market-update) likely production shortfalls for the remainder of 2023, meaning that the supply side will be tighter than previously expected. We’ll see how this might impact the uranium price in the week ahead.

I don’t have a firm view on the next few months, but I continue to view uranium trusts (Sprott Physical Uranium Trust or Yellow Cake plc) as attractive assets to hold with a multi-year view as a small slice within a diversified portfolio. Nuclear power plants will need to buy a lot of uranium contracts over the next few years, and ongoing mine production is below ongoing consumption levels.

Now that there’s been some continued follow-through to the upside, I am re-iterating a bullish view on uranium for the intermediate and long term. It’s not an asset that I try to trade, but rather just have a buy-and-hold position as a small portfolio slice until 1) the commodity is more expensive than the cost of production and 2) sentiment becomes overdone to the upside.
