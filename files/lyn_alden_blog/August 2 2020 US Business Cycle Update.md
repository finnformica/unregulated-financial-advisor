## U.S. Business Cycle Status

Business activity in the United States and elsewhere has picked up from the April bottoms, but remains a very mixed bag depending on sector.

**COVID-19 Updates**

The spread of COVID-19 has slowed in recent weeks in the United States.

Here are daily new cases and deaths for Texas, as a key example:

![COVID-19 Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-2-covid19.png)

*Chart Source: Worldometer*

Daily death numbers happen with a lag behind daily new cases.

Last month, when we had rising new cases but not yet a rise in deaths, I wrote that we need to look for a potential increase in deaths by mid/late July, and that’s what we got.

This month, we can probably say the opposite: with daily new cases no longer increasing, the daily death rate also looks set to level out in the coming weeks as well. Hopefully that happens.

**Unemployment Updates**

Jobless claims, on the other hand, are less rosy:

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-2-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

I’ve written about my view for the past few months that when PPP loans begin to mature at the end of June, meaning employers reach the date where they can lay off employees but still have those PPP loans turn into grants, we’re likely to see some job losses from that.

This is still playing out but essentially, July did see a leveling-off of the jobs situation, rather than the improving trend it saw in May and June. Initial jobless claims are still coming in at 1.3 million or more per week (way above 2009 peak weekly levels), and continued claims have been sticky at over 17 million.

We’re also at the stage where seasonal adjustments start to work against the headline numbers rather than in favor of them.

If we dig into the details, more of the job losses that were classified as temporary are becoming permanent, and the job losses are moving up the income ladder, affecting more white collar workers as general downsizing occurs. Data from Yelp confirms this from the employer side as well; each month, more of the business closures since March have become permanent rather than just temporary.

And of course if we include the Pandemic Unemployment Assistance program, which provides unemployment benefits to 12 million self-employed people that lost income, there are still over 30 million total people receiving unemployment assistance at this time.

The early spike of job losses was mostly the front line blue-collar workforce getting furloughed from restaurants, hotels, airlines, retail stores, casinos, and so forth. But as this persists month after month, the normal recession credit cycle is coming to the forefront, as all sorts of companies begin to trim their workforce and get lean, which includes making many of those furloughs permanent and laying off a lot of white collar professionals whose work wasn’t directly impacted by COVID-19, in order to cut costs.

**Housing and Remodeling**

Average selling prices for U.S. homes declined a bit in Q2 of this year:

![Average House Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-2-home-prices.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

On the other hand, there is an increase in demand for mortgage refinancing, as mortgage rates have hit record lows. In addition, more people are remodeling their homes to adjust to a work-from-home approach, and because if they are traveling less and stuck at home, they might as well get around to some of those fixes and improvements they’ve been putting off.

Real estate is a very local market. Suburban and rural housing in many places has been getting a lot more attention, while expensive condos in city centers have been under pressure.

Although much of the economy did not have a V-shaped recovery, new home sales did:

![New Home Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-2-new-home-sales.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As a result, the iShares Home Construction ETF (ITB) which holds the major homebuilder stocks, surged to an all-time high.

Overall home ownership increased 2.6% between Q1 and Q2 of this year, this is a remarkable move in such a short period:

![Home Ownership](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-2-homeownership.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

## Summary and Fiscal Cliff

The key development this month is to see how the next round of U.S. fiscal stimulus plays out. The $600/week in extra federal unemployment checks that 30 million unemployed people are currently receiving just expired.

Members of Congress and the White House are currently debating the next round of fiscal stimulus, which is expected to be over $1 trillion and could top $2 trillion, but there are significant disagreements to work through to get to a final bill:

![Stimulus Round Two](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-2-stimulus-debate.jpg)

*Chart Source: [New York Times](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2020/07/30/upshot/coronavirus-stimulus-bill.html?smtyp=cur&smid=tw-nytimes)*

Depending on what exactly is counted, about $3.2 trillion has already passed, with a heavy emphasis on business aid. For this next round, Democrats want a larger plan with more emphasis on state and local government aid and more direct consumer aid, whereas Republicans want a smaller bill with more emphasis on continued business aid. I suspect we’ll end up with a bill somewhere in the middle, but we’ll see.

The one area where they overwhelmingly agree, is another round of direct stimulus checks.
