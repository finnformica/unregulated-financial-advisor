**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

![Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

## Digital Assets Note

In the first couple weeks of the spot bitcoin ETF launch, a lot of funds flowed out of the Grayscale ETF (GBTC) and into the newer cheaper spot ETFs (IBIT, FBTC, etc). The funds that were previously in GBTC were kind of “trapped” by a discount to net asset value, and the conversion from a trust to an ETF closed that discount and enabled those funds to get out into better vehicles.

Over the past couple of weeks, that effect has been slowing down, and incoming fund flows are still solid, meaning that the total number of coins held in the ETFs is rising (as marked by the green arrow in the chart below). I’ve spoken to a number of executives in the spot bitcoin ETF industry and my understanding from them is that they are still in the early phases of marketing their products to their network of clients. Their clients are various wealth managers that then either market the funds to their clients (“solicited”) or at least allow the clients to get into them if they bring the topic up (“unsolicited”).

**Bitcoin Layer Two Development**

Aside from the new spot ETFs that continue to gather assets, the main trend in the digital asset space recently is the rekindling of interest in Bitcoin Layer-2 designs by developers and venture capitalists.

During the prior crypto cycle, tens of billions of dollars worth of venture capital went toward altcoin (non-bitcoin) ecosystem development, and only a tiny fraction of that amount went toward bitcoin ecosystem development. And yet despite all of that overwhelming imbalance of venture investment, Bitcoin remained by far the most valuable network.

Bitcoin is simple by design in order to optimize security and decentralization. In fact, near the beginning of the project, Satoshi *removed* some of the original opcodes to further simplify the code, and thus reduce the number of attack surfaces against Bitcoin. Most altcoins on the other hand are more programmable and scalable, but this comes at the cost of major complexity, increased centralization due to higher bandwidth and storage requirements, a vastly larger attack surface, and less immutability.

However, tight constraints over time can breed innovation. In recent months, a significant amount of capital has been rotating *back toward* the Bitcoin development ecosystem due to new developments in Bitcoin smart contract methods that work around its limitations and mostly remain on higher layers of the network where they don’t meaningfully risk the base layer itself.

In January 2023, Casey Rodarmor developed Ordinal Theory, which is an off-chain model for “ordering” the smallest denomination of bitcoin, which are known as “sats”. This, along with the practice of inscribing large data files (mainly images) into the signature portion of the Bitcoin blockchain which was inadvertently enabled by some soft forks in 2017 and 2021, allowed users to “own” images and other NFTs as long as they subscribe to the concept of Ordinal Theory. This began a speculative frenzy of creating and trading bitcoin NFTs known as “inscriptions”. As an analog example, collecting certain “rare sats” or “sats inscribed with images” is kind of like collecting rare versions of coins that have a higher value than their face value (at least to those who care about the unique attributes).

In March 2023, an anonymous developer created the BRC-20 protocol, which allowed people to define new token standards. People can create all manner of altcoins on top of bitcoin, in other words. People could previously always do that on higher layers, but this makes it possible to do on the base layer. It’s a very data-inefficient method, however. A similar protocol called Stamps or SRC-20 has a similar effect. Casey Rodarmor, the previously-mentioned inventor of Ordinal Theory, is currently working on a project called Runes that does a similar thing to BRC-20 and SRC-20 but with more data efficiency.

The main impact of inscriptions and BRC-20/SRC-20 tokens have had on Bitcoin itself thus far, is that they’ve resulted in higher average fees because they take up some of the blockspace. There have always been some non-monetary uses of the Bitcoin base layer blockspace because it’s the most immutable database in the world, but these developments resulted in a large uptick of those activities.

In December 2023, Robin Linus released a [whitepaper](https://bitvm.org/bitvm.pdf) called BitVM, which is a method of bringing some additional smart contracting to the Bitcoin network without a soft fork. It’s limited in terms of complexity due to being constrained by the Bitcoin network’s purposely simple base layer, but by designing programs to only require on-chain computation for fraud proofs (i.e. only resorting to on-chain computation when there’s a problem, and leaving all normal operation off-chain), it potentially allows for some interesting scaling solutions on the Bitcoin network. Unlike Inscriptions and BRC-20/SRC-20 tokens, BitVM has attracted a lot of capital that is focused on utility rather than speculation.

Other than Lightning, most Bitcoin second layers currently rely on a multi-signature federation to custody the bitcoin that collateralizes the second layer. The second layer, unlike the base layer, can be faster, more programmable, and more private, but at the cost of more trust and centralization: if a majority of federation members become dishonest then they can potentially steal the bitcoin from the layer.

BitVM, however, might be able to eventually bring optimistic fraud proofs to Bitcoin federations. This means that only *one* member of a federation needs to remain honest in order to prevent all of the other members from stealing the funds, because even if all of the rest try to do so, the honest member can provide the fraud proof and force the layer into on-chain computation and settlement to stop the theft. Similar tech exists on Ethereum and elsewhere since it’s easier to program on those types of blockchains, but Bitcoin is a larger and more rock-solid base layer. If optimistic fraud proofs become fully developed and available on Bitcoin, it is potentially a powerful improvement for scaling solutions by improving the trust model of existing Bitcoin layers and also allowing for new Bitcoin layers to exist. Certain soft fork proposals, if enabled one day, could make those types of systems even more trustless.

There is already a considerable amount of venture capital pouring into this research and development. I’ve spoken with some of the key developers for this including Robin Linus, and according to him and other developers, BitVM tech is extremely complex (more-so than Lightning-related technology) and expected to take time to mature. But it’s a tremendous narrative shift, and seemingly based on a genuine pathway toward making layers on Bitcoin better over time.

My base case is that we’ll see this trend get over-hyped and overvalued eventually, and that much of it will risk getting washed out in the next downcycle in a couple years, but that the tech that emerges on the other side will be powerful for scaling the Bitcoin network in a layered fashion, along with Lightning.

**LN Markets’ Capital Raise**

The most recent investment announcement by ego death capital, which I’m a partner at, is [LN Markets](https://egodeath.capital/blog/39cy8eq1r34hqbco3ha0xyg72o0juj). Founded back in 2019, LN Markets allows non-US users to trade bitcoin price derivatives (futures and options) via the Lightning network.

Exchange users, especially outside of the top jurisdictions, historically face a dilemma between speed and counterparty risk. Keeping money on an exchange makes the user vulnerable to loss of funds from exchange insolvency or malfeasance. Alternatively, keeping money off an exchange can mean missed opportunities when tradeable (high volatility) events occur, because transferring bitcoin into or out of an exchange can be a slow process.

Usage of the Lightning network optimizes for these trade-offs up to a certain scale by allowing for instant deposits and withdrawals. This allows the user to keep their bitcoin collateral custodially on the exchange only when a trade is active, instantly pull bitcoin back out to self-custody when no trade is active, and instantly deposit bitcoin back into the custodial trading environment when a trading opportunity arises.

As part of LN Market’s capital raise, they announced a new offering called [DLC Markets](https://dlcmarkets.com/). DLC Markets provides a suite of applications to manage OTC derivatives trading. It uses discrete log contracts on the Bitcoin network to minimize or avoid the use of trusted third parties when trading larger-scale derivatives. Users can design derivatives products on various underlying assets with custom margin rules, using the Bitcoin network and DLC Markets’ interface for the underlying automation.

## Exxon Mobil Deep Dive Analysis

If the re-acceleration of the U.S. economy remains in effect (which seems likely but not certain), then the energy sector is potentially mispriced. The same is true if the recent Chinese stimulus has any significant legs to it.

As a sentiment check, Exxon Mobil (XOM) currently has the highest percentage of short interest in modern history:

![XOM Short Interest](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-xom-short-interest.png)

This uptick in short interest has also occurred for other energy majors like Chevron (CVX). It’s an industry-wide thing.

Now to be clear, none of these energy majors are short squeeze candidates, because the overall percentage of shorted shares relative to total liquidity is low. However, doubling from 1% of shares being short to 2% of shares being short is an interesting sentiment check.

Investors in aggregate are heavily focused on AI, and are heavily in the disinflation camp. Energy doesn’t matter anymore from many of their perspectives.

I’m bullish on AI and tech in general, except for extreme valuation companies. But at the same time, I think energy is undervalued.

In terms of valuation, Exxon and other energy majors are in great shape. The company has a low valuation of 11x earnings, pays a 3.66% dividend yield with over 40 years of consecutive annual dividend growth, and its balance sheet has a spectacular AA- credit rating.

![XOM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-xom-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* + black line: the current and historical stock price
  + blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
  + orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
  + white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
  + dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

As a quick comparison, Nvidia (NVDA) is now worth 4.5x as much as Exxon Mobil:

![NVDA vs XOM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-nvda-xom.png)

This is despite the fact that Exxon still earns twice as much income and 7.5x as much revenue as Nvidia:

![XOM vs NVDA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-xom-nvda-fundamentals.png)

Now, this doesn’t mean I’m bearish on Nvidia per-se. I actually think it’s reasonable for the largest AI hardware company to be worth $1.8 trillion. It might take a few years to *grow* its fundamentals into that valuation, but it’s not as though I expect that valuation to collapse back down to where it was a few years ago unless they are heavily disrupted in some way. It wouldn’t surprise me to see Nvidia surpass Exxon in annual net income within the next couple years or so.

Instead, I think the market is undervaluing energy. Energy is what enables the cars, the ships, the planes, the electrical grid, the manufacturing, and the processors, to function. And I think the shale oil supply impact from 2015-2020 is largely behind us. Shale oil production is not growing as quickly as it used to, and instead now shale is a structural part of the oil supply/demand balance rather than an entirely new and disruptive source of supply.

A large part of the market is building their portfolios around four decades of disinflation. The 60/40 portfolio is based on the back-test of ever-lower interest rates and ever-higher equity valuations during most peoples’ investing lifetimes. However, I think a better-constructed portfolio should include a slice of inflation protection for this decade.

I am long equities, bitcoin, gold, real estate, cash-equivalents, TIPS, and energy/commodity producers.

-If fiscal deficits and money supply keep growing and consumer price inflation does not (either thanks to low energy costs or high AI productivity growth rates), then the equities and bitcoin should do very well. This is growth without major inflation.

-If fiscal deficits and money supply keep growing but consumer price inflation gets triggered to the upside, likely due to limited energy capex and substantial energy demand, then I’m happy to be long the energy sector at 12x earnings or less with strong balance sheets and decades of consecutive annual dividend growth. It’s a positive carry hedge against the latter outcome.

Historically, all major inflation spikes in the modern era have occurred alongside a rapidly rising oil price. Oil tends to go through capex cycles, and either periods of heightened demand or supply constraints (generally associated with war or major dollar index downcycles) can cause a major price spike and an associated capex cycle to resolve it. The following chart shows the five-year rolling percent change in both oil and CPI:

![CPI vs Oil](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-cpi-oil.png)

The oil price spike of the 2000s decade had the least inflation associated with it, and this fact was largely due to China and globalization. The globally deflationary effects from China ramping up their manufacturing capability, and with much lower costs than those of developed countries, helped to offset the otherwise inflationary effects of rapid oil price increases in that decade.

If we were to see another major oil price spike and capex cycle within the next decade, it’s unlikely that China or other manufacturing hubs would be able to offset it this time, although productivity efficiencies from AI might be able to offset some of it. I would definitely want to have some oil and gas exposure in that environment.

Importantly, even without a major oil price increase, energy companies are currently inexpensive, well-capitalized, profitable dividend-payers at current oil prices as a baseline.

**The Buffett Effect**

Warren Buffett in recent quarters seems to have a similar energy bull theme. Berkshire Hathaway’s latest 13F filing showed that they have been trimming their Apple (AAPL) position and adding to their already-large Chevron (CVX) and Occidental (OXY) positions. Adding to their existing energy names has been a theme for them for several quarters recently. When in doubt, they seem to just throw more cash into energy.

Back in 2020, Buffett made an excellent investment. His company Berkshire sold yen-denominated bonds, and bought large stakes in the five biggest Japanese trading houses. These companies known as the “[Soga shosha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sogo_shosha)” are unique Japanese-style conglomerates that engage in all sorts of commodity-related and supply chain activities. Berkshire has been holding them ever since as long-term investments and dividend-payers, and they have outperformed broad Japanese equities and the S&P 500, even in dollar terms let alone in yen terms.

![Buffett Investment](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-2-18-buffett.png)

I’ve been long four of the five of them as well, and previously had some of them in the No Limits portfolio until I trimmed them out of that portfolio due to major gains.

I think Buffett sees similar value in the energy companies today as he saw in the Soga shosha companies four years ago. The shares of energy producers are cheap, and in particular they are basically short positions on currency and long positions on scarcer assets like hydrocarbon deposits and infrastructure. This is because most of them have liabilities denominated in long-term corporate bonds with low fixed rates, and they hold substantial cash-equivalents in variable-rate securities like T-bills. So if interest rates stay high or even head higher, they are in the opposite position that banks are in: they earn *more* interest income.

## Final Thoughts: Heterogeneous Recovery

As loose fiscal policy seems to be winning over tight monetary policy in the United States, it’s important to note that not all parts of the economy are impacted equally. I have emphasized this fact quite heavily in many recent reports. This is because some sectors are not interest rate sensitive and are directly or indirectly on the receiving side of major fiscal deficits, while other sectors are interest rate sensitive and are not receiving much of the fiscal support.

In fact, recovery in some of the stronger areas can *worsen* the prospects for the weaker areas.

For example, commercial real estate is a particularly weak area economically at the current time. The office portion of it is structurally impacted by work-from-home trends, and broadly it is a sector that is very sensitive to interest rates, because commercial properties tend to be heavily leveraged with shorter debt durations than residential properties.

If the rest of the economy was cooling off, then the Fed would have an easier time justifying rate cuts, which would relieve some of the pressure on commercial real estate. However, because several other parts of the economy are doing fine or are re-accelerating for various reasons including strong fiscal deficit support, it makes Fed rate cuts harder to justify, which keeps the pressure on commercial real estate.

Barry Sternlicht, a billionaire commercial real estate magnate, has repeatedly pointed to problems in his sector. As [Bloomberg reported](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-01-30/billionaire-sternlicht-sees-1-trillion-loss-in-office-values):

> Once a $3 trillion asset class, offices now are “probably worth $1.8 trillion,” said Sternlicht, chief executive officer of Starwood Capital Group. “There’s $1.2 trillion of losses spread somewhere, and nobody knows exactly where it all is.”

So according to Sternlicht, about $1.2 trillion may have been wiped out of office real estate value. Other commercial property has likely been impacted too, so we could perhaps say there is $1.5 trillion in commercial real estate value wiped out as a baseline expectation.

The entities that were wiped out the most were the equity holders of these impaired properties. The distant second category of impairments were the lenders; banks and investment funds that lent money to the commercial property equity holders. The second category generally only starts being impacted once property values fall *a lot*, since they use conservative loan-to-value ratios to start with.

The biggest seven banks in the United States do not have much commercial real estate exposure relative to their assets. The regional banks in general do have a lot of exposure, and various commercial real estate credit funds of course have a lot of exposure by definition. So that’s where the lending impairment is.

Now, to put this presumed ~$1.5 trillion in commercial property loss in context, that’s smaller than the annual U.S. fiscal deficit currently, which is a stimulatory force. Every year, the federal government is pumping more money into the economy than the estimated cumulative losses on commercial real estate.

And for a bigger (albeit somewhat apples to oranges) context, U.S. households collectively have [$161 trillion](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BOGZ1FL192000005Q) in assets, or [$142 trillion](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BOGZ1FL192090005Q) in net worth after factoring out their [$19 trillion](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/BOGZ1FL194190005Q) in liabilities. Households are the major owners of the properties, the real estate funds, the banks, and so forth. So, we are talking about a 1% economy-wide asset impairment here, although it’s certainly not evenly distributed.

I generally view the economy as being able to absorb the losses in commercial real estate, especially given the structurally loose fiscal backdrop. However, I continue to monitor signs of this, and I expect periodic issues to materialize within parts of the economy that are heavily exposed to commercial real estate.
