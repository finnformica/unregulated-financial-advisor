*April 11, 2021*

**Inflation and Base Effects**

As I’ve been describing for a while now, we are likely to see some pretty high year-over-year consumer price index (inflation) prints in April and May (reported in May and June) thanks to low base effects from the prior year during the heart of the lockdowns:

![Inflation Baseline](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-3-14-inflation-baseline-1.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

There was an [article in the Wall Street Journal](https://www.wsj.com/articles/dont-be-fooled-by-the-stock-markets-newest-magic-trick-that-changed-one-year-returns-11617976919?mod=hp_lead_pos10) a few days ago that I contributed a quote to about these base effects:

![WSJ Quote](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-wsj.png)

That’s one reason I think things like gold and bitcoin could have a nice tailwind later this spring, while bonds might have another bout of weakness. The numbers and narratives will be on the side of the inflationists in year-over-year terms for a few months.

The Federal Reserve has already warned about this, and their position is that the effects will be transitory and that they’re not ready to change monetary policy until they see more persistent core inflation over 2%, going by the way they measure it.

That will be a general theme: deflationists will argue that the inflation pop is completely transitory, while many inflationists will cherry pick the year-over-year figures and claim that major inflation is happening now.

I think the reality will be somewhere in the middle; low year-over-year base effects will exaggerate the numbers for the April/May period, but then I think there’s a good possibility that inflation will surprise to the upside and then be somewhat more persistent than deflationists expect.

The reason I think that’s quite possible is because the growth in the broad money supply (monetary inflation) is rather substantial, and probably enough to overcome the disinflationary structural trends (debt, demographics, offshoring, and technology) for a while as we shift out of various state partial lockdowns, especially if a big infrastructure bill is also passed in 2021 or 2022:

![M2 vs Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-m2-vs-inflation.png)

*Chart Source: Lyn Alden, Data from US Federal Reserve, US Census Bureau*

In other words, I think there has been a significant public and political shift, at least in the United States, to do a persistent amount of fiscal spending beyond this year, leading to larger broad money supply and some degree of higher inflation. This will depend, however, on continued bills this year or next, such as the planned infrastructure bill.

Focusing back on the intermediate term, producer price index updates came out for March during this past week, and they were above consensus expectations. The PPI for final demand is now over 4% for the year over year period, and has a good chance of hitting 7% in April or May due to low base effects for those months:

![PPI vs CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-ppi-vs-cpi-ycharts.png)

The commodity portion of the producer price index is already at 12% year-over-year (the highest since 2008) thanks to low base effects , and could touch 15-20% in April, which gives it a chance of reaching the highest year-over-year figure since the 1970s.

![Commodity PPI vs CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-ppi-vs-cpi.png)*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

If so, the commodity producer price index would just be breaking out from a decade-long sideways base. This following chart shows the commodity PPI in blue, and the year-over-year percent change of that commodity PPI in red:

![Commodity PPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-commodity-ppi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The 1970s had a sustained rise in commodity prices, and so did the 2000s. The 2010s were a disinflationary decade without higher prices for most commodities.

I do think that this commodity PPI breakout is important, and that the 2020s could see a persistent increase in commodity prices out of that base, and end up looking more like the 2000s and 1970s. However, we must separate that outlook from the weird base effects that we’ll see in April and May.

The persistence of these higher commodity prices will depend in part on supply, and in another part on ongoing fiscal policy including infrastructure stimulus.

The US Senate parliamentarian recently ruled that the Senate can pass another budget reconciliation bill this year, meaning that Democrats could potentially pass an infrastructure bill here in 2021 with 51 votes, avoiding the filibuster-proof-requirement of 60 votes. However, there are some centrist Democrat senators like Joe Manchin that push back on Biden’s proposals and may partially reduce their size and scope, since with such a tight vote, every dissenter among the Democrats has a lot of power.

If we look globally rather than just in the United States, input costs also suggest somewhat higher consumer prices ahead, at least temporarily, unless the entire difference is absorbed in the form of lower profit margins by companies:

![JPM Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-jpm-inflation.jpg)

*Chart Source: JPMorgan and IHS Markit*

**10-Year Treasury Yield**

The 10-year Treasury rate has consolidated in recent weeks. It hit as high as 1.74% in mid-March, but is now down slightly to 1.67%.

![10 Year Treasury Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-10-year-rate.png)

The orange line on that chart is likely to pop up to 3% or more due to base effects in April and May, so we’ll see to what extent the bond market can look through that.

Overall, I’d rather be in things like gold or bitcoin or value stocks and benefit from the inflation narrative, than the 10-year note that could be further hurt by the narrative, as we head into these base effects.

When we potentially have PPI in the ballpark of 7% year over year, and CPI inflation over 3% for a couple months, will bond yields still hang around below 1.7%?

However, it’s all a matter of probabilities in terms of how the market will digest these numbers. A lot of institutions are basically mandated to own sovereign bonds, and so along with the Fed purchasing $80 billion per month, it might be enough to hold yields low.

**Dollar Index**

The dollar index corrected downward since the start of April, and is currently a Rorschach test.

Dollar bulls will see that it’s still making higher highs and higher lows in this current mini-bull-trend since the beginning of 2021.

Dollar bears, on the other hand, will call this recent top a lower-low than the September 2020 bounce, and may see this move as the beginning of the next leg down to lower lows.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-dxy.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

I have the structural dollar bear side as my base case, but I don’t know if this particular rally is done or not. We would need to see a break below 91 (interesting) and then a break below 89 (very interesting) to get me focused again on the bearish direction. Conversely, higher-highs above 95 start to get pretty interesting for the dollar bulls.

The US is doing the most fiscal stimulus, and has been more lax at suppressing the long end of the Treasury curve as many other regions are doing, which has been relatively good for the dollar and dollar-based assets since the start of 2021. However, if yields get too high and start breaking things in the market (housing, credit, and/or growth stocks), and the Fed steps in to suppress long end yields more, that would likely result in the dollar rolling over.

As of now, I watch.

## Gold Miners and Royalties

I covered two gold royalty companies, Franco-Nevada (FNV) and Sandstorm Gold (SAND) in my deep dive report from two weeks ago.

After gold miners and royalty stocks had a big correction from August 2020 until recently, I’m pretty bullish on them again. Nobody wants them, they’re really out of favor, but their fundamentals have almost never been better relative to the price of gold.

This chart is the ratio of the gold miner index to gold. When it’s going higher, it means gold miners are outperforming gold. When it’s going lower, it means gold is outperforming the miners:

![XAU vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-xau-vs-gold.png)

During the long gold bear market from the 1980s to the early 2000s, the ratio of gold to its miners chopped along sideways.

Then, during the big gold bull market that went into 2012, gold miners destroyed a lot of capital by failing to control costs and making overpriced acquisitions. When the gold price rolled over after 2012 into a bear market, gold miners crashed very hard, never having accumulated profits or strengthening their balance sheet during the biggest gold bull market in decades.

After bottoming in 2015/2016, the gold mining industry found religion and started focusing on deleveraging their balance sheets, cutting costs, and being more disciplined with acquisitions and new projects. They spent the next 5-6 years doing that, and now they have some of the best balance sheets and strongest free cash flow profiles that they’ve ever had.

Newmont (NEM), for example, reached a peak of $5.4 billion in net debt in 2012, and now has $200 million, meaning they have almost as much cash as debt. Barrick (GOLD) reached a peak of $13.4 billion in net debt in 2013, and now has $100 million. These balance sheets of the two largest miners in the indices are super strong, and many others are as well.

Here’s Barrick’s F.A.S.T. Graph for free cash flow:

![GOLD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-gold-fastgraph.png)

While I like both gold and gold miners here, I prefer gold miners over gold at this time.

Meanwhile, the gold miner ETF just had a positive MACD crossover on the weekly chart, for the first time since April of last year:

![GDX Technical Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-gdx-technical.png)

I’m not ruling out another lower low as a possibility from here, but for now unless proven otherwise, I’m going with the base case that this was the local bottom in gold miners and royalties. Part of investing is making a base case based on probabilities and then monitoring it, so that’s my view here. If GDX goes below $31, then that view is invalidated for now, with a lower low.

![ETH Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-eth-price.png)

The ETHBTC ratio chart remains well below all-time highs, but has been in an uptrend since its bottom in mid-2019. Currently, however, the ratio is still below its local high set back in late January 2021:

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-ethbtc.png)

*Chart Source: TradingView*

The amount of ETH on exchanges has been in a downtrend since August 2020, similar to what’s happening with BTC. All else being equal, that’s bullish.

Part of this exchange drawdown is because ETH staking was introduced several months ago, meaning that people can lock up their ETH on the new beacon chain and serve as validators. Currently over 3.8 million ETH are staked and earning an average of 8% yield. That’s kind of the equivalent of ETH bulls “HODLing” their coins, since they are committing long-term regarding ETH’s success, rather than trading their coins around on exchanges. Some stakers do, however, sell their staked ETH to other people. So even though staked ETH can’t be unstaked during this transition process, owners can sell their staked ETH to other people who are willing to hold staked ETH. It’s kind of like trading around shares of a private company; less liquid but doable.

Developers also accelerated their plans to introduce EIP 1559 to Ethereum, hoping to make the change in 2021. If enacted, this will give the protocol a clearer monetary policy, including a deflationary variable to offset the inflationary issuance, although it remains to be seen if the monetary policy will remain unchanged from that point or if they will change it again in the future.

I prefer the risk-adjusted return potential of BTC, although some of my readers prefer a broader crypto portfolio so we were due for an ETH update here. Basically, I have a tactically bullish outlook for ETH with a 6-month view along with BTC, but not a ton of conviction on that protocol in general, especially longer-term. As the second largest digital asset by market capitalization, I’ll continue to cover it from time to time.

For my DeFi testing, I have a little bit of ETH. It’s not a meaningful percent of my net worth (less than 1%) but it has made some positive gains and I consider it a useful area to maintain knowledge and awareness in, so that I have an informed opinion on parts of the ecosystem. I also maintain an allocation to stablecoins, which run on the Ethereum blockchain.

In terms of stablecoins (crypto tokens backed up by US dollars), I’ve been using AAVE to earn 11% or so on USD Coin deposits, and BlockFi to earn 8.6% on Gemini Dollar deposits. Both USD Coin and Gemini Dollar are some of the more regulated/reputable USD stablecoins out there. With these stablecoin deposits, I take some counterparty risk, but not really much price risk.

Between the AAVE and BlockFi, I like BlockFi better, although a bit of diversification is nice. AAVE is a somewhat decentralized “DeFi” protocol while BlockFi is a regulated financial company. The rates fluctuate over time, especially for AAVE, but BlockFi has free deposits and a free withdrawal each month vs AAVE that has high fees due to Ethereum in general having high fees for smart contracts, and BlockFi can work better during illiquidity events.

I wrote a [review of BlockFi](https://www.lynalden.com/blockfi/) a couple months ago, after testing it out myself, speaking with one of my acquaintances who is a VC for the company about risk management, and after speaking with BlockFi’s CEO about risk management as well.

## Facebook and Amazon Analysis

For this report, I’ll be focusing on the top five stocks of the S&P 500, since it’s not an area that I cover very often and yet they are big drivers for S&P 500 returns. We often go into out-of-US markets, or into value sectors, but it’s important to monitor the US mega caps as well.

Based on my analysis, some of them likely have better return prospects than others. In particular, with a 3-year outlook, I would expect Facebook (FB) and Amazon (AMZN) to outperform Microsoft (MSFT), Apple (AAPL), and Alphabet (GOOGL) as a base case. It probably won’t work out that cleanly, and we’ll have some surprises, but it’s important to monitor these globally relevant companies with some semblance of fair value.

Currently, four of these stocks have market capitalizations over $1 trillion. Together, the five of them make up over 21% of the 500-company S&P 500 index.

![Market Capitalizations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-market-capitalizations.png)

All five of them have absolute fortress balance sheets.

In terms of S&P credit rating, Microsoft holds the top spot with perfect AAA credit. It’s tied with only Johnson and Johnson (JNJ) as being a company with a perfect AAA credit rating. The other top stocks all have near-perfect credit ratings, ranging from Amazon with AA- to Apple and Alphabet with AA+. Facebook has no debt and thus no S&P credit rating.

In terms of the balance sheet numbers, four out of five of the stocks have more cash equivalents than long-term debt, and thus have negative long-term net-debt. Only Apple has positive net debt levels, meaning it has more debt than cash equivalents. On this chart, down is good, meaning more cash equivalents than long-term debt:

![Net Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-net-debt.png)

So, this isn’t an analysis of whether these are powerful companies. Clearly they are. Instead, it’s an analysis of valuation risks and fundamental tail risks for their ongoing growth rates.

For all of these F.A.S.T. Graphs below, the black line is the stock price, and the blue line is what the stock price would be if it were at its average historical earnings multiple over the course of the charting period. The white lines are dividends.

**Apple**

Apple is currently the largest company in the world by market capitalization.

As we can see, Apple’s price and earnings completely separated from each other, with the stock price quickly tripling, and the earnings continuing on as normal:

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-aapl-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

I personally have a tough time finding a bullish case for a company worth over $2 trillion, trading at well over 30x earnings, when earnings growth isn’t very significant. And a big chunk of the earnings growth comes from the company’s share buybacks.

That doesn’t mean I think the stock price has to fall all the way back down to the blue line. The valuation could very well remain elevated. However, it wouldn’t be shocking for the stock to fall to $100 or so. At these levels, the price mostly trades on sentiment rather than fundamentals, and thus has a big range for where it could go, up and down.

If we look at the company’s cash flow growth, it’s rather sluggish in recent years. Meanwhile, the price to cash flow ratio is at modern highs. For these YCharts including this one below, I’m putting company-wide operating cash flow in orange, and the price/cash-flow ratio in purple:

![AAPL CFO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-aapl-cfo.png)

Overall, Apple is not an investment that I find particularly appealing in terms of risk-adjusted returns at the current time with a 3-5 year outlook from a fundamental point of view.

**Microsoft**

I’ve had Microsoft in the newsletter portfolio continuously since its inception in September 2018, and it hasn’t let us down yet:

![MSFT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-msft-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

It has not been an easy investment to stick with, and I’ve repeatedly resisted urges to sell it and take profits. It has been above its historical average valuation, but because it shifted towards a cloud computing model with recurring revenue, it fundamentally changed its value proposition and boosted its growth rate, so I granted it a big allowance to have a valuation premium. That has paid off well, as it has continued to stretch upward.

However, at current levels it’s near the top end of my reasonable valuation range (as it has been for a while), and it’s often a stock that I consider selling, but that never quite makes the cut to sell. In other words, it never quite became so egregiously overvalued relative to its strong fundamentals, that I simply had to cut it loose. So, I’ve held.

![MSFT CFO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-msft-cfo.png)

Although Microsoft’s valuation is near the top end of its range like Apple, it has a better growth rate than Apple, and less of an abrupt decoupling between price and fundamentals.

Microsoft is a stock that I continue to be long and watching closely, often considering a sale but choosing to hold for now.

**Amazon**

Amazon has been an absolute powerhouse over the past decade, becoming the largest online retailer and cloud computing provider in the world.

Unlike many stocks I cover, F.A.S.T. Graphs don’t work well for analyzing Amazon, since it doesn’t trade based on earnings. The P/E ratio is always extremely high, as they constantly plow their money back into capital expenditures, and thus have low reported earnings relative to their size.

![AMZN FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-amzn-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

However, Amazon stock historically trades at around 25x operating cash flow, in a very tight band:

![AMZN FASTGraph OCF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-amzn-fastgraph-ocf.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Over time, as Amazon reaches critical mass and as cloud computing becomes a larger percentage of its business, the margins have been improving. Prior to 2017, the company had a net profit margin of consistently below 2%. For 2018 and 2019 it went over 4%, and in 2020 it went over 5%. So, the complete lack of focus on reported earnings is finally starting to result in major earnings growth, as their previous foundation of heavy investment pays off and the company harvests the gains from the seeds it has been planting for a long time.

So, unlike Apple and Microsoft, Amazon is actually near the low end of its historical valuation range in terms of cash flow multiples.

![AMZN CFO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-amzn-cfo.png)

Eventually, the company should reach such a large size that its growth rate and valuation level go down.

In the meantime, I do like Amazon stock here with a 3-year outlook. Either cash flow would need to massively disappoint expectations, or the cash flow multiple would need to massively break down below its decade-long trend, for Amazon stock to not do well over the next 3 years.

Either of those outcomes are certainly possible, but rather than bet on either of those two outcomes happening, I’ll remain long Amazon for now. My base case is that the upward trend holds for a while, since I don’t see a catalyst (other than regulation as a tail risk) to stop it in the next 3 years.

Alphabet, the parent company of Google, YouTube, Android, and a variety of smaller businesses, has been another powerhouse. I’ve had it in the newsletter portfolio since January 2019, and it has gone up a thousand dollars per share since then:

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-googl-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

But, it’s not exactly cheap at these levels. In fact it’s at a decade-high valuation.

![GOOGL CFO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-googl-cfo.png)

Alphabet, like Microsoft, is near the top end of my allowable valuation range, but given the strength of its balance sheet (over $100 billion in cash-equivalents and nearly no debt, which gives it tons of flexibility), it hasn’t been egregious enough for me to sell quite yet. It’s closer to my sell level than Microsoft though; right on the border, and I’m closely watching this one for a possible sale.

**Facebook**

Facebook is out of favor at the moment, but like Amazon, is actually kind of cheap by its historical standards.

![FB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-fb-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Facebook is trading near the low end of their historic cash flow valuation range:

![FB CFO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-fb-cfo.png)

A lot of people think of Facebook as an unfashionable social network, reaching maturation and losing younger users. In the United States, it’s increasingly used by generations aged 40+, while youngsters in their teens, twenties, and thirties have flocked to Instagram (owned by Facebook), Twitter (TWTR), Snapchat (SNAP), and TikTok.

However, even though a majority of Facebook’s revenue comes from rich nations in North America and Europe, the majority of Facebook’s users are in ex-China emerging markets.

In ex-China emerging markets, young users still stick to Facebook very strongly. Most of my family and friends in Egypt use Facebook heavily, for example, whether they’re in their 20s or in their 60s. The same is true for most other emerging markets that Facebook is active in. Both Instagram and WhatsApp are used heavily around the world by all age groups.

![Facebook MAUs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-facebook-maus.png)

*Chart Source: Facebook Q4 2020 Earnings Presentation*

The average US/Canadian user generates almost 3x as much revenue for Facebook as the average European user, and more than 10x as much as the average user from other parts of the world.

In that sense, Facebook’s intermediate-term outlook rests heavily on the United States market, but the company is a long-term indirect play on the per-capita wealth growth (and thus advertising spending) of ex-China emerging markets. Facebook has a huge userbase and low revenue per user from those regions, with plenty of room for average per-user revenue growth.

Facebook has over $60 billion in cash-equivalents and no debt. Through a combination of cash usage and debt issuance, they could comfortably buy any $100 billion company, retain a balance sheet that’s as strong as Apple’s balance sheet, and add that new company’s cash flow to their income statement. Or they could start a massive share buyback and dividend program like Apple.

Like Amazon, earnings-based FASTGraphs don’t really work for TSLA. This isn’t uncommon for growth stocks. However, unlike Amazon, even focusing on operating cash flow doesn’t yield very useful results either.

Tesla is primarily a hardware company that augments its hardware with advanced software, but trades at the typical valuations of a pure software company. The price/CFO chart doesn’t work because they haven’t had much CFO…

![TSLA CFO](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-tsla-cfo.png)

Tesla has about a sixth as much cash flow as Facebook, and unlike Facebook, Tesla doesn’t have persistent positive net margins or a massive $60 billion cash war chest, but nonetheless trades at a similar market cap as Facebook, and briefly surpassed it in 2020 before correcting recently.

Tesla is a very narrative-driven stock, based on prospects for self-driving cars and licensing its battery technology to other carmakers around the world in the future.

Meanwhile, however, leading competitors like Toyota (which owns Lexus) and Volkswagen (which owns Porsche, Audi, Lamborghini, Bugatti, and Bentley) are making some strides in the electric vehicle and self-driving race. For example, the Porsche Taycan is a well-received competitor to Tesla’s Model S.

![Car Market Cap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-car-market-caps.png)

If I could choose to buy either Tesla for $649 billion, or Toyota and Volkswagen combined for $391 billion with $258 billion left over for other investments, I’d take the latter two. Maybe I’ll check back on that five years from now and see if that made sense or not.

I generally continue to worry that Tesla ends up after 2020 like Cisco (CSCO) did after its hype bubble in 2000. Did Cisco end up growing revenue and becoming very important over the next 20 years? Yes. But did they justify their 2000 market capitalization with that growth and importance? No.

![CSCO YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-4-11-csco.png)

Overall, among the top five (or six, with Tesla) stocks in the S&P 500, Amazon and Facebook have some of the more historically reasonable valuation levels. And between those two, Facebook generates more clear free cash flow and earnings, and still has a lot of capacity to partially lever its balance sheet one day to boost per-share returns like Apple.

**Risks**

If the top five US companies share one major risk besides overvaluation, it is anti-trust regulation.

Apple and Google control app stores in what is basically a duopoly. Facebook and Google rely on using a ton of user data for targeted ads. They serve as digital gatekeepers and arbitrage our privacy in return for offering products and services that are free or cheap.

Amazon has the largest number of employees out of this group. It’s often praised as a job-creator, but in the grand scheme of things, those jobs were created by putting countless smaller retailers out of business, like the Borg spreading through the galaxy of retail. They made use of the fact that state sales tax laws were slow to keep up with ecommerce, and mostly reinvest their profits and thus report rather low taxable income. Plus, they operate an online market place for other sellers to use, where they often analyze the data of companies that use the marketplace and then compete against them with that data. Plus, by combining cloud computing with ecommerce, they can operate their ecommerce business basically at break-even profitability, and make money from the cloud side, while their competitors need to actually make profits from their ecommerce side. In the long run, they naturally want to automate their warehouses and delivery services as much as possible, which could put an end to their job additions as well.

Microsoft, which was the target of anti-trust activity two decades ago, is ironically one of the least-scrutinized of the mega-caps on that front now. It lost the mobile operating system race, but has a highly diversified business in terms of PC operating systems, cloud computing platforms, gaming, LinkedIn, GitHub, and so forth. There is no single area that they are considered to be too dominant in, other than desktop operating systems that are less important over time relative to mobile devices and the cloud.

With massive market capitalizations, large cash war chests, and still-solid growth rates, these five companies are ripe targets for anti-trust activity and new tax methods by policymakers.

Besides anti-trust regulation, some of them have other risks as well. Being so large, Apple is constantly at risk of saturating its market and rolling over sideways in terms of growth, which wouldn’t be good for a stock that trades at over 30x earnings. Microsoft is in a similar position, being so large, although it likely has a longer runway from cloud growth. Facebook is at risk of user boycotts (often attempted, but so far not successful), and platforms that put a wall around user data and make it harder for Facebook to access and use. Amazon could one day be a victim of major boycotts as well if its trajectory continues as it has been.

## Final Thoughts

We remain in an intermediate period, with no clear direction for major trends.

-After a big decline in 2020, the dollar strengthened compared to other currencies since the start of 2021, but lately in April is rolling over. It’s unclear if this is the end of the bull rally, or just a mini-correction within a larger bull rally.

-Gold has been in a correction since August 2020, but in recent weeks is showing some initial signs of bottoming. Is it a fake-out, or a sign of a real bottom? Either way, gold miners and royalty companies look pretty cheap relative to gold here, with plenty of free cash flow and strong balance sheets.

-The 10-year Treasury yield rolled sideways since mid-March after a strong increase since August 2020. If inflation is of any guide, the yield could easily hit higher highs in the months ahead, but at the moment the trend is questionable.

-After an enormous run, Bitcoin is consolidating sideways but still has on-chain indicators suggesting a good probability of another leg higher. I remain bullish here, viewing a break-out as more of my base case than a break-down.

-Value equities began outperforming growth equities in September 2020, but over the past month we’ve seen a mild resurgence in growth stocks from oversold levels. This may very well be a contrarian rally. The large growth stocks are strong, but pricey. I like a mix of some value and some growth, with a focus on some individual names over others.

In my view, the low base effects and heightened year-over-year numbers from April through the end of Q2 (reported in May through the early part of Q3) are potentially a catalyst for inflationary assets doing well over the next 3-6 months. This would include precious metals, bitcoin, and perhaps value equities. It will depend on how markets perceive the inflation; if they consider it to be transitory then they might look past it, but if they start to view it as persistent, then inflationary assets are probably still underpriced.

So, my base case is that the dollar index is rolling over and gold is bottoming, although it remains only mild conviction until we see more follow-through. It’s a working hypothesis for now.

I will continue to monitor these unclear trends in relation to asset valuations, and inform readers once a clear trend establishes itself again from my perspective.
