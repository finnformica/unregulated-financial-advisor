# Analysis of the Market Drop

*Published February 25, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report is a brief unscheduled note to update readers about the large stock market declines over the past two days.

## Back-to-Back -3% Days

Yesterday, the S&P 500 had first daily drop of more than 3% since 2018, and then followed it up with a second 3% daily drop today.

As recently as Wednesday last week, the S&P 500 was at all time highs. It declined on Thursday and Friday, and collapsed hard early this week on Monday and Tuesday after weekend reports about the coronavirus spreading in multiple countries. The equity market suddenly took seriously what the bond market had already been fretting about for a while now.

With four down days in a row, equaling about -7.6% total, this appears to be the fastest drop from all-time highs on record for the S&P 500, going back decades. There have of course been far bigger and swifter drops in the past, but not one this big in a week directly from all time highs like this.

Back on January 26th, I sent out an “[Elevated Risk Alert](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-1-26/)” note, which like this one was an unscheduled note between normal semi-monthly premium reports. The point of the note was that the market is now quite risky, with overbought market conditions combined with the coronavirus shock on supply chains and tourism. This risky environment remains in place.

A sizable stock price drop can open up some opportunities in good stocks, but the overall market remains expensive, we continue to see recession indicators flashing yellow or in a few cases red, and we have unresolved and still relatively unknown impacts from the coronavirus working their way around the global supply chain and global tourism industry.

If we look at the daily chart, the S&P 500 broke straight through its 50-day moving average, remains above its 200-day moving average, and is now slightly oversold on the RSI.

![S&P 500 Daily](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-25-spx-daily.png)

I wouldn’t be surprised to see some sort of bounce come soon, as dip buyers come back at some point, but that bounce (if we get it) doesn’t necessarily give any sort of “all clear” signal.

The weekly S&P 500 chart recently had a bearish MACD crossover from high levels:

![S&P 500 Weekly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-2-25-spx-weekly.png)

In addition, 10-year Treasury note yields reached a record low below 1.34%, and 30-year Treasury bond yields reached a record low of 1.81%. The official inflation rate is currently 2.50%, so the entire yield curve is below the current official inflation rate.

The market is now pricing in 2-3 Federal Reserve interest rate cuts this year. The currency market is responding as well, with a slight decline in the dollar index since its recent high last week. Considering where bond yields are compared to inflation, I consider [precious metals](https://www.lynalden.com/precious-metals-investing/) to be a useful part of a diversified portfolio.

## What to Do?

What investors should do depends on their unique situation, and their specific investment process. There is no one-size-fits-all approach.

Some people are asking, “Should I buy the dip?” Others are asking, “Should I get out?”

I personally have not made any significant changes. My portfolios are designed for my specific situation, and I’m already relatively defensively-positioned for my situation, with some allocations to U.S. Treasuries and precious metals to offset the exposure to U.S. equity markets and international equity markets.

The Google Drive link in the [members area](https://www.lynalden.com/members/) has a list of all my portfolio positions.

During the late-2018 equity sell-off, the S&P 500 fell by nearly 20% and then rebounded. My portfolios fell by about 10% and then rebounded, due to their diversified and relatively defensive allocation. For me, having a portfolio that may fall by about half of what the market does in a bear market, meets my needs. An older investor may need a bit more defensive positioning. And it’s impossible to say ahead of time exactly how much a given portfolio will be impacted by a sell-off.

Investors should not put themselves into a situation where they have to make tough decisions on the spot, when the Dow is down 1,000 points in a day. Whether you will sell or stay the course should be mostly decided ahead of time. If you don’t have a process, and are just figuring things out in real time, then you are more vulnerable to potential missteps.

The main question to ask yourself or your financial advisor is if your portfolio’s risk profile fits your situation. If you have too much risk, then sell-off or not, it’s probably time to de-risk. If you had too much risk, it was time to de-risk back on January 26, and the next best time is now. On the other hand, if your risk profile is appropriate for your unique situation, with sufficient defensive hedges, then an investor should stay the course, trusting their investment process and their asset allocation, and to look for bargains as certain stocks or indices become attractively-valued.

Personally, I’m not making changes now. If the selling continues, it may open some more buying opportunities and for shifting some Treasuries into stocks, but I’m not at that point yet. Instead, I continue to dollar-cost average into my existing allocations as they currently are.

Going into this year, the market was already expensive, corporate debt levels were very high, and many economic indicators were showing signs of elevated risk. I recently finished [a detailed article](https://www.lynalden.com/global-dollar-short-squeeze/) about a financial bottleneck that has been anchoring the global economy for about five years now, so I suggest giving that a read if you want a broad macro view.

The coronavirus, now with worsening headlines about its global spread, continues to impact global supply chains and restrict tourism on top of what was already a vulnerable condition. A strong economy with cheap stocks and low leverage is more able to withstand such an impact, but an aging economic expansion with expensive stocks and high debt, is more vulnerable to such a disruption.

The next premium report comes out this weekend, we’ll have a longer update on market conditions, and see where we are then. We’ll go over recent economic data, and look into an attractively-priced stock.
