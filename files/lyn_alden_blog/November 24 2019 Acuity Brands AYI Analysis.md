## U.S. Business Cycle Status

If you didn’t read last week’s free newsletter issue, check it out [here](https://www.lynalden.com/november-2019-newsletter/). Most premium members are already on the newsletter, but if you’re not, you should be ([and you can join here](https://www.lynalden.com/about-lyn-alden/)). It comes out every six weeks and gives high-level updates, whereas this premium service gives more frequent tactical updates and deep-dive analyses.

In that issue, I discussed that we are at an inflection point, meaning that we have completed three “mini-cycles” of growth during this decade-long economic expansion, and are now in a risky phase where we could potentially sink lower into a recession in 2020, or could rebound for a fourth “mini-cycle” of growth.

As of this week, we have a few new data points but are still unfortunately in that unclear inflection phase. I’m focusing primarily on cold, hard, rate-of-change data rather than economic narratives.

As of the past two weeks, my view was uncertain but sided more with the “continued economic slowdown” camp. This recent week’s data has been slightly more optimistic, and pushed me back a bit towards neutral. Specifically, both the manufacturing and services purchasing manager’s index rebounded in November, which is one of the most real-time data points we have:

![Manufacturing and Services PMI, November 2019](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-pmi-1.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

The University of Michigan’s Consumer Sentiment indicator also beat expectations and came in higher than last month.

The bearish argument for these positive data points is that demand is simply being pulled forward due to US/China trade dispute escalation, giving the illusion of a rebound.

Specifically, the U.S. is set to potentially add further tariffs on Chinese imports on December 15th, so businesses may be buying extra goods ahead of time to stock up before tariffs go into effect. Additionally, China has a constant threat of the U.S. government blocking U.S. semiconductor companies from shipping critical chips to Chinese firms, so China may be stocking up ahead of any potential closure of that window.

If this is true, we may see resumed weakening in December/January, and it would show this October/November period to be a “fake out” to the upside from pulled-forward demand. I lean slightly towards this view but can’t be sure until we get more data points in the coming months.

We’ve also had an uptick in initial jobless claims over the past two weeks, which is worth watching and is a potential precursor to higher unemployment. If that becomes a trend, it may confirm a bottom in unemployment, which would be a strong bearish argument that the business cycle is coming to an end. We should have more clarity on this in the next issue, two weeks from now.

## Acuity Brands (AYI) Deep Dive Analysis

Acuity Brands (ticker: AYI) is a mid-cap company that provides lighting and building management solutions.

Since their inception in current public form in 2001, they have beat the S&P 500 by a wide margin. An investor that put $10k into the company in 2001 and reinvested dividends would have multiplied their money by 16x, compared to less than 4x for the S&P 500 with reinvested dividends.

![AYI Historical Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-ayi-returns.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

They’ve also outperformed over the past decade, but they have underperformed over the past 5 years due to that big reduction in the stock price that you can see in the chart above. The company is about 54% down from its all-time highs, despite being of relatively high quality.

Acuity originally came to my attention nearly a year ago when it started showing up on the Greenblatt stock screen, which filters for companies that have high returns on invested capital (a measure of quality) and low valuations (as measured by EBIT divided by enterprise value).

Last year, Acuity brought in over $3.6 billion in revenue and $330 million in net income, and they currently have a market capitalization of $5 billion. They pay a small and flat dividend with a yield below 0.5%, but they are historically a relatively fast-growing company from a combination of organic growth and acquisitions.

This stock has been involved in an interesting misconception and hype bubble, so it went from grossly overvalued to reasonably valued in the past few years, which is what put it on my radar. After watching it and analyzing it for a number of months, I’ve developed a positive long-term bullish conviction on the company, as part of a diversified portfolio.

**Company Overview**

To start with, here’s the company’s own description, from their 2019 10-K:

> We are one of the world’s leading providers of lighting and building management solutions and services for commercial, institutional, industrial, infrastructure, and residential applications throughout North America and select international markets.
>
> Our lighting and building management solutions include devices such as luminaires, lighting controls, controls for various building systems, power supplies, prismatic skylights, and drivers, as well as integrated systems designed to optimize energy efficiency and comfort for various indoor and outdoor applications.
>
> Additionally, we continue to expand our solutions portfolio, including software and services, to provide a host of other economic benefits resulting from data analytics that enables the Internet of Things (“IoT”), supports the advancement of smart buildings, smart cities, and the smart grid, and allows businesses to develop custom applications to scale their operations. We have one reportable segment serving the North American lighting market and select international markets.

-Acuity Brands 2019 10-K Filing

They are primarily a manufacturer of lighting solutions. Indoor lights, outdoor lights, commercial, residential, infrastructural, and so forth. They have about 12,000 employees.

The flashy side of their business is that they also provide “Internet of Things” (IoT) solutions via their Atrius and DG Logik brands.

Modern buildings use sensors to provide light only when people are in a room, which means these sensors can also be used for HVAC regulation, as well as more novel use-cases. For example, in a retail building, sensors can be used in retail buildings for targeted advertisement on nearby display screens, or can be used in office buildings for recording usage data room-by-room for building owners, so that they can optimize the space.

In addition, Acuity’s products are used for outside infrastructure, so they are part of the “smart city” themes that are likely to be tailwinds over the next decade. Like many countries, the United States is estimated to have a large need for infrastructure spending and maintenance over the coming decade, which includes electrical/lighting/IoT upgrades.

Therefore, Acuity’s expansion into building management and IoT solutions makes a lot of sense, and helps tie their products together for customers.

The company estimates that the total annual addressable market for lighting and building management is over $20 billion per year, and that the installed base is approximately $500 billion, or 25x the annual amount. With $3.6 billion in annual revenue, that gives Acuity 15-20% estimated market share, which makes them one of the leaders in the industry. They serve the United States and Canada almost exclusively, with very little international exposure.

Much of their work comes from new commercial, residential, and infrastructure construction, but they also earn revenue from modernizing a portion of that $500 billion installed base of lighting and building management systems each year. Energy efficiency and expense minimization are big themes currently and for the foreseeable future, so this is an industry with a long potential runway of growth and attractive potential investment returns.

**The Hype Bubble**

Readers will of course notice based on the charts above that from 2013 to 2017, Acuity’s stock price decoupled from their fundamental growth, went into bubble territory, and then came crashing back down to reality.

This appears to be for two main reasons.

First of all, the stock partially got caught up in “Internet of Things” (IoT) mania. IoT refers to the concept that most of our devices, ranging from our cars to our refrigerators to our washing machines to our lighting and heating systems, will eventually be connected to the internet, sharing information for optimization, maintenance, security, and convenience, although this of course brings up privacy concerns.

The IoT space had a hype bubble during the 2015/2016 time frame, where many investors became overly optimistic on how fast these technologies would be deployed and how fast companies in the industry would grow. For its part, Acuity Brands, despite primarily being a lighting manufacturing and building solutions company, has seemingly been happy to emphasize its IoT capabilities for investors despite being a relatively small part of its business.

Secondly, this company’s fundamentals are tied considerably to the U.S. construction industry. Here is the year-over-year percentage change in U.S. construction spending:

![Construction Spending YoY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-construction-spending.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The U.S. recession in 2001 had modest impact on construction spending. It dropped from a 5-10% growth rate per year to about flat briefly, before resuming growth.

However, the U.S. recession in 2008 was extremely severe and centered on the real estate industry (and real estate banking leverage), so construction spending fell significantly and took years to recover.

From 2013 to 2017, construction spending fully recovered and enjoyed 10-15% year-over-year percent growth, but this started to slow again in 2018, and as of 2019 is now in a mild contraction once again.

Acuity rode this construction spending wave, with 20% earnings growth in 2014, 36% growth in 2015, and 45% growth in 2016, when the stock price peaked. However, their 8% growth rate in 2017 killed investor enthusiasm and the stock came crashing down throughout the year as disappointing quarterly results came in.

It’s a common mistake for investors to extrapolate current growth rates into the future, and get caught off guard by the fact that most things, like construction spending, are cyclical.

If a company can grow at 20-30% per year for the long-term, then reaching a P/E ratio of over 40 like Acuity did can make sense. However, when it’s just a short cyclical growth burst within the context of a more realistic 8-12% long-term average growth rate, then investors need to discount that growth more heavily. In this case, investors seemed to get caught up with IoT mania and thought the company would grow at a faster rate for longer, when in reality it was very much tied to the construction industry.

Right now, Acuity is in a down cycle, with mid-single-digit growth within the context of a construction industry that is facing mild contraction.

Previous investors’ loss is our potential gain. With the stock down 54% from its 2016 highs, its main industry in a mild contraction, and the company currently being assigned rather low valuation levels, it’s a stock for long-term value investors to consider. The stock has stabilized in a sideways pattern over the past year.

**Management and Company Metrics**

Vernon Nagel has been the CEO for the past 15 years, since 2004, and has been with Acuity since its inception as a public company in 2001, where he started as the CFO. This long stretch of solid performance has been under his leadership.

The company has moderate reviews by employees on Glassdoor.com, but not as good as some other companies in the industry including privately-owned Lutron.

Acuity consistently generates a high return on invested capital, which is part of what put it on my screen in the first place. During most years, the company earns a double-digit return on invested capital, and even during recession or down years, they at least remained profitable. They also have attractive metrics for return on equity and return on assets.

![AYI ROIC ROE and ROA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-ayi-roic-roe-roa.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

In addition, the company has maintained a strong balance sheet. Specifically, they have kept their gross debt levels at almost exactly $350 million for the past 15 years, despite growing revenue, earnings, and assets along the way. As a result, they have become less leveraged over time. Additionally, their net debt (meaning debt minus cash equivalents) has been on a choppy downtrend, because their cash levels have generally increased over time while their debt levels have remained static.

![AYI Debt Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-ayi-debt.png)

Chart Source: Y Charts

At the moment, Acuity has more cash than debt, meaning they are debt-free on a net basis.

Many investors look for growth without considering balance sheet strength enough in their analysis. Acuity could be growing faster if they were willing to take on additional leverage like many companies are doing today, but they are playing it cautiously. Investors that focus on risk-adjusted returns should appreciate this fact.

Acuity performs regular acquisitions of various sizes. The big reduction in cash levels after 2016 was due to sizable acquisition spending. With cash levels continuing to build on the balance sheet today, and plenty of space for leverage should they decide to temporarily use more leverage, Acuity has room for further acquisitions if management finds good opportunities.

**Risks**

Acuity faces normal manufacturing risks, such as the possibility of faulty products, recalls, and lawsuits. There’s always the possibility of abrupt management changes, or surprise events. But aside from those normal risks of doing business, there are a few company-specific risks investors should be familiar with for Acuity.

*Economic Cycle- Major Risk*

In my view, the biggest risk for Acuity is a major contraction in construction spending and a broad U.S. recession. Construction spending is already in a mild contraction which is why I’m happy to start picking up relatively cheap construction-related names like Acuity, but if construction spending falls into a severe contraction like it did after the subprime mortgage crisis, then even Acuity’s slow expected growth over the next couple years may not materialize.

To be clear, Acuity’s balance sheet strength and its proven ability to remain profitable during previous recessions gives it relatively little existential risk from this scenario.

However, the main concern is that if earnings slow down for longer, or even decline, then we’d need to re-adjust our return expectations and valuation model. For example, suppose the company grows earnings by just 4% per year over the next five years, followed by 10% during the five years after that (boom years), and that we are able to sell the stock for a relatively modest P/E ratio of 15 after that decade-long period.

A two-stage earnings model would look like this, and give a fair price today of just $128 for 7% returns, compared to the current price of $127.31.

![AYI StockDelver Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-11-ayi-bearish-model.png)

In other words, under such a scenario, the stock would be expected to produce just 7% average annual returns during the next decade from its current price level. My base case is more optimistic than that, but we need to be aware of these more lackluster outcome potentials.

Investors that bought Acuity stock right at the market top in 2007 matched the S&P 500 by the time the market bottomed in 2009, and outperformed the S&P 500 over the next 5-year and 10-year periods. So, even in this disappointing scenario, I’d still be reasonably happy with Acuity in my diversified portfolio at current levels.

Since the company pays a very low and non-growing dividend, and only pays modestly towards share buybacks, management must produce decent growth organically and through acquisitions to provide strong shareholder returns. Historically, they’ve been able to do that. If at some point this level of growth becomes unsustainable, the company should return more capital to shareholders with higher dividends.

*Stock Market Selloff- Moderate Risk*

Regardless of economic and earnings performance, Acuity’s stock could easily be subject to a 20%+ sell-off should the S&P 500 face a severe correction.

For example, in December 2018, Acuity’s stock fell from $130 to $106. Nothing specifically changed, and they didn’t report earnings during that time. They lost roughly 20% of their valuation in a matter of weeks purely due to market sentiment. Investors need to be aware of this possibility and willing to buy into or dollar-cost average into those periods of weakness. At this stage in the business cycle, this is why I have some cash-equivalents and precious metals in my portfolios. This risk has a high likelihood of occurring but its impact is muted for investors that hold their shares and buy more.

*Tax Changes- Moderate Risk*

U.S. corporations are enjoying the lowest tax rates they’ve had in modern history. Prior to 2018, Acuity’s tax rate was in the mid-30’s percent, but after the U.S. corporate tax cuts that went into effect for 2018 and thereafter, Acuity’s tax rate is now in the lower-20’s percent.

These tax cuts were not paid for by reduced government spending; there were merely additive to the U.S. deficit, which now stands at 5% of GDP and growing. It’s quite possible that future administrations will reverse these corporate tax cuts, which would negatively impact the net income of most U.S. companies including Acuity.

Many investors believe that they are “globally diversified” if they own an index like the S&P 500, since this collection of 500 U.S. companies gets over 40% of total revenues from outside of the United States. However, all companies in the U.S. are subject to the same jurisdictional risk- that the federal government could raise taxes on them or perform other acts to the detriment of their shareholder returns. For that reason, it’s useful to have a truly global portfolio that includes stocks headquartered outside of the United States as well.

*Tariffs- Mild Risk*

Acuity manufactures 82% of its products and purchases 18% of its products. As the company describes in their most recent 10-K:

> Certain critical processes, such as reflector forming and anodizing, high-end glass production, surface mount circuit board production, and assembly are performed (not exclusively) at company-operated facilities, offering the ability to differentiate products through superior capabilities. Other components, such as LEDs, certain LED drivers, lamps, sockets, and ballasts are purchased primarily from third-party vendors.

Combined, 26% of products are made in the United States, 60% are made in Mexico, 11% are made in China, and 3% are made elsewhere. The fact that the bulk of their production is in Mexico rather than China gives them a considerable degree of protection from current geopolitics. Although President Trump has threatened Mexico with tariffs, Mexico has long been a trading partner with the United States, and is not seen as a direct competitor for global influence the way that China is.

In addition to being a potential risk, tariffs are a potential benefit to Acuity. From their 10-K:

> Aggressive pricing actions by competitors, including Asian importers and those within the technology and services sectors, may affect our ability to achieve desired revenue growth and profitability levels under our current pricing strategies. […] In addition, there have been a growing number of new competitors, including lower cost Asian imports, small startup companies, and global electronics, technology, and software companies, offering competing solutions, sometimes deploying different technologies. Asian imports have also increased competition within the lighting market.

They’ve had considerable competition from low-cost Asian imports, so any tariff implementations that affect China without punishing Mexico can benefit Acuity’s pricing power. In addition, Acuity has done a good job of maintaining margins; despite their notes about Asian importers, Acuity has consistently maintained 40%+ gross margins and 12%+ operating margins in recent years.

*Lawsuit- Mild Risk*

Some investors are unhappy with Acuity’s stock price bubble and crash, so there is an ongoing class action lawsuit against Acuity since 2018, claiming that management made misleading statements about potential growth or failed to disclose material headwinds against their business. Acuity of course rejects such allegations.

I am not a legal expert but I have seen little basis for this lawsuit. It’s a cyclical business, and the equity valuations became silly several years ago. Reviewing previous 10-Ks shows that they have disclosed numerous risks, although in earnings calls management may have made optimistic statements that failed to materialize as hoped due to the slowing construction industry.

*Customer Concentration- Mild Risk*

In many previous years, Home Depot was Acuity’s largest customer, accounting for over 10% of Acuity’s sales, both for lighting its own stores and selling Acuity lighting products to customers. Today, there are no customers that account for 10% of sales, although one customer (undisclosed, but likely Home Depot) accounts for 10% of receivables.

*Commodity Prices- Mild Risk*

Acuity purchases about 90,000 tons of steel and aluminum per year for their products, and uses six million gallons of diesel fuel for distribution, and these raw material costs are largely un-hedged. A sharp and sustained rise in commodity prices would adversely affect Acuity’s bottom line, but would also likely affect their competitors.

**Conclusion**

I always like finding a good mid-cap stock.

Based on Wilshire Associates data going back four decades, mid caps are the best-performing size category, outperforming both large caps and small caps on average. They’re typically big enough to have proven business models (unlike a lot of speculative small caps that fail), but small enough to have plenty of growth ahead of them (unlike a lot of large/mega cap stocks that have already saturated their markets).

On top of that, stocks within the Greenblatt screen historically have good returns on average, stocks that have a history of outperformance generally continue to do so (as long as they’re not overvalued), and stocks with a strong balance sheets and long-tenured management tend to be safer than average.

This is a company that has the proven ability to remain profitable during severe recessions, while also outperforming the S&P 500 over nearly two decades since inception. It operates in a competitive but good industry, and is reasonably-valued.

There’s no guarantee that any individual stock like this will outperform, but collecting a few dozen of these sorts of investments historically gives a good recipe for outperformance.

However, investors face a difficult choice in this environment.

On one hand, Acuity’s stock is already down more than 50% from its bubble highs, and is fairly priced for slow growth in the next few years, compared to the S&P 500 which by most historical metrics is demonstrably overvalued. Construction spending is already in a mild contraction, which puts this type of company out of favor (which is good for value investors looking for a rebound in the coming several years).

On the other hand, if the economy enters a full-on recession in 2020, construction spending could continue to decline and we may very well see this stock noticeably lower. While Acuity is certainly not at its high point now in terms of cycle strength or valuation, it’s also not in its worst possible shape, like it was early 2009, for example.

As a result, I’ll be splitting the difference. I plan to add this to my newsletter portfolio and to start dollar-cost averaging into it, with space to add more if we get a full-blown recession and broad stock market sell-off. That’s why the Fortress Income model portfolio, and my other portfolios, have some defensive elements like short-term bonds and precious metals at this stage in the business cycle.
