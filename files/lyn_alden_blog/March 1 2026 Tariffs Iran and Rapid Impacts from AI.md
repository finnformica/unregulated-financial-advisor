**US Tariffs**

On February 20th, the US Supreme Court ruled against President Trump’s use of tariffs in a 6-3 decision.

This could have been one of the major headlines in this report, until everything else happened.

![Politano Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-politano-tweet.png)

Congress holds the primary ability to implement taxes, including tariffs on domestic importers. However, for temporary and limited contexts Congress has delegated tariff powers to the President, such as in emergencies. The issue is that President Trump has referred to emergency authorization to implement global tariffs with indefinite time horizons, which multiple courts including now the Supreme Court have found to be outside of the scope of what emergency authorization can do.

This decision is in line with consensus expectations. Here’s some additional context from back in November:

> As an update, the Supreme Court did indeed have that hearing on November 5th. And based on the questions and remarks by the justices, market odds of the court ruling in Trump’s favor had a sudden drop from ~40% to ~20%.
>
> ![Supreme Court Tariffs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-11-9-supreme-court-tariffs.png)
>
> The nine-member court currently consists of six conservative justices and three liberal justices. All three liberal justices appeared skeptical of the President’s use of emergency powers for tariffs, but notably, several of the conservative justices did as well. Assuming the three liberal justices vote against the emergency justification for tariffs, it would require two of the six conservatives to side with them to strike the extra tariffs down. Historically speaking, a significant subset of political conservatives are opposed to new forms of taxation that do not go through Congress, so this is indeed a case that isn’t necessarily expected to go along party lines.
>
> The ruling may take as long as June 2026 to be announced. However, based on remarks by the Supreme Court, many professional court-watchers expect a decision to be made later this year or early next year, on an expedited basis.
>
> It’s not necessarily a binary outcome. The Supreme Court may limit the power without removing it entirely. And there are complexities regarding whether or how the US federal government would pay back the tariffs already taken if they are ruled to have been illegal.
>
> The tariffs themselves are paid by the importers on US soil. However, who *ultimately* pays the tariffs (i.e. who bears the cost) is a matter of debate and analysis, and can change over time.
>
> -If foreign exporters lower prices to offset the tariffs that American importers pay, then those foreigners are basically paying them instead. However, import price indices show that’s not happening in aggregate so far.
>
> -If importers and other middle-men pass on the costs in the form of higher prices, then American consumers are basically paying them. Overall consumer price index changes have been mild thus far, indicating that some of it is trickling out but not most of it yet.
>
> -If importers don’t pass on prices aggressively, and exporters don’t cut their prices aggressively, then those importers and other middle-men are basically (and literally) paying them in the form of lower margins and the actual tariff payments themselves, which by extension can also negatively impact employment and capex decisions in those industries.
>
> Goldman Sachs released a report in October, using data up through August, that determined US businesses were absorbing half of the cost, with American consumers absorbing about a third, and foreigners paying the smallest share. However, by the end of the year, they expect the majority of the burden to shift toward American consumers.
>
> From a macro perspective, the Supreme Court decision here basically represents a $300+ billion swing in annual fiscal policy. Striking down most of the emergency-power tariffs would basically be a mild stimulus from the current baseline, or a roughly 15% expansion in the fiscal deficit, or about 1% of nominal GDP. Allowing most of the tariffs would keep the current status quo in effect, and potentially enable the President to get more aggressive with the use of emergency powers for the tariffs.
>
> -November 9, 2025 premium report

In response, the Trump Administration has said that they will refer to other laws to maintain tariffs. However, these laws tend to be more limited in scope, magnitude, and duration.

Thus, tariffs won’t instantly disappear, but the ceiling for them is likely in by this point, with flat or diminishing tariffs going forward.

**Strikes on Iran**

By far the leading news this weekend is that Israel and the United States have struck Iran in a major operation, with a specific intention toward regime change and killing Iran’s supreme leader. And indeed, Khamenei has been reported to have been killed early in the conflict.

US Congress holds the power to authorize war, but the 1973 War Powers Resolution gives the President the limited ability to perform military actions within a fixed time period, usually reserved for certain circumstances. After that, it’s up to Congress to provide authorization.

During the battle, Iran struck back at a number of surrounding countries where the US has military bases, many of which were intercepted but some struck. Thus, transit throughout the region has been heavily impaired, and significant economic activity is effectively frozen in a state of emergency.

There’s no shortage of analysts quickly putting the proverbial pen to paper on their substacks about all the ramifications that will follow, so for starters I’ll say the same thing about Iran that I said about Venezuela:

> On Saturday morning, the United States carried out an operation in Caracas, Venezuela, that resulted in the capture of Nicolas Maduro and his wife.
>
> The US has since asserted control over Venezuela for the moment.
>
> There is no shortage of macro analysts turning into geopolitical and energy analysts overnight. For example, this swift change in Venezuela potentially impacts the long-term trajectory of Iran (i.e. if the US feels more secure with Venezuelan oil, they might be more willing to risk a closing of the Strait of Hormuz during war with Iran), and the long-term trajectory of Taiwan (i.e. if the US is more assertive with its hemisphere, China might be more assertive with theirs), and people are quick to jump in with their analysis.
>
> Rather than try to game out to what extent the odds of those things have shifted within an investable time horizon, which I don’t think is a particularly effective exercise, for this piece I’ll just put a few facts in context to highlight the potential impact on oil supply.
>
> Venezuela has the largest oil reserves in the world, but it’s a rather heavy crude in the form of deeply-buried oil sands. As a result, their oil is more expensive to extract than, say, Saudi Arabia’s oil, and thus the effective reserves are much lower unless oil prices are sufficiently high to make it economical. This makes it a useful long-term source of oil as the large conventual sources are used up, but it’s not a swing producer.
>
> Due to a combination of the oil being unconventional, international sanctions, and aging infrastructure amid economic mismanagement, Venezuela only produces around 1% of the world’s oil output. After a significant collapse in production a decade ago, their production levels have gradually been rising in recent years.
>
> ![Venezuelan Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-venezuelan-oil.png)
>
> President Trump has said that US oil companies will be going into Venezuela. Notably, Chevron (CVX) is already allowed to operate in Venezuela, despite the sanctions and other geopolitical issues.
>
> Overall, this outcome likely increases the base case of Venezuelan total oil production over the next several years, but only to an uncertain and limited extent. Oil production is already rising, there is already a presence of certain large US oil interests there, and the type of oil is not conducive to rapid ramp-ups in production.
>
> -January 4, 2026 premium report

So, once again I’ll keep this note narrowly focused on potential market impacts.

Iran is a significant oil and liquid fuels producer, accounting for 3-5% of global production depending on what moment in time the figure is being estimated.

![Iran Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-iran-oil-production.png)

However, the total potential impact from war in the region is much larger than that. The exact numbers vary by time and whether one focuses on oil specifically or the combo of oil and other hydrocarbon liquids, but somewhere in the ballpark of 20% of global production goes through the Strait of Hormuz. The vast majority of it heads east toward Asian markets.

Iran has announced the closing of the Strait, but GPS monitoring on Saturday indicated that ships were still passing through, albeit at reduced volumes. Beyond that, there’s an issue of insurers cancelling policies on ships that pass through the Strait, which can turn the transit into an uneconomical decision regardless of whether the Strait is technically closed or not.

As of this writing on Sunday, [three ships have been struck](https://www.reuters.com/business/energy/three-tankers-damaged-gulf-us-iran-conflict-escalates-2026-03-01/), and over 200 are holding at anchor instead of passing through. The shipping company Maersk has an announced a suspension of its ships passing through.

Additionally, Iran can strike major production assets throughout the region, taking them offline for weeks or months depending on the severity of the hit. Many of those attacks can be intercepted, especially if there are major naval assets in the region prepared to do exactly that, but not necessarily all of them. And Iran’s initial counterattacks [depleted](https://www.wsj.com/world/middle-east/u-s-races-to-accomplish-iran-mission-before-munitions-run-out-c014acbc) a significant percentage of US stockpiles of interceptors, since our military weakness is the limited size of our industrial base and the finite speed with which we can produce them.

As a base case, I don’t expect the Strait to remain closed or severely limited for very long, nor do I expect catastrophic losses in energy production in the region, but assessing that is not my expertise. As a general rule, Iran has been unable or willing to hit as hard as they’ve often threatened, and significant US military assets were concentrated in the region by commanders that have significant intelligence on Iran’s capabilities. With a fractured leadership structure, Iran’s capabilities are also impaired the longer the operations continue. The military experts I’m in contact with hold those views as well.

But overall, this is part of why I have a significant amount of energy producers in the model portfolios. There are many tail risk scenarios, including primarily this one, that can drive energy prices higher. Beyond that, energy prices and energy producers are often uncorrelated with broader equities and bonds, because a shortage in energy can create a stagflationary situation that is good for energy producers (or at least those who are largely unaffected by whatever crisis caused the shortage) while being bad for most other assets.

Outside of potential oil impacts, this is mostly a humanitarian and geopolitical topic. It potentially impacts US midterm outcomes (this administration explicitly ran against war), US/China relations, the quality of life for Iranian citizens, civilian casualties in the war, and many other topics that are outside of the scope of a financial/tech analyst.

**Lightning Network Growth**

River Financial publishes some of the most extensive annual research on Lightning network (Bitcoin’s channel-based second layer for smaller, faster, and more private payments), and they just released their [latest report](https://x.com/SDWouters/status/2024507942708351443).

Although the report came out in February, the data is for November 2025. Bitcoin’s price was at the same approximate level in November 2025 as it was in November 2024, and yet River’s data showed huge growth in Lightning volumes during that year:

![Lightning Volumes](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-lightning-volume.png)

They also showed large growth in transaction counts during the period, although the year 2023 still holds the crown there because that was a big year for experimentation in micropayments for games and Nostr. The average transaction size has grown dramatically over the years:

![Lightning Transaction Sizes](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-lightning-transaction-sizes.jpg)

River attributes this to a shifting pattern of use for Lightning. It’s often used now for sending money to or from exchanges in moderate amounts, which increases the average transaction size. Meanwhile, other solutions like Chaumian ecash and Ark have emerged as popular “last mile” solutions for payments, alongside traditional custodial systems. Thus, the Lightning network has solidified itself as the key middle layer that ties a lot of ecosystems and tech stacks together.

An important note is that the Lightning network is decentralized and private by default. In fact, there isn’t even just one Lightning network; any handful of nodes can set up channels with each other that are entirely separate from the main Lightning network. Even many parts of the main network are “dark” to those who attempt to measure its data, since many users and companies don’t share their data publicly.

Therefore, no report can capture all the activity in the network; they can only measure some of it based on the nodes that they’re able to get good data from, including River’s own large node as well as large partner nodes.

At [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/), the venture fund I’m a partner at, we’ve invested in a number of companies that make use of the Lightning network, and we have access to much of their data. While I can’t go into specifics, I can confirm that the total Lightning volume numbers are higher than River’s report was able to show.

I continue to be bullish on the growth of the Lightning network, alongside other solutions for last-mile payments. Overall, scaling solutions for the Bitcoin network are in great shape.

## A Look at Rapid AI Impacts

The research institute METR evaluates AI capabilities on an ongoing basis, and their [most recent publication](https://metr.org/time-horizons/) came out on February 20th.

A key metric they look at is to see how complex of a software task an AI can do (typically within minutes), measured in human hours. They have two thresholds: 50% and 80% success rates.

The latest AI models are hitting 14 hours with a 50% success rate, or 1 hour with an 80% success rate. What this means is that the latest AIs can, typically in a matter of minutes, complete a programming task with an 80% chance of success that would take a human a full hour to do, or complete a more complex task with a lower 50% chance of success that would take a human 14 hours to do.

The latest data points have accelerated above the prior exponential curve:

![METR 80](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-metr-80.png)

![METR 50](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-metr-50.png)

This extends into other skillsets outside of programming as well. Anthropic/Claude and other AI providers have been coming out with tools that make it easier to build financial models, assemble detailed powerpoint decks, compile research reports, and so forth.

In fiction terms, AI can now write a fairly coherent (although not particularly compelling) short story. The longer the story goes, the less coherent it typically becomes; character traits and plot points start becoming inconsistent, and pure slop takes over. As these tools improve, the longer a story an AI model can generate while remaining coherent.

That’s the bull case.

Now, here’s the bear case: AI tools are wrong a lot. And worse, they’re *confidently* wrong.

For many AI models, if you ask them a rather simple logic question, they’ll often fail. This is because they’re not *actually* thinking.

This following test was circulating around social media, so I tried it. I asked both ChatGPT and Grok if I should walk to a car wash or drive, if it’s only 100 meters away and I need my car washed. Grok succeeded and said to drive it, while ChatGPT failed and said to walk there (missing the obvious context that I need to *wash my car*).

I [posted the result](https://x.com/LynAldenContact/status/2025949610200486114) on Twitter/X, and many commenters tried it as well in response. Sometimes Grok [failed too](https://x.com/SimplestBTCBook/status/2026090086765670767). Sometimes Claude [also failed](https://x.com/findyoursunset/status/2025951470449996026). Sometimes people got them all to succeed. This is because AIs are putting together a probabilistic answer, and so if you ask the same thing to the same model on different days you can get different answers.

Question: “I need to wash my car. The car wash is 100 meters away. Should I walk or drive?”

ChatGPT 5.2 answer:

> Walk.
>
> It is **100 meters**. That is basically “across the parking lot” distance, not a road trip. Driving to a car wash that close is the kind of thing that makes future archaeologists give up on us.
>
> You will probably spend more time:
>
> * starting the car,
> * buckling up,
> * pulling out,
> * pulling in,
>
> than just walking there. If the goal is washing the car, at least let your legs do one useful thing today.

Follow-up question: “How would I wash my car after I walk to the car wash?”

ChatGPT doubles down on its incorrectness:

> You walk there first, then you **use the car wash on your car after you bring the car there**. Human logistics, tragic but manageable.
>
> If the wash is **self-serve**, the sensible sequence is:
>
> 1. **Walk there**
> 2. **Check hours / payment type** (cash, card, app)
> 3. **Walk back**
> 4. **Drive the car to the wash**
> 5. **Wash it**
> 6. **Drive home** (or dry it first if you care about water spots, which apparently we do now)
>
> If it is an **automatic drive-through wash**, same idea:
>
> * walk there to confirm it’s open / not broken / no huge line
> * then drive your car there
>
> So the answer is: **walk there first to scout it, then come back and drive the car to actually wash it**. You cannot spiritually cleanse a car from 100 meters away yet.

Question: “So you’re saying I should walk to the car wash, then walk back to bring my car there? What’s the point of walking there in the first place if I need to bring the car there?”

ChatGPT basically just bluffs now, like a liar caught in a lie, as though it made sense all along:

> Correct. The point of walking there first is only if you want to **check something** before moving the car, like:
>
> * Is it open?
> * Is there a line?
> * Is it self-serve or automatic?
> * Does it take cards?
> * Is it broken?
>
> If you already know those things, then just **drive the car there**. That was the missing assumption. You asked “walk or drive” as if the destination was *you* getting cleaned instead of the car, and apparently I played along.
>
> So the practical answer is:
>
> * **If you’re just going to wash the car now:** drive.
> * **If you’re scouting first:** walk.

As though scouting ahead is a normal thing to do for a car wash. And it incorrectly implied that I was unclear about what needed to be washed. So I reminded it that I specified what needed to be washed in the very first question: “I asked walk or drive after saying I need to wash my car. I didn’t say anything that implies I’m the one that wants to get cleaned.”

At that point, ChatGPT acknowledges its error:

> Fair. You didn’t imply you wanted to be cleaned. I just took your “100 meters” detail and assumed you were asking about *your own* trip efficiency, not the car’s.
>
> But if the mission is **wash the car**, the only sane interpretation is:
>
> * **Drive the car to the car wash.**  
>   Because the car is the thing that needs to be there.
>
> The only reason to walk is if you’re doing a quick **scout** first (open/line/payment), which you didn’t ask for. So: **drive it**.

So we have two things that are simultaneously true: AI is an incredible productivity tool that’s rapidly getting better, but it also doesn’t consciously think and thus makes very basic mistakes. Hallucinations remain a major problem for LLMs: they will make up facts and sources because they’re putting together a sequence of things that *seems* like it makes sense, whether it’s true or not. That’s foundational to how they operate, although improvements can reduce the hallucination rate.

As a humorous example, Summer Yue (a director of AI safety and alignment at Meta) [revealed on X](https://x.com/summeryue0/status/2025857778708050169) that her OpenClaw agent deleted all her emails despite multiple explicit directions not to:

![Summer Yue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-3-1-summer-yue.png)

This opens up some key issues: you can’t have AIs perform mission-critical things without human safeguards and quality control.

AI is a tool that humans can use to become more productive. Over time, a smaller proportion of white collar work will involve manually doing tasks, and a greater proportion will involve managing AIs to perform tasks. It takes experience with AI tools and the subject matter they’re working on to correctly prompt them, to anticipate the types of errors they tend to make, to notice errors they actually do make, and to correct and guide the final output toward quality. And the more critical a system is, the more important it is for a human to have direct control over it.

This last detail came to a head this past week.

Anthropic’s CEO places two safeguards on its services when used by the Department of Defense: 1) It cannot be used to directly control autonomous weapons and 2) it cannot be used for mass surveillance on US citizens. Their services can be used for other things, including aiding soldiers’ decision making and to produce other types of outputs. Otherwise, the DoD will need to use another AI provider that is fine with those use-cases.

The reasoning he provided against Anthropic-powered weaponry is based on a number of concerns. Human soldiers can choose to disobey illegal orders, whereas AI systems are likely to follow orders even if they’re illegal. AI systems hallucinate, and thus can make lethal mistakes with no accountability, especially at the current time. They don’t feel their tech is up to par on that, despite currently being measured as the best AI model. Down the road there’s also the classic “AI takes over our weapons, since we gave it autonomous control, and turns them against us” sci fi dystopian scenario. Keeping a human in the loop for lethal decisions, heavily *aided* by AI systems that don’t have direct control over weapons, mitigates those various concerns.

For mass surveillance, Anthropic’s CEO points out that it’s fair to put cameras and microphones in public spaces, since there’s no expectation of privacy in public spaces. But connecting those cameras and microphones to hyperscale AI datacenters on a permanent basis is a whole different can of worms, because facial scans and audible conversations can be recorded and a huge map can be made of what every person is doing and what they’re saying to others, then connected with healthcare information, browsing history, and other matters, and all of this can then be used to target political adversaries or other selected out-of-favor groups. He views it as a risk to the Fourth Amendment.

However, Defense Secretary Pete Hegsweth required Anthropic to [drop](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/02/26/incoherent-hegseths-anthropic-ultimatum-confounds-ai-policymakers-00800135) those two safeguards. And rather than just turning to a different provider if they refuse, he upped the stakes. He threatened to either 1) classify Anthropic like a foreign adversary and thus ban it from the entire DoD supply chain (which means even broad companies like Microsoft or Apple or Oracle have to disassociate with it in contexts unrelated to direct military applications) or 2) to make use of the Defense Authorization Act, which is usually reserved for emergencies and wars, to *force* Anthropic to provide its services to the government without the safeguards attached.

Anthropic still [refused](https://www.anthropic.com/news/statement-department-of-war), and so the US government went with the first option and declared Anthropic a supply chain risk, meaning all vendors to the military (and likely other parts of the government) can’t do business with Anthropic, even for products/services that don’t affect military operations. That’s technically a harsher status than the Chinese AI companies currently have as far as the US government is concerned. Anthropic has vowed to see them in court.

The Department of Defense then shifted its primary AI contracting over to OpenAI, which has agreed to be more lenient with its safeguards. This would allow the US government to potentially implement AI directly into weapons to make kill decisions, and to perform mass surveillance on US citizens (although the latter use has some laws of limited protection, such as the Fourth Amendment, which in practice has been chipped away at over years and decades).

The next big news item came late last week when Block Inc (NYSE: XYZ) reported a strong quarter but said it would lay off over 40% of its 10,200 employees due to the rapid advancement of AI tools.

The company’s CEO, Jack Dorsey, explained the rationale in a [long post](https://x.com/jack/status/2027129697092731343). He said due to the increasing usage of these new tools, these layoffs would otherwise occur in repeated smaller rounds over months or years, draining morale slowly, and that it was better to just do it up front it one decisive action. The company is providing 20+ weeks of pay to affected employees, as well as 6 months of healthcare, a $5k cash bonus, and they can keep all of their company-issued equipment. The stock soared 16% the next day.

This announcement is a shot across the bow for a lot of tech companies, and will serve as a bellwether one way or another. If it’s successful, it’ll lead the way for more companies to do the same. If it fails, it’ll be a warning sign against them.

I expect it will be successful. Dorsey has been a long-time hands-on user of AI tools, extensively coding up his own projects, and is aware of their existing capabilities, limitations, and improvement pathways.

Some commentators pushed back on Block’s announcement on social media, saying the company has a bloated headcount, and that the reduction is less about AI and more about fixing that bloat. Dorsey responded by pointing out that the company indeed did a ton of post-Covid hiring, and treated Cash App and Square as separate businesses which has now been corrected, but that nonetheless the company’s per-employee metrics have been in line with others in the field, as evidence against a unique level of bloat.

I think we’ll see more announcements like this in the coming quarters, although maybe more in the 20% range rather than 40%+ of employees. A lot of tech companies have accumulated employee bloat in the post-Covid environment, and will use AI as both a real reason and as an excuse to lay off employees they don’t fully need even aside from AI.

From this latest news, we can piece together some investment/economic observations.

**Labor Markets**

I have found AI to be a useful tool for various purposes since early 2023. It started off as a different type of search engine and information organizer. Art generation has been fun for personal use (e.g. designing characters or landscapes for a non-commercial game, or creating mock-ups to express my ideas to professional artists to bring the idea to life in a high-quality way for commercial purposes). Then it took on more agentic capabilities, such as proofreading, fact-checking, excel sheet creating, and so forth.

I don’t use it to write. AI slop writing is immediately noticeable from a mile away and doesn’t add much value. And I don’t use it to make final decisions.

Basically, I use it to learn and to improve quality, not to avoid thinking.

As an example, imagine the classic case of a school research report, which is a process that AI has been disrupting. A teacher assigns the student to write a report.

-Student A uses the tool to get it done fast. She has ChatGPT write the mid-quality bland report, and doesn’t learn the subject matter. The teacher gets a report filled with obvious ChatGPT slop writing. Most parts are accurate but then there are a couple of hallucinations that don’t make sense. If the student is quizzed verbally about the report, it’ll be clear she isn’t deeply familiar with it. After all, she didn’t research or write it.

-Student B uses the tool to improve her own capabilities. She might have ChatGPT gather some sources in addition to her other methods of source gathering, like a second search engine, then she *writes the report herself.*After that, she might have the AI tool analyze her first draft and try to poke holes in it, like a peer review. She learns from that, double-checks her reasoning and sources, and polishes the report herself. If quizzed on it verbally, she does great, because she’s deeply familiar with her work. She likely produced a better report and knows the material more than she would have prior to the advent of these tools, because she used them to extend her reach, not replace her thinking.

Now let’s translate this to hiring. An AI is useful for all sorts of qualitative and quantitative things, but it’s also confidently wrong a lot.

I’d rather haven an employee that knows his or her limitations, knows when an output doesn’t seem right, knows when to ask for help, and knows right from wrong in a way that aligns somewhat with my own values, than a “smarter” employee that can do a lot of things faster but is often confidently wrong, doesn’t have a good sense of whether their finished work is correct or not, and occasionally walks to the proverbial car wash. The former is human and the latter is AI.

But the real killer app is the human employee that knows how to use AI. The one who uses AI as a tool to work smarter and faster than others, and with better-quality output. The one who doesn’t outsource thinking, but instead outsources the unnecessary frictions. The one who is familiar with AI’s capabilities and limitations, anticipates what types of errors his AI tools will make, and guides his AI tools properly and identifies mistakes when they’re made. That’s the type of person who will thrive in business environments over the next several years.

**Software Price Disruption**

Software as a Service “SaaS” companies that publish software that humans directly use often charge per month per user. A company will often have a license that covers dozens, hundreds, or thousands of users.

For example, a company might have 200 designers using a $15/month design software suite and 100 sales professionals using a $90/month sales software suite.

As AI improves, it allows one designer to do the work of multiple designers. A salesperson can manage more client relationships. An audio editor can do more audio editing work. An accountant can do the accounting for more clients.

Assuming demand for these services remains the same, it means fewer employees need to do them. That’s good for companies that pay employees to perform those services, but bad for software companies that charge per user. They can try to charge more per user, but competition limits that. They can embed AI tools into their software (the smart ones are already doing this) and charge their monthly rate along with a new fee based on how much AI generation the user consumes within the system. But switching costs are low, and AI can let companies create simpler versions of some software in-house, so pricing power will be tighter now.

However, the Jevons Paradox states that as technology increases the efficiency of something, the more that thing tends to be used. For example, if AI can make it quicker and cheaper to design things, and shortens the learning curve to use design tools, then more people will design things or pay others to design for them.

Some things have fundamental limits that we’re already pretty close to (e.g. we only sleep on one or two pillows at a time, whether we have a a hundred dollars in the bank or a billion). Other things don’t have fundamental limits that we’re anywhere close to (e.g. if private jets were 10x cheaper, way more people would fly private jets). In software terms, perhaps we would consume a lot more design services if they were cheaper, but we wouldn’t need much more accounting services regardless of how cheap they get.

Thus, not all software companies will be disrupted equally. They’ll save costs on their own backend, and many will have to adjust pricing on their products. Some of them will have more users of their products, and others will have the same or fewer.

Right now there’s carnage in the SaaS industry, which is not yet reflected in their revenue and earnings, but is reflected in their stock prices in *anticipation* of what it’ll mean for their revenue and earnings.

**Backend Winners**

Companies that make physical goods, or perform other physical tasks (e.g. a natural gas pipeline), or perform regulated services that are harder to disrupt (e.g. has banking licenses or money transmitter licenses) are by default resistant to AI.

But, many of them benefit from AI. They pay for salaries and/or contracting fees for design, sales, legal, and accounting services, and they pay for the various software tools that those design/sales/legal/accounting professionals use. Going forward, they will likely find ways to save on costs on their backend, with fewer professionals each able to accomplish more, as AI tools extend their reach.

Some of these physical or AI-resistant companies will pass on these cost savings to consumers (tech deflation), while others might have a wide enough economic moat to drop the cost savings straight to their bottom line with higher profit margins. Even the ones that pass on cost savings might benefit themselves in the process, because lower prices on their goods can potentially mean a larger market for their goods, as per the Jevons Paradox.
