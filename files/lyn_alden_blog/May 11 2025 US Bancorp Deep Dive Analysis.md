## Macro View

**US Economic Update**

Since gross domestic product or “GDP” is such a complicated measure, it is reported with three different estimates after a fiscal quarter ends. As such, sometimes the second or third estimate will revise the numbers higher or lower than the first estimate.

The first estimate is in for Q1 2025, and it showed the first negative quarter since Q1 2022:

![Real GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-real-gdp.png)

A trade deficit weighs negatively on real GDP, and the record trade deficit that occurred in Q1 2025 was the primary variable for the contraction in real GDP. This was temporary, as many businesses front-ran tariffs and pulled as much imports as possible into the quarter. This was partially offset by a dramatic rise in inventories that those imports went into, which are positive for the GDP calculation.

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow subcomponent tool showed the negative impact from tariff front-running in light blue and the positive impact from inventory increases in purple:

![Import Front-Running](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-import-front-running.png)

The Atlanta Fed does have a positive outlook for Q2 2025, but all of the economic data remains uncertain and could change quickly:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-gdpnow.png)

Meanwhile, the US manufacturing sector continues to be in mild contraction, in what has thus far been the longest ever period without a sustained growth in the ISM manufacturing purchasing manager’s index. This has remained bearish for longer than my base case, with two failed breakouts to the upside:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-ism-pmi.png)

**Domestic Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity conditions continue to be flat, as they have been for three years since mid-2022:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-domestic-liquidity.png)

This flat liquidity situation will likely to persist into Q4 2025. They’ve already slowed their rate of quantitative tightening twice, which extends the time they can continue to do it. By 2026, the Fed will likely shift toward persistent balance sheet expansion, albeit at a mild pace. At that point, they’ll most likely be accumulating Treasuries but will continue to let mortgage-backed securities mature off their balance sheet.

The Treasury General Account has $583 billion in liquidity. It got a boost from tax season as it normally does, but is now likely to roll over again. Treasury Secretary Bessent recently told Congress that there is significant uncertainty for when it will deplete, and that there’s a decent chance it’ll happen during the August recess of Congress. Therefore he urged them to pass a debt limit by mid-July:

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-tga.png)

As the TGA depletes into late summer, it’ll ironically be good for liquidity, and offset some of the ongoing Federal Reserve quantitative tightening.

**Global Liquidity**

The dollar index has stabilized recently around 100, which makes sense amid ongoing Fed tightness in terms of both interest rates and their balance sheet.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-dxy.png)

All else being equal, the dollar starting to firm up puts downward pressure on global liquidity. I expect it’ll have further weakness over the next 3-5 years, but movements are rarely in a straight line and it’s normal to try to bounce here.

In the meantime, the market has pushed back its anticipated date of Fed rate cuts. Markets previously placed good odds on a June rate cut, but now place the highest odds on the next cut happening in July or September. For context, short-term rates in the United States are about 4.3% vs only 2.4% in the Eurozone. This differential helps support the dollar’s exchange rate.

Meanwhile, China continues to stimulate a bit which is beneficial for global liquidity. They’re not doing major fiscal stimulus, but around the margins they keep performing monetary stimulus and occasionally a bit of fiscal stimulus. All else equal, this is adding to global liquidity:

If there’s a broad 10% tariff on all of that, and it sustains all year, then on its surface that would represent a $400 billion tax hike, mostly on American consumers and businesses. In reality, import volumes are likely to diminish somewhat due to those tariffs if sustained, and so tariff revenue will likely be a bit lower, and it will be spread out among American consumers and businesses as well as Chinese shipment volumes.

## US Banking System Update

The banking system in the United States continues to be in surprisingly good shape, and the recent sell-off may present some investment opportunities.

**20 Years of Banking History in 5 Minutes**

Back in 2007, the US banking system was the most leveraged it had been since shortly before the Great Depression. It was the biggest private sector debt build-up in 80 years.

Blindly looking at capital ratios didn’t reveal it, but instead looking at the percentage of bank assets that were nominally “risk free” was at record lows. The highest proportion ever was “at risk”.

Specifically, they had the lowest ever ratio of cash as a percentage of assets. And they had the lowest ever ratio of cash+Treasuries as a percentage of assets:

![Cash and Treasuries Percent of Bank Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-cash-treasuries-percent-of-bank-assets.png)

Banks were over-exposed to risky loans, and particularly risky mortgages. That all blew up tremendously in 2008, similar to 1929. The difference between 2008 and 1929 was that rather than taking three years to respond with a massive bank recapitalization program, the Fed and Treasury acted within months, bailing out broad swaths of the system with a massive expansion of the monetary base. Homeowners weren’t bailed out, but most of the big banks were.

Regulators then added all sorts of new liquidity requirements and risk-weighted capital requirements to banks, and over the next decade through 2019 the banking system operated pretty normally.

During 2020 when the pandemic lockdowns occurred, banks initially piled up provisions for anticipated loan losses, but the fiscal bailouts were so huge directly to consumers and businesses that the banking system never really took much of a hit from loan losses. Banks went into that crisis with a strong capital and liquidity position, and didn’t really have to tap into that strength due to all the money-printing that occurred.

However, as the inflation hangover from all that money-printing started to hit in 2021 and especially 2022, the Fed shifted to very hawkish policy. They raised rates from 0% to over 5% at a record pace. Banks were holding a lot of Treasuries and agency-backed securities that were essentially risk-free in terms of default potential, but that could be underwater in terms of price for periods of time if they were purchased at low interest rates.

![Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-fed-funds-rate.png)

In other words, some banks took in a ton of deposits, and then bought a bunch of long-duration Treasuries and agencies that were ostensibly risk-free (i.e. they can’t realistically default) at low interest rates. However, the Fed jacked up interest rates very high, and inflation and the bond market pushed long-duration Treasuries to high rates as well, and high bond rates means lower bond prices. This meant those banks were sitting on huge temporary losses; if they were to sell those bonds, they’d be selling them at a much lower price than they bought them for. This would likely be fine if they could hold those securities until maturity and get their full principle back, but some of them could not.

Specifically, in those handful of banks that couldn’t hold them, depositors pulled their funds out in March 2023, which would have forced those banks to sell their securities and/or loans at a major loss and become insolvent. This became known as the regional banking crisis. A handful of banks with very specific issues blew up together, and the Fed and Treasury had to respond again to put out the fire. This crisis didn’t spread very far, because for the most part it required two major components for banks to fail, and most banks didn’t have both components together.

Component 1 was that a bank needed to have an unusually high ratio of uninsured deposits. Depositors that are under the $250k FDIC insurance limit tend to feel quite safe, even though FDIC doesn’t even have much money in its insurance pool. It’s broadly assumed that even if a banking failure were to occur that is bigger than FDIC can handle, they’d get a bailout from Congress. However, depositors that are over the $250k FDIC insurance limit (which could be high net worth individuals or businesses that have a bunch of employees on payroll) tend to be quite skittish if they have any concerns about the bank they use, because they have much less of a guarantee to get all of their money back in the event of a bank failure.

Component 2 was that a bank needed to have unusually heavy unrealized losses from their securities portfolio or loan book. Unlike 2007/2008, this wasn’t because a lot of defaults were occurring (far from it), but rather that interest rates had risen so much that the re-sell value of securities and loans was much lower than the initial price. They could normally hold to maturity and get their money back, but not if depositors pull their funds out at that moment of weakness.

Silicon Valley Bank, the epicenter of the crisis, had both of those components. They mostly served startup businesses, and thus had an unusually high ratio of uninsured deposits (Component 1). They also had a huge deposit surge during the pandemic when interest rates were low, bought a ton of securities at low interest rates, and thus had massive unrealized losses when interest rates rose (Component 2). When depositors collectively pulled their money out after getting spooked by this realization, it was a kill shot for the bank. First Republic collapsed shortly thereafter for similar reasons.

Banks that had only one of these components were generally fine. For example, Citibank (C) serves a lot of business customers and therefore did have an unusually high ratio of uninsured depositors (Component 1), but they didn’t face major losses on their securities or loan portfolio. On the other side of the coin, Schwab (SCHW) had negative tangible equity from major unrealized securities losses (Component 2), but it did not have an unusually high ratio of uninsured depositors, and so it faced no bank run that would force them to sell and realize those losses.

A chart from the FDIC circulated widely around social media that showed that the US banking system had about $650 billion in unrealized losses from their securities holdings. Here’s an updated version of the chart showing that the losses have lessened a bit since then. These unrealized losses are still sitting on bank balance sheets:

![Unrealized Losses](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-unrealized-losses.png)

The context that many people did not realize as they spread charts like this around social media, is that banks went into that period with $2.35 trillion in aggregate equity capital. That $650 billion hit temporarily wiped out less than 30% of their capital, and for the vast majority of them, it would take away little or no capital permanently because they would hold until maturity and recoup those losses. The handful of exceptions were banks that had both problematic components mentioned above, and thus received kill shots.

The number of “problem banks” listed by the FDIC remained near cycle lows throughout the crisis (only the normal baseline of several dozen out of nearly 4,500 total), because it was a small handful of semi-large regional banks with both components that the problems were concentrated in:

![FDIC Problem Banks](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-problem-banks.png)

After the dust settled, banks mostly went back to normal throughout the rest of 2023 and into 2024, and only recently did their stock prices face turmoil again due to the 2025 trade war.

**US Bancorp and Truist Financial**

Back during the 2023 crisis, US Bancorp (USB) and Truist Financial (TFC) were fine. They did not have unusually high ratios of uninsured deposits, and had moderate but acceptable unrealized securities losses from the sharp rise in interest rates. And they’re much bigger than Silicon Valley Bank and First Republic, with broader deposit bases.

In fact, each of those banks provided liquidity to First Republic during the crisis, to help ensure that it wouldn’t fail in a disorderly way.

The biggest tangible consequence that these banks faced was that they had to be more conservative for a couple of years to retain earnings and build up capital. They kept paying their dividends but slowed down their annual dividend growth and were not aggressive with share buybacks.

For example, we can see here US Bancorp getting clipped for about 30% of its tangible book value, and gaining it back in two years:

![USB Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-usb-book-value.png)

Analysts are quite bullish on US Bancorp’s earnings over the next few years:

![USB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-usb-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

We can see the pandemic loss provisions on that FAST Graph in 2020, which were unwound, and then we can see the “echo” in 2022/2023 as the hangover hit and jacked-up rates gave them big unrealized losses and lower net interest margins (the difference between their asset interest rates and their deposit rates they have to pay to depositors).

US Bancorp has 13 million retail clients, 1.4 million business clients, 500k wealth clients, and 45k corporate and institutional clients. They’ve got over $500 billion in assets under management for their clients, and they process over $900 billion in payment volume as one of the nation’s largest merchant acquirer banks. They have over 2,200 branches spread across the western two-thirds of the country.

![USB Branch Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-usb-branch-map.png)

Analysts are bullish on Truist Financial’s earnings as well. As a note, their 2022 losses look bigger mainly due to one-time merger costs rather than strictly due to this jump in interest rates:

![TFC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-tfc-fastgraph.png)

The question is, how will banks like this be affected by the trade war?

Directly, they have virtually no exposure. However, some of their banking clients do, and so they could face loan losses from business loans. If there’s a broad economic slowdown or outright recession, then they’re likely to face an uptick in loan losses on both business loans and consumer loans.

I’m actually slightly more bearish than consensus expectations on US Bancorp and Truist Financial’s earnings potential over the next few years. I wouldn’t be surprised to see both banks undershoot those expectations a bit. However, the recent sell-off in their stock prices already accounted for some pretty bad scenarios going forward, and those scenarios are lower than my base case.

There are several reasons why I’m not too worried about banks like this and view the current share prices as quite attractive:

First, they’re super-regional banks. US Bancorp is the 5th biggest bank in the country, and Truist is the 8th biggest. They have more deposit diversification and asset diversification than most banks. They’re not in the “Big 4” nationwide globally systemic US banks (JP Morgan Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, and Citigroup), but they’re bigger than regional banks and community banks, and thus are “super-regionals”.

Second, the Fed has a lot of ammo to cut rates if the economy goes south. While I don’t think rate cuts would be very effective for stimulating the *economy as a whole* this time, it would alleviate pressure on banks like this. Specifically, it would likely reduce the interest that entities like US Bancorp have to pay on deposits, and give them a higher net interest margin that could alleviate a subtle uptick in loan losses.

![USB Average Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-usb-average-yield.png)

Third, the US Treasury is still running structurally high fiscal deficits, which are stimulative. The only meaningful obstacle that could lower those deficits are the high tariff levels, and in a recession (especially a self-imposed one), it’s more likely that those tariffs would be reversed or offset. This would be especially the case in 2026, since it’s a mid-term election year.

Fourth, Jerome Powell’s term is up in May 2026, and President Trump is likely going to nominate someone who will be more accommodative. I’d be surprised if we don’t see a steeper yield curve in 2026 and 2027, which should be good for super-regional banks. This record-long inverted yield curve has been rough for banks in general. And either way, I think we’ll see a return to Fed balance sheet expansion by 2026.

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-yield-curve.png)

I continue to be bullish on Bank OZK as a small position. I classify their construction loans as quite different than commercial real estate loans in general, and they’re trading roughly at tangible book value.

![OZK FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-ozk-fastgraph.png)

And I continue to be bullish with moderate positions on super-regional banks. This includes entities like US Bancorp, Truist Financial, PNC Financial, and Fifth Third Bancorp.

I do expect that we’ll have time to accumulate more, since I would guess that the rest of 2025 will remain somewhat choppy and uncertain in a lot of aspects. This current buying opportunity looks a lot like spring 2023. Many of these stocks have 4-5% dividend yields, so they pay investors to accumulate them while share prices are down and fundamentals remain resilient.

For risk management purposes, I wouldn’t go all-in on any of them, and would limit overall bank exposure to a reasonable level. I round out these types of banks with some international bank exposures in Brazil, Singapore, India, and elsewhere, which have different risk profiles.

## Final Thoughts: A Century of Banking History

For those that haven’t been following my work for years, I figured I’d end this report with a list of resources for those that want to dig deeper into US banking system history, which can help when further analyzing overall risk.

My 2o23 book [Broken Money](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR) provides my most comprehensive history of the US banking system, but a number of public articles are also relevant, so I’ll organize them here.

Back in 2020, after major fiscal stimulus hit the economy and CPI measures were still low (sub-2%), I published a series of articles and newsletters explaining why an inflation wave was likely coming, unlike the post-2008 period.

Specifically, I compared the 2010s decade to the 1930s decade (disinflationary private sector debt bubbles) and compared the 2020s to the 1940s (inflationary fiscal-dominance and public sector debt bubbles).

When private sector debt bubbles unwind (like the 1929 and 2008), that tends to be terrible for banks. But then, once the banking system is bailed out, the new bubble is at the sovereign level. Banks at that point are pretty well-capitalized and safe even as people continue to worry about them, while the currency itself is more challenged with inflation.

While I warned about the [bond bubble in July 2019](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/) and then highlighted [Treasury risks in February 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/treasury-bonds-risks/), it was my September 2020 piece called “[A Century of Fiscal and Monetary Policy](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/)” that really expanded on it, because at that point we firmly had the catalyst of a massive fiscal response rather than just the assumption of one.

During that autumn 2020 period, there were many analysts still arguing for disinflation to occur, and said that what was happening was not real money-printing. So, in November 2020 I wrote what at the time was my longest-ever article called “[Banks, QE, and Money-Printing](https://www.lynalden.com/money-printing/)” to break down what is and what is not money-printing, and to articulate why what was happening at the time was totally different than 2008, and thus more likely to be inflationary, especially if the stimulus keeps up.

By April 2021, after another round of stimulus, serious price inflation started to break out. My May 2021 newsletter issue “[Fiscal-Driven Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/)” covered the topic when year-over-year CPI breached 4%. It ultimately went on to reach 9% in June 2022, and as of mid-2025 it’s still above the Fed’s 2% target despite slowly coming down for years.

This following section is an excerpt from from that Fiscal-Driven Inflation newsletter, so readers can get the summary of a century of US debt cycles and banking history without necessarily digging through those links:

> ## The Long-Term Debt Cycle Unfolds
>
> One of my main topics over the past 18-24 months was exploring the [long-term debt cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/fiscal-and-monetary-policy/), popularized years ago by Ray Dalio, which describes a framework for expecting trend-changes in fiscal policy, monetary policy, and inflation/deflation.
>
> I’ll summarize the cycle again for folks who haven’t been following my work, but then also supply some new charts and tie it into some of the current trends we are seeing as of mid-2021 for those who are familiar with it.
>
> Each normal five-to-ten year business cycle in modern US history has ended with higher debt as a percentage of GDP and lower interest rates than the previous cycle. As many of those short term debt cycles string together, debt as a percentage of GDP keeps making higher highs and interest rates keep making lower lows, until debts reach untenable levels and interest rates hit zero.
>
> Rates hit zero and private debts hit extremely high levels in 2008 during the Global Financial Crisis, and the last time this happened was in the early 1930s in the early phases of the Great Depression. In both instances, there was a systemic banking crisis (marked in green), which led fiscal and monetary policymakers to expand the monetary base and take other measures to recapitalize the banking system.
>
> ![Long-Term Debt Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-longterm-debt-cycle.png)
>
> What makes the end of a long-term debt cycle different than the end of a short-term debt cycle, is that the later stages of a long-term debt cycle both in US history and elsewhere, are generally characterized by a significant degree of fiscal dominance and currency devaluation.
>
> This is because at the end of a long-term debt cycle, debt levels get so large relative to the size of the economy that it becomes impossible to deleverage them nominally without crashing the economy, so instead the denominators are increased: the monetary base, the broad money supply, and nominal GDP with a significant inflation component. The mechanism to do this involves large monetized fiscal deficits, accompanied by a central bank willing to hold rates well below the prevailing inflation rate for a while, which effectively inflates portions of the debt away.
>
> The alternative to this process is a deflationary economic collapse, which is a route that the public and policymakers around the world rarely if ever choose. In other words, when push comes to shove and the system is pushed to its limits, policymakers invariably print.
>
> ## The 80-Year Echo
>
> History can’t tell us everything, but along with analysis of current conditions, it can point us in the right direction.
>
> In terms of fiscal and monetary policy, the 2010s were very similar to the 1930s (disinflationary private debt bubble deleveraging), and the 2020s are shaping up more like the 1940s (inflationary fiscal-driven wartime finance). The previous chart above shows the similarities clearly, but this next section elaborates on the details.
>
> **1930s and 2010s**
>
> After the debt bubble popped in 1929, the 1930s had a long deflationary private debt bubble deleveraging, resulting in sluggish economic growth and rising political populism. Fiscal stimulus in that decade was large in absolute terms, but small compared to the amount of money destroyed from loan losses. Most of the focus was on recapitalizing the banking system. As such, the decade was disinflationary.
>
> One very basic way to measure financial leverage in the financial system is to look at the monetary base as a percentage of total bank loans. The lower the ratio is, the more leveraged the system is, since there is less base money backing up the total amount of bank loans. This ratio hit a bottom at 12% right at the start of the Great Depression.
>
> ![Monetary Base vs Bank Loans 1930s](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-monetary-base-1930s.png)
>
> Approximately 80 years later, this process began playing out again, as the next long-term private debt bubble popped in 2008 and continued deleveraging into the early 2010s.
>
> Interestingly, the ratio of the monetary base compared to total bank loans hit as low as 12.5% right before the crisis:
>
> ![Monetary Base vs Bank Loans 2000s](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-monetary-base-2008.png)
>
> *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*
>
> There is more data available for the 2008 event since it’s in modern times, which allows us to examine the overleveraged bank situation from more angles.
>
> For example, here is a chart of cash levels and Treasury security levels as a percentage of total assets for large US banks:
>
> ![Bank Leverage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-bank-leverage.png)
>
> *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*
>
> In 2008, large banks had less than 3% cash and 10% Treasuries, or about 13% combined risk-free assets, as a percentage of their total assets. That’s historically very low. Most of their other assets were loans, including a lot of risky loans that went bad when the overvalued housing market declined. As banks crumbled under the private debt cycle implosion, fiscal and monetary policymakers recapitalized the banking system, keeping the majority of the banking system solvent but mostly letting overleveraged homeowners go bust.
>
> Looking at it another way, this next chart shows total public and private debt as a ratio of broad money supply (M2) in the United States in blue on the left axis. I also added the Federal Funds Rate and the monetary base as a percentage of GDP on the right axis.
>
> ![Debt to M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-debt-to-m2.png)
>
> *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*
>
> Total debt kept increasing faster than money supply for decades (blue line going up), with the total-debt-to-money-supply ratio reaching over 700% at the peak, until the 2008 financial crisis occurred. After that implosion, the money supply began growing faster than debt (blue line going down), and 2020 kicked this deleveraging process into high-gear with another leg down. The ratio is already down to below 450% and heading lower.
>
> Looking at it a third way, here is total private debt in dollar terms in the United States in blue on the left axis, private debt as a ratio of GDP in red on the right axis, and private debt as a ratio of broad money supply in green on the right axis.
>
> ![Private Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-private-debt.png)
>
> *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*
>
> When private debt builds for decades up to nearly 300% of GDP and over 600% of broad money supply, there’s no way to deleverage that monster nominally. From 2008 through 2012, the total deleveraging of private debt in absolute terms was only about $3.6 trillion or 8% of the $45 trillion total, and then it began heading up again. Now it’s over $55 trillion.
>
> Instead, the denominator is what mainly changed. Debt started growing at a slower pace, while broad money started growing faster, resulting in a declining ratio of private debt to money supply.
>
> Similarly to the 1930s, the government response after the 2008 crisis and into the early part of the 2010s decade had a relatively large fiscal component, but it only partially offset the loan losses in the economy, rather than stimulate it. The decade was generally disinflationary as this debt bubble rolled over for years.
>
> By the end of the 2010s decade, despite recovery in some areas including a big boom in asset prices, many aspects of the economy, including the industrial sector and overall labor participation rate, failed to fully recover from the 2008 debacle. My January newsletter called [The Hindsight Depression](https://www.lynalden.com/january-2021-newsletter/) covered this topic.
>
> **1940s and 2020s**
>
> After the 1930s decade of economic stagnation and rising political tensions around the world, World War II broke out in the 1940s.
>
> This war represented an external catalyst that forced an utterly massive fiscal response from the US federal government and other governments, which dwarfed the fiscal stimulus of the 1930s. The US built a ton of domestic industrial production, sent soldiers overseas, and then when they came home, eight million of them were given college education or training on government expense, along with other benefits such as mortgage assistance. Excess industrial production was repurposed for domestic use.
>
> Federal budget deficits were so large, and so many Treasuries were issued to pay for it, that the Federal Reserve had to create a lot of new base money to buy a big chunk of the Treasury bonds on the secondary market to keep yields low, also known as deficit monetization and yield curve control. Unlike the disinflationary 1930s, this amount of fiscal spending and deficit monetization in the 1940s was highly inflationary. Money supply and total demand increased faster than supply could keep up with.
>
> Similarly, as the 2020s began up, private debt was still rather high in the system despite a decade of mild household deleveraging in the 2010s. Plus, with so much wealth concentration, tens of millions of people in the US couldn’t go more than a month without a paycheck. Due to a combination of multiple factors, the economy began to slow by late 2018 and throughout 2019.
>
> When COVID-19 came in early 2020, and countries initiated economic shutdowns to slow the virus, it caused the highly-leveraged global economy to fracture. A global economy with this much debt is fragile, and can’t persist without constant cash flow.
>
> In response to the pandemic and shutdowns, countries everywhere did fiscal stimulus on a scale not seen since World War II, but the US fiscal response in particular was one of the largest as a percentage of GDP, dwarfing the fiscal response to the 2008 crisis. As a result, broad money supply grew at the quickest year-over-year pace since the 1940s.
>
> ![Broad Money Supply](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-m2.png)
>
> *Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*
>
> At this point, the long-term debt cycle in the 2020s entered the second phase; the public debt bubble portion, similar to the 1940s. Rising wealth concentration and populism, along with an external catalyst, changed public and policymaker perception about fiscal deficits, and the floodgates were opened.
>
> In many ways the math was inevitable by this point, with high debts and low interest rates. A number of institutional-scale asset managers were predicting years ago that the next recession would involve these sorts of unusually large fiscally-driven responses, based on how high debt was and how low interest rates were at this point in the long-term debt cycle, which would make monetary policy on its own relatively ineffective.
>
> In other words, the pandemic was a catalyst and an accelerant, but the pieces (high debt, major wealth concentration, and rising populism) were already set. Such a fragile system had no defense against any external shocks, and an external shock we had.
>
> Ray Dalio (co-founder of Bridgewater, the largest hedge fund in the world) described in his 2018 book [Big Debt Crises](https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B07GLBHM48/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&camp=1789&creative=9325&creativeASIN=B07GLBHM48&linkCode=as2&tag=lynalden-20&linkId=ef213796f7e6a03cfaf5ddae88a8b4e3) the concept of “monetary policy 3” which involves blending accommodative monetary policy with fiscally-driven cash infusions into the economy, potentially up to and including helicopter money. He popularized the concept of the long-term debt cycle over the past decade, then began saying that the late 2010s were in many ways like the late 1930s, and by 2019 was making the case that the next recession would likely involve these “monetary policy 3” responses not seen since the 1940s.
>
> Similarly, BlackRock published a [paper](https://www.blackrock.com/corporate/insights/blackrock-investment-institute/publications/global-macro-outlook/august-2019) along with Stanley Fisher (former Vice-Chair of the US Federal Reserve Board of Governors) in 2019 that also laid out the concept of having to “go direct” in the next economic downturn. The paper describes aggressive use of fiscal stimulus up to and including helicopter money, due to the ineffectiveness of monetary policy alone at this stage, and then coordinating with monetary policymakers to use financial repression to maintain low interest rates even if there is an inflationary response from the fiscal stimulus. (Hat tip to financial analyst [Luke Gromen](https://twitter.com/LukeGromen) for bringing that paper to my attention a few months ago.)

Overall, it continues to be the case that the 1940s are the closest comparison we have for the banking system. Banks will face challenges just like any other sector, but they’re unlikely to be the “main event” in any given crisis for a while, given how sturdy their financial position is and given the fiscal dominance that the country finds itself in.
