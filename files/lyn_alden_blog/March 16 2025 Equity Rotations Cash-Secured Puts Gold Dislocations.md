# Equity Rotations, Cash-Secured Puts, and Gold Dislocations

*March 16, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report looks at ongoing economic volatility, including the noisy impact that gold imports and other potentially front-running of imports is having on economic data.

The digital asset section looks at BTC and ETH price action, the strategic bitcoin reserve executive order, and discusses the concept of bitcoin as protocol money.

The investment analysis section looks at the recent rotation from U.S. equities to global equities and provides a sample cash-secured put trade, then finishes with a gold update.

## Macro View

**Gold Imports Distort Economic Data**

At the moment, the number of tariff proposals and retaliation proposals is too many to comment on, especially given they could be delayed or reversed by the next report. This is a period of heightened negotiation and positioning. My prior premium report was called “A Headline Driven Market” and indeed that continues to be in strong effect.

The recent tariff uncertainty has resulted in an abnormally large spike of gold imports to the United States. A typical month might see $1-2 billion in gold imports, but in December it was $10 billion and in January it was $30 billion. These numbers are reported with a bit of a lag. Investors are likely protecting themselves around the margins in the event that gold they own overseas gets tariffed.

This won’t have a large impact on GDP because most of that gold is not about economic activity. We can think of it more like a capital repatriation flow. But it does distort certain economic metrics that don’t separate it out from other types of imports.

Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow continues to have a comical dislocation, with gold imports playing a big role in it. In my prior report, I warned that we can’t take this at face value even if it is notable:

![Atlanta Fed GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-gdp-now.png)

The overall U.S. trade balance has a similar comical-looking drop to record lows, thanks partially to these gold movements which realistically are different than most other aspects of the trade balance:

![Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-trade-balance.png)

Data aggregates aside, a more serious question is to ask, if some entities are front-running the risk of tariffs by importing gold, what *other things* might they be doing this with that *do* affect the economy in a business operation sense?

For example, in response to U.S. tariff proposals, Europe threatened retaliatory 50% tariffs on U.S. whiskey, and in response President Trump threatened retaliatory-on-retaliatory [200% tariffs](https://apnews.com/article/trump-tariffs-wine-spirits-200-alcohol-dabead2d0e0fad64dc5e6250293ef016) on European wine and spirits. This is one of many ongoing uncertainties, and for example how many U.S. wine sellers and importers would go ahead and front-load some of their orders to protect against the probability of that going into effect? If some of them do, that would impact the trade balance within a given month or quarter, but it’s not really economic activity; it would just be pulling forward some demand as a protective measure which would mean fewer orders later, whether or not the tariffs go into effect since they would’ve already have stocked-up on inventory.

This is likely to continue to be a noisy data period, both for investors and businesses. Even when the Q1 GDP report comes out, it won’t necessarily be high quality data.

**Domestic Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity has inched slightly up lately:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-domestic-liquidity.png)

This was largely associated with the quick drop in the Treasury General Account. A drop in the TGA typically pushes liquidity back into the banking system:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-tga.png)

As I’ve reported about before, during a debt ceiling impasse, the Treasury Department has to start drawing down its cash balance to meet its obligations without issuing net new debt.

And my base case was that this one would not be as protracted as prior ones, because prior ones where the cash balance went almost to zero involved a Republican Congress contesting a Democratic President, whereas this impasse comes at a time when Republicans have broad control over the government, with some intra-party contentions.

On Friday, [with some controversy](https://abcnews.go.com/Politics/schumer-defends-support-gop-funding-bill-ahead-senate/story?id=119799590), the Senate passed a House-approved bill to avert a government shutdown. They still have yet to pass debt ceiling legislation, but in general this bill is a sign that the debt ceiling impasse will go in a similar direction. The timeline remains unclear, and so in the meantime, an ongoing TGA drawdown is somewhat pro-liquidity. I don’t expect the TGA to entirely drain this time as it did in 2021 and 2023, but it’s possible.

**Global Liquidity**

The dollar index peaked at 110 in mid-January, and has had a rather abrupt slide to under 104 within a two-month period of time.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-dxy.png)

This drop in the dollar index is pretty good for global liquidity, as it eases pressure on developing countries with dollar-denominated debts. However, it’s somewhat overshadowed by the turmoil associated with tariff concerns, fiscal consolidation, and the recent U.S. equity sell-off.

This dollar downtrend has been a bit different than other recent ones, because normally a strong dollar puts pressure on asset prices while a weak dollar helps support them, and this weak dollar period (which is relative; it’s still strong on a longer-term chart and on a purchasing power parity basis) has occurred alongside a sell-off in U.S. equities. That represents a potential capital outflow.

This is discussed in a bit more detail later in this report in the investment section about international equity performance.

**Bitcoin Strategic Reserve Executive Order**

President Trump signed an [executive order](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/03/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-establishes-the-strategic-bitcoin-reserve-and-u-s-digital-asset-stockpile/) to create a bitcoin strategic reserve and a separate digital asset stockpile. I started writing about bitcoin in 2017, and if you would have asked me back then if we’d see that headline in 2025, my guess would have been no.

The key points of the executive order are:

-Bitcoin is treated uniquely as a “digital gold” type of reserve asset. Existing bitcoin that the U.S. holds from criminal seizures/forfeitures will be transferred to the reserve to capitalize it, subject to the laws around the individual assets (i.e. some might need to be returned to rightful owners who committed no crime). Bitcoin will not be sold from it, and the Secretaries of Treasury and Commerce (Scott Bessent and Howard Lutnick) are authorized to find budget-neutral ways to acquire more. (Note: A future executive order from this president or later presidents could still result in sales of bitcoin.)

-Other digital assets will be held in a separate stockpile. It would only be added to by criminal seizures/forfeitures, and the Secretary of the Treasury is authorized to figure what to do with them on an individual basis, including sales of the assets. (Note: This means altcoins held due to seizure/forfeiture could be sold and used to buy bitcoin for the reserve, as a budget-neutral acquisition strategy.)

So far, this is right in line with my base case, with a small bit of upside on top of it. My base case was that they’ll ringfence existing holdings from seizures/forfeitures into a reserve, and might buy a little bit more. That’s basically what this is; the new reserve is capitalized from these existing assets, and they’re authorized to acquire more but it’s unclear to what extent they’ll prioritize it or find ways to do so.

The separation of bitcoin and other digital assets is also a good detail in my view, because many digital assets aside from bitcoin and a few others pass the Howey Test as probable securities, and for the United States to hold some digital asset securities but not others in any significant scale opens up myriad conflicts of interest, similar to and arguably worse than if the U.S. were to buy one stock but not another stock.

**Abundance 360 Summit**

Although I hesitate to travel too frequently, I speak at a decent number of conferences per year, and most of them are either macroeconomic/investing conferences or bitcoin conferences.

I switched things up this past week and spoke at more of a broad tech conference, Peter Diamondis’ [Abundance 360 Summit](https://www.abundance360.com/summit) in Los Angeles. The big themes there were artificial intelligence, longevity, and other technologies like drones. They had a crypto segment, and so I had a fireside chat and then panel discussion, with my focus being on bitcoin and stablecoins.

I found the conference organizers and attendees to be a very high signal group of people, and had some excellent discussions. It was also useful as a pulse check, to see what kind of questions I receive and how my points are received, in that context.

One of the themes I emphasized was the idea of protocol money. People who live and breathe technology tend to think in terms of rapid disruption, and assume that new things will displace old things. But a point I emphasized is that while that’s a true trend for many things, it hasn’t historically applied to communication protocols.

Things like Ethernet, Simple Mail Transfer Protocol, Universal Serial Bus, Internet Protocol, tend to have indefinite lifespans once they achieve dominance. We don’t really know how long they’ll last, because so far the answer is “permanently in the internet age.” In other words, 30 to 50+ years and going very strong with no disruption in sight for most of them.

This is because they are purposely simple and benefit from massive network effects. For some of them, the protocol gradually updates over time, so newer versions of Ethernet and USB replace older versions of Ethernet and USB.

Similarly, the Bitcoin Core client is updated typically every six months, in ways that help the network run better but that don’t affect consensus itself (i.e. not a soft fork or a hard fork, but instead just various code refinements). And on a historical cadence of every few years, it also undergoes a soft fork that makes more fundamental changes to the protocol, while still being backward compatible. That latter type of update is challenging because it requires broad consensus, and indeed when it comes to a $1.5 trillion monetary network, technological conservatism is warranted.

Thus, from a technical and economic perspective I categorize the Bitcoin network as a communication protocol, specifically in this case a monetary communication protocol, which means it benefits from network effects and robust simplicity, and is quite resistant to disruption.

## Investment Analysis: International Rotation and Selling Vol

My report on September 1st, 2024 focused on international equity rotations, and my October 27th piece provided an update on that view.

In general, I’ve been in the camp that international equities are cheap, the entire world is basically on one side of the trade (i.e. overweight U.S. equities), and with a gradual rate cutting cycle in play and other factors, it doesn’t take too much to start a rotation.

However, compared to other trends I’ve timed rather well (fiscal dominance, structural bond bear, pro-gold, pro-bitcoin, etc.) my weakness thus far has been my bullishness in value equities or international equities broadly. I’ve been premature on this rotation, expecting it to have happened some years ago, and so that side of my portfolio has weighed a bit.

Since autumn of last year, I’ve been highlighting this window of another shot for rotation. That was largely because it’s the first U.S. Federal Reserve cutting cycle since 2019, which opens a bit of a window. Being a bit shell-shocked from prior expectations on international equities, I’ve been kind of a “skeptical bull” on the rotation, thus wanting to see if it starts to follow through before pounding the table too hard on the idea.

Here was my description of this back in October, with a bit more focus on emerging markets:

> There are two main camps of thought going forward.
>
> One camp is that there aren’t really going to be meaningful cycles going forward; that it’s structurally different now. U.S. tech stocks will continue to dominate, global capital will continue to gravitate toward the United States, and emerging markets are structurally uninvestable in the broad sense.
>
> The other camp is that we’re approaching a sentiment bottom. Emerging markets are considered uninvestable right at the bottom, and they’re super cheap. With China starting to stimulate, and the U.S. starting to cut rates, if there’s a time for a reversal, it’s right about now.
>
> I’m more in the second camp, but am not betting the farm on it. The way I view it is that we can identify catalysts, but we then need to monitor for follow-through. What 2001 and 2024 have in common is that global capital is stuffed disproportionately into U.S. markets, and the Fed is starting a cutting cycle.
>
> There was a Fed cutting cycle in 2007, but global capital was not stuffed into the U.S. at the time. Quite the contrary; that was a top of an emerging markets cycle and capital was stuffed into the BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa). That was the cycle when the BRICS were named in the first place.
>
> There was also a Fed cutting cycle in 2019. Global capital was stuffed into the U.S. at the time, but not quite as much as now, and then the whole 2020-2022 period was disrupted by a global pandemic and lockdowns and extreme fiscal and monetary actions.
>
> So, 2024 might or might not be the start of a reversal, but there are conditions present for it. Perhaps another disruption will occur and prevent any sort of reversal, such as a war that sends oil to $200/barrel. Maybe major emerging market leaders will shoot themselves in the foot with war, anti-market political actions, or other types of own-goals.
>
> And so I’m in the phase of watching for follow-through or the lack thereof, and am treating countries separately even though they are partially all linked to the dollar and global capital flows.
>
> -Premium Report, October 27th 2024

For a few months after that, there really wasn’t any follow-through, and indeed all the tariff talk was rather dollar-strengthening. However, since February, there’s been an unusually sharp divergence between underperforming U.S. equities and outperforming international equities, to the tune of 8-10%:

![Equity Performance YTD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-equity-performance-ytd.png)

There have been a lot of events in this past month or so that contributed to that.

One is that tariff uncertainty makes it hard for businesses to plan capital spending, or for investors to determine what valuation they want to pay on U.S. company stocks. A small bit of uncertainty is not really an issue, but if investors don’t know if there are going to be across-the-board 25% tariffs on the biggest trading partners or not, or industry-specific massive retaliatory tariffs of 50-200%, then that’s a pretty rough business spending and investor environment.

Two is that with the United States taking a more rhetorically isolationist policy regarding the war in Ukraine, European nations have agreed to step up military spending. For the past five years, I’ve highlighted the power of fiscal policy over monetary policy, and for example during the pandemic stimulus, I compared it to wartime finance for its generally inflationary and stimulatory effects when done at scale. Reuters [reports](https://www.reuters.com/world/europe/eu-leaders-back-defence-surge-support-zelenskiy-after-us-aid-freeze-2025-03-06/):

> BRUSSELS, March 6 (Reuters) – European leaders on Thursday backed plans to spend more on defence and continue to stand by Ukraine in a world upended by Donald Trump’s reversal of U.S. policies.
>
> The European Union’s defence summit in Brussels took place amid fears that Russia, emboldened by its war in Ukraine, may attack an EU country next and that Europe can no longer rely on the U.S. to come to its aid.

Both China and Europe have historically been more reticent to perform big fiscal stimulus, but given certain catalysts (China’s economy hitting some pain points, and Europe facing a fork-in-the-road defense decision), they’ve inched more in that fiscal-heavy direction.

Meanwhile, while I have expressed skepticism on how large the DOGE-related fiscal cuts in the United States will be, it’s clear that 2025 is not a particularly fiscal expansionary year. In general, DOGE is racking up a lot of small optical cuts, but given that the vast majority of U.S. fiscal spending consists of Social Security, Medicare, Interest Expense, Defense, and Veterans Benefits, which are generally third rails that are hard to cut, I wouldn’t hold my breath for major cuts to them. However, both the rhetoric and the actions are toward fiscal trimming in the U.S. and are toward fiscal expansion elsewhere, for now.

The sentiment on my Twitter/X feed has been rather extreme in the past couple of weeks about the strong relative move in international equities. It’s partially because the “Magnificent 7” stocks have underperformed broad U.S. equities, while broad U.S. equities have underperformed international equities, and that full reversal was a very non-consensus move.

And yet the actual rotation thus far has been pretty small in the grand scheme of things. It’s hard to differentiate it from a bump after tax loss harvesting. This is a ratio chart between international stocks and U.S. stocks, where up means international stocks are outperforming and down means U.S. stocks are outperforming:

![ACWI vs US](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-acwi-vs-us-ratio.png)

For those of us with diversified portfolios (U.S. stocks, international stocks, hard monies, cash-equivalents, etc.), this period felt pretty much like any other period. Obviously it hasn’t been great in general, but there are pockets of strength and weakness and it’s been nothing to write home about.

If this type of rotation *does* build some momentum, it can be a rather strong self-sustaining flywheel for a while, like it has been in the other direction.

-Strong U.S. equity and dollar performance boosts the U.S. wealth effect, which provides more spending capacity for the top quartile of U.S. households. This strong consumer spending then benefits U.S. companies’ bottom lines, causing them to report good numbers, and their stocks go up. That strong performance causes global fund managers to chase that performance, adding to the trend, and contributing to U.S. stock market strength and dollar strength. The strong dollar squeezes developing countries around the world that have a lot of dollar-denominated debt, resulting in economic stagnation for them, further highlighting the performance differential both in terms of asset prices and economic performance. This keeps repeating until it eventually hits extremes and exhausts itself, or gets actively disrupted.

-Weak U.S. equity and dollar performance hurts the U.S. wealth effect, reducing wealthy consumer spending capacity, impacting bottom lines of U.S. companies, and causing some global fund managers to pull money out and look elsewhere. A dollar-sell off gives some reprieve to dollar-indebted developing countries, which start to show signs of life, at a time when they are historically inexpensive and under-owned. Thus more fund flows can move toward them, and keep that momentum flowing the other way for a while.

I’m inclined to continue to think that this is the start of a pretty big international equity rotation, albeit not in a straight line, and instead with starts and stops along the way. But given that it would be the first such rotation in 15 years, and international equities have done nothing but bleed out vs U.S. equities during that time, I can’t help but reference one of my Arrested Development memes:

![Arrested Development](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-arrested-development-meme.png)

So in summary, I remain cautiously optimistic on international equities. I think the conditions continue to be right for a potential rotation of some significant magnitude and duration, with the awareness that it’s been kind of a “widowmaker trade” for over a decade. Thus, rather than press all-in on the idea, I just keep a healthy slice of my portfolios in international assets, which might turn from anchors to boosters for a change.

**Selling Cash-Secured Puts on MSTR**

In my prior report, I discussed how if this high volatility period continues, then selling options might re-introduce itself as a useful strategy after a couple of years of being on the backburner:

> I expect that as headline volatility remains elevated, and as there are more headwinds to the buy-the-dip trend given the macro themes described earlier in this report, I’ll be focusing more on selling cash-secured put options. It’s a strategy to get paid to wait to enter positions that you like the fundamentals of.
>
> Literally eight years ago (how time flies), I posted [an article on selling put options](https://www.lynalden.com/selling-put-options/). Some examples might be dated, but the core article content itself was written to be evergreen.
>
> ![Selling Put Options Graphic](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-2-Selling-Put-Options-Rate-of-Return.jpg)
>
> There are plenty of equities on the market that I’d love to own for the next five years, but would prefer to buy a bit lower, and get paid to wait to do so. Selling cash-secured puts is one of my preferred ways to do that, when market conditions are right for it.
>
> That way, I either buy them at a lower price amid a sell-off (having done a lot of fundamental analysis on their long-term bullish potential), or I get paid despite the stock never falling to my target price. The more volatility there is, the higher option premiums tend to be.
>
> During 2023 and 2024 in particular, I was focused on other aspects of the market, and equities were relatively docile, so selling options wasn’t my focus. However, here in 2025 we’re entering a market that I think is more conducive to emphasizing that area a bit more.

A trade I’m going to make in my other holdings (i.e. not in a model portfolio, given the minimum $25k size of the trade) is to sell June 2025 cash-secured put options for MSTR stock at a $250 strike price.

![MSTR Stock](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-mstr-stock.png)

The numbers will change depending on next week’s stock price, but right now this option gives you about $30/share in option premiums for a 3-month period.

“Cash secured” means it’s not a levered trade. The cash is set aside to buy the equity if the option is assigned. In this case, $25k per 100-share contract needs to be set aside. In practice, because the option premium you get comes immediately, it effectively means setting aside about $220/share net up front, or $22k per contract.

-If MSTR remains above $250/share, then the option will expire worthless. I’ll have made ~$30/share or about 12% in three months.

-If MSTR dips, then I’ll be buying at what is effectively a $220 entry price (buying it at $250 strike, minus the fact that I get to keep the $30). If MSTR totally tanks, and let’s say goes down to $170, I still have to buy it at that $250 strike price a.k.a. the $220 effective price (or buy back the option, which would be worth way more money at that point).

This type of trade has a pretty high hit rate. The majority of options expire worthless. So if you do it a bunch, you’ll usually earn the option premium and not have to buy. Every once in a while you’ll have to buy. The main risk of the trade is that it’s asymmetrical: the most that can be made in the three-month period is $30/share, whereas in theory the stock could go down to zero resulting in a $220/share net loss.

The reason I would take such a trade, however, is that it’s a company I’m long-term bullish on and have followed closely for 4.5 years. I would not do it with any and every stock. I would do it with a high-volatility stock that I have some degree of fundamental conviction on and that I’m willing to buy major dips on. Basically, I’m pre-committing to buy a potential dip, and getting paid ahead of time to make that pre-commitment, and thus generally don’t have a preference for whether the option is exercised or not.

If MSTR soars more than $30/share within the next three months, then it would be better to have just bought MSTR outright. In any other scenario, it works out better to have sold the option than to own the stock. Given that I’m *already* long bitcoin, and am long MSTR in model portfolios, I’m fine with missing out on a potential upside scenario for this particular trade. My main goal for this trade is to sell the volatility as something to do on the side.

## Final Thoughts: Gold $3k

The price of gold recently touched $3,000 for the first time. Large round numbers tend to be areas of resistance more often than not, so we’ll see to what extent it gets stuck here or just pushes through.

Seeing as how gold is up 38% year-over-year, I wouldn’t be surprised by a period of consolidation. However, I continue to like it structurally, and view a portfolio slice as warranted.

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-gold.png)

In addition, gold has been an outperformer vs the S&P 500. Looking at the gold/SPX ratio, it would have been better to buy a chunk of yellow metal eight years ago than the S&P 500.

![Gold/SPX](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-gold-spx.png)

That particular chart doesn’t take into account S&P 500 dividends, although at 1-2% per year recently (currently a 1.33% dividend yield), that doesn’t make a massive difference. I still think there’s a decent probability that looking back 5+ years from now, gold will have outperformed the S&P 500 during that time. If anything, the gold/SPX ratio looks like a gradual bottom forming.

Another way to explore that possibility is to flip the chart around, and look at the SPX/gold ratio, which is basically the S&P 500 priced in ounces of gold rather than in dollars:

![SPX to Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-spx-gold.png)

In that sense, we’d be asking whether the S&P 500 is at risk of its third major bear market against gold within the past 50 years. The first one was in the 1970s. Then there was a second one in the 2000s. And lately it’s been gradually rolling over for a third time. Could a combination of tariffs, global capital flow shifts, and broad trade re-alignments result in another 5+ year bear market in that chart?

It’s certainly possible. I’d err toward it happening.
