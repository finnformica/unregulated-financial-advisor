## Macro View

**Tariff Update**

The United States is currently collecting about $30 billion in tariff revenue per month, which if sustained translates to about $360 billion per year. August numbers may come in higher.

The government has about a $2 trillion fiscal deficit otherwise.

Each year, the United States buys approximately $3.2 trillion in goods imports. If the average tariff rate on them ends up being 15-20%, then that’s a headline rate of $480 billion to $640 billion in tariff revenue, but then after various exemptions and other details, the number seems to be pulled down closer to the current figure.

Import prices (which are measured pre-tariff) are flat since April, meaning that foreign exporters in aggregate have not absorbed the cost of the tariffs. American consumers and businesses have been the ones absorbing it. Between those two, it’s mostly businesses so far, with a combination of tariff front-running (importing extra goods in Q1 2025) and temporary margin compression to retain market share.

![Import Prices](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-import-prices.png)

The longer the tariffs are sustained, the more we should expect a portion of those price increases to trickle out to consumers. Much like how money-printing affects prices with a lag, tariffs tend to do the same. However, we should also expect it to be combined with at least a minor change in consumption behavior and a mild change in volumes, since US households (particularly in the bottom half of the income spectrum) don’t have a lot of disposable income flexibility.

**US Business Cycle**

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow tracker currently forecasts 2.5% annualized GDP growth for Q3 2025:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-gdpnow.png)

As Juan Correa-Ossa of BCA Research has highlighted, there’s basically the AI economy and then the rest of the economy. The rest of the economy continues to decelerate, with for example S&P 500 sales growth trickling down, while the big tech platforms continue to have strong sales numbers.

![BCA Research Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-correa-ossa-tweet.png)

The challenge currently with AI is that it’s mostly the hardware side that is profitable. The GPU chipmakers are profitable, and the hyperscalers that run datacenters and get paid for buying and hosting GPUs are profitable (e.g. the Mag7 stocks), but AI companies that offer those services to customers and businesses (and pay the datacenters and GPU chipmakers) are generally nowhere near profitable. That difference is funded by huge ongoing venture capital bets.

Currently the winner of all of this is the end-user, as they are getting a lot more service than they’re paying for. The backend costs should decrease over the next couple of years as GPUs and models continue to rapidly improve, and that will eventually saturate capex for a period of time.

In other words, AI will continue to impact companies and consumers, but the growth rate will likely reduce in the coming years as that big middle gap of unprofitability normalizes.

When we look at S&P 500 [analyst forecasts](https://advantage.factset.com/hubfs/Website/Resources%20Section/Research%20Desk/Earnings%20Insight/EarningsInsight_080825.pdf) for sales growth here in Q3 2025, information technology companies are at the top, while consumer discretionary companies, consumer staples companies, and oil and gas producers are near the bottom. Given the contrast between the AI boom and the tariff headwinds on consumption, this dynamic makes sense.

There’s been a lot of action since then; the Treasury has begun to refill its general cash account with earnest. This has sucked over $200 billion out of the financial system over the past month and on its own would be negative for domestic liquidity:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-tga.png)

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-reverse-repos.png)

According to its own target, the Treasury still has about $350 billion in refilling to do by the end of September, and there’s only around $30 billion left in reverse repos, which means the next leg of refilling can’t be neutralized in the same way. The easy fruit has been picked, so we’ll see what happens next.

My base case is to see a mildly negative domestic liquidity situation going forward, which could be somewhat offset by the Treasury not refilling their cash levels quite as fast as they forecasted they would.

The short-term interest rate market is currently pricing in one interest rate cut by the Fed in September, and an average of three cuts by March. **The Arkade Trial**

Back in May 2023, I briefly wrote about the newly-proposed Ark scaling method for the Bitcoin network:

> To the extent that another major soft fork is possible, there is growing interest in a soft fork that would add covenants to Bitcoin. This would add a bit more programmability to transactions, and enable new scaling features. A new second-layer network called [Ark](https://www.arkpill.me/) was proposed at the conference, which requires a soft fork for covenants to reach its full potential. Ark has some pros and cons relative to the Lightning network, and is interoperable with the Lightning network. However, it remains to be seen if developers will be able to form a consensus on which specific covenants-related soft fork to push forward, and then if the majority of miners and nodes will agree to it or not. Beyond that, there are other future soft fork potentials that would enable zk rollups and other scaling methods to the base layer.
>
> This is a trend I’ll be watching closely.
>
> -May 28, 2023 premium report

And then in September 2024, I provided an update on the progress:

> **Ark Labs Capital Raise**
>
> Back in spring 2023, a new second-layer bitcoin payments concept called “Ark” was proposed and quickly captured a lot of mindshare in the ecosystem.
>
> Compared to the existing Lightning network, Ark wouldn’t rely on inbound liquidity which is a major usability improvement. However, it would be less capital efficient by a wide margin. And the two networks could also be combined to improve each other.
>
> Ark will rely on the activity of centralized payment processors, but those centralized processors don’t custody funds, and due to various time locks and such, they are trustless for the user. Thus, a centralized Ark payment processor is required to process transactions but cannot steal user funds. It is “centralized but trustless” in other words. And there can be a few separate Ark processors, and ones to rise up if existing ones cease operation.
>
> However, the problem was that Ark required certain covenant soft forks in Bitcoin’s base layer to work economically, and so the idea kind of stalled. It also went through changes regarding who the key people working on it are.
>
> However, developers seemingly [found a way](https://gist.github.com/RubenSomsen/a394beb1dea9e47e981216768e007454?permalink_comment_id=4633382#gistcomment-4633382) to make it economically viable without a soft fork. And a company called Ark Labs [recently raised $2.5 million](https://www.reuters.com/technology/tim-draper-leads-25-mln-funding-round-crypto-startup-ark-labs-2024-08-22/) in pre-seed funding to build on the idea.
>
> And Ark Labs’ website explains the trade-off [here](https://arkdev.info/docs/learn/clark/):
>
> A final big difference between covenant-less Ark and covenant Ark is that in the VTXO tree, the commitments to child transactions are the signatures of the transactions and not the transaction templates. In Ark, child transactions are encoded into the output covenants of the parent, so they can just be deterministically created and spent as such.
>
> In covenant-less Ark, however, in order to create valid child transactions, they need to have valid signatures. These signatures obviously can’t be deterministically generated, so they have to be stored. This means that additional data has to be stored for users to have a safe exit path. For Ark, knowing all the VTXO leaves and some tree-specific parameters is sufficient, while for covenant-less Ark, the signatures of the tree, or at least for your exit branch, have to be stored alongside the other information.
>
> The detail that I need to see more information on, is how significant those storage requirements are, which can affect whether the covenant-less option is indeed economically viable or not. I’m currently looking into those details via my deep-tech developer contacts.
>
> If we picture the current Bitcoin development landscape as a spectrum, I’d place Ark somewhere in the middle. On one side there is Lightning, Chaumian Ecash, and Nostr as a powerful set of private payment technologies along with social payment discovery. They exist in operation and are well-studied. On the other side there is research into complex optimistic roll-ups via BitVM technology, which can enable all sorts of smart contracts but are very complex. Ark sits in the middle between those two poles, with relative simplicity in terms of how complex it is to implement and how expressive it is once it is implemented.
>
> I don’t have a strong view on Ark’s economics yet, given how capital intensive it is and because I need to see more detail on the storage requirements. But it’s more newsworthy than a given startup raising money, because it’s about an overall protocol that multiple companies can build on, and something I’ll be following closely.
>
> -September 1, 2024 premium report

I’m happy to report that there’s been another significant milestone for Ark.

At the popular Baltic Honeybadger Conference in Riga here in August 2025, Ark was used behind the scenes for merchants accepting bitcoin payments. I’m increasingly confident that the tech is here to stay, and in a useful way.

Some of the biggest conferences are known as being increasingly political, corporate, and mainstream; users there tend to care more about price action than the tech. Other conferences tend to be known as more techy or cypherpunk, with a bigger focus on innovation and hardly any politicians or corporate suits around. The annual conference in Riga is on the far end of the spectrum for the latter- a very techy conference with a lot of per-capita payment volume in bitcoin by attendees.

People paid for things as they normally would with the Lightning network, but the technical plumbing used Ark ([see thread here](https://x.com/ArkLabsHQ/status/1955617205967782203)):

![Ark Labs Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-ark-labs-tweet.png)

One thing I’ve been emphasizing for a while is that as various Bitcoin layer-two solutions emerge, Lightning is likely to be the glue that ties them together. That’s because Lightning is a truly stateless and decentralized layer two (with all the pros and cons that come with that), and so it can tie various other layered parts of the ecosystem together in a way that others can’t.

![Jan8 Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-jan8tweet.png)

![May28 Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-may28tweet.png)

And this Ark showcase in Riga further cemented that view. [This article](https://insider.btcpp.dev/p/lightning-swaps-are-the-connective) by Janusz highlighted how the successful usage of Ark was also a successful integration of the Lightning network, calling Lightning the connective tissue.

![Janusz Article Feature](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-janusz-article.png)

Notably, the piece mentions that Lightning served as the bridge for a successful transaction between a Fedimint (Chaumian ecash) user and an Arkade user:

> This use case isn’t restricted to merchant payments. Also in Riga, [two bitcoin developers](https://x.com/EricSirion/status/1954577791539654672) engaged in an Ecash to Arkade transaction. Ecash notes can not be transferred to Arkade users directly. Swap providers, on both sides of the payment, execute the transfer across the Lightning Network.
>
> In Riga, the Fedimint user initiated the payment request to their counterparty by swapping locally minted Ecash with the [Fedimint Gateway](https://github.com/fedimint/fedimint/blob/master/docs/gateway.md). On the Arkade side, the user received a VTXO that was swapped to them via Boltz who generated the invoice, and received the payment, on the Lightning side. This payment was executed just like our example outlined above.
>
> Users in this scenario don’t need to have channels open with each other directly. They don’t even need to be on the same network. Instead, they used systems that are able to communicate with a “bridge” protocol: the Lightning Network.

Overall, between ongoing BitVM development and Ark-style scaling, I continue to be impressed with the development pace on the Bitcoin network.

## A Look at the Health Care Sector Selloff

The past two-and-a-half years have been rather catastrophic for health care sector stock performance in the United States.

This chart shows the ratio of the S&P 500 health care sector relative to the full S&P 500. These past few years have undone decades of relative performance, bringing the ratio to lows not seen in 25 years:

![S&P 500 vs Health Care](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-spx-vs-healthcare.png)

There are a few reasons for this.

-Many people delayed medical procedures during the 2020-2022 pandemic years, which created a build-up of extra procedures to do in recent years. Health insurers have found themselves with above-average costs.

-The rapid growth of the money supply during 2020-2021 trickled out to broad prices with a multi-year lag, and by the time these procedures were being performed, the costs of all sorts of things including health care professional salaries and hospital services had risen dramatically.

-The Biden Administration pushed back on government-funded health care costs to some extent. Here in 2025, the Trump Administration’s One Big Beautiful Bill Act substantially tightened the restrictions on Medicaid, which over the coming years is expected to reduce the number of people able to use it.

As a result, health insurance companies in general have underperformed on earnings releases and have lowered their forward guidance for earnings numbers going forward.

The long-term bull case for investing in the health care sector is that aging demographics should continue to fuel its growth, and the performance of the companies tends to be relatively uncorrelated to the standard business cycle, given that it’s a necessary (and often partially government-funded) expense.

The bear case, in contrast, is that the United States already has the highest per-capita health care costs in the world by a wide margin, and it’s absolutely ripe for disruption. The relationships between providers and insurers and middle men are opaque and often rather corrupt, which reduces the ability of customers to be price sensitive and shop around to contain costs.

In the cyclical sense, could the near-term bottom be in? Is the industry oversold? The odds point to yes.

Berkshire Hathaway (BRK.A) recently disclosed a new $1.5 billion position in United Healthcare (UNH):

![UNH FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-unh-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

My preference for dip-buying here is Elevance (ELV). Shortly after earnings when the stock fell considerably, the CEO of Elevance [personally bought $2.4 million](https://www.investing.com/news/insider-trading-news/elevance-health-ceo-boudreaux-buys-24-million-in-stock-93CH-4142393) of the stock.

![ELV FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-17-elv-fastgraph.png)

I generally view this as a wash-out period for the health insurance space. Nobody wants to touch it, and it has become very cheap, which is why I think it’s likely due for a rebound.

Elevance is one of the largest health insurers in the country, and is a Blue Cross / Blue Shield licensee. They also have other offerings in their portfolio. Their latest 10-K summarizes:

> We have organized our brand portfolio into the following core go-to-market brands:
>
> * Anthem Blue Cross/Anthem Blue Cross and Blue Shield — represents our Anthem-branded and affiliated Blue Cross and/or Blue Shield licensed plans;
> * Wellpoint — unites select non-BCBSA licensed Medicare, Medicaid and commercial plans under the Wellpoint name; and
> * Carelon — this brand brings together our healthcare related services and capabilities, including our CarelonRx and Carelon Services businesses, under a  
>   single brand name.

After a few years, once the industry gets past this deep-value patch, then I think it’s worth doing a big re-assessment to see what’s still worth holding.

In the coming decade, as nothing stops this fiscal train and American demographics age into the most expensive health care system in the world, the sector will probably be punctuated by further rounds of crisis and attempts at cost control. As I wrote before, it’s certainly ripe for disruption.

For example, [Cost Plus Drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cost_Plus_Drugs) is attempting to spear through the oligopoly of pharmacy benefits managers by distributing generic medicines to consumers with fewer middle-men. It was co-founded by Mark Cuban in 2022 and is currently what he spends the majority of his social media focus on promoting. Their broad approach is to have the drugs manufactured and add a low 15% overhead cost on top of that to run their operations.

I think we’ll see more alternatives like that continue to pop up in the coming years, as Americans seek to go around the opaquely-priced and expensive health care system. Some insurers will integrate with those types of providers, while others may be bypassed, and it’ll be important to monitor industry trends and see which companies are on the right side of cost control rather than disrupted by it.

## Final Thoughts: Headlines Continue

We continue to be in what I refer to as a “headline-driven market” as opposed to a more mechanical liquidity-driven market.

However, I think the rate of change of headlines is likely to reduce in the coming quarters. The impact of going from near-zero tariffs to $30+ billion per month, for example, was a big series of headlines for markets to absorb. We still have to see how that shakes out in court (i.e. the usage of emergency powers to bypass Congress for tariffs), but aside from that, future changes quarter-by-quarter should be lower in either direction.

As a result, conditions should fall back toward a more mechanical procession over time, with changes in liquidity being relevant to monitor, along with the broadening impact of the tariffs as businesses gradually try to pass them on to consumers.
