# Alphabet Deep Dive Analysis

*November 10, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report covers liquidity conditions, the rise of long-end bond yields, the U.S. business cycle, and some election outcomes to monitor.

The digital asset section looks at the bitcoin price breakout, bitcoin consensus changes in the modern era, and post-election implications for bitcoin and crypto securities.

The investment analysis section looks at Alphabet as a potential investment. It’s one of the least-loved of the Magnificent 7, and the question is whether some of its risks warrant that or not.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be flat:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-domestic-liquidity.png)

The red sweep of the Presidency and seemingly both houses of Congress makes it much less probable that we’ll see a debt ceiling dispute and associated TGA drain in the first half of 2025. I covered this in my prior report, and now with the election results we have a pretty firm answer on the likely outcome:

> The likelihood of this repeating again in the first half of 2025 is mostly based on politics and will need to be monitored in real time. There are a few points worth making, though.
>
> -The debt ceiling has been around for over a century but it has mainly been used as a political tool by the out-of-power party against in-power party starting about a decade and a half ago when the Tea Party faction of the GOP used it against President Obama. Since then, it has become a recurring political tool, but one that is primarily used by Republicans against Democratic presidents.
>
> -However, the sample size for that is small. During that time, there have been three terms of Democratic presidents (two Obama, one Biden) and only one term for a Republican president (Trump). Democrats didn’t press Trump on the issue to the same extent that Republicans pressed Obama and Biden on the issue, but it doesn’t mean for sure that they wouldn’t do it in the future.
>
> -My base case is that a Harris victory is more likely to result in a prolonged debt ceiling dispute between her administration and Republicans, and thus a higher likelihood of almost $800 billion getting drained back into the financial system from the TGA in the first half of 2025. If Trump wins, which betting markets and polls are leaning toward, then a prolonged debt ceiling dispute is less likely, since Democrats seem less likely to use it. However, it would depend on the make-up of the House, and whether Democrats start using the tool more aggressively or not. So regardless of the election outcome, it’s a variable to watch for. There are of course many other ramifications from a Harris or Trump victory, but this section is focusing on liquidity, of which the $800 billion TGA and the associated debt ceiling is a huge factor in the first half of 2025.
>
> -October 27th premium report

So, that’s probably the biggest binary liquidity-related question that’s been resolved for now. We should therefore expect business-as-usual in terms of domestic liquidity as a base case, meaning that the Fed will continue to drain liquidity via quantitative tightening, but that ongoing T-bill issuance will offset that by pulling liquidity out of reverse repos and back into the financial system, with the TGA being relatively uneventful.

If something changes on that front, then I’ll report about it.

As we progress later into 2025, I think we will likely see the end of quantitative tightening in order to maintain liquidity in the banking system and avoid regular usage of the Fed’s repo facility, but we’re not there yet.

**Global Liquidity**

The dollar index surged on the election outcome, which is a near-term headwind for liquidity:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-dxy.png)

Basically, a Trump administration is viewed by the market as more likely to run hot than a Harris administration. We’ll have to monitor specific legislation as it comes.

One thing I’ve pointed out for a bit is that fiscally speaking, the 2024 Republican party has a different makeup than the Paul Ryan era of the party from the 2010s. They’re no longer a party that emphasizes cutting entitlement spending. And so for example, the 2024 GOP platform lists its 14th promise as *not* cutting Social Security or Medicare (capitalization theirs):

> 14. FIGHT FOR AND PROTECT SOCIAL SECURITY AND MEDICARE WITH NO CUTS, INCLUDING NO CHANGES TO THE RETIREMENT AGE

In the United States, Social Security, Medicare, Defense, Veterans Benefits, and Interest Expense pretty much equal tax receipts. The rest of the federal government expenditures combined constitute a smaller chunk on the expenditure pie chart than those big mega-areas.

And so, my view has been that we’ll likely continue to have large deficits and operate in a regime of fiscal dominance regardless of the election outcome, and that the election will instead affect the recipients of those deficits a bit but not have an enormous impact on the magnitude of those deficits. Key things I’ll be monitoring for from a fiscal perspective will be:

1) Tax changes including whether to let the tax cuts from Trump’s prior term automatically expire or not (a red sweep including with Trump back himself implies they’ll be renewed).

2) Potential changes to the Affordable Care Act and its associated impact on the budget and healthcare industry (it’s the biggest opening for Republicans to change that since John McCain blocked them from doing so in the first two years of Trump’s prior term when he also had a red sweep).

3) Tariff policies or lack thereof. It remains to be seen to what extent they’ll be implemented at scale, or merely used as a negotiating tactic in practice.

**Long-end Bond Shenanigans**

When the Fed cut interest rates by 0.50% in mid-September, long-end rates ranging from Treasuries to mortgages shot *up* by quite a bit:

![10 Year Treasury Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-ten-year-treasury-rate.png)

We also saw a rise in inflation breakevens, which I normally don’t take too seriously because they lack predictive power, but it’s still notable:

![Breakevens](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-breakevens.png)

The Fed went on to cut an additional 0.25% this past week.

Basically, the market is pricing in a higher likelihood of sticky inflation, and so even as the short-term rates decrease, long-term rates remain elevated or go higher. This was a historically unusual outcome from an interest rate cut, and shows why I continue to avoid long duration bonds in my model portfolios; the risk reward of owning them when 1) the yield curve is inverted and 2) deficits and nominal GDP are running kind of hot, is just not particularly compelling in my view.

So I’ve been in the camp that I like stocks, gold, and bitcoin over bonds, and to the extent that I own paper, I like short duration or TIPS.

All else being equal, these higher long-end bond yields put some downward pressure on liquidity conditions. Borrowing rates for actual consumers and small businesses aren’t really going down from the Fed rate cuts, in other words.

Mortgage refinancing remains historically low, and what little bump has occurred is now behind us with these higher rates:

![Mortgage Refinancing](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-mortgage-refinancing.png)

I continue to expect mortgage refinancing activity to remain low for quite some time, with only little bumps like this one from September.

**U.S. Business Cycle Summary**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy was decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, entered stall speed for a while, began to show signs of stabilization, and is now kind of stuck in that phase. Construction spending has been getting a fiscal boost from the Inflation Reduction Act. Retail spending and capital goods spending are negative on an inflation-adjusted basis.

![Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Bitcoin Consensus Analysis**

One of the most important aspects of bitcoin is that it’s decentralized and thus hard to change. If it was easy to change, including things like the monetary policy or transaction control or other things like that, then it would be far less valuable. And like many successful protocols, bitcoin is purposely simple; it keeps complexity low to reduce technical attack surfaces.

And yet, any protocol needs to update gradually in small backward-compatible ways. We all use Ethernet and USB despite them being many decades old, and it’s largely because they are ubiquitous but can update to be faster in ways that are still backward-compatible with their older versions. They are simple and achieved network dominance, and then evolved in a backward-compatible way to remain technologically current.

The main decentralized limiter for open source software is that nobody can force you as the runner of the software to update. Developers can make new versions of the software, but they can’t necessarily force you to update to their new version. You can pull updates toward your version; they can’t push updates toward you.

In addition to the list of updates that have occurred for the bitcoin protocol, even the *way or method* that the bitcoin protocol is updated has changed over time. In the early era, like when Satoshi Nakamoto himself was still updating the client in 2009 and 2010, he pretty much just unliterally updated it and everyone else would update too. Even though it was a decentralized protocol, it was so small and centered on him as the creator that when he said, “Hey guys, here’s the next update,” that was just taken as law for the first year or so and everyone updated their clients since this guy literally built it.

But after he left, which was for the best in terms of network decentralization, and the protocol became larger, and more people held it and mined it and ran nodes, then it became more complicated to update it. There’s no formal process to update bitcoin. What worked in 2009 was different than what worked in 2017, and what worked in 2017 is likely different than what will work in 2024 and beyond now that ETFs and sovereign nations and other large entities are involved, and as the network is more spread out in general.

Bitcoin software does have regular minor updates every six months to stay current, but here I mean major consensus updates, like soft forks that change something more structural about how the network operates. Some soft fork updates can add useful features with minimal risks, while other updates could be potentially destabilizing, which is why many node operators are resistant to changes unless they are *very* highly vetted.

Over the past few months, I co-authored a blended technical and economic research paper, along with Steve Lee of Block Inc (SQ) and Ren of Electric Capital, that analyzes how bitcoin consensus has evolved during the network’s lifetime, and how consensus updates were made in the past, and how consensus updates might be made in the future, along with various risks to be aware of during certain types of consensus updates. And last week we published it.

Here’s the v1.0 PDF: [Analyzing Bitcoin Consensus: Risks in Protocol Upgrades](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bcap_v1.0.pdf)

[![BCAP Abstract](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-bcap-abstract.png)](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/bcap_v1.0.pdf)

During the process, we had several people review it, representing some of the biggest bitcoin ETFs, biggest bitcoin-holding corporate treasurers, biggest exchanges, long-time developers, legal experts, philosophy professors, and so forth to capture all sorts of details for how different types of stakeholders might act during a contentious soft fork update or technical issue, or any other thoughts they have on the paper, and made a number of updates based on their feedback along the way.

We also released the paper to the public domain on Github and will continue to maintain it. People can submit proposed updates, or even help maintain it along with us. It’s a living document that is meant to best describe and educate about how bitcoin consensus works and what some of the risk scenarios are for updates, so that all sorts of different entities are aware of those risks and can navigate around them with a higher probability of success.

Here’s the Github repository: <https://github.com/bitcoin-cap/bcap>

One of the more interesting parts to me was the analysis around [bounty claims](https://github.com/bitcoin-cap/bcap?tab=readme-ov-file#how-might-this-occur-with-a-soft-fork). If miners update a soft fork, but economic nodes are slow to do so, then it can create a scenario where value can build up on a layer-two protocol, and then be drained or “bounty claimed” by a brief 51% miner attack. This is a relevant risk to consider at a time when some people have proposed alternative clients to activate contentious soft forks.

**Election Implications for Bitcoin/Crypto**

When I do interviews, I get a lot of questions about election outcomes on bitcoin.

Trump’s actual promises about bitcoin are pretty moderate thus far. Unlike RFK Jr. or Cynthia Lummis, he didn’t personally propose to build a massive multi-million BTC strategic bitcoin reserve, even when speaking at the 2024 Bitcoin Conference. He just said the government under his administration would stop selling the bitcoin they already had from seizures, which isn’t much, among some other items like embracing mining. The biggest thing he floated was the idea of making bitcoin transactions non-taxable, although to my knowledge he didn’t commit to that. Overall, it’s still unclear what he will do when all is said and done, other than that he seems to be far more in favor of the asset for his second term than he was in his first term.

The market in general perceives the Republican party as more bitcoin/crypto friendly, and so a red sweep seems to have served as a bullish price catalyst. On election night, bitcoin broke out of its 8-month consolidation.

Unless the U.S. *does* build a big bitcoin strategic reserve or make it non-taxable (which I consider unlikely in this 4-year term, but would watch for), I don’t consider the election to have much of a direct impact on bitcoin itself. I’m already bullish on bitcoin, and I don’t see clear political catalysts for it other than sentiment and lower overall likelihood of government action against it, which is indirect support. So while the election is more impactful for this asset than it might be for other assets, I generally take the “under” on some of the more bullish scenarios. If those scenarios occur, then I’d have a higher price expectation for an asset that I’m already bullish on to begin with.

However, for entities running altcoin crypto security businesses, this election is probably a big outcome. As the makeup of the SEC will shift under the next administration, it seems there will be a lot more pro-crypto security rulings. The broad crypto industry lobbied heavily to get influence on this matter, and will most likely receive it.

And thus there is some renewed industry bullishness on altcoins. The U.S. SEC has been very much against crypto securities in general. Bitcoin is a commodity, so the SEC hasn’t had much to do with it other than declining to allow a spot bitcoin ETF until they lost a lawsuit on the topic, which was embarrassing for them. I expect this election outcome to ease the SEC’s stance against crypto securities.

I think we might have a temporary bottom in place for a variety of altcoins vs BTC. Basically, I’m less bearish on them for the moment until I see a new lower-low for those ratios, and thus would follow price as my guideline, with the framework that this election is generally good for crypto securities in the temporary sense. However, I don’t personally trade them, and instead I tend to focus on bitcoin.

## Deep Dive: Alphabet

Alphabet (GOOGL) is currently one of the least well-loved among the “Magnificent Seven” mega-cap U.S. tech stocks.

With a price/earnings ratio of about 23x, Alphabet trades at a similar earnings multiple to Coca Cola (KO) and less than half of the earnings multiple of Costco (COST):

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-googl-fastgraph.png)

Consensus analyst expectations are for about 12% annualized earnings growth going forward, which if correct gives us a price/earnings/growth ratio (“PEG ratio”) of under 2x. That’s a rather reasonable valuation in my view. From that point, we then have to analyze the risks that could cause the company to fail to achieve that level of growth, which are categorized further down in the risk section.

**Company Overview**

Alphabet started out its life by redefining how search engines work. Rather than just looking for a bunch of keywords on pages like many other search engines did (which is highly game-able by content writers), Alphabet’s co-founders used a concept called “page rank” to identify better quality signals for websites in order to rank them properly.

Specifically, they would catalogue and analyze the vast number of links that websites gave each other, and would rank websites higher if they had 1) a lot of links from other websites pointing to them and 2) links from high-authority websites pointing to them (e.g. *Reuters* linking to a page is more impactful than a hobby blogger linking to a page). And so when a user searched for something, Google would look for both relevant content signals such as keywords, but then would also look for these quality/link metrics. By doing so, they dominated the search engine market. And they monetize it by selling adds on those search pages, rather than just showing the organic search results.

They acquired YouTube when it was small, which has grown into the dominant and ubiquitous video-hosting social media website, and gives them more ad surface to work with.

Their next big win was to dominate the *portals* to which people interact with the internet and applications. Alphabet’s Chrome browser is the world’s most popular computer browser for accessing the internet, and Alphabet’s Android operating system is the world’s most popular smartphone operating system. This helped them entrench their search engine into peoples’ lives, and gave them a near-duopoly along with Apple when it comes to mobile app stores.

With their large datacenter scale, they then entered the cloud computing market. Since they already needed massive datacenters to host their own stuff, they benefit from significant economies of scale by building some additional datacenters and hosting other companies’ and consumers’ computing needs in them. On that front, their main competitors are Amazon (AMZN) and Microsoft (MSFT).

Lately they’ve gotten into AI, which is very processing-intensive, and thus can benefit from their massive scale. Their AI offering, Gemini, has secondary market share after ChatGPT, although it’s a distant second at the moment.

Overall, the company is undergoing a rotation and diversification of its revenue sources. Its search engine business is still its leading business, but is not growing as fast as other areas like service revenue or cloud revenue, and so we’re seeing a gradual transformation of the company in that sense.

![GOOGL Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-googl-revenue.png)

For some areas, their business lines give them powerful synergies or economic moats. For example, Alphabet’s cloud offering is not as big as Amazon’s or Microsoft, but an important advantage that they have is that unlike those two companies, they are dominant in mobile operating systems, and thus their cloud services specifically for mobile applications tend to punch above their weight, thanks to partnerships with Samsung and other companies.

**Balance Sheet**

Alphabet has one of the strongest balance sheets in the world, with far more cash-equivalents than debt:

![GOOGL Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-10-googl-balance-sheet.png)

However, in recent years they have been upping their capex for rapid datacenter scaling and GPU purchases to compete in the cloud and AI space. I think that’s a good thing because it puts the cash to better use, but it also presents some risk because investors have to monitor the return on investment that this type of capex is achieving.

The tremendous cashflow generation potential of Alphabet has recently also been turned toward shareholder returns. Starting in 2019, the share count began decreasing due to net buybacks. Here in 2024, Alphabet initiated a regular dividend. So instead of more and more cash accumulating on the balance sheet, more is going back to either 1) increasing the ownership stake of each share or 2) paying cash to owners of each share which they can choose to reinvest into more shares or not.

Overall, I think this is a sensible balance sheet policy. It’s important for them to hold a large cash hoard because that gives them a lot of optionality, but only up to a certain point where it becomes dilutive, and thus at those very lofty levels it’s better to give some of it back to shareholders as excess capital (unless they want to do what MicroStrategy does and buy bitcoin with it and thus outperform everyone).

**Key Risks**

Since Alphabet has been very focused on the search business, any threat to that search business is a key threat to the company.

One of the early threats was the rise of social media and the relative downfall of individual websites and blogs. Alphabet benefits when people search for websites, or when websites put ads on themselves. But if people spend more time on large super-sites like Instagram or Twitter/X in a walled garden type of environment, then it kind of takes market share away from the whole search/website environment around the margins.

Another long-term threat has been anti-trust risks. Their market share in several areas has become so dominant that they’re always facing risk of regulatory crack-down in multiple jurisdictions. This became more tangible this summer, when Alphabet [lost a key anti-trust case](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-10-09/google-breakup-what-s-at-stake-in-the-us-antitrust-case). The court found that Alphabet’s payments to companies like Apple and Samsung to be their default search provider locked out competition. Alphabet intends to appeal the decision, and it’s still not clear what the outcome will be. It’s possible that Alphabet might have to break off parts of its business so that the search business and the “portals” business (Chrome, Google Play app store, Android, etc) are not under the same roof. But there are also a lot more benign outcomes. And in general I expect some of the more benign outcomes as a base case while acknowledging “non-benign” outcomes as an upside risk. And notably, due to the election outcome, we’ll be seeing a shift at the Department of Justice, which could affect cases like this.

Finally, the newest threat to Alphabet’s search business is the rise of AI. If we want to know something, rather than search for the relevant website to tell us about it, we can now ask ChatGPT. That can indirectly eat into Alphabet’s market share by changing how we search for information. In response, Alphabet has gotten into the AI business itself, incorporates AI into Google searches, and has had to spend a lot of capex money on this.

One thing that I think is important to realize is that not all searches are equal in terms of ad revenue. Some types of searches inherently have more value to advertisers and thus to the advertiser platform. If I search for “plumber near me” then it’s pretty clear I want to find a plumber and spend money on plumbing services, and so plumbers are going to compete to get my attention. But if I want to know “what’s the difference between Sci-Fi and Fantasy?” then whether I type that into Google or ChatGPT I’ll get an AI answer that helps explain the difference and it’s not nearly as monetizable. So if Google loses market share to people searching for various less-monetizable information, then that’s not really a big issue.

And AI in general “expands the pie” for what’s possible. So for example we can ask AI to summarize something for us, or create something for us, which wasn’t possible with search engines. Since Alphabet is getting into the AI business, it expands the areas in which it can provide value. And so in that sense, new entrants like ChatGPT are a challenge, but also the technology itself is harnessed by Alphabet and thus multiple entities can grow larger at the same time.

That’s not to say that AI isn’t a threat to Alphabet’s search business, but rather it’s to say that not all searches are equal and that the pie is growing, and thus it may take more time for AI to eat into Alphabet’s search revenue than bears think, which by extension gives Alphabet more time to expand its cloud and AI businesses.
