## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

While the Federal Reserve has attempted to maintain tight monetary policy, the U.S. Treasury has run rather loose fiscal policy, including shortening the average duration of their debt security issuance, which lets them effectively pull money out of the Fed’s reverse repo facility and put it back into the broader financial system. In other words, as this line falls, it is positive for liquidity, all else being equal:

![Reverse Repo Facility](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-reverse-repos.png)

That pro-liquidity reverse repo drain is somewhat offset by the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet reduction (quantitative tightening) which is negative for liquidity. Overall domestic liquidity continues to be flat-to-up, which has been supportive for broad asset prices. The recent liquidity surge came after the Treasury announced its Q4 refunding details that included way more short-duration T-bill issuance than market participants had expected.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity has been similarly decent but not fantastic:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-global-m2.png)

And as the sharp drop in the orange line shows, we are starting to get the base effect retracement that I wrote about last month:

**Yen Carry Trade**

One particular variable within the global liquidity landscape is the yen carry trade. Japan is a large economy, with a large currency, and some of the lowest interest rates in the world. In addition, they have a very large positive net international investment position, meaning they own a lot more foreign assets than foreigners own of their assets, which is a result of decades of accumulated trade surpluses.

Due to the yen’s interest rate being so low, two things tend to happen:

-Japanese investors go out and buy foreign assets, including unhedged assets. They’ll buy U.S. stocks, U.S. bonds, other foreign assets, etc. It’s better than holding cash that pays zero.

-Foreign investors borrow yen at very low interest rates, and then sell the yen to buy foreign assets. It could be anything from the S&P 500 to Brazilian bonds. This is the carry trade in particular- borrowing from a currency with low rates and buying a currency that offers higher real yields.

During 2022 and 2023, the whole world tightened monetary policy… basically except Japan. The yen weakened against most other major currencies, and anyone doing the carry trade made a lot of money. A Japanese investor holding unhedged U.S. T-bills made great returns in yen terms, for example, both in terms of higher yield and the USDJPY currency appreciation.

In 2024, if most central banks no longer tighten, then that differential is probably mostly over. There are even some reports that the Bank of Japan might tighten monetary policy slightly, although that remains to be seen. If that happens, then these trades risk going in reverse: Japanese investors could bring some of their capital back home, and foreign investors might have less incentive to keep borrowing yen and buying other assets, because the yen can moderately strengthen and move against their leveraged position.

**Business Cycle Indicators**

If we look at the three biggest economic blocks, the United States, Euro Area, and China, we see three very different business cycles playing out, with different areas of strength and weakness. In short:

-The United States has weak production but strong consumption.

-The Euro Area has weak production and weak consumption.

-China has strong production but weak consumption.

The United States is still fueling decent domestic consumption metrics due to its unusually large fiscal deficits. However, manufacturing PMIs are sub-50, indicating mild contraction on the production side. Small business lending is rather weak as well, in large part due to the central bank’s tight monetary policy. The Atlanta Fed currently projects positive real GDP growth in Q4 2023, but at a slower pace than the prior two quarters.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-gdpnow.png)

Some key parts of Europe have mismanaged their energy policy, which the disruption of gas supplies from Russia exacerbated and brought forward. As a result, Germany in particular is going through a period of outright deindustrialization rather than just weak cyclical production, as various energy-intensive industries move elsewhere. Meanwhile, European fiscal deficits are moderate, and so consumers are not being highly stimulated either. Manufacturing PMI is in contraction.

![Euro Area PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-euro-area-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: Trading Economics*

The good news is that the rate-of-change of Europe’s economy seems to be stabilizing at the present time. They’re basically at recessionary levels but it’s quite possible that this period right now is the worst of it. Sabrina Khanniche of Pictet Asset Management had some good charts and commentary on this subject:

![Khanniche Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-khanniche-tweets.png)

China continues to have strong industrial production, mainly for exporting, which gives them a strong trade surplus. However, domestic consumption is weak, as the property sector deleverages. China is running rather tight fiscal policy as well, and so consumers are not being stimulated. China is a mirror image of the United States at the moment in terms of fiscal and monetary policy and the consumption/production divergence.

![Zweifel Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-zweifel-tweets.png)

Overall, weak production in the United States, weak consumption in China, and weak production and consumption in Europe are collectively putting a cyclical drag on global energy and copper demand. I expect this to continue into the first half of 2024, but by 2025 (and potentially sooner, although that remains to be seen) I expect another upturn in global PMIs.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin broke above $44k for the first time this cycle, and is up over 160% year-to-date. The past few weeks have been particularly intense in terms of bullishness.

Is it overbought in the near-term sense? Probably. It’s hard to say. I certainly wouldn’t be surprised by sharp pullbacks or periods of consolidation at any point, and I have no firm opinion on the next six months of price action. It could drop 20% from here, or go up 30% and then drop 20%. Or go sideways for a few months. Who knows.

Is it overvalued structurally? I would firmly say no. I think it’s just getting started for the next two years.

![Bitcoin Price Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-bitcoin-chart.png)

Traders in general make a lot of mistakes with bitcoin by trying to get too cute with it. They do all sorts of technical analysis and look for specific price targets without deeply understanding the asset, like trying to wait to buy at $12k as some did when it was $16k, and then watching it run to $44k without them ever getting onboard. The trap that many of them fall into is always expecting one more deeper correction before they buy, and then when that comes, they expect just a tiny bit more correction, and ultimately they never buy in size and just let it run away from them in the upcoming years.

The people that gain a lot of value with bitcoin tend to be really straightforward buyers that study the asset a lot and want to own it for the next five or ten years or more, and don’t try to predict near-term price action. Throughout the bear market, report after report, I just kept writing about dollar-cost averaging and focusing on the long-term, rather than try to predict specific price targets or corrections. The key for those that like the asset is to own some, manage the position size as needed via rebalancing so that massively volatile corrections don’t bother you, and then otherwise just let it do its thing.

## Value Stock Opportunities

The year 2022 was the year of the value stock. Most growth stocks got hammered, while value stocks in aggregate held up pretty well. And thus far, 2023 has been the opposite, with most growth stocks staging a tremendous recovery, and value stocks mostly lagging.

I think 2024 could see a reversal yet again, although it’s hard to say for sure. My approach with value stocks is to lean into them on weakness, but only when they have rather future-proof businesses. In other words, I don’t particularly want to buy a melting ice-cube, but rather want to buy solid businesses that happen to be trading at cheap valuations and spinning off major cashflows. To the extent that I even come close to buying melting ice cube type businesses, it better be at like 8x earnings or less, and not as a core position. My sweet spot tends to be growth-at-a-reasonable-price (“GARP”) businesses.

My model portfolios tend to be weighted toward value/GARP, although if we include bitcoin as a growth category investment, then I have quite a bit of growth exposure, especially on a volatility-adjusted basis. Like many growth stocks, bitcoin had a rough 2022, but has outperformed most stocks in 2023 except for Nvidia (NVDA).

The rest of this section will review some value stocks that have struggled in 2023 relative to mega-cap tech stocks, but that still look like decent investments to me.

**American Express (AXP)**

Throughout this year I have been emphasizing that there is a big divergence between fiscal policy and monetary policy. In fact it’s arguably the biggest magnitude in which they have ever diverged.

Fiscal policy is extremely loose and somewhat simulative, especially toward those who are on the receiving side of the fiscal deficits. Meanwhile, monetary policy is quite tight and restrictive on sectors that are sensitive to interest rates.

American Express cardholders tend to be on the right side of this arrangement. The main demographic there is upper-middle class with a fixed-rate mortgage and a decent amount of assets. They often travel more than average. As the Fed raises interest rates, if anything their money market accounts and T-bills pay them *more* income, while their mortgage payments don’t change. Travel is booming, and in fact this past Thanksgiving period saw the highest number of daily flight passengers in history.

American Express has consolidated pricewise and fundamentally for the past 2-3 years, and I think the next few years will likely be better.

![AXP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-axp-fastgraph.png)

**Cigna (CI)**

Cigna is one of the few large and high-quality companies with a PEG ratio below 1x out there. A PEG ratio refers to the price/earnings/growth ratio. For example, a stock with a price/earnings ratio of 10x and that is growing earnings at 10% per year, has a PEG ratio of 1x which is unusually attractively-valued. In contrast, a stock with a price/earnings ratio of 20x that is growing earnings at 10% per year would have a PEG ratio of 2x, which is decent.

I personally use a dividend-adjusted PEG ratio, where I add the dividend yield to the earnings growth rate when calculating the PEG ratio.

For example, a stock with no growth that has a price/earnings ratio of 10x and that pays out all of its income as dividends would have a 10% dividend yield, which could be reinvested into more shares to grow the dividend over time and is similar to a company with no dividend that grows earnings by 10% per year. Most stocks aren’t that extreme, but for example a company with 5% growth that pays a 5% dividend yield is very different than a stock with 5% growth that doesn’t pay a dividend. The dividend-adjusted PEG ratio puts growth stocks and value stocks on an even playing field for valuation analysis.

The market likely prices Cigna this way due to regulatory risk. It’s growing, it’s cheap, and it has been performing well, but the healthcare industry is prone to regulatory changes. Given how polarized Congress currently is and likely will continue to be, I view *major* overhauls to reduce costs as being unlikely.

The most recent price hit came when it was reported that Humana (HUM) and Cigna (CI) were in talks to potentially merge. Both stocks dropped on the news.

If they merge, it would be one of the biggest healthcare sector mergers in history, and mergers of that scale in this industry face a lot of regulatory scrutiny. The advantage to merging is that it would have a lot of cost synergies. Overall, I prefer Cigna to Humana from an investor’s perspective, and all else being equal prefer that they don’t merge. Cigna is already doing well so I don’t see why they wouldn’t just keep doing what they’re doing, but then again I’m just an outside observer. The combined entity would be attractive as well. And due to this merger-agnostic view, I think the recent sell-off is a buying opportunity.

![CI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-ci-fastgraph.png)

Although they are resilient against recessions, I keep any given healthcare stock exposure at a moderate level because regulatory changes can affect them sharply. This includes Cigna.

**Chevron (CVX)**

Energy stocks in general are inexpensive. When oil soared, energy stocks didn’t soar as much, as investors priced in the idea that a lot of that surge was temporary. And as oil has recently corrected, energy stocks haven’t corrected as sharply, because the good ones are still highly profitable at current oil prices.

Some of the least expensive major oil producers, like the large European and Canadian ones, are trading at single-digit price/earnings ratios. The large American ones like Chevron tend to be trading at low double-digit price/earnings ratios, but come with historically better performance. I’m bullish on the sector globally with a multi-year view. They have good balance sheets, they’re cheap even at current oil price levels that have been in a 15-year consolidation, and the overall supply side of oil is pretty tight.

The world has now fully accounted for U.S. shale oil, unlike the 2010s decade where it was blindsided by that new source of (largely unprofitable) supply.

![CVX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-cvx-fastgraph.png)

Much of the recent declines in oil price have been a result of 1) economic weakness in Europe and China and 2) major drawdowns in inventory levels. Whenever the global economy has another re-acceleration, it should be good for oil prices and oil stocks. And inventory drawdowns have a limit on how far they can go; they’re ultimately not sustainable.

**British American Tobacco (BTI)**

Tobacco companies have been significant underperformers in 2023. This is probably the one area where I’ve been willing to invest a bit into what I consider to be a “melting ice cube”, and it’s largely for two reasons. The first is that they’re super cheap. The second is that they’re uncorrelated to most of my other assets and not really tied to the business cycle.

Their fundamentals have held up decently but their stock prices have just become cheaper and cheaper. BTI in particular has a low single-digit price/earnings ratio and a dividend yield that is nearing double digits even though its payout ratio isn’t very high. It has a 16% earnings yield compared to a 4.2% yield on the 10-year Treasury, which seems like a decent equity risk premium to me even for a sluggish company. However, due to the nature of the industry, I don’t over-commit to this type of position, and rather just keep it as a little portfolio slice.

It returns much of that capital to shareholders in the form of dividends, net debt reduction, and occasionally share repurchases.

![BTI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-bti-fastgraph.png)

BTI recently wrote down $31.5 billion in brand equity off its balance sheet as a non-cash item. However, the tobacco business is not capital intensive. These aren’t impairments of major capital assets like facilities and infrastructure. It’s just intangible value. What matters is what their forward cash flows will be, and how to price those expected cash flows.

Wall Street analysts expect substantial nominal earnings growth ahead even though the stock is currently trading under 7x earnings, although the sample size is small and analysts have a very mixed track record:

![BTI Earnings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-bti-earnings.png)

So let’s assume way more bearishness. If the company’s EPS per share remains flat forever, which means it’ll be shrinking in real inflation-adjusted terms forever, then the current price of $29/share for the ADR still can provide 10% annualized returns:

![BTI StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-bti-stockdelver.png)

*Chart via [StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/stockdelver-book/)*

More realistically, I expect income to be flat-to-up in nominal terms, but weak in real inflation-adjusted terms, as the company sends a double-digit total yield back to shareholders via the sum of dividends, net debt reduction, and/or share repurchases. The main structural risk for investors would be an accelerated drop in nominal earnings (i.e. the ice cube melts faster than expected), and so investors have to weigh the probability of that risk.

**Enterprise Products Partners LP (EPD)**

Certain energy transportation infrastructure businesses continue to be appealing.

EPD continues to be my favorite one on a risk-adjusted-return basis. And it’s an example of where the dividend-adjusted PEG ratio is worthwhile for valuation purposes rather than just the PEG ratio. With EPD, you’re likely to get around 3-5% annual earnings and distribution growth, and a distribution yield well over 7%. Meanwhile the P/E is below 11x. So the dividend-adjusted PEG ratio is around 1x, which is attractive.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/epd-fastgraph.png)

As a note, EPD is a partnership rather than a corporation, issues a K-1 during tax season, and therefore is not suitable for some foreign investors. In my opinion, this puts some mild downward valuation pressure on the business relative to some of its peers that are structured as corporations, and therefore actually makes it *more* attractive to domestic U.S. investors, because you can get similar assets for a cheaper price, and thus higher distribution yields and better compounding for long-term holders.

**Grupo Aeroportuario Del Centro Norte (OMAB)**

Mexico has three major airport companies (tickers PAC, ASR, and OMAB), that each own several airports. They are effectively monopolies and thus have wide economic moats unlike airlines, but are heavily regulated by the government. Mexico is a beneficiary of any “near-shoring” trends that may continue to occur, and Latin America in general is rather isolated from a lot of the geopolitical conflicts around the world.

I bought OMAB for the Newsletter portfolio during the depths of the crisis in March 2020, and have been holding ever since then as a modest position, which has resulted in strong gains.

In September/October 2023, the stock prices of OMAB and the two other airport companies in Mexico were slammed, because the Mexican government unilaterally changed their fee structures and other details. That’s the downside of being a regulated monopoly.

![OMAB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-omab-fastgraph.png)

Prior to this decision, OMAB was a massive outperformer vs the S&P 500 since my entry price in March 2020, but it got a bit ahead of itself. After the correction, it’s still a mild outperformer. I believe most of the information is priced in at this point, and the total forward return expectations over the next five years look decent. Perhaps more importantly, it’s a very different type of investment than many of my core sector/asset exposures, which is why I like it as a little portfolio slice.

**Itau Unibanco (ITUB)**

Brazil continues to be one of my preferred investment areas with a 5+ year view. Decades of emerging market performance have resulted in major capital flows out of them and toward the United States. As a result, U.S. growth stocks in aggregate are pretty expensive, while plenty of emerging market equities are cheap.

It only takes a small spark to start a virtuous cycle where more capital wants to flow into emerging markets, which bids up both valuations and the currency, and then can boost the economy, and then can attract more capital. That’s why emerging markets tend to have vicious bear markets and epic bull markets- there are multiple flywheels layered on top of each other.

In the meantime, Brazil’s largest bank stock continues to slowly make higher highs and higher lows in dollar terms:

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-10-itub-fastgraph.png)

Brazil is an emerging market that would benefit from a weaker dollar index and global liquidity levels, but that is solid enough in terms of its reserves/debts that it can survive a long period of stagnant liquidity conditions (in other words, the orange bar is bigger than the blue/gray bar in the chart below):

![Debt and Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-29-em-debt-and-reserves.png)

And they’re running major trade surpluses at the moment due to better commodity price conditions and other factors:

## Final Thoughts: Another Gold Update

We continue to be in a potentially pivotal time for gold, so I am giving it a concluding section in most reports recently. As it crossed $2k a while ago, I pointed to it and suggested a potential breakout to watch. Indeed, it surged over $2,150 to new all-time highs last week, but then sharply corrected back down to slightly over $2k again.

I’m very curious to see if it can hold and then shoot back up, or if it falls down into its recent 3.5 year consolidation range for yet more time. I don’t know which way it’ll break yet, but it’s one of the key assets and support levels that I’m watching. And historically, gold does well in the years after the Fed pauses its interest rate increases, so a decent number of variables are aligning in gold’s favor.
