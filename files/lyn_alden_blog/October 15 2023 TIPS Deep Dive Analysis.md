## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

The Fed continues to draw down its balance sheet (negative for liquidity) while at the same time the reverse repo facility is being drained to buy ongoing T-bill issuance (positive for liquidity), and as a result the net domestic liquidity situation has remained rather flat:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity has been flat as well. My preferred metric for this is global broad money supply denominated in dollars, which is affected by both the rate of money supply growth and the strength or weakness of the dollar relative to other currencies:

![Global M2 Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-global-m2-liquidity.png)

All else being equal, neither of these liquidity charts are particularly bullish for risk assets over the next several months. Notably, in about a month the year-over-year global M2 metric (orange line) is going to start rolling over again, because global M2 (blue line) bottomed in absolute terms in November 2022. So, the year-over-year comparisons will begin getting harder as we move past November 2023.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy has been decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, and has been in stall speed for a while. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as most of them are now except for construction spending), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms. Imports and exports are additionally negative in nominal terms. Construction spending has been getting a big fiscal boost from the Inflation Reduction Act.

![Cycle Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-cycle-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin price action has several variables that historically affect it, but based on my research there are two that stand out.

The first notable variable is the tightness of coins, which affects the available liquid supply of bitcoin when new buyers enter the market. During bull markets, long-term holders tend to be up multiples on their cost basis, and some of them begin trimming their holdings into that price strength. And then during bear markets, new buyers are few in number, fast money traders exit their positions, and long-term holders tend to coins with little intention to sell until the coins are up multiples in price again (if ever). Along with the halving of new supply coming to market every four years, these tight supply conditions act as a coiled spring, so that whenever there is an influx of new demand, the price is primed to respond strongly and begin another round of coin distribution.

This chart shows the percentage of coins that have not moved on-chain in over one year, along with the dates of the supply flow halvings for context:

![HODL Waves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-hodl-waves.png)

The second notable variable is about the other side of the equation: fresh demand or lack thereof. Tight supply doesn’t matter much for price until there is fresh demand from new buyers. Most risk assets including bitcoin are highly correlated with liquidity conditions, and bitcoin is the purest correlate to liquidity conditions among assets that I track. Specifically, the change in global broad money supply in dollar-denominated terms has been highly correlated with bitcoin price ever since bitcoin became large enough to be affected by broader macro forces such as this.

This liquidity metric is affected by both global money supply growth and the strength of the dollar relative to other currencies, and it helps to look at it in both absolute terms and year-over-year terms:

**Bitcoin vs M2 (Absolute $):**

![BTC vs M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-btc-vs-m2.png)

**Bitcoin vs M2 (YoY %):**

![BTC vs M2 YoY%](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-btc-vs-m2-yoy.png)

So far, this bear market in bitcoin has been acting much like the ones before it. Amid a period of stagnant global liquidity conditions as central banks try to rein in credit growth, bitcoin supply has been coalescing into longer-term illiquid hands.

The next several months look very uncertain in terms of liquidity, and indeed starting after November 2023 the year-over-year global liquidity figure will begin rolling over again due to higher base effects. However, when looking out further than that, bitcoin’s price seems to be well-primed for the next leg higher in money supply, especially considering that once again, the halving in 2024 will reduce the supply of new coins coming to market, and potential spot ETFs in the United States will make bitcoin more accessible to large traditional pools of capital.

**Tokenized T-bills**

I continue to have a generally critical view of the cryptocurrency industry ex-bitcoin, with an exception for stablecoins and the tokenization of real-world assets more broadly. Stablecoins, in addition to serving as a unit of account for trading in offshore cryptocurrency exchanges, serve as a way for people in inflationary environments around the world to hold dollars. They’re like offshore bank accounts for the middle class in places like Argentina, Turkey, Nigeria, Lebanon, and so forth.

With the rise in interest rates, stablecoin issuers are making a ton of money. The stablecoin tokens offer no yield, and the collateral (often T-bills now) is held by the issuer, and so they can collect billions of dollars in interest payments.

However, there has been a new class of yield-bearing stablecoins or “tokenized T-bills” entering the market. For various securities laws reasons, they are generally not available in the United States, but that’s not the best market for them anyway (Americans have plenty of ways to own T-bills). People from around the world can now hold dollars that yield 5%, fully backed by T-bills.

A key macro-scale impact from bitcoin and other cryptocurrency tech is that they poke holes through the financial borders of countries, which was a key theme of mine in [*Broken Money*](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG83QBJ6). There are approximately 160 different currencies in the world with financial firewalls around their borders, and outside of the top handful, they tend to lose value quite quickly and have little or no foreign acceptance. Many people in developing countries are stuck in a currency monopoly with money supply growing at 20% or more year after year, and without access to good domestic capital markets to invest in. Bitcoin, stablecoins, and potentially other tokenized assets like T-bills or even equities, can provide exposure to some of the better assets globally to people in these regions. Their governments might not like it, but this benefits the billions of people who live in these persistently inflationary environments by giving them more choices for saving, and more overall capital portability.

## Treasury Market Deep Dive Analysis

The long duration bond market has been absolutely killed over the past three years; it has been the worst sell-off in the history of U.S. Treasuries and in the history of many other types of bonds.

I’ve been generally bearish on bonds since 2019, and have purposely had little to no long-duration exposure in my portfolios other than for brief periods.

![Bond Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/october-2023-newsletter-bond-drawdown.png)

Back in 2019 I perceived them as being in a bubble. With $18 trillion worth of negative-yielding debt outstanding in the world, it was hard to come to any other conclusion. What I didn’t have yet was a catalyst. I was seeing how gridlocked the financial system was, and was expecting that the next recession would probably have monstrous levels of money-printing:

> I read thousands of emails from my readers, and one of the key themes I see is that people are concerned about the next stock market crash, and perhaps rightly so. By many measures we have high stock valuations in the United States after a decade-long bull market, and we have high corporate debt levels both inside the United States and globally.
>
> But one question I rarely receive is: “Are bonds safe?”
>
> From a historical perspective, the bond market is acting a lot weirder than the stock market at the current time. The stock market looks like it often does at this point in the business cycle, which is not great for the probable range of forward returns, but bonds are doing things they haven’t done *ever before in history*, which should give investors pause.
>
> […]
>
> So far, central bank tools have not been inflationary because they have primarily benefited asset prices rather than middle class consumption. They printed money, but kept the money on the central bank balance sheets by buying bonds.
>
> If central bank actions get more aggressive, combine with fiscal policies, and start targeting the middle class, they have the power to override these various deflationary forces with sheer monetary expansion. They can issue helicopter money to pay off debts, boost inflation, build infrastructure, bail out unfunded pension systems, and prop up the middle class if that’s what policymakers decide to do.
>
> I wouldn’t want to be holding a 20-year or 30-year bond at super-low fixed yields in that kind of environment. Negative yields would be even more vulnerable. Sometimes Superman goes all out, and every few decades, central banks do unusual things.
>
> -“[Are We In a bond Bubble, or is This the New Normal?](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/)” July 2019

Well when 2020 came around several months later, we indeed got that catalyst in the form of COVID-19. When we started to get the massive liquidity bazookas from both the Fed and the Treasury, that potent combination of fiscal and monetary stimulus, I kept re-iterating a pro-inflationary view. In March 2020, right when things seemed darkest, I wrote an article called “[Why This is Unlike the Great Depression](https://www.lynalden.com/great-depression/)“. It was in the midst of both the stock market and economic data looking cartoonish- things had gone absolutely straight down that month, but my view of the fiscal situation and other factors led me to believe that the 2020s would be more like the inflationary 1940s, not the deflationary 1930s.

Starting as early as August 2020 (e.g. “[Treasury Market Dissonance](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2020-newsletter/)“), I was looking at the sheer supply of Treasury issuance compared to how little the Fed was buying, and started to write about a supply problem.

There was, however, one key macro thing I got wrong about fiscal and monetary policy. The Fed has been a lot more hawkish than I had anticipated once the inflation came. And by extension, the dollar has been firmer, gold has been lackluster, banks have been weaker, and so forth.

Rather than let some of the debt burn away with negative real yields, the Fed has aggressively moved back to positive real interest rates.

The problem, however, is that this isn’t the 1970s, where public debt was low and most money creation from was from bank lending, and thus where positive real interest rates act as a powerful downward force. Instead, public debt is high, demographics are much more aged, and more money creation comes from fiscal deficits each year than from new bank loans. Higher interest rates do put downward pressure on bank lending, but they raise deficits *even more*, which is ironically stimulatory and inflationary. It’s a very mixed bag.

The U.S. is now running $1.7 trillion in deficits, of which a big chunk is interest expense. Those deficits, including the interest expense, is all someone else’s income.

![CBO Debts and Deficits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2023-7-cbo.png)

In my opinion, this is one of the most important charts out there. It’s from the Wall Street Journal and shared here by Jesse Felder. It’s consumer spending by age bracket:

![Consumer Spending](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-consumer-spending.png)

A lot of analysts have a mental model that looks like the 2005 time period, where spending by the 65+ group was low. Now, they’re the biggest spenders! This is partly because there are more of them, as our society reaches higher average ages. But it’s also because they’re primarily the ones with the capital. And their income is not from the job market; it’s from their accumulated capital and from the government in the form of Social Security and Medicare.

In general, that’s the group that’s on the right side of the fiscal deficits. The 25-34 year old group is now effectively shut out of the housing market due to high rates. But in many cases, their parents and/or their grandparents are doing just fine economically. They’ve already got their house locked in with a low fixed-rate mortgage or paid off, and they are earning more and more interest expense on their cash equivalents.

Of course, there’s still a big class divide. Many seniors are struggling financially, and many others are very wealthy. But in aggregate, that’s the group with the capital.

**Bond Math**

If the Fed merely holds rates where they are, then more and more of the existing $33.5 trillion in debt will be refinanced at these high current rates. In other words, if all of the debt was yielding 5% on average, the total interest expense would be ~$1.7 trillion, rather than the ~$900 billion today. It would take time to get there, but that’s the direction. The average duration for U.S. public debt is around 6 years, but a lot of it is front-loaded.

But then we also have to consider upwards of $1.7 trillion in new debt per year. If we add $1.7 trillion to the debt for the next four years, that’ll be upwards of $40 trillion in debt. If that yields an average of 5%, that’s $2 trillion in interest expense.

If we encounter a recession next year, interest rates may go down, but total deficits generally rise in a recession due to weaker tax receipts.

**Treasury Inflation Protected Securities**

Long-duration bonds might have a tradeable bottom around these levels, but I still don’t love the risk/reward for them. The yield curve is still inverted, after all. I continue to like T-bills, and notes up to around 2 years or so.

When I look at the broad equity market, I’m not particularly confident that it will produce 5%+ nominal returns per year over the next couple years. Maybe it will, but it’ll have more volatility along the way. Cash equivalents continue to make a lot of sense to hold, especially for conservative investors.

I’m starting to like Treasury Inflation Protected Securities here. I’ve owned the short-duration ones for a while (via the STIP ETF, which averages 2.5 years in duration), but the intermediate duration ones are starting to get interesting after their sell-off.

The iShares TIPS Bond ETF (ticker: TIP) has an average duration of about 7 years. You can also buy TIPS directly from the Treasury.

The way that TIPS work is that their principal and interest adjust with CPI. So, you get a lower yield, but then unlike a normal bond, as the CPI goes up, the bond value goes up, and the interest you receive is adjusted higher based on that increase in value. TIPS are sold in 5-year, 10-year, and 30-year durations, and the various ETFs will hold some weighting of them.

Specifically, an investor can get a 2.36% real yield on 10-year TIPS, and 2.46% real yield on 5-year or 30-year TIPS.

![TIPS Real Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-10-15-tips-real-yield.png)

Currently, nominal 10-year Treasury notes yield 4.70%. This is 2.34% higher than the TIPS yield (2.36%). That’s the spread, which is also known as the “breakeven” rate.

Roughly speaking, if inflation is less than 2.34% per year on average over the next ten years, then it will turn out to have been better to buy a 10-year nominal note at 4.70% and hold it to maturity. On the other hand, if inflation is more than 2.34% per year on average over the next ten years, then TIPS will have been the better bet.

I see more upside risks to inflation than downside risks to inflation, especially when looking at the combination of large structural U.S. fiscal deficits and global energy supply constraints in a world of questionable geopolitical security, to put it lightly.

In other words, if I’m wrong then maybe inflation averages 1.6% for the next ten years. However, it could average 4%, 5%, or more. The spectrum of outcomes has more upside deviation than downside deviation.

Overall, I’m interested in 5-year and 10-year TIPS at current real yields. They could go lower in price (i.e. higher yields) during the next 8 months or so as the Fed keeps tightening its balance sheet, but an investor could layer into them in tranches if that ends up being the case. The real yields are already back up near the higher end of their historical range.

**TIPS Risks**

TIPS have three main risks.

The first risk is that real yields could keep heading higher for a while, even though they are already higher than average. If this is the case, TIPS prices will weaken for a time. That’s what happened over the past few years as they went from negative real yields to high positive real yields. But the overall risk/reward is getting less and less from these higher real yield levels.

The second risk is that inflation can undershoot the breakeven rate during the duration of the holding. That’s not the end of the world; an investor still gets a positive real yield on their money, but will mildly underperform nominal bonds in that case. At least they’ll have had more peace of mind along the way. Also, it should be noted that TIPS adjust up or down with CPI inflation or deflation, but when they mature they never pay out less principal than was put in. In other words, there’s a deflation floor for them but there’s no inflation ceiling, which is good.

The third risk is that the CPI can fail to measure inflation accurately. Your actual expenses could increase faster on average than CPI says they are, and thus your TIPS income might not fully keep up with “real” inflation. This is indeed a concern, but at these positive real yields there is a buffer against that scenario, and TIPS are best when combined with other assets anyway.

Specifically, as I wrote about in [*Broken Money*](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG83QBJ6) and elsewhere, inflation is a spectrum.

-Over the long term, U.S. broad money supply has grown by about 7% per year. Truly scarce things like fine art and waterfront property tend to appreciate on average in line with broad money supply.

-Down from there, energy intensive and labor intensive things like median houses, gold, oil, hospital services, beef, and so forth tend to increase in price at a rate that is higher than CPI but lower than broad money growth. We get moderately more efficient at making them over time.

-Down from there, some things we get way better at making like electronics, software, industrial products, textiles, grains, and so forth. This is from a combination of technological advancement and offshoring. This category drives down the overall CPI basket.

And I expect this trend to continue. Advancements in AI are likely to drive down prices of a lot of “white collar” stuff thanks to major productivity boosts, while I think “blue collar” stuff like energy, materials, hands-on labor, infrastructure, and those areas will be more inflationary than they were in the 2010s decade.

Overall, while I do think CPI understates actual inflation, the fact that you get a decent positive real yield on top of CPI gives you a decent buffer. In my view, TIPS are best paired with some gold or bitcoin or energy producer stocks which can benefit from scenarios where money supply is growing a lot faster than reported CPI. Right now, money supply is growing very slowly and so the positive real yields of TIPS are particularly compelling.

## Final Thoughts: Energy Markets and Global Instability

The terrorist attacks by Hamas on Israel have sparked Israel to officially declare war for the first time since 1973. Israel has performed a significant number of military operations between 1973 and the present, but this was the first time it became severe enough for Israel to officially declare outright war. Presumably, more Palestinians will die in this action than in prior military operations, and so far the public numbers are pointing in that direction.

There are no distant armchair solutions for this. Violence and geopolitical tensions in the Middle East have existed for a long time. Hamas has committed atrocities, but Hamas is not the same as the Palestinian people in aggregate. Israel is striking Gaza hard in retribution against Hamas, resulting in the deaths of many civilian Palestinians. This is a complex, humanitarian, geopolitical situation with no easy answers, and it has been that way for a lot longer than my lifetime. Disputes in the Middle East are the quintessential pop culture example of unworkable problems.

My heart goes out to all civilians involved, and the best I can do is analyze it from my lane in a global macro perspective. And much of my family is just 250 miles away from Gaza in Cairo. Egypt has a severe domestic financial crisis that they’re focused on trying to handle, and so the country is vulnerable to regional military conflicts and refugee crises.

Notably, the attacks by Hamas were on the 50th anniversary of the 1973 Yom Kippur war (rounded to the nearest holiday, several hours later in the morning of October 7th rather than October 6th, for strategic advantage). During that prior war, the United States supported Israel, and a number of OPEC nations embargoed the United States for that involvement, which contributed to the high inflation and oil shortages in the country during that time.

This is an important regional conflict, and along with Ukraine it represents a second warfront that the United States is involved with financially and strategically while it has significant fiscal headwinds domestically.

For it to escalate into a global conflict, more players would need to be involved. Iran is a significant oil producer in a globally tight oil market, and some U.S. senators like Lindsey Graham have publicly put striking that infrastructure on the table, although that appears to be the minority view at this time. Oil prices have been catching a bit of a war premium, because all else being equal, the probability of intermediate-term oil supply disruptions now is higher than that probability was before the attacks.

**BRICS+ Macro Notes**

The core BRICs nations consist of Brazil, Russia, India, China, and South Africa. Among those, China and India have already generally been on unfriendly terms due to various borders disputes.

In January 2024, Saudi Arabia, Iran, the United Arab Emirates, Egypt, Ethiopia, and Argentina are set to join BRICS+. The group is attempting to grow their non-Western trade framework. But at the same time, having a larger number of members opens the group up to a whole new level of potential disunity.

-Although both of them have human rights issues in their own countries, Egyptian and Saudi Arabian leadership mainly want peace in the region for their own pragmatic purposes. Egypt has a domestic financial crisis and is focused on boosting tourism and business development. Saudi Arabia is focused on boosting business development and has been working with the U.S. and Israel on a multilateral deal to normalize relations with Israel. Tourism and business deals have been picking up between Israel and surrounding Arab countries in recent years. Meanwhile, Iranian leadership is generally more supportive of disrupting Israel and supporting its adversaries, which puts them at strategic odds with other BRICS+ nations.

-Terrorists of multiple groups do this for the sake of geopolitical chaos. They know more Palestinians will die than Israelis, but they do what they do to reduce the chances of political normalization and to stir up another generation of hate. In their minds, normalized relations between Saudi Arabia, the UAE, and Israel, are a victory for Israel. That scenario would benefit the status quo that they don’t like. So, they resort to asymmetric attacks to try to stir that up for another generation.

-These actions, therefore, put Saudi Arabia in particular in a tough spot in terms of optics regarding its ongoing geopolitical negotiations as this war unfolds. In some ways, they have been courting both BRICS+ and the Western alliance, and this can lead them to pause things toward the West.

-Egypt and Ethiopia have an ongoing dispute over Ethiopia’s Grand Ethiopian Renaissance Dam on the Nile. They’re both joining BRICS+ with an existing decade-long dispute.

-With 140% current official year-over-year inflation, Argentina might have political outsider Jaiver Milei as its president before January 2024, which shakes things up. Milei has already indicated a preference toward alliance with the United States and Israel, rather than toward BRICS+. However, he has indicated that it would be up to the private sector to perform trade deals.

I have no firm view on what’s going to happen in the Middle East, but with energy supplies as tight as they are, I continue to have a long bias on energy. Oil and gas producers in general have strong balance sheets, low valuations, and plenty of profitability. Oil between $70 and $90 is quite profitable for them. If oil breaks out due to the next round of global money supply growth or due to supply disruptions, they would have extra upside. And at some point during the remainder of this decade, I expect that to likely occur.

Therefore, oil and gas producers are long plays on energy instability and potential shortages, with a positive carry along the way. I continue to view them as a useful part of a diversified portfolio, because spikes in energy represent a key risk to many other assets that benefit from disinflation and order.
