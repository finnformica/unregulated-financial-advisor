# Cash and Energy Update

*September 17, 2023*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The digital asset section discusses recent industry news relating to bitcoin custody.

The investment focus section covers cash equivalents and energy producers.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity indicators continue to be relatively flat. The U.S. stock market has broken out of its correlation with liquidity, but I continue to view significant headwinds against strong equity performance over the next six months:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity, measured below as the dollar-denominated value of the combined broad money supply of the top five currency blocs, has been stagnating in recent months and is back down near the bottom of its decade trend:

![Global M2 Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-global-m2-liquidity.png)

The dollar index has been practically straight up for two months since mid-July, and is back over 105. A rising dollar is generally negative for liquidity, all else being equal, because the dollar is the unit of account for the largest portion of global debt:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-dxy.png)

The consumer price index continues to rise, and in year-over-year % change terms has bounced off of 3%. In absolute terms, it’s up 22% over the past five years:

![CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-cpi.png)

Core CPI (which excludes volatile food and energy) is still falling, but remains over 4%.

Notably, U.S. diesel prices are up 20% since mid-July 2023. Diesel prices are still below their mid-2022 highs, but the trend is back toward energy price strength across the board. Diesel prices are a key input to transport goods across the country, and if they remain elevated, they are likely to eventually push into broader prices. This is likely to keep the Federal Reserve relatively tight on monetary policy, for as long as they can be.

Roughly speaking, I think the Fed’s reverse repo facility is a good clock to watch for how long the Fed can remain tight regarding the monetary base. The reverse repo facility is a stored up “battery” of liquidity that was filled up after the 2020/2021 massive stimulus efforts, and is now being sharply drained by record amounts of T-bill issuance. As this number falls, it pushes liquidity back into the broader financial system, which partially offsets the Fed’s ongoing quantitative tightening:

![Fed Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-rrp.png)

The facility had about $2.2 trillion in spring 2023 and is now down to just over $1.4 trillion. That’s about a $800 billion drain in five or six months. Give it another year, and this facility could be drained.

Back in spring 2023, small and medium banks in aggregate ran into very low cash levels as a % of assets (under 6%). With the Fed’s various loan facilities and the draining of the reverse repo facility since then, the number has been steady at around 7%:

![Small Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-small-bank-cash.png)

Overall, liquidity is “workable” at the moment, but is stagnant. And interest rates are high, with some parts of inflation cooling off, and other parts re-accelerating. If it continues in this fashion over the next 6+ months, then it’s likely to put downward pressure on most risk assets, or at least put them back into a sideways choppy range rather than a strong up-only bull market.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator continues to be unusually strong for the quarter:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-gdpnow.png)

I expect to sound like a broken record by saying this for the rest of the year, but we’re currently in a tale of two economies.

-With ongoing quantitative tightening and high interest rates, the Fed is putting downward pressure on interest-rate sensitive entities such as commercial real estate owners that have to refinance short duration debt, junk-rated companies that have to refinance short duration debt, small businesses that have to refinance short duration bank loans, developing countries that need to refinance net debt balances, and so forth.

-Meanwhile, entities such as households with low fixed-rate long-duration mortgages, large investment-grade corporations with low fixed-rate long-duration corporate bonds, and/or entities that are on the receiving end of the U.S. Treasury’s $2 trillion annualized fiscal deficits, are generally doing pretty well. I continue to have a constructive view toward the energy sector, the travel sector, and most businesses that cater to upper middle class senior citizens in general.

Basically, the Fed’s inflation-fighting tools are geared toward squeezing the weaker segments of the economy, including low/middle income households, insecure companies, and insecure nations. I expect pain to continue in those areas for a while.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin’s supply conditions are a coiled spring, but I don’t expect much action out of that spring until global liquidity conditions improve. As long as the USD is strengthening, domestic liquidity is trending sideways, and global M2 is chopping along near the bottom range of its trend, it’s important to have sober expectations about bitcoin’s price.

I do, however, expect a significant bull market to occur in the next upward liquidity cycle, which will probably occur in 2024 or 2025 (which of those, I’m not sure yet, but will continue to monitor signs for). An approval of a U.S. spot bitcoin ETF could accelerate that timeline.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

I continue to view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the best way to acquire bitcoin for most people that want some as part of their asset mix.

**Bitcoin Custody News**

One of the biggest issues in the bitcoin industry and indeed the broad crypto industry has been safe custody. Institutions have been able to access great custody, but for retail investors, the custody environment has been filled with some landmines. Exchanges have failed, custodians have lost regulatory access to certain U.S. states, and so forth.

Within the bitcoin-focused industry, a big question has been one of vertical custody integration vs custody separation.

-Some companies such as [River](https://river.com/) and [Cash App](https://cash.app/bitcoin) have focused on vertical integration, where they onboard bitcoin-buying customers and then also custody their bitcoin until the customer wants to withdraw.

-Other companies such as [Swan](https://www.swanbitcoin.com/) have focused on custody separation, where they maintain separation between the custodian and the rest of the company (especially for advisor or IRA products, but also for other products), and focus on working with trust companies that provide bankruptcy-remoteness to the customer (i.e. the bitcoin is held directly in their name rather than being a pooled IOU liability of a company).

-A third category of company, like [Bull Bitcoin](https://www.bullbitcoin.com/), avoids custody all together and only lets the user buy bitcoin and bring it to self-custody, although that approach necessarily filters for more advanced users that are familiar with generating and maintaining their own bitcoin wallet prior to purchase.

In U.S. securities markets and indeed many other securities markets, separation of custody has long been a regulatory matter. This is because vertical integration of financial companies allows for more fraud and rehypothecation. Separation of brokers, exchanges, custodians, and so forth allows for more transparency to reduce the likelihood of self-dealing and fraud. In financial markets, some types of vertical integration can be dangerous, and are thus disallowed by regulators.

Swan from the beginning chose to go with the model that is common in U.S. securities markets: separation of the broker side of the business and related services from the custodian, along with an emphasis on bankruptcy-remoteness in the form of a trust company rather than a pooled IOU liability of Swan itself. Even though bitcoin itself is not a security, some products built on top of bitcoin can be treated like securities, and some pools of capital have to interact with this type of separation of duties for regulatory purposes. The downside of that approach is that in this rather new industry, the number of options for external qualified custodians is not very big. Swan ended up having to switch custodians earlier this year due to that limitation.

In what I view as great news for the industry, Swan and Bitgo announced last week a plan to build a bitcoin-only qualified custodian, subject to regulatory approval. In other words, Swan plans to maintain their emphasis on custodial separation, while also taking more control of the process by having a partial stake in building one rather than relying on the rest of the industry to offer a decent one:

> **BitGo and Swan Announce Plans for USA’s First Bitcoin-Only Trust Company**
>
> BitGo and Swan Bitcoin today announced plans to create the first Bitcoin-only trust company, pending regulatory approval. The new joint venture will combine BitGo’s best-in-class Bitcoin cold storage technology and regulatory compliance expertise with Swan’s industry-leading capabilities in onboarding, fraud prevention, AML/KYC, and dedicated Bitcoin experts.
>
> BitGo CEO Mike Belshe said, “We believe the best model for the Bitcoin industry is the same battle-tested model that has been part of the US financial industry for over a century: the separation of exchange and custody. Our teams have worked closely together for nearly a year on stronger qualified custody models. Early in 2023, we recognized the opportunity to establish a Bitcoin-only custodian, combining the unique capabilities of each company and supporting the innovators that will be at the forefront of pushing Bitcoin adoption.”
>
> –[September 15th press release](https://www.prweb.com/releases/bitgo-and-swan-announce-plans-for-usas-first-bitcoin-only-trust-company-301928557.html)

In addition, Swan is going to [start offering collaborative custody](https://www.swanbitcoin.com/vault/) later this year. With this type of offering, which is currently available from companies like Casa, Unchained Capital, and Nunchuk, they will help customers set up a multi-signature vault. For example, they can set up a 2-of-3 bitcoin address, where the customer holds two keys and Swan’s custody partner holds the third key. Any two keys are needed to control the funds. This means that the customer has control over the funds, but Swan is there to assist with any problems (such as lost keys) that may arise. This is an option exclusive for digital assets that is not possible with assets like cash and gold. It serves as a method for protecting large amounts of bitcoin without strictly relying on custodians, makes it harder for the customer to accidentally lose funds via technical mistakes, and enables various types of inheritance planning.

## Investment Focus: Cash-Equivalents and Energy Prices

My portfolios have four main types of investments: 1) growth-at-a-reasonable-price stocks, 2) dividend-paying value stocks, 3) energy/commodity producers and scarce money allocations, and 4) cash-equivalents.

This section provides an update on the third and fourth types of investments, and shows how from a macro perspective they can also hurt liquidity and other types of investments when they perform well.

**The Status of Cash-Equivalents**

T-bills, money market funds, and FDIC-insured savings deposits (at smaller banks that pay some actual yield), are among the most attractive investments from a risk/reward perspective at this current time. The relatively high interest rates are the obvious reason, but I think it’s worth taking a few paragraphs to dissect specifically why that’s the case.

In modern times, cash-equivalent savers have mostly been the “suckers at the card table”, constantly getting diluted. A lot of people compare interest rates to CPI inflation, but it’s worse than that. The supply of money and the prices of scarcer financial assets like real estate and high-quality equities grow *faster* than CPI, while CPI in aggregate is weighed down by all sorts of technologically-deflationary things like electronics, software, and offshoring.

To see this, we can compare interest rates to money supply growth. The following chart shows the broad money supply in blue, the year-over-year % change in money supply in red, and short-term interest rates in green:

![M2 Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-m2-growth.png)

-In the 1980s and 1990s, interest rates generally kept up with money supply growth, meaning the green line was roughly as high as the red line on average.

-In the 2000s and then especially in the 2010s (and during the pandemic stimulus in the early 2020s), interest rates fell far short of money supply growth, meaning the green line spent most of its time way below the red line.

If interest rates are significantly below the rate of money supply growth, then holders of money in its various forms are being diluted. The total “pie” of money is growing faster than their share, so their percentage of the pie keeps shrinking. Meanwhile, that expansion of the money supply raises the price of scarce, energy-intensive, or labor-intensive goods and services at a faster average rate than CPI growth. So, house prices, stock prices, beef prices, medical insurance prices, college tuition prices, and so forth are appreciating in price much more quickly than the value of cash and cash-equivalent balances.

On the other side of that, people with long-duration loans or bonds at low fixed rates are benefitting from this arrangement. They are *shorting* the ever-diluting money pie, and using it to buy scarcer collateral like real estate and business equity. For decades, they’ve been the sharks at the card table, while savers have been the suckers at the card table. Wealth in various forms flows from the suckers to the sharks. And it’s a rather opaque process because it’s hard for people to see how quickly their savings are being diluted, and when they venture into more volatile assets as novice investors they often make bad decisions when prices move against them, such as selling at the bottom.

From spring 2022 to spring 2023, the broad money supply was decreasing, as the Fed aggressively tried to slow down price inflation. After the banking crisis in spring 2023, money supply has been basically flat. Meanwhile, holders of cash equivalents are getting an interest rate that is *higher* than this near-zero rate of money supply growth. If we’re being more complete about it, we should probably add money market balances and T-bills to the money supply total as other forms of cash-equivalents, but even then the results are pretty decent: holders of cash-equivalents are actually doing okay right now. They’re not really being diluted at the moment.

This following chart shows short term interest rates minus the annual growth rate of the broad money supply. When it’s green, cash-equivalent savers are generally keeping up with supply inflation, and not being diluted. When it’s red, cash-equivalent savers are not being compensated with much interest rates relative to the rate of money supply growth, and are thus being diluted toward a smaller and smaller share of a growing monetary pie:

![M2 Cash Dilution](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-m2-cash-dilution.png)

Right now, being a saver in high-quality cash-equivalents isn’t so bad. You’re gaining interest at a faster rate than money is being created.

Given how high public debts and deficits are, I don’t expect this period of time to continue indefinitely. However, as long as the Fed is actively reducing its balance sheet and holding rates high, the risk/reward remains rather decent for cash-equivalents in general, especially when paired with some inflation hedges as a barbell approach. When we start to see a rapid change in this trend, such as during the repo rate spike of September 2019 (which ended the Fed’s balance sheet reduction last time), then it’ll be time to consider reducing cash balances toward other assets.

**The Status of Energy Markets**

From 2008 to 2020, oil was in a prolonged bear market, with a series of lower highs and lower lows.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-wti-crude.png)

The rise of U.S. shale oil added to the supply side, while a strong dollar put a damper on dollar-indebted emerging market growth rates, which curtailed their growth of oil demand. However, as I’ve been covering for a few years, I believe that the excess supply has largely been worked off, and demand has steadily caught up. I think the oil price bottom of spring 2023 was likely a major higher-low.

Now, U.S. oil inventories are at multi-decade lows, whether measured in absolute amounts or in days-of-consumption:

![Crude Oil Inventory](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-crude-oil-inventory.png)

Commodity bull/bear markets are caused by a few factors.

-A big factor is the long capex cycle. It takes years and a lot of capital to bring big new projects online. Usually a lot of new supply comes online when prices are high, which helps bring prices down, but then they are stuck in that oversupply low-price state for a while (which is good for consumers but bad for producers). However, demand keeps inching up, and excess supply gets worked off, especially since low prices disincentivize new projects. The conditions become fertile for the next bull run and for the cycle to repeat anew.

-Another factor is the global dollar cycle or liquidity cycle. For example, strong dollar policy in the 1980s crushed Latin American economies, that in general were very dollar-indebted, which curtailed their oil demand and allowed the U.S. to keep oil prices down for itself. Major bear markets in energy and commodities tend to coincide with some major region somewhere suffering economic stagnation. In the 1920s it was largely Europe that suffered. In the 1950s it was largely China that suffered. In the 1980s it was largely Latin America that suffered. In the 2010s most of the world experienced slow growth, with certain parts of Latin America, Africa, and the Middle East stagnating the most.

![Incrementum Commodity Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-longterm-commodities.png)

*Chart Source: [2023 Incrementum IGWT Report](https://ingoldwetrust.report/)*

High energy and commodity prices generally coincide with significant currency devaluation and dollar bear markets in particular, but detract from abundance elsewhere. The 1910s, 1940s and the 1970s were characterized by scarcity in many aspects (either war or stagflation). The 2000s fared better, in large part because rapid globalization helped offset a lot of inflation pressures.

This chart is [from 2021](https://www.lynalden.com/inflation/) and shows the 5-year rolling price change in oil relative to CPI captures that pretty well:

![Oil vs CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-oil-vs-cpi.png)

This chart was from before CPI inflation took off in late 2021 and throughout 2022, and before oil spiked amid the Russian invasion of Ukraine in 2022. As it shows, rapid energy price increases and rapid CPI increases are usually the same thing. The 2000s decade was the main exception, as energy prices soared and CPI remained muted, since that inflation was offset and exported elsewhere via rapid globalization.

I continue to view the 2020s/2030s as the fifth major commodity bull market of the modern (post-telegraph) era. The timing will depend on numerous factors, especially fiscal and monetary policy of the largest countries, but I think the low is already in. My main question now is whether we chop along sideways for a while and then take off, or take off more quickly. Either way, if I can buy inexpensive profitable energy companies with strong balance sheets that pay dividends, I’m happy to buy and hold.

A lot of energy stock analysts assume mean reversion in oil prices. In other words, rather than try to predict oil prices, many of them assume a baseline low-ish oil price, and evaluate the company with that assumption. And so we get F.A.S.T. Graphs that look like this, with relatively muted earnings expectations for the next couple years amid assumptions of medium-priced oil:

![CVX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-cvx-fastgraph.png)

Putting aside the earnings spike of 2022, the energy supermajors can be bought for a *forward* price/earnings ratio of 7x for the European ones (e.g. BP and Total), or 13x for the United States ones (e.g. Exxon and Chevron). And that assumes no major increase in the price of oil. In general they have strong balance sheets, and some of them are earning more interest on their cash-equivalents than they are paying on their long-term fixed-rate debt.

So, the top tier energy supermajors are sitting there with fortress balance sheets, directing incoming free cash flow towards dividends and share buybacks, and deploying capex carefully. They are basically massive options with positive carry for the next oil bull market.

**Cash and Energy Combined**

The Fed and many other major central banks continue to tighten monetary policy or hold it at a relatively tight level. The Fed in particular continues to decrease its balance sheet, which has been somewhat offset by Treasury liquidity injections and a partial drawdown in the reverse repo facility.

And yet, despite that tightening effect, energy prices have found a bottom and have begun pushing back up. The supply side is tight, Saudi Arabia’s supply cuts are making it tighter, and low oil inventories don’t give the United States as much flexibility to manage it anymore. China has some inventory that they could sell, but it’s unclear whether they will. Tight liquidity and a strong dollar, combined with an uptick in energy prices, is a pretty toxic combination for many economies and asset prices while it lasts. That’s why cash and energy/commodity exposure can serve as a good form of diversification for an equities portfolio in many contexts.

And ironically, tighter monetary policy constrains energy production. U.S. oil rig counts continue to fall:

![U.S. Crude Market](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-us-crude-market.png)

The absolute level of rig counts on that chart is not useful information, because technology and well types change over time. As you can see, rig counts are structurally down while oil production is structurally up during the course of the decade. However, the rate of change within a year or two *does* matter, and after a lag starts to affect oil production.

Many investors assume that if the United States has a recession, then the price of oil will crash. However, a lot of that is from 2008 recency bias; oil usually doesn’t crash in recessions, but rather tends to chop around in them. Oil tends to crash in major recessions when there was a big run up in the price and supply in the years preceding that recession, which is what occurred for 2008.

While I don’t have any particular conviction about oil prices over a multi-month period, I continue to have a bullish long-term view. And in particular, the combination of tight monetary conditions and tight energy conditions can put downward pressure on any emerging market that imports oil and has a lot of dollar-denominated debt, and can put downward pressure on any expensive stock that doesn’t have strong growth to back up its valuation.

For this reason, I like both cash-equivalents and energy exposure as a meaningful part of my portfolios at this time.

## Final Thoughts: China’s Auto Exports

A recurring theme I have been focusing on in a number of reports this year is China’s sharp rise in auto exports, which I view as not being covered enough by Western financial media.

China is now the largest auto exporter in the world, and with a strong upward trend:

![China Auto Exports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-car-exports.png)

Here in Cairo, I see quite a number of Chinese cars mixed in with European models, Korean models, Japanese models, some U.S. models, and so forth. This is the type of region where Chinese cars are growing in market share a lot.

While China’s domestic economy continues to suffer a period of stagnation, their exports in general continue to be very strong, with their autos being the crown achievement over the past few years. Since 2021 they have more than 3x’d their auto exports, on the way to 4x.

Patrick Zweifel, the chief economist at Pictet Asset Management, consistently publishes some of the best Chinese economic charts out there. Chinese data is hard to gather, and often is displayed by either China perma-bulls or China perma-bears, whereas the Pictet group has been consistently objective and data-oriented. His [latest thread](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel/status/1702599296921502015) is great (these are the first two of eight parts):

![Zweifel Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-9-17-zweifel-tweets.png)

China’s industrial production continues to be strong, but their domestic consumption is weak (but potentially stabilizing). The weakest part of the Chinese economy is their real estate sector, as demonstrated by [these charts](https://twitter.com/PkZweifel/status/1702599309961654568).

On the other side of China’s strong industrial output, Germany’s industrial output is weakening. Their auto exports are stagnant, and due to last year’s energy crisis, they have had some flight of energy-intensive industries such as chemical manufacturing and so forth. Recent data show that they have the highest rate of construction project cancellations in at least three decades. Germany is the industrial powerhouse of the Eurozone, so as long as this continues, it can put downward pressure on the economic situation across Europe.
