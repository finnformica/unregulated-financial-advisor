## Macro View

In my prior two reports over the past month, I showed that the number of global virus cases was bottoming and turning up again. This trend has continued and accelerated:

![Global COVID-19](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-global-covid.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

Germany for example has their highest number of new daily case counts ever. Fortunately, deaths are pretty low:

![Germany COVID-19](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-germany-covid.png)

*Chart Source: Worldometer*

In the US, Vermont is around the top of the list of states in terms of having the highest vaccination rate in the country, and yet they are currently experiencing record daily new cases compared to prior waves they encountered. These big waves can strike almost anywhere.

The good news is that vaccines do seem to have persistently lowered the death rate, along with natural immunity as more people get it and recover over time. The case count is decoupling with the death count in more countries as treatments improve and as more people have antibodies. The bad news is that vaccines don’t seem to be limiting transmission of cases too much.

There is also breaking news over the past few days of a new variant out of South Africa that they are calling Omicron. After they announced it, multiple countries immediately shut travel down from South Africa and other nations in the region, but instances of the variant have already shown up on multiple continents anyway. Dr. Angelique Coetzee of the South Africa Medical Association has stated that the international response is an overreaction and that even though the variant appears to transmit quickly, the cases so far appear to be mild. Time will tell.

This headline of a new variant served as a significant catalyst for the risk-off event on Friday, and we’ll see to what extent it spreads globally and if it changes anything noticeably about the transmissibility or severity of the virus.

**Risk of Policy Error and Public Polarization**

A number of countries’ policymakers continue to escalate their approaches on domestic restrictions for unvaccinated people, but 18 months into the pandemic the logic for that is weakening.

If the data showed that only places with low vaccine rates were having big breakouts of the virus and that vaccinated people can’t transmit it, then this would be an eradicable disease and the trade-off would be between bodily autonomy and public safety (e.g. someone deciding to get the vaccine or not would notably affect the death rate of other people, rather than mostly just themselves). Different countries would come to different balancing points on that topic based on their cultures. The overall death rate of the virus is pretty low in the grand scheme of things compared to other diseases that exist.

However, COVID-19 vaccine effectiveness wears off over time, and the vaccines aren’t doing a great job of preventing record case counts even in highly-vaccinated regions. Vaccinated people can still transmit the virus. Shortly after the vaccination, evidence suggests that they transmit at a reduced rate, but that over time their rate of transmissibility increases again. This virus keeps coming in seasonal waves and keeps mutating.

> The study shows that people who become infected with the Delta variant are less likely to pass the virus to their close contacts if they have already had a COVID-19 vaccine than if they haven’t. But that protective effect is relatively small, and dwindles alarmingly at three months after the receipt of the second shot.
>
> –[Nature, October 2021](https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-021-02689-y)

That’s why some people said that vaccines for coronaviruses weren’t possible last year, and that’s why we don’t have vaccines for the common cold. The anti-coronavirus vaccines that people developed don’t have the same level of effectiveness as the types of vaccines that people have developed for other types of viruses that children routinely get, which actually do permanently or near-permanently prevent recipients from getting those viruses, which makes those viruses eradicable or nearly so.

Instead, the vaccines for COVID-19 mainly seem to reduce someone’s *own rate* of hospitalization and death, especially if they are elderly or have preexisting conditions, and then moderately reduce transmissibility for a period of time. It’s mostly a personal decision if that’s the case, and people can sort through the research, try to avoid misinformation, and determine with their doctor if it’s right for them. A lot of people, especially those who have no preexisting conditions, simply don’t want the vaccines, especially on a repeated basis.

One would think that effective policy, then, would entail making sure there are resources available so that people who do want vaccines can get them easily for free like we do for the flu, and then getting vaccines distributed to developing countries that don’t have as much access to them, so that people who do want vaccines in those countries can get them too. There is a difference between being in favor of a vaccine vs being in favor of vaccine mandates, and the latter doesn’t seem to make much sense given the data. If there is a sizable minority of the population that simply doesn’t want the vaccines for one reason or another, and the vaccines are only so-so effective when it comes to reducing transmissibility anyway and are generally impermanent, then the social cost to enforce it can override the social benefit.

Trying to segment the population and delineate rights to citizens based on vaccine status (including the ability to be employed, the ability to move freely within a country, and/or the ability to participate in several types of commerce) when big case count breakouts can happen even in highly-vaccinated states and countries anyway, seems challenging to defend as a policy choice. There are protests against this on one hand, and supporters of the policies on the other hand, and basically a lot of public polarization around the topic that doesn’t necessarily have to be there.

The rapidly-mutating coronavirus is endemic at this point, so along with the case breakouts even in highly-vaccinated areas, from a macro investment standpoint I would be inclined to lower my economic expectations of countries that seem intent to stay on that sort of policy path.

Fortunately, better treatments keep coming out over time (both Merck and Pfizer have pills in the testing phase that show decent results for reducing the hospitalization rate if you take it after you show initial symptoms), so I’m optimistic that people will be able to adapt to this virus as we adapt to other diseases, and try to mitigate them where possible.

**The US Business Cycle**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 and the early months of Q4 have shown decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation even as year-ago base effects wore off:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

Overall, Q3 was stagflationary in rate-of-change terms, meaning that economic growth decelerated while inflation continued to run hot. Q4 looks as though it will have higher inflation and growth, although the Omicron variant and government reactions to it represent a new wildcard for the last month of the quarter and into Q1 of 2022.

**Fed Policy Update**

Earlier this month, the US Federal Reserve began a plan of reducing its asset purchases at a rate of $15 billion per month, from a starting point of $120 billion per month. That would eventually put the Fed’s balance sheet flat at around $9 trillion by summer 2022. By the autumn of 2022, there could be some initial interest rate hikes to try to lift rates off of zero.

Some of the recent inflation numbers and jobless claim numbers, however, have led a few Fed officials to publicly state that they should increase the asset purchase tapering speed to $30 billion per month. That would put the balance sheet flat by spring 2022 at around $8.8 trillion.

Indeed, when reverse repo activity shot up during the prior round of QE back in 2014, that eventually became the sign for it to end. It’s a sign of excess reserves and collateral shortages:

![Reverse Repo Activity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-rrp.png)

*Source: St. Louis Fed*

There are some analysts that say the Fed can never tighten policy again, and there are other analysts that say this can be like a normal tightening cycle where the Fed can reduce the balance sheet, raise interest rates, and completely normalize their policy.

I disagree with both extremes and fall in the middle. I do think the Fed is likely to reduce its rate of asset purchases, and we’ll see if they get some 0.25% rate hikes in. The question then becomes how long they will be able to tighten for, and how loose will they be compared to the prevailing inflation rate (e.g. even if you raise rates to 1%, that’s not very comforting when inflation is 6%).

The caveat to that is if the latest virus variant leads multiple governments into another round of economic lockdowns, it’s possible that the Fed could delay further tapering. That’s not my working assumption at the moment but it’s possible. At the end of the day, they will make sure the Treasury market remains liquid enough, and that overrides other plans they have.

Initial jobless claims came in at 199,000 for the past month, which is the lowest reading since 1969, indicating a very tight labor market. In a normal cycle, that would be a signal to the Fed to be focusing heavily on controlling the inflation side of things, rather than the employment side of things.

At the same time, however, the labor participation rate is stagnant at 61%. It has not improved since summer 2020. Some of that is related to demographics, but then again, the US currently now has a labor force participation rate slightly *below* that of Japan, despite Japan having a median age that is 10 years older.

In other words in the US, a historically low percentage of the population is working, and yet also a historically low percentage of the population is looking for a job. There are more job openings than unemployed people. This seems to be partly from a skills mismatch, partly from a wave of early retirees, partly from some households shifting back towards one worker so that the other can take care of the kids, and partly because wages are not high enough to attract certain types of work after decades of disinflationary wage pressures and outsourcing.

**Gold and the Dollar**

Gold continues to be relatively flat for the year:

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-gold.png)

Gold started to break out a bit in November but then came back down. On the big down day among risk assets on Friday, however, gold held up decently.

There’s not much to say about it until we see better signs of a break out or break down. Deeply negative real interest rates on dollars and other developed market fiat currencies are favorable for gold, but many younger investors have preferred bitcoin instead as their alternative money of choice. The main question, I think, is whether some portion of the much larger bond market will want to get into gold as it becomes increasingly clear that interest rates will likely remain submerged below the inflation rate for a long time.

The US dollar itself has had a weird year. It is slightly down vs the Chinese yuan over the past year, and flat vs the Canadian dollar. But the dollar is up big vs the euro and the yen, and those are the two currencies that make up most of the dollar index basket. Mainly I would describe this year as one of euro and yen weakness, rather than specifically dollar strength, which is an important distinction.

During the past several months the dollar index has been grinding up, but then on risk-off Friday, it actually went sharply down rather than up. This adds to its weirdness this year.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-dollar-index.png)

My dollar view is neutral at the moment.

I became bearish on the dollar in October 2019 at a dollar index level of 99, and after it fell considerably in 2020 to sub-90, I began to re-iterate that although I remain structurally bearish on it, that the next several months were uncertain because it already came down so far so fast. From there, I mainly watched support and resistance levels, and eventually it grinded up through resistance, which led me to shift to a neutral outlook, as it went higher and for longer than my base case was expecting.

I retain that neutral outlook for now. Japan and the Euro Area are maintaining the most dovish monetary policy stances among large central banks, especially because their economies rely on exports to a considerable degree. Plus, their energy shortages aren’t exactly bullish for their currencies.

Meanwhile, the comparatively strong dollar and domestic fiscal stimulus has given the US its largest trade deficit ever, at $80 billion per month. We boosted our demand for things but not our supply of things, so those dollars pour out to the rest of the world to supply us with those things.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin has been in a correction for a few weeks, similar so far to its September correction:

![Bitcoin Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-btc-technicals.png)

On-chain indicators still look rather constructive, similar to September as well (and so far unlike the May crash, although that could change).

The spent output profit ratio or “SOPR” is a measure of whether people are selling coins at a profit or a loss, compared to their entry price. In a bull market, corrections often come down to around 1, meaning that people are selling for breakeven but won’t sell at a loss. That’s roughly the level for the price to bounce a bit if the bull market is to remain in place (green circle examples below). However, in bear markets and crashes, more people start selling at a loss and SOPR breaks firmly below 1 (red circle examples below).

![Bitcoin SOPR](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-bitcoin-sopr.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

This level, or somewhere roughly over $50k at the lowest, is probably where it should start to bounce if an intermediate-term bull market is to remain intact. If it starts breaking lower than that, then we may be in for a lengthier consolidation. I lean towards the ongoing bull market base case, but either way, most of my focus is on a longer timeframe than that.

Swan Bitcoin has a monthly newsletter that they send out to their private clients (high net worth individuals and small businesses). I have an agreement with Swan’s CEO to be able to share it with my premium members but please do not share it publicly. Here’s their [November issue](https://insight.swanbitcoin.com/hubfs/Swan_Private_Insight-November_2021-77927.pdf).

Swan Bitcoin recently raised $6 million for a Series A financing at a $90 million company valuation, and I joined their board of directors this quarter as their independent director. **El Salvador’s Bitcoin Bond**

El Salvador announced that they will issue $1 billion in 10-year duration bitcoin bonds in early 2022, and they will do so on the Liquid network which is a sidechain that runs on Bitcoin and allows for securities to be issued on it.

El Salvador will put half of the proceeds from the bond into bitcoin with a 5-year lockup period, and the other half into developing bitcoin mining infrastructure and city infrastructure around that mining area (which uses geothermal energy).

The bonds come with a lower interest rate (6.5%) than El Salvador’s distressed traditional bonds of similar duration (around 13%), but also come with long-term upside if bitcoin appreciates in price, since the bonds have a “bitcoin dividend” profit-sharing aspect for the second half of the term. The final terms of the bond haven’t been given yet but part of the bond might also be backed up by the bitcoin they buy with it. So in the event of a default, bond holders may be able to recoup some losses with the bitcoin.

Overall, El Salvador remains in a distressed situation in terms of their economy and debt, and it remains to be seen how their president and this bitcoin plan will work out for them. The president is showing increasing authoritarian tendencies, which is problematic from many points of view. In addition, it’s the first area where bitcoin was imposed in a top-down way rather than adopted organically bottom-up, and was rushed, resulting in things like their government wallet app facing technical issues.

Yet at the same time he is positioning the country as a mini-hub to attract bitcoin and crypto people and companies. They’ve already managed to attract a lot of wealthy tourists and international attention due to their policy of making bitcoin legal tender, which likely results in funds flowing into the country for now.

If the project fails it may prevent some other countries from following suit, while the more successful it is, the more it could attract other small nations to do a similar approach.

The math of these proposed bonds does make sense for bitcoin bulls, because \*if\* bitcoin goes up a lot over a 5-10 year period, they will substantially outperform other types of bonds. It reminds me of the lending structure that Warren Buffett made to General Electric, Bank of America, and Goldman Sachs back during the 2008 global financial crisis. Those companies were distressed and needed financing, and Buffett was optimistic on their prospects if they could get through this liquidity storm. Buffett gave them billions of dollars in capital at moderate yields but with an agreement that allowed him to convert to equity at very low prices many years later if the companies emerged well from the crisis. As a result, Buffett made major profits for his own company Berkshire Hathaway, while those distressed businesses got access to capital in a crisis at lower rates than they normally would, given the level of financial stress they were under.

Nonetheless, there remains significant political and business execution risk in El Salvador, both for the country itself and for the global perception of the Bitcoin network.

## Roku Update

Most of the fast-growth stocks out there have been pummeled recently. Nearly all of the stocks that you would generally find in an Ark Fund have taken a beating lately as investor sentiment shifted even if their revenue numbers kept going up. Anything that didn’t substantially beat quarterly earnings generally took a big valuation haircut.

Etsy soared to all-time highs as one of the few exceptions, but for example Roku has been beaten down.

I’ve been dollar-cost averaging into a small 0.8% position in the newsletter portfolio for Roku, and have some cash-secured puts that are likely to be exercised in my other holdings, and wouldn’t mind a second tranche here. However, I’d like to see a bit more evidence of a bottoming process. Here’s the revenue and price/sales chart:

![ROKU Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-roku-valuation.png)

Roku is doing fine overall, with the number of users and the revenue per user going up. They’ve had two newsworthy challenges lately. One is that they are facing some margin pressure on their hardware boxes due to supply chain issues, which I don’t consider a big deal long-term since it’s not a huge part of their business. The second is that they are in a disagreement with Youtube which could result in Youtube being unavailable on the platform. That’s a moderately more concerning issue that I’ll be monitoring, and opens up larger topics around ongoing antirust issue with the largest platforms.

Although I like Roku more than most companies of its type, these positions in high-growth companies are quite risky. I prefer a value tilt for a portfolio where possible in these conditions, but I like adding small positions in some of these platform tech growth companies where possible as well, as sort of a barbell portfolio approach. I’m happy with the current small position here and will be looking for a setup that offers a good entry into a second tranche if possible. I’m not taking action at the moment.

## Suncor Energy

Suncor Energy (NYSE: SU) is a large Canadian integrated energy company. Their portfolio of assets includes oil sands production, offshore production, refineries, pipelines, and gas stations.

![Suncor Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-suncor-map.png)

*Source: [Suncor Q3 2021 Investor Presentation](https://www.suncor.com/en-ca/investors/presentations-and-key-dates)*

Suncor invests in non-oil projects as well. They have a series of wind farms, and they added electric vehicle charging stations to many of their gas stations. Canadians can now drive from coast-to-coast across the country with an electric vehicle using entirely Suncor-owned charging stations. Most of Canada’s population is along the southern border with the US, basically along their green electric vehicle charging network in the picture above.

The company can sustain itself as long as WTI crude oil is over $30, and can sustain the dividend as well as long as WTI crude is over $35. Their forecast is for these breakeven numbers to decrease in the years ahead, such that they may be able to one day sustain a dividend even with sub-$30 crude prices.

![Suncor Breakevens](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-suncor-breakevens.png)

*Source: Suncor Q3 2021 Investor Presentation*

Crude is currently $68, after Friday’s sharp decline on a semi-holiday of low liquidity. It was as high as $84 in the beginning of November. At these levels, the company is raking in free cash flow, which it can put towards dividends, share buybacks, debt reductions, and small growth plans.

The stock is firmly in the value side of things. Their forward cash flows aren’t expected to grow much, and aren’t priced to grow much. Instead, returns (if successful) will mainly come from per-share value accrual. With a low valuation, the company pays a hefty dividend yield, has a hefty buyback yield, and is reducing its net debt level.

![Suncor Capital Plan](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-suncor-capital.png)

*Source: Suncor Q3 2021 Investor Presentation*

Overall, I like Suncor as a value/dividend/energy play as part of a diversified portfolio. Oil companies in general have been terrible performers over the past decade but commodity supply goes in cycles, and the oil market is shaping up to be more tight for the 2020s decade than the 2010s decade. The growth in North American shale oil appears to have peaked, and oil companies are being more conservative with their growth capex and instead are emphasizing capital discipline and shareholder returns.

**Risk Analysis**

Suncor has a significant number of risks for investors to consider.

*Long-term Oil Price*

Suncor’s biggest risk is the long-term price of oil. Fluctuating oil prices aren’t necessarily a big deal but the company relies on producing and selling oil, and thus needs reasonable average oil prices during the course of a year.

For the near-term, the Omicron virus variant and government responses to it can potentially affect the oil price, as we saw Friday. We’ll see in the coming months if Friday’s big oil down day was an overreaction or more telling.

In the long-term, oil demand mainly comes from emerging markets, and is tied to overall global growth and stimulus. My bullishness on oil is not so much that global growth will be fast, but that supply will continue to be relatively tight due to a lack of investment and various ESG mandates among investors.

*Operations Risk*

Suncor’s second biggest risk is operations quality. Suncor has had a challenging track record over the past couple of years in terms of operating issues, resulting in them underperforming other oil companies. Oil sands, refineries, pipelines- any of these can break or face various damage that results in extra costs and downtime.

I’ve not had a position in this stock, and have instead focused on other energy companies. However, after the recent increase of their dividend and the relative underperformance of the company relative to its peers, I do think the risk/reward has improved here.

*Political Risk*

Oil companies face political risk in multiple jurisdictions, as countries try to reduce their reliance on oil, and not always in a particularly rational way.

Oil companies then find themselves in a difficult position, politically. On one hand as politicians call for less oil production and as big pools of capital divest from oil companies for ESG reasons, the companies want to reduce risk by limiting their oil-related capex and ending their reliance on outside capital, being entirely self-funding if possible. On the other hand when the price of oil goes up, some of those politicians criticize oil companies for not producing enough supply.

My way to mitigate that risk is to spread out my energy company exposure into small positions in multiple stocks, across various jurisdictions in the United States, Canada, Europe, and Russia.

## **Final Thoughts**

Consumer sentiment in the US is deteriorating. It crashed from a high level in spring 2020 when the pandemic came, partially recovered for about a year, and then in recent months rolled back over to new cycle lows:

![Consumer Sentiment](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-28-consumer-sentiment.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The St. Louis Fed delays their consumer sentiment chart by one month at the request of the University of Michigan, so actually what is not shown there is the latest November data point that fell to a deeper cycle low of 67.4. The chart above only shows the prior data point of 71.7 from October.

Overall, policymakers are in a tough spot. Fiscal stimulus isn’t happening much at the moment, the Fed is tapering, inflation is going up faster than wage growth on average, and COVID-19 keeps mutating and flowing around the world to cause seasonal case spikes. Lockdowns were tolerated when people were paid to remain home, but it’s harder to do lockdowns when there is no compensation for lost work.

A generally defensive posture makes sense here until there is more policy clarity. I maintain an inflationary tilt and a value tilt, but with an emphasis on strong balance sheets that can withstand liquidity shocks and periods of slower growth, and with an inclusion of short-duration Treasuries or TIPS which can serve as dry powder for rebalancing.
