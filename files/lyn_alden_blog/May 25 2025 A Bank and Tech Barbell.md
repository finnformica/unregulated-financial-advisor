# A Bank and Tech Barbell

*May 25, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report takes a look at tariff updates and fiscal updates in the United States.

The digital asset section takes a look at Bitcoin’s new all-time high and the role of leveraged corporate buyers in the ecosystem.

The investment analysis section looks at a barbell approach that I’m increasingly interested in.

## Macro View

**US Economic Update**

The US economy continues to chug along. Ever since tariffs between the US and China were temporarily dropped amid ongoing negations, the level of uncertainty for many businesses improved from unworkable to merely difficult, which enables business to continue.

This quarter will likely be a noisy one, considering that all sorts of tariff front-running occurred in the previous quarter, and considering that many businesses are still operating in a conservative way until they see more clarity.

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator suggests positive GDP growth for this quarter:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-gdp-now.png)

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index is currently pointing toward stall speed, although their historically accurate model has had a rough time in this post-pandemic era of fiscal dominance. Their model suggested recession throughout 2022 and 2023, but recessions work differently amid such large ongoing fiscal deficits:

![LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-lei.png)

**US Tariffs Update**

We’ve been in a relatively quiet period regarding US tariffs ever since the US and China agreed to a 90-day partial ceasefire, but two days ago the issue heated up again.

President Trump called out the $3 trillion company Apple (AAPL), saying they’d be tariffed at 25% unless they bring iPhone production to the US:

![Trump on Apple](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-trump-post-apple.png)

He also called out the European Union, threatening 50% tariffs starting in June due to a lack of progress on trade talks:

![Trump on EU](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-trump-post-eu.png)

When interpreting this, I think it’s useful to look at market action. US equities have retraced the majority of their losses, and while they’ve not claimed new highs yet, they’ve been having a very good several weeks:

![SPY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-spy-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Back when equities were crumbling, and companies were basically frozen in the face of triple-digit tariff rates between the US and China, one can only imagine the number of phone calls that officials in the administration were receiving from influential connections to get things back on track.

As things de-escalated, and as equities have climbed back up, those phone calls would have ceased. This means the president is once again in an emboldened position to attempt a hardline negotiating stance until markets get rough again.

Thus, I think this recent S&P 500 peak of 5963 on May 19th has a decent chance of marking a local top for the next few weeks as uncertainty ramps up again. I’m not a short-term trader, so I’m not really taking any action around that view, but that’s the base case I’m operating under unless I start to see evidence to the contrary.

![S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-spx.png)

**US Budget Bill**

Last week, the House of Representatives narrowly passed a budget package that extends many tax cuts, adds to some of them, trims some other areas, and raises the debt ceiling by $4 trillion. Among other things, the bill would have the following effects:

* Extend the income tax cuts from Trump’s first term.
* Increase the deduction limit for state and local tax (SALT).
* Increase the childcare tax credit.
* Eliminate taxes on tips and overtime pay.
* Boost military spending and direct additional funds towards deportation efforts.
* Tighten funding and increase requirements for Medicaid and food stamps.
* Tighten student loan assistance.
* Eliminate tax incentives for EVs and solar/wind energy.
* Increase the debt ceiling by $4 trillion.

The net result is an increase in the expected cumulative deficit over the next decade. The Wall Street Journal [estimates](https://www.wsj.com/politics/policy/what-is-in-republican-tax-bill-39809182) it’ll result in a $2.75 trillion cumulative deficit increase, while [other sources](https://www.cbsnews.com/news/gop-tax-bill-reconcilation-deficit-moodys-debt-spending/) point toward between $3 trillion and $4 trillion. This amounts to some hundreds of billions of dollars per year, which may partially be offset by new tariff revenue.

President Trump has urged the bill to be passed, but it has to go through the Senate first, and its fate there is unclear.

I’m not a political analyst so I’ll be following the news on this particular package to see if it passes in this form or gets tweaked first. Either way, I expect some similar version of this to pass, which gives us a good directional view on what to expect for the next few years of federal expenditure and revenue.

Nothing in the bill is particularly surprising, and it reinforces my “[nothing stops this train](https://www.lynalden.com/september-2024-newsletter/)” thesis that US fiscal deficits will indeed stay large for many years to come.

**Domestic Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity conditions continue to be flat in a managed way, as they have been for three years since mid-2022:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-domestic-liquidity.png)

Meanwhile, bank cash levels remain in a reasonable position. Large banks are starting to drain cash ratios pretty quickly, but are still above problematic levels. Once these get back toward their problematic levels, the Fed will be going back toward structural balance sheet increases, and raising the overall domestic liquidity floor again:

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-bank-cash.png)

Liquidity expert Michael Howell of Crossborder Capital pointed toward good liquidity conditions among large US banks at the present:

![Crossborder Capital Post](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-crossborder-capital-tweet.png)

I don’t view it as particularly odd, however. The most supportive element in domestic liquidity recently has been the resumed drawdown of the Treasury General Account. In my report from four weeks ago, I highlighted that April tax season brought in a burst of revenue, but that in the coming months the Treasury’s cash balance would likely start to decrease again:

> The Treasury’s cash account is currently in drawdown mode (which is good for liquidity) due to the ongoing debt ceiling limit. However, they had a $300 billion increase in mid-April during tax season:
>
> ![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-tga.png)
>
> They will likely go back to cash drawdown mode in the coming months, but this tax haul was a bit higher than consensus expectations and lengthens the estimated runway for how long the Treasury has until they run out of cash due to the debt ceiling. They can likely push into autumn of this year without a debt ceiling deal, although a deal could come before then.
>
> -April 27, 2025 premium report

As an update, we do indeed have four consecutive weeks of Treasury cash balance drawdowns, dropping the balance from around $600 billion to $500 billion. This money is pouring back into the US financial system, and thus is good for liquidity:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-tga.png)

There is significant variability in when the Treasury cash balance might run out, which if not addressed by a debt ceiling increase can lead to technical default. The expected depletion date is in late summer or early autumn. However, Congress spends August on recess, so Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has urged Congress to pass some sort of debt ceiling increase by late July (whether it’s this House bill or a stand-alone item) to avoid the possibility of running out of cash during their recess.

I fully expect Congress to avert a debt ceiling issue and to pass a budget that is similar in magnitude to the one described above. However, the timing of the debt ceiling increase does somewhat matter for liquidity conditions due to its affects on the Treasury General Account. Ironically while they do nothing, the Treasury cash account will keep draining into the banking system, which is temporarily good for liquidity. The hard part comes once the debt ceiling is passed and the Treasury Department is expected to refill its cash account (which drains liquidity from the banking system).

It will be interesting to see how Bessent will address liquidity at that point. He was critical of his predecessor Janet Yellen for issuing extra T-bills to fund the deficit, and presumably wants to issue fewer T-bills and increase the issuance of longer duration Treasuries. However, he has also stated an explicit goal to get long-duration yields lower. Those longer duration Treasury yields are pretty high currently (20- and 30-year Treasury yields are over 5%), and terming out the debt would do those yields no favors.

On Friday during interviews, Bessent brought up the possibility of Supplemental Leverage Ratio relief for US banks, which around the margins would reduce their constraints for purchasing Treasuries. All else being equal that would be a boost for  liquidity in the Treasury market, albeit probably a small one, similar to very minor quantitative easing.

Quarter-end periods tend to put the most strain on available liquidity. I expect Q2 this summer to end with pretty reasonable liquidity conditions due to the TGA drain, but Q3 this autumn is where things might get more interesting and require usage of the Fed’s repo facility. However, we’ll cross that bridge when we get closer and see if that view needs updating. It’ll partially depend on when Congress passes a debt ceiling increase, and at what rate and method Bessent chooses to refill the Treasury General Account, which are both hard to predict in advance. The key thing is to keep them on our radar.

**Global Liquidity**

Global liquidity continues to be decent, largely because the dollar index has been subdued. However, there’s not much to write home about unless or until the index breaks below its April 21st low point:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-dxy-annotated.png)

I do think there’s a high probability of breaking below that, and even potentially below the lows of 2020/2021 over the next 3-5 years. If it does, this would likely be good for emerging market equities, commodities, and neutral global monetary assets like gold and bitcoin.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin reached new all-time highs, up to around $112k per coin last week. It has since cooled off a bit, but remains near its highs.

For several months, bitcoin was in an unclear range. I mainly just re-iterated long-term bullishness on it until it finds its way out of its consolidation.

But then, back in my late-April report from four weeks ago, I highlighted a good combo of on-chain fundamentals and an uncommon positive technical signal. From that point, there has been a rather rapid jump from $94k to $112k:

> From an on-chain perspective, the market capitalization has let off a lot of steam relative to its cost basis since it began consolidating in November 2024. This metric continues to indicate that while bitcoin may go through various consolidations going forward, it’s unlikely to experience a massive multi-year drawdown from current levels, because its overall sentiment and euphoria hasn’t reached anywhere near the highs of prior multi-year tops.
>
> ![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-27-bitcoin-mvrv.png)
>
> Bitcoin is also starting to look good on the weekly technical chart for the first time in months. The relative strength index “RSI” and moving average convergence/divergence “MACD” look similar to when its prior March-to-October 2024 consolidation ended and started a new leg higher:
>
> ![Bitcoin Weekly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-17-bitcoin-weekly.png)
>
> I don’t personally pay much attention to technical analysis. However, I do like to check some of the most basic technical indicators for signs of momentum shifts and sustained price floors or price ceilings. In this environment, global liquidity, on-chain indicators, and technical indicators are all pretty favorable, pointing to the idea that $75k on April 9th was likely the price floor for this period, and that the next 6-12 months look pretty good as a base case.
>

The positive technical signal remains in place, supported by good on-chain fundamentals as well. My base case is to see it grind higher over the next 6 months.

![BTC Weekly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-btc-weekly.png)

The biggest risk to that base case is that as I previously described, I think US equities have a decent probability of rolling over for a few months on renewed tariff concerns. That could add some chop to bitcoin along the way. If US equities don’t rapidly sell-off but instead merely meander lower, then I think bitcoin has a better chance of shrugging that headwind off and grinding sideways-to-up anyway. I won’t attempt to predict it, because it will partially depend on what President Trump decides to do in terms of tariffs on the European Union and other geopolitical matters.

**Plotting the Rest of This Cycle**

David Bailey, the head of BTC Inc (the parent company of Bitcoin Magazine and the world’s largest Bitcoin Conference), recently announced that he raised $710 million for a new corporate treasury vehicle called Nakamoto:

> **SALT LAKE CITY, May 12, 2025 –**KindlyMD, Inc. (NASDAQ:KDLY, “KindlyMD”), a leading provider of integrated healthcare services, today announced that it has entered into a definitive merger agreement with Nakamoto Holdings Inc. (“Nakamoto”), a Bitcoin-native holding company, to start a Bitcoin treasury strategy.
>
> Nakamoto is a new holding company founded by David Bailey. In partnership with BTC Inc, Nakamoto seeks to build the first global network of Bitcoin treasury companies.This is the first step in Nakamoto’s vision for an ecosystem of Bitcoin-native companies, including media, advisory, and financial services, that exist to accelerate Bitcoin adoption and utility.
>
> The combined company will aim both to accumulate Bitcoin and grow the Bitcoin owned on a per share basis, or Bitcoin Yield, through a variety of equity, debt and other offerings. This merger gives the public market exposure to Bitcoin within a compliant, transparent structure backed by a uniquely experienced Bitcoin management team, including a partnership with one of the world’s most influential Bitcoin marketing platforms.

There has been a big surge recently in the number of companies adopting a levered bitcoin accumulation strategy similar to what MicroStrategy (MSTR) has done for nearly five years.

I think it makes a lot of sense to form and expand bitcoin treasury companies while there is market demand for it. And yet, when I look out into the future, I can’t help but assume that this strategy will serve as key fuel for the rest of this bull market and then provide some major *downward* catalysts for the next major bear market.

Well-run bitcoin accumulation companies will term out their debt as much as possible, maintain sustainable leverage ratios, and otherwise put themselves in a position to get through a bear market without a liquidation. Back during the 2022 bear market, many market pundits were calling for MicroStrategy to blow up, but it never did. I’ve been long MicroStrategy since August 2020 when they began their bitcoin strategy, and have personally met the key executives that handle their corporate treasury activities due to my involvement in the Bitcoin ecosystem. Thus far I have been fully confident in their actions.

However, as the popularity of this strategy increases, there will likely be some worse-run companies in the mix, and we may find them having to liquidate bitcoin in the subsequent bear market to service their debts. Thus, a well-run bitcoin treasury company contributes to bitcoin price increases during a bull market but then does not contribute to bitcoin price decreases during a bear market, while a poorly-run one similarly contributes to price increases during the bull cycle but then is far more likely to contribute to decreases during a bear market.

Back in late 2020, the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust was the key bull market fuel. There was an arbitrage for investors to go long GBTC and short BTC to capture the positive premium to NAV, and that resulted in GBTC buying a ton of bitcoin. But once that premium went away in early 2021, that type of leveraged market-neutral buyer went away. I highlighted this at the time:

> Up against the structural bull market of the post-halving adoption cycle, there are two fundamental headwinds against bitcoin at the moment.
>
> The first is potentially bond yields as described previously. If they keep rising, and the dollar index remains firm, and liquidity gets more tight, that should continue to pressure highly-valued growth stocks, and bitcoin may temporarily be lumped into that category by investors as well, especially in a broad risk-off environment.
>
> The second is that the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) no longer has a positive premium over its net asset value or “NAV”. In fact, it’s trading at a slight discount to NAV. Canada now has a solid bitcoin ETF, firms such as NYDIG and SkyBridge have come to market over time and provided competition for institutional and accredited investor allocations into bitcoin. So, there is less of a reason for GBTC to have a premium. Most existing closed end funds for other asset classes trade at a discount to NAV, and GBTC has at least temporarily joined the discount club.
>
> This discount to NAV is both good and bad. It’s good because it makes GBTC a better vehicle for holding bitcoin than it used to be, since you’re getting it at around net asset value rather than at a premium. It’s bad because it removes one of the sources of bitcoin demand.
>
> GBTC was the biggest buyer of bitcoin in 2020. Some of this was for long exposure, and another big chunk was for market-neutral arbitrage. For that second part, accredited investors and institutions could buy into GBTC at NAV with a six-month lock-up period, and then sell six months later at the market price, and that market price generally had a premium over NAV. Simultaneously with their purchase at NAV, they could short bitcoin elsewhere, and thus make a non-directional investment that simply lets them extract the premium over NAV every six months with no exposure to bitcoin’s price.
>
> This process made a lot of money for folks, and permanently sucked up a lot of bitcoin. GBTC basically converted liquid bitcoin to illiquid bitcoin, locked away in cold storage. If the GBTC premium remains minimal or negative, that trade is done, and thus that specific source of demand and liquid-to-illiquid conversion process is done.
>
> One of the most interesting and bullish things happening with bitcoin over the past year is how it was sucked off of exchanges, which never happened before. This chart shows bitcoin’s price in black, and the number of bitcoin on exchanges in orange:
>
> ![Bitcoin Exchange Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-2-28-exchange-balance.png)
>
> For its entire existence, the number of bitcoin on exchanges kept increasing, except for a short period in the second half of 2016.
>
> Well, ever since early 2020, the number of coins on exchanges has been decreasing, and for the longest and biggest drawdown ever. This represents all sorts of institutional demand and cold-storage buyers, with PayPal and Cash App and Swan Bitcoin and Grayscale and NYDIG and SkyBridge and a number of corporations buying up bitcoin and removing it from exchanges.
>
> With Grayscale potentially out of the picture, it’ll be interesting to see if the number of bitcoin on exchanges finally bottoms, or not. It’ll depend on how much demand there is from these other funds, including new ETFs and ongoing institutional allocations.
>
> Overall, I remain structurally bullish on bitcoin but as we get higher and deeper into this bull market, the risks for reaching a significant top gradually increase. The key things I’ll be watching in the coming weeks for bitcoin are 1) do bond yields keep rising or do they take a break with something like SLR relief, and 2) does SOPR turn persistently below 1 or does this mark a local bottom.
>
> -February 28, 2021 premium report

Bitcoin reached a top in March 2021 in the mid-$60k range, fell briefly below $30k in July 2021 as CeFi lenders blew up, then reached a double top of up to $69k in November 2021 as things briefly stabilized. After that, bitcoin rolled over into a multi-year bear market that fell below $16k in November 2022 as FTX imploded, at which point I was reiterating my view of it as a long-term buy in each of my reports.

![Bitcoin HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-bitcoin-hodl-wave.png)

Based on a variety of metrics, the bitcoin market currently looks nothing like February 2021, which is a good thing. On-chain data (e.g. market cap to on-chain cost basis) is way healthier, and this corporate buying strategy is still full of ammo.

However, later this year or in 2026, I suspect that I’ll be highlighting similar concerns about bitcoin treasury companies running out of steam as I did about GBTC in February 2021. Right now, I think we’re still firmly in bull market territory as a base case.

## An Investing Barbell: Accepting Uncertainty

For quite a while, there has been a very strong structural bid for large cap growth US companies that are perceived to be safe.

What these companies generally have in common is that they have low earnings uncertainty/volatility. Due to steady business operations combined with recurring share buybacks on top of that, these companies are viewed as the bluest of blue chips, and are viewed to be rather recession-resistant.

However, risks form when valuations become very high. Even if the underlying company continues to do well, the stock price can suffer for 3-5 years or more as the valuation resets back toward normal as earnings catch up.

I’ve highlighted Costco (COST) and Walmart (WMT) as examples of this phenomenon:

![COST FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-cost-fastgraph.png)

![WMT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-wmt-fastgraph.png)

Apple (AAPL) is also one of them, although it has already been losing steam lately:

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-aapl-fastgraph.png)

Companies like Costco and Walmart are very strong, but they operate on razor-thin margins, and thus have to navigate the complex tariff situation for the foreign-made portion of their goods.

Meanwhile, Apple is chopping along at a semi-rich valuation relative to how little growth it has, and is under the renewed tariff spotlight.

One of the most bulletproof companies in the world is arguably Microsoft. Its software focus allows it to largely ignore tariffs, and it’s on the right side of the AI trend. However, its market cap is already over $3.3 trillion, and it’s trading at over 34x earnings. I’m not bearish on the stock, but I’m not exactly pounding the table on it as a buy, either:

![MSFT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-msft-fastgraph.png)

Microsoft is one of two companies that have a perfect AAA credit rating. Even the US federal government doesn’t have an AAA credit rating anymore, and indeed Microsoft’s short-term corporate bonds trade at lower yields than short-term US treasuries, reflecting ironically lower technical default risk.

Overall, I view these types of companies as overpriced. Investors are paying a very big premium for large companies with low earnings uncertainty/volatility. Some of them are at more extreme valuations than others (e.g. COST seems crazier to me than MSFT), but they’re quite richly priced as a group.

In contrast, I continue to prefer companies that have more earnings uncertainty/volatility, but for which the market valuation acknowledges that and prices it attractively.

Two weeks ago, I highlighted the sell-off in banks as interesting. They’re buffered from direct tariff risks, and the combination of fiscal dominance and strong balance sheets protects them from the worst aspects of recession risk. Their earnings have a nontrivial amount of uncertainty over the next few years, but in many cases they’re trading at 7x to 12x earnings, pay a 3-5% dividend yield, and are retaining earnings to build up their capital.

![USB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-usb-fastgraph.png)

Meanwhile, looking abroad, we have a quiet new cycle dollar-denominated high in Itau Unibanco (ITUB), Brazil’s largest bank that I keep highlighting. It pays sizable dividends as it chugs higher as well:

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-itub-fastgraph.png)

Itau Unibanco has plenty of uncertainty, but it has been priced well for it, and its risks are quite different than many other companies. A weaker dollar benefits them, and there are some good reasons to believe we’re in a multi-year weaker dollar cycle going forward, similar to the 2003-2007 period. I would of course recommend managing position sizes carefully for an equity like this.

On the complete other side of the equity spectrum, I’ve been tracking the potential bottom in names like Advanced Micro Devices (AMD) and Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing (TSM). I continue to find them attractive at current pricing levels, but more-so AMD now since it has had less of a bounce:

![AMD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-amd-fastgraph.png)

Selling cash-secured puts against some of these volatile tech names also helps to reduce risk and generate returns from a choppy market.

In summary, amid this period of fiscal dominance and heightened uncertainty, I find more value in buying names that are priced for uncertainty, versus overpaying for names that are perceived to have little uncertainty.

The net result is somewhat of a barbell effect, where I find both value dividend stocks and cyclical growth stocks interesting, but am less interested in expensive noncyclical stocks that perhaps have tail risks that haven’t been fully discounted.

## Final Thoughts: A Narrative Pivot

With the proposed spending bill that will expand the budget deficit going forward, there has been somewhat of a shift in the way that investors and commentators perceive the deficit, and the way that the debt and deficit are talked about by those within and adjacent to the administration.

The sentiment shift on Twitter/X has been pretty profound. Anthony Pompliano with 1.7 million followers exemplifies the shift as a clear example, but there are many like him. This is within the span of two and a half months (kudos to his transparency):

![Pomp Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-pomp-tweets.png)

The Wall Street Journal’s chief economics correspondent, Nick Timiraous, summarized the change in institutional sentiment with an [article](https://x.com/NickTimiraos/status/1925357290695127045). Here was the takeaway from his post about it on Twitter/X:

![Timiraos Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-timiraos-tweet.png)

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent previously emphasized getting the budget deficit down to 3% of GDP. While he still references that as an ideal, and references the importance of cutting waste where possible, in this week’s interview circuit he has shifted toward emphasizing growing the economy faster than the debt, rather than emphasizing slowing down debt growth. This is a “run it hot” fiscal dominance type of playbook:

![Bessent Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-bessent-tweet.png)

Elon Musk, who previously talked about cutting $1 trillion off of annual budget deficits by 2026, has reduced his involvement in DOGE and has now emphasized, along with Bessent, trying to focus on growing the economy more-so than realistically reducing the fiscal deficit. He recently wrote of DOGE as something that can merely “postpone” the country’s financial problems rather than address them:

![Musk Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-25-musk-tweet.png)

As someone who strongly emphasizes the fiscal impact on the economy and markets, I’ve paid attention to these sentiment and narrative changes quite closely. Elections and political shifts can get people fired up, but the core of these fiscal deficits is locked in from decades of cumulative political decisions rather than just a byproduct of recent decisions, and thus has very low probabilities of being meaningfully addressed. After a honeymoon period of many people expecting the fiscal deficit will be greatly reduced, the baseline is reasserting itself.

In my view, this was all pretty clear from the start. The [January 2025 piece](https://www.lynalden.com/full-steam-ahead-all-aboard-fiscal-dominance/) I collaborated with Sam Callahan on directly discussed how much of the forward deficits are due to Social Security, Medicare, Defense, and Interest Expense, which are all things that could not or would not be meaningfully touched. Prior to that, in my [September 2024 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/september-2024-newsletter/) (which was published two months before the election and thus took no view on who would even be president), I highlighted six reasons why the deficit is almost certainly going to remain high.

Thus, I view the “run it hot” fiscal dominance playbook to be the primary one for quite some time. There will be cycles that are hotter or cooler than others, but structurally we’re in a very different environment than the 2010s decade, and many market participants are waking up to that fact.
