## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be rangebound. This is calculated as the Fed’s balance sheet, minus the Treasury General Account, minus the reverse repo facility:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Fed continues to perform quantitative tightening, which all else being equal is negative for liquidity:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-fed-balance-sheet.png)

The reverse repo facility has been flat lately, and there’s still $433 billion in it. The big draining of the reverse repo facility from over $2 trillion in May 2023 to the current low levels was the main reason why the Fed was able to keep performing quantitative tightening without causing further liquidity problems in the banking system. The Treasury Department can indirectly drain it by issuing more T-bills for money market funds to buy in lieu of holding money in that facility, and as the reverse repo facility drains, the funds go back into bank reserves which is positive for bank liquidity:

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-reverse-repos.png)

The Treasury General Account is currently at $745 billion, which is around their target level. All else being equal, when the TGA goes down it is good for liquidity since it drains the funds back into the banking system. (Early 2020 was a big exception to that, since the fiscal impulse and the rate of quantitative easing were so big that they overrode the rise in the TGA, which on its own would be negative for liquidity. The TGA was only increased that high basically for extra financial buffer to support everything else that was happening, since the sheer magnitude of funds moving around was off-the-charts.)

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-treasury-general-account.png)

If we look at cash as a percentage of bank assets, both large banks and small banks are maintaining levels higher than the March 2023 regional bank crisis. This has mainly been because for the year and a half since then, reverse repos have drained back into bank reserves faster than the Fed’s quantitative tightening has removed bank reserves. However, large bank cash levels are firmly heading downward. The repo spike of 2019 was associated with big banks hitting their effective liquidity floor, while the regional bank crisis of 2023 was associated with small/medium banks running into solvency problems due to the rapid rise in interest rates amid tight liquidity conditions:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-bank-cash.png)

This has affected broad money supply as well. After a sharper-than-normal rise in money supply due to pandemic-era stimulus, the money supply began sharply contracting in spring of 2022. It reached its low point in October 2023, and has gone mildly upward since then. I think the low is very likely in:

![Broad Money Supply](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-m2.png)

In the coming months, we can expect the following liquidity impacts:

-The Fed plans to continue quantitative tightening for now, although the New York Fed expects this to cease sometime in 2025, so that they don’t touch the effective liquidity floor like they did in September 2019 and kind of did again in March 2023.

-Reverse repos remain a bit of a wildcard. I expect gradual draining as a base case, and we will get more updates at the end of October regarding whether the Treasury Department will accelerate the drainage with extra T-bill issuance or not.

-On January 2, 2025 the U.S. debt ceiling will go back into effect. It’s currently suspended due to the prior debt ceiling deal in 2023. If the debt ceiling is not raised at that time, then the Treasury Department will have to begin reducing its cash level for months to keep paying the government’s bills without the ability to issue net new debt, to avoid a technical default, and this can go on until they drain their $700+ billion balance. During the months where this TGA cash drain happens, it tends to be quite positive for liquidity. The likelihood of that happening mostly depends on election outcomes; historically in the post-GFC polarized era, a Republican-controlled Congress typically uses the debt ceiling as a negotiation tool against Democratic presidents, but the other way around hasn’t occurred yet. So I’ll cover that when we get to it, partially based on election outcomes.

The reason these liquidity indicators are important is because liquidity conditions have been strongly correlated to financial conditions and asset prices in the post-GFC era, as we’ve moved into structural fiscal dominance. Domestic liquidity affects the banking system and the dollar index rather considerably, which then affect global conditions.

Global liquidity, which I measure as global broad money supply of major currencies, translated into a dollar denomination, continues to be rangebound as well. The weaker dollar index has been supportive for global liquidity growth in recent months, but China’s flat money supply from March 2024 to the present has been a drag on global liquidity growth.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

There have been some key revisions in U.S. economic data recently. Some good and some bad.

The first and more notable one was the payroll data. Back on August 21st, the BLS revised job numbers downward by 818,000 or about 30% for the 12-month period from April 2023 through March 2024. This is the biggest downward revision since 2009. They still estimate that jobs were created, but 818,000 shy of the previously reported cumulative number of 2.9 million.

The market basically ignored this revision since it wasn’t too surprising; many analysts have been aware that there’s a big discrepancy between household surveys and payroll surveys. I highlighted this back in my June 9th report, for example:

> There is currently conflicting data between the household survey and the payroll survey for overall U.S. employment trends, which is a data quality issue that complicates analysis. The payroll survey continues to show strong numbers, while the household survey has been notably weaker, with a widening gap. The payroll survey counts people with multiple jobs, whereas the household survey does not, and there are a variety of other differences as well, which post-pandemic remote work and gig work might be amplifying to some degree.
>
> -June 9th premium report

This revision wasn’t good, but at least it helped to narrow that gap and resolve some of the discrepancy.

Slightly offsetting that was the Q2 GDP (April-June 2024) estimate revision. Whenever U.S. GDP is reported, it comes with some pre-scheduled revisions. First there’s a preliminary estimate, and then as more data come in there’s a second and third estimate. These estimates are pre-scheduled well in advance since every GDP report has them.

The second estimate for Q2 2024 GDP came in this week, and was revised *up* from the preliminary estimate. They originally estimated a 2.8% annualized growth rate, but now they estimate it to have been a 3.0% growth rate. The Associated Press reports:

> WASHINGTON (AP) — The U.S. economy grew last quarter at a healthy 3% annual pace, fueled by strong consumer spending and business investment, the government said Thursday in an upgrade of its initial assessment.
>
> The Commerce Department had previously estimated that the nation’s gross domestic product — the total output of goods and services — expanded at a 2.8% rate from April through June.
>
> The second-quarter growth marked a sharp acceleration from a sluggish 1.4% growth rate in the first three months of 2024.
>
> –[Associated Press](https://apnews.com/article/economy-growth-inflation-gdp-consumers-federal-reserve-0fc268d1feb244322f79b4bd9d80a54f)

The Atlanta Fed GDPNow estimator currently expects 2.5% annualized real GDP growth here in Q3, which is the quarter that is currently underway:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-gdpnow.png)

However, evidence continues to mount that there’s a two-speed economy in the United States.

-People in the lower half of the income spectrum are generally struggling, with wages that fail to keep up with inflation, and many of them are locked out of the housing market due to the combination of high house prices and high mortgage rates. Any borrowing that they do is at very high interest rates.

-Meanwhile, people in the upper half of the income spectrum are generally holding up better, as many of them already own a house with a fixed rate mortgage. With sizable cash-equivalent balances, any interest rate hikes have meant *income raises* for them, while their debts are generally locked in at low fixed rates.

Dollar General (DG) stock plummeted 32% on Thursday to lows not seen since late 2017. The catalyst was that their quarterly earnings announcement missed consensus expectations in terms of both revenue and earnings, and they lowered 2024 guidance. I have no current position in Dollar General in any portfolios, but as it plummets its starting to enter my watch list as a curiosity and potential opportunity eventually. Dollar General and other dollar stores are frequented by people in the lower half of the income spectrum that are generally struggling.

Ulta Beauty (ULTA), which I am long, also missed consensus expectations, but by less magnitude. Revenue missed expectations by 0.4% (despite being up 4.0% year-over-year), and earnings came in 2.8% below expectations of $5.30/share. And they reduced their full-year 2024 guidance from $11.5-$11.6 billion in revenue to $11.0-$11.2 billion in revenue. Their stock dropped about 8% on Thursday, recovered some of it on Friday, and is still above their August 9th low. Buying into Ulta, the thesis was that the whole industry is weak and increasingly inexpensive, and that Ulta is fine structurally. Although this particular quarterly update was disappointing, it doesn’t change the structural thesis, and further weakness like this was anticipated as a possibility. Mainly, it’s a macro indicator about the weak consumer in the lower half of the income spectrum.

One of the themes I’ve had for a while is that recessions look different in economies experiencing fiscal dominance compared to what we’re normally used to. This is similar to how recessions tend to show up differently in emerging markets than in developed markets. Developed market recessions tend to be more about bad credit cycles and high unemployment rates, while emerging market recessions tend to be more about stagnation and currency crises.

And so for example when we look at consumer sentiment in the United States, we can see that it tends to be inversely correlated to the “misery index”, which is sometimes used by economists to look at two important variables together. Consumer sentiment is a qualitative measure based on surveys, while the misery index is simply the quantitative sum of the unemployment rate and price inflation rate:

![Consumer Sentiment](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-consumer-sentiment.png)

Back in 2020, the misery index briefly spiked due to lockdown-induced unemployment, and consumer sentiment fell, but that reversed quickly as lockdowns ended and as fiscal stimulus went out to people and businesses. In 2022, as inflation washed over the economy, the misery index rose to very high levels, and consumer sentiment fell to deeply recessionary levels. The misery index has since gone back down, and consumer sentiment has rebounded, but remains below average.

There are many things that could explain that moderate discrepancy, but I think the two-speed economy sums it up well. There is no singular economy; everyone experiences the economy differently depending on their unique circumstances, and right now there’s a big difference between asset owners and non-asset owners in general.

For example, the consumer sentiment section that looks at conditions for buying a house is at a record low right now, and that’s backed up by data (the house price to income ratio is near record highs, *and* mortgage rates are pretty high). Buying conditions for vehicles and large consumer durables are also very low:

![Buying Conditions](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-buying-conditions.png)

Basically, if someone went into these past couple of years owning a house with a low fixed-rate mortgage, a portfolio of financial assets, and decent income, then they’re probably feeling pretty okay right now, economically speaking. However, those who didn’t manage to get a house before mortgage rates rose sharply, and/or who don’t have a lot of financial assets going into these years, are generally feeling a lot of economic pain right now.

It’s very hard for young people to buy a first house and start a family in these conditions. It’s very hard for anyone to move if it’ll mean giving up their low fixed rate mortgage. It’s very hard for many people to afford a vehicle. Even used vehicles are on average 30% above their pre-pandemic price levels according to the Manheim Used Vehicle Value Index, and many people haven’t received 30% cumulative wage increases in the past four years.

In terms of on-the-ground experience that people go through, we can characterize two types of recessions.

-The first type of recession leaves most people relatively unaffected, but is really bad for the much smaller subset that lose their jobs. This is a classic disinflationary developed market recession.

-The second type of recession spreads the pain a bit more broadly. Perhaps a third or half of people are experiencing some pain, as the cost of things they need has escaped them. They’re earning an income, but they’re able to buy less. This is a more stagflationary type of recession, typically associated with emerging markets.

There’s a classic Phil Knight (co-founder and former CEO of Nike) quote that goes something like, “everyone was looking for the next Michael Jordan on the basketball court, but he was walking down the fairway.” In other words, everyone was wondering who the next generationally dominant basketball player would be, assuming that would be the next big and marketable athlete, when instead the next generationally dominant and highly marketable athlete was Tiger Woods, in golf. Everyone was looking for a repeat of the previous thing, rather than the next thing.

Analysts have been trying to forecast or spot a recession in the United States for years, looking for the classic disinflationary recession. And maybe we’ll still get one in the year ahead. But fiscal dominance is a different environment than the past four decades in the United States, and in many ways the United States already went through an emerging market style recession in 2022. It was one where the pain was less concentrated but more widespread. Where it didn’t show up in the unemployment rate, but rather showed up in a significant subset of the population having paychecks that don’t go as far, which doesn’t necessarily show up in GDP, especially since government spending is a component of GDP. But it does show up when you look at those who don’t own a house and don’t have a sizable asset portfolio specifically.

This also showed up in lending conditions. Banks already went through a credit tightening cycle in 2022 similar to that of a recession, but without having an official recession (which are marked in gray on the chart):

![Senior Loan Officer Survey](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-sloos-annotated.png)

As we perform analysis in this unusual economic environment, it pays to make sure we’re treating it with fresh eyes each time. By many metrics, ironically other than the traditional developed market metrics, we’ve a*lready* had a recession, and are arguably still in the later stages of it. But instead of showing up as a credit event and unemployment increase, it showed up as inflation and major affordability problems.

-The actual unemployment rate is rising, but from a low level, and that still looks like an early recession indicator. And the Fed is preparing to cut interest rates in response to that, which is also an early recession indicator.

-Credit conditions are easing a bit but still tight, and look how they typically look shortly *after* a recession or in the later stages of one.

-The most expensive stocks currently are generally the defensive non-cyclical ones. The strong performance of the S&P 500 as of late is not because investors are crowding into bullish cyclical stocks on a strong economy; they’ve instead crowded into wide-moat “sure thing” risk-off stocks with low earnings volatility, such as big tech and companies like Costco (COST). The only cyclical area that’s performed particularly well is semiconductors, due to the momentum around AI. The overall S&P 500, inclusive of reinvested dividends, has mildly underperformed gold over the past three years. This looks kind of late-stage recession too.

I continue to view this economy as likely to surprise people in multiple directions. Being familiar with fiscal dominance and emerging market recessions can help us see some of the unusual characteristics of this cycle, and navigate through it.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

In terms of price action, bitcoin is exhibiting similar price behavior to what it did in late 2019:

![Realized Cap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-realized-cap.png)

Back in 2019, the [PlusToken Ponzi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PlusToken) had seemingly pulled forward demand for a while, resulting in bitcoin’s price doing better than it otherwise would have in the first half of the year. As the Ponzi unwound, bitcoin spent the next year stagnating in what was still a bear market at the time.

Here in 2024, it’s not quite like that, but the ETF launches seemingly pulled forward some demand and excitement, and so the bitcoin price has been consolidating without reaching new highs since March.

I continue to expect bitcoin to likely track global liquidity, which is rangebound lately but has a significant potential to break out as the Fed becomes more dovish:

![Bitcoin vs M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-bitcoin-vs-m2.png)

Regarding this topic, I commissioned a detailed report on bitcoin’s price correlation with global liquidity indicators by another analyst and advised him on it, and we’ll likely have that published in a few weeks. The preliminary findings are as I expected: the direction of bitcoin’s price over any given 12-month period has historically been more correlated to global liquidity than other major asset classes, including stocks, gold, and bonds. It’s nice to see more quantitative rigor on the observation.

Specifically, 83% of the time that global liquidity moves in a given direction up or down for any rolling 12-month period, so does bitcoin’s price. And we analyzed confounding factors (extreme valuation levels and industry-specific events) for why the other 17% of the time bitcoin can go against the trend of global liquidity.

We’re not currently in any valuation extremes that would increase the likelihood of being in the 17% of periods where bitcoin price action decoupled from liquidity conditions. An example of a useful valuation metric is the ratio between market capitalization and realized capitalization, with the former being the current market cap of all bitcoin and the latter being the on-chain cost basis of all bitcoin.

**Ark Labs Capital Raise**

Back in spring 2023, a new second-layer bitcoin payments concept called “Ark” was proposed and quickly captured a lot of mindshare in the ecosystem.

Compared to the existing Lightning network, Ark wouldn’t rely on inbound liquidity which is a major usability improvement. However, it would be less capital efficient by a wide margin. And the two networks could also be combined to improve each other.

Ark will rely on the activity of centralized payment processors, but those centralized processors don’t custody funds, and due to various time locks and such, they are trustless for the user. Thus, a centralized Ark payment processor is required to process transactions but cannot steal user funds. It is “centralized but trustless” in other words. And there can be a few separate Ark processors, and ones to rise up if existing ones cease operation.

However, the problem was that Ark required certain covenant soft forks in Bitcoin’s base layer to work economically, and so the idea kind of stalled. It also went through changes regarding who the key people working on it are.

However, developers seemingly [found a way](https://gist.github.com/RubenSomsen/a394beb1dea9e47e981216768e007454?permalink_comment_id=4633382#gistcomment-4633382) to make it economically viable without a soft fork. And a company called Ark Labs [recently raised $2.5 million](https://www.reuters.com/technology/tim-draper-leads-25-mln-funding-round-crypto-startup-ark-labs-2024-08-22/) in pre-seed funding to build on the idea.

And Ark Labs’ website explains the trade-off [here](https://arkdev.info/docs/learn/clark/):

> A final big difference between covenant-less Ark and covenant Ark is that in the VTXO tree, the commitments to child transactions are the signatures of the transactions and not the transaction templates. In Ark, child transactions are encoded into the output covenants of the parent, so they can just be deterministically created and spent as such.
>
> In covenant-less Ark, however, in order to create valid child transactions, they need to have valid signatures. These signatures obviously can’t be deterministically generated, so they have to be stored. This means that additional data has to be stored for users to have a safe exit path. For Ark, knowing all the VTXO leaves and some tree-specific parameters is sufficient, while for covenant-less Ark, the signatures of the tree, or at least for your exit branch, have to be stored alongside the other information.

The detail that I need to see more information on, is how significant those storage requirements are, which can affect whether the covenant-less option is indeed economically viable or not. I’m currently looking into those details via my deep-tech developer contacts.

If we picture the current Bitcoin development landscape as a spectrum, I’d place Ark somewhere in the middle. On one side there is Lightning, Chaumian Ecash, and Nostr as a powerful set of private payment technologies along with social payment discovery. They exist in operation and are well-studied. On the other side there is research into complex optimistic roll-ups via BitVM technology, which can enable all sorts of smart contracts but are very complex. Ark sits in the middle between those two poles, with relative simplicity in terms of how complex it is to implement and how expressive it is once it is implemented.

I don’t have a strong view on Ark’s economics yet, given how capital intensive it is and because I need to see more detail on the storage requirements. But it’s more newsworthy than a given startup raising money, because it’s about an overall protocol that multiple companies can build on, and something I’ll be following closely.

**France Arrests Telegram CEO**

Telegram, a communication platform used by hundreds of millions of people worldwide, recently had its [CEO arrested](https://apnews.com/article/france-telegram-pavel-durov-arrest-6e213d227458f330ed16e7fe221a696c) upon his arrival to France.

Telegram’s CEO, Pavel Durav is originally a Russian citizen, but has also been granted citizenship in Dubai, France, and some other countries. He lives and works in Dubai, but it was French authorities who arrested him upon his arrival.

He is facing preliminary charges for not moderating his communication platform enough, and for not turning over information when requested. Notably, the charges are very harsh- they are basically treating him as being complicit in all manner of crime that people did on Telegram amid the hundreds of millions of users. For instances of sharing illegal pornographic material on Telegram, or money laundering communications on Telegram, or any sort of illegal activity, the fact that he didn’t directly participate isn’t being considered. The fact that he provided a platform for it, didn’t sufficiently moderate it, and didn’t turn over data when requested, is argued to make him an accomplice to all of those things.

There are, of course, any number of encrypted communication methods that people use. Telegram ironically isn’t as encrypted as some others are, like Signal. European politicians in general have recently raised oppositions against allowing encrypted communications, and so it’s a broad issue in general. The fact that Durav *could* turn over more information than he has but has refused to do so, is likely more problematic for him than if he had designed Telegram with more default encryption in the first place so that he doesn’t even *have* the information to turn over, which is more like how Signal operates.

Back in the 1990s, the United States had a legal conflict regarding using encrypted communications. Phil Zimmerman developed Pretty Good Privacy or “PGP” in the early 1990s, which was free and open-source peer-to-peer encryption software. It spread internationally, and so the U.S. government launched a criminal investigation into Zimmerman for “exporting munitions without a license”. He responded by writing his full source code in a book, which was published by MIT, and claimed protection under the First Amendment by saying that he’s not exporting munitions; he’s exporting a book. He’s exporting free speech, in written form.

Other people also responded by putting code on T-shirts with big warnings that the T-shirts are dangerous munitions that can’t be exported, for the purpose of demonstrating a *reductio ad absurdum* argument. Basically at this point Pandora’s box was open, and it was so well-known how to do encryption, that the government would have to make it a thought crime to make it illegal, would have trouble enforcing it since the code is out in the wild now, and would have to figure out a way around the First Amendment to convict Zimmerman. Instead, the U.S. government ended their investigations, liberalized their regulations around encryption, and encryption became a ubiquitous aspect of paying safely online so that ecommerce could flourish in the years that followed.

Telegram is different in the sense that it’s a centralized platform, but the parallel is that there are all manner of ways, centralized or decentralized, for people to share encrypted information ever since the 1990s. And there are all manner of important reasons to do so. People understandably don’t want their sensitive business communications, or sensitive personal communications, spied on by corporations and governments. But of course, a small subset of users of encryption do so for criminal purposes.

So this opens up an important new chapter in the ongoing issues related to encryption and moderation, and incentivizes using more decentralized methods, and methods where the creator literally has no useful information to turn over.

**Brazil Bans Twitter/X**

The Brazilian government, particularly under the direction of one of their supreme justices [Alexandre de Moraes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandre_de_Moraes), has over time forced a number of social media companies to remove certain profiles or content that they determine to be disinformation. Sometimes this has included politicians.

This has been controversial, because although there is a lot of disinformation online and Brazil recently went through a period of [significant political turmoil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2023_Brazilian_Congress_attack), it can be problematic when a government gets to define what is or is not disinformation broadly. Many politicians globally are themselves sources of disinformation on social media. Who watches the watchmen?

This conflict is currently heightening, because Elon Musk and his platform Twitter/X has defied Justice Moraes on this issue. And so Brazil’s supreme court issued a ban on Twitter/X this weekend, and also installed a massive fine for any Brazilian individual that dares use a VPN to go around the ban and access Twitter/X anyway (which is rather unusual even compared to other countries that ban Twitter/X). Brazilian authorities also froze Starlink’s finances within Brazil.

There has been a mixed track record of Twitter/X and takedown requests over the years. Under prior management, Twitter as a company would routinely challenge takedown requests through the legal process, internationally. But they also performed controversial actions of banning certain content from being shared on the platform in the United States during election season.

While Musk has taken a more open approach on Twitter/X in the United States than prior management, he has actually *increased* his compliance with international takedown requests, putting up *less* of an opposition to Turkey, Germany, India, and certain others. That has kind of gone against the free speech brand that Musk has sculpted in the United States. As [Rest of World](https://restofworld.org/2023/elon-musk-twitter-government-orders/) reports:

> -Since Musk took ownership, the company has received 971 government demands, and fully complied with 808 of them.
>
> -Before Musk, Twitter’s full compliance rate hovered around 50%; since the takeover, it is over 80%.
>
> Basically, there are certain conflicts of interest associated with having a rocket company, a car company, and social media company. If you were running all three, and wanted to sell rockets and cars to Turkey, Germany, and India, would you readily adhere to their takedown requests on your much smaller and unprofitable social media company, or would you battle them every step of the way whenever it comes up? So far for these types of countries, Musk’s Twitter/X complies with takedown requests more than prior management did.

But Musk has chosen to be more defiant in Brazil’s case, and battling them more in the court of public opinion than in law.

It remains to be seen how this will turn out. How will Brazilians in general feel about losing access to Twitter/X and Starlink? Will this be too much of an own-goal for Brazil’s government to maintain, or will they be able to maintain these bans and force Musk to back down? Already there are Brazilians using VPNs to access Twitter/X, and it can be hard to identify them if their account is primarily used to read rather than write, or if they write with a pseudonym. According to Google Trends, searches for “VPN” in Brazil have spiked since the ban was announced:

![VPN Searches](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-vpn-search.png)

The reason I include these Telegram and Twitter/X news items in the digital asset section, is that they relate to Nostr and adjacent technologies. Nostr is a decentralized social media protocol, and with some elements of encryption as well. Jack Dorsey, the co-founder and former CEO of Twitter, has been a big advocate of Nostr for the past couple of years, and has donated a lot of money to open-source Nostr developers to further enhance it.

I wrote a detailed “Nostr 101” article [here](https://www.lynalden.com/the-power-of-nostr/).

Unlike a centralized platform, Nostr is an open-source communication protocol. So, multiple different platforms can build upon it, and be interoperable with each other. And while it’s not a blockchain itself, it does integrate the Bitcoin network so that anyone using the Nostr protocol can attach a Lightning wallet to their profile to send and receive payments to others in the ecosystem.

Nostr received a sizable boost of new activity in the days following the Telegram CEO being arrested, and seems to be getting another small one as Twitter/X goes dark in Brazil. Notably, Nostr’s pseudonymous creator is himself Brazilian.

## Deep Dive: Emerging Markets

In my [August 4th report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2024-8-4/), I focused on the possibility of some asset rotations in the years ahead. If you haven’t read that report, I suggest doing so.

For many years, there have been a lot of investors and analysts looking for a major asset rotation too early, so we have to be mindful of timing. The way that I currently phrase this is that it’s the first significant catalyst for a rotation since 2019, but it’s not assured. We have to look for follow-through.

-There are periods of time where growth stocks outperform value stocks, and vice versa. And recent years have been quite volatile on that front:

![Growth vs Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-growth-value.png)

-There are periods of time where developed markets outperform emerging markets, and vice versa:

![Emerging vs Developed](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-emerging-developed.png)

-There are periods of time where equities in general outperform gold, and vice versa:

![SPX vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-spx-gold.png)

-There are periods of time where the dollar strengthens vs other currencies, and periods of time where it weakens:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-dollar-index.png)

And they tend to approximately rotate together. Decades where American growth stocks do well and the dollar is strong tend to be the decades where value stocks, emerging market stocks, and gold/commodities collectively underperform. And then when an asset rotation occurs, American growth stocks (which at that point are quite expensive along with a strong dollar) tend to stagnate and the dollar weakens, while all that other stuff has a period of outperformance for a while.

And a lot of it is tied to capital flows and the strength of the dollar. Emerging markets are responsible for the majority of marginal economic growth and commodity demand growth in the world, since they collectively have a larger population and have far less per-capita commodity usage as a starting point compared to developed markets. Emerging markets also have a lot of dollar-denominated debt, which is lent to them from lenders across the world that want to minimize local currency risk when lending globally.

So when the dollar strengthens relative to other currencies, it hurts most emerging markets, because their dollar-denominated liabilities begin appreciating relative to their assets and cashflows. Their economies get impaired, reducing their commodity demand and growth prospects. That hurts commodity prices, and many emerging markets are themselves commodity producers, so that further hurts them too. Global investors then pull capital out of emerging markets and stick them into U.S. capital markets for safety, which further strengthens the dollar and hurts emerging market currencies. The dollar gets very strong, and U.S. equity valuations become very high. It’s a vicious cycle for emerging markets.

But eventually that runs out of steam. In 1971 it was because the United States defaulted on its gold peg, having created a lot of dollars while its gold reserves dwindled. In 1985 it was because the United States initiated the Plaza Accord to weaken the dollar in an attempt to defend its troubled export industries. In 2001 it was because the U.S. encountered a recession and cut interest rates, at a time when capital from around the world was heavily stuffed into its equity market at very high valuations.

When we use Brazil as a quintessential example, we can see how much their dollar-denominated GDP suffered during the three times where the dollar index spiked significantly:

![Brazil GDP Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-brazil-gdp-annotated.png)

We can also see this in more tangible metrics. In two of those three dollar spikes, Brazil’s nationwide oil consumption went down for many years, indicating severe economic distress:

![Brazil Oil Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-brazil-oil-annotated.png)

A lot of investors have been early when looking for the latest asset rotation, including myself. The previous window for a rotation was back in 2019. The U.S. economy began slowing, the Fed cut interest rates, and they hit the domestic banking liquidity floor with the September 2019 repo spike, and went back to balance sheet growth. This could have led to a rotation.

However, the pandemic hit in 2020, global economies and markets were locked down and scrambled, developing countries generally had fewer tools to handle this than the United States, Europe’s energy crisis was exacerbated by the Russian invasion of Ukraine, and the big winners of the pulled-forward trend of remote work were the American software companies. And then as the Fed began to fight back against inflation in 2022, it sharply strengthened the dollar index.

The Fed has indicated that they will begin cutting interest rates here in September 2024. And we do see significant weakening of the U.S. economy, or at least it has become a two-speed economy with significant deterioration in the economic prospects of people in the lower half of the income spectrum that don’t own a lot of assets. The dollar index has rolled over from its highs, and might have plenty of room to decline if indeed we get a substantial rate-cutting cycle.

As the Fed lowers interest rates and as the dollar eases a bit, it gives emerging markets a lot of breathing room. And as they start to perform better, they can attract more global capital, and experience a multi-year virtuous cycle and period of growth. Capital flows contribute to fundamentals, which then entice more capital flows.

So this is a period of time to be locked in and focused to see if we do get follow through with a rotation, or not. Here in late 2024, the catalyst is a slowing U.S. economy and expectations of rate cuts. In 2025, an end to the Fed’s quantitative tightening could be a second catalyst to provide more follow-through. I don’t expect this story to unfold in one month or one quarter; it’s a process to watch over multiple quarters to see if we stay on course or not.

**Potential Rotation: Latin American Equities and Gold**

If we look at the S&P 500 to Latin American equities on a total return basis (i.e. inclusive of reinvested dividends), Latin American equities reached a low against the S&P 500 back in December 2021, and haven’t made a lower-low since then. Instead it has now been almost three years of sideways choppy relative performance between the two:

![ILF vs SPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-ilf-spy.png)

If we look at Brazil again, as the largest economy in this mix, they’ve got 4.5% inflation but a whopping 10.5% interest rate, giving them some of the highest real rates in the world currently. Their central bank has been in currency-protection mode for years, holding the economy down like a coiled spring, and as the Fed eases their monetary policy it gives Brazil a lot of room to change policy.

Similarly, the S&P 500 peaked against gold on a total return basis in December 2021 and has mildly underperformed since:

![SPX vs Gold Total Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-spx-gold-total-returns.png)

**Delayed Rotation: Chinese Equities**

Broader emerging markets haven’t shown signs of a rotation yet, since Chinese equities are a big weighting in the index and they’ve been a self-imposed limiter in recent years.

China’s government cracked down on its own tech companies a few years ago, bringing them to extremely cheap valuations, and resulting in the first net capital exodus from China in decades. Their housing sector continues to deleverage, and they’ve been reluctant to stimulate fiscally, opting to protect their bond market over their equity market.

Whether one looks at earnings multiple or books values, a lot of the Chinese tech, media, and ecommerce stocks are extraordinarily cheap, as global investors don’t want to touch them with a ten foot pole:

![China Tech Stocks](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-china-tech.png)

Isolating JD.com (JD) in particular, compared to a few years ago it’s now profitable, cheap, paying a dividend, and rapidly buying back shares, and yet with a near record low stock price:

![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-jd-fastgraph.png)

Investors don’t really want it at any price, because they are afraid of getting zeroed out of a position much like how American investors got effectively zeroed out of otherwise high-quality Russian stocks. So when I analyze JD, I am extremely bullish over the next 3-5 years as a 90% base case, but then 10% concerned about the investment effectively getting zeroed out as the company continues to perform fine enough, due to sanctions or confiscation. And there’s a lot of global capital looking at it the same way, along with many other similar stocks, and deciding to invest minimally or not at all.

I think the bottom in Chinese stock indices is likely already in, and there are myriad catalysts that could propel them higher in the years ahead, including thawing tensions between the United States and China, or significant stimulus from the Chinese government. However, there’s no sign as of yet that Chinese stocks have bottomed vs American stocks, as the more relevant comparison. So that’s still in a wait-and-see mode.

## Final Thoughts: Gold Tactically Overbought?

I’ve been bullish on gold for a while, although it is beginning to show signs of tactical overbought status. These levels of relative strength often lead to a price consolidation:

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-9-1-gold.png)

During gold’s bull run in the 2000s decade, it reached overbought status a number of times, would consolidate a bit, and then continue to keep pushing up higher. That’s how most big bull runs work, and for example that’s what the S&P 500 has done throughout the 2010s decade and half way into the 2020s here as well.

I don’t see any signs that gold is in bubble territory like it was in 2011, so I plan to remain long gold as part of a portfolio. We could push a bit higher and then consolidate for a time, or consolidate directly from these levels, but either way I remain structurally bullish as the U.S. Federal Reserve begins a rate-cutting cycle, as U.S. fiscal deficits continue to run at about 7% of GDP structurally, and as global central banks diversify their holdings around the margins including substantially into gold as an allocation.
