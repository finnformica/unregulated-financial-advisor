## Macro View

As I’ve been covering for a while, most of the world is firmly past the Delta variant peak of the virus:

![Global COVID-19](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-covid.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

Various countries, however, continue to respond rather differently to the seasonal waves of the virus. Some countries like Australia continue to pursue a stringent lockdown policy, while other countries like Norway have opened up and are shifting their policy mix to treat COVID-19 as an endemic seasonal respiratory disease that is more dangerous than the flu but not something to prioritize the bulk of society around.

The overall death rate of the virus continues to mildly decrease with time, as people build some immunity from either having recovered from it, or having been vaccinated against it, or both. In addition, treatment methods continue to improve.

My expectation is that it will become less common next year than it is now for countries to pursue strict lockdowns or vaccine passports, although I’ll adjust that view if the trend doesn’t head in that direction.

Merck (MRK) [recently announced](https://apnews.com/article/merck-says-experimental-covid-pill-cuts-worst-effects-a9a2245fdcee324f6bbd776a0fffcc60) that a new oral pill trial was very effective at reducing hospitalization rates of infected patients. If someone is infected with COVID-19 and takes this pill in the early/moderate stage of the virus, the results showed that it can cut the hospitalization rate in half vs the placebo group, and sharply reduce the death rate. They had to end the trial early after a sufficient sample was reached, because the data were one-sided enough that it was no longer considered ethical to give patients the placebo, which is a common policy for very clear and one-sided studies involving health. Other companies are working on similar treatments.

There are already intravenous methods that can sharply reduce symptoms, but they are expensive and cumbersome. A pill that can cut the hospitalization rate from say 1% to 0.5%, is great. Over time, humanity will have more and more tools that people can use to reduce the severity of what has become an endemic virus, and partial immunity should continue to build up as well.

**The Egypt COVID-19 Situation**

I’ve been visiting family in Egypt for a little over two weeks now, keeping track of markets and maintaining my researching/writing schedule, but declining new interviews due to a less stable internet connection and less spare time.

I’ve used this as an opportunity get an on-the-ground read of how a developing country is experiencing and interpreting the virus, and then confirm or contrast that with official data where possible.

Only about 5% of the Egyptian population is considered fully vaccinated, which is much lower than the global average. Among my family and friends here (who have more resources than average for Egypt), about 3/4ths of them are vaccinated, and they all basically have different vaccinations ranging from Pfizer to J&J to AstraZeneca to Sinovac, based on what was first available to them.

For the family and friends that are not yet vaccinated, it was mainly because the vaccine was not made available to them yet. Specifically, an elderly couple and their two sons, one of which is a physician, have still not been able to access a vaccine, despite being high-priority candidates for it based on age and/or profession. Meanwhile, I know plenty of young people in the country that were able to access a vaccine many months ago. It’s very inconsistent here; the vaccine roll-out has not been well-organized.

I don’t have much confidence in the accuracy of Egypt’s Ministry of Health COVID-19 data (which is not necessarily their fault; I don’t know how I would accurately track virus cases in a 100 million population country with $3k in GDP per capita either), but according to that official source ([collected here by Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/country/egypt/)), Egyptian cases and deaths have been below average by global per capita standards. They have reported about 18,000 deaths from the virus out of a population of 100 million people (0.018% of the population killed in 18 months or so), compared to Indonesia with about 145,000 official deaths and 270 million people (0.054% of population killed), and the US with about 720,000 official deaths and 330 million people (0.220% of population killed). As a starting point, if I multiply official Egyptian virus deaths by 3x based on the guess that they are under-counting, it would be similar to Indonesia, and about a fourth the death rate of the US.

While I don’t think anyone knows what the precise death rate is here, Egypt’s young population (median age 24) likely plays a role in Egypt not having a particularly stand-out death rate. The warm and dry environment may play a role as well, but I’m not a medical professional so I really have no idea.

Among family and friends I directly know in this country, which is in the ballpark of 25+ people, three of them got symptomatic COVID-19 at some point over the past 18 months and recovered fully. Those three are in the same household; one of them received the virus and spread it to the other two. I also asked some of them about their friends and co-workers, to increase the size of my anecdotal sample.

The family member who originally received the virus is an engineering manager, and said that nearly a third of his perhaps 200-person office got COVID-19 at some point over the past 18 months, and two of them passed away (one in his forties and one in his sixties, both with pre-existing conditions), which is significantly above-trend. That seems to have been a big cluster, and it was in one of the early waves of the virus when treatment methods were not as clear. Right now he says there are no cases there.

Another family member said that her friend group of 5 young women were all infected and recovered a while ago, likely from one of them having received it from a cousin.

Speaking to a friend who is an advertising executive (and who did not get the virus), he said that probably half of his office was infected by the virus at one point or another over the past 18 months. There were no deaths out of those dozens of people, but one case became pretty serious for a person who had a habit of smoking hookah.

Speaking to a relative that works in logistics, he said that out of his 70-person office, about half got the virus at some point. One of them was hospitalized for a period of time as a precaution. Currently he said his office doesn’t really have any cases.

The official case count in Egypt seems extremely under-reported compared to this decent sample of anecdotes, although as previously-mentioned, Egypt’s death rate hasn’t been notable relative to global peers, either in terms of official numbers or anecdotally in terms of hospital strain, global news reporting, etc. Looking at the bright side, the population in aggregate here likely has significant natural immunity by now.

I spoke with a physician family member about the rate of hospitalizations. Like other countries, Egypt had a few waves of hospital capacity issues whenever cases spiked seasonally, but nothing that stands out against global peers in terms of severity. It hasn’t really been one of the countries to make global headlines about its virus situation, and the country did certain rounds of partial lockdowns in 2020 like many other countries. The physician has pointed out that the country currently is in an uptick of cases (which I reported in my prior premium report), and he thinks the country is getting close to the top of that wave.

Having been in the cities of Cairo and Hurghada so far (heading to Alexandria tomorrow), life is currently normal here (similar to how it was when I last came in 2019), with minimal change in consumer behavior. Restaurants and hotels are packed, the Cairo International Airport seemed as busy as I remember from last time, and many Europeans are flying into Hurghada to vacation at Egypt’s various Red Sea resorts. Restaurant waiters and hotel clerks typically wear masks, but few other people do. Some white collar professionals work from home a bit more than they used to where possible, while other types of workers need to do in-person work as normal. A vast network of bridges was completed throughout Cairo since last time I was here, and rapid real estate development continues, so construction personnel have certainly been hard at work during 2020 and 2021.

**Inflation and Interest Rates**

Inflation remains pretty sticky in many parts of the world, fueled by rising energy prices, supply chain limitations, and rent increases.

Natural gas and coal prices are at prices not seen since 2008. Oil isn’t quite at that relative level but it’s bouncing around multi-year highs. The Baltic Dry Index, which measures the price of moving raw materials by sea, is also at highs not seen since 2008.

Overall, the CRB index that measures a basket of commodities, is at highs not seen since 2014:

![CRB Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-crb-index.png)

Europe is facing a [natural gas shortage](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-09-27/europe-s-energy-crisis-is-about-to-go-global-as-gas-prices-soar), driving up electricity prices. China has rolling electricity outages, [in significant part due to a coal shortage](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-09-28/china-s-coal-shortage-means-higher-prices-for-the-whole-world).

In the US, rent prices have been rising rapidly:

*Chart Source: [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-09-29/u-s-rents-are-accelerating-at-shocking-rates-of-more-than-10)*

Upcoming rent inflation is a theme I’ve been predicting and writing about since early summer of this year, and now we’re in the thick of it.

Various real estate websites show double-digit rent inflation, but as measured in the consumer price index, it’s still small (but turning up with a clear bottom):

If that’s true, and I think directionally that is pretty true, that will keep broad year-over-year CPI at rather elevated levels for a while.

Meanwhile, job openings in the US are at record highs, with almost 11 million open positions:

![Job Openings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-job-openings.png)

There is a lot of evidence for mounting wage inflation in the years ahead. Many blue collar job wages in particular have not kept up with the cost living, and have not been attracting enough applications. As I discussed in my September 5th premium report, this is true even among US states that terminated extended unemployment benefits several months ago.

The impacts of [partial de-globalization](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2021-newsletter/), if sustained, will give domestic workers more leverage for wage increases.

The UK has a [severe shortage of truck drivers](https://www.politico.eu/article/europes-looming-truck-driver-shortage-trucker-haulier-brexit-hgv/), which is limiting its ability to keep petrol stations supplied. It’s not a very attractive job, and not well-paid enough to attract young people to the profession. A truck driver’s salary can’t buy what it used to, decades ago. The country had a truck driver shortage back in 2018 and 2019, but it has grown more acute since the 2020/2021 economic disruptions. The government has tapped into its soldier ranks to potentially fill in for drivers, which basically subsidizes those corporations with taxpayer dollars rather than forcing them to come to a wage equilibrium with the market.

The European Union and the United States also had truck driver shortages prior to the pandemic, and still do.

For the theme of wage inflation and unprecedented job openings in the United States, Luke Gromen and Preston Pysh [had an interesting discussion](https://www.theinvestorspodcast.com/bitcoin-fundamentals/luke-gromen-on-china-evergrande-macro-bitcoin/) on the Investor’s Podcast the other day, and Luke provided an anecdote:

He can pass any drug tests you want. God love him. At least he better be able to… no I’m just kidding, I know he can. The clearing wage, I think has moved in some areas where just this marginal supply of labor… I think, in some places, you’re seeing a supply, demand mismatch of labor.

The Atlanta Fed currently [places nominal wage growth](https://www.atlantafed.org/chcs/wage-growth-tracker) at about 3-4% pear year overall for the past several years, which works if official inflation is around 2%, but doesn’t work if official inflation is over 5% as it is during this past year. If official inflation was still measured the way it used to be in the 1980s, the current year-over-year percent change would be in the low double-digits.

In comparison, rents have also gone up 3-4% pear year over the past several years, stagnated for about a year in 2020, and now are making up for lost ground with double-digit 2021 rent growth according to most real estate sites (but not yet according to official inflation statistics).

**Growth vs Value Equities**

The past several months have been challenging for investors. Many stocks have been weak, precious metals have been weak, bitcoin and cryptos have been weak, and bond yields have drifted up (meaning bond prices have been weak).

The few areas of strength have been in some of the value equity sectors, such as financials and energy producers. Energy producers are benefiting from higher energy prices (but remain very cheap, most investors don’t seem to expect sustained high energy prices, and many pools of capital have divested for ESG reasons), and financials are benefiting from renewed yield curve steepening. Higher long-duration bond yields also put theoretical downward valuation pressure on highly-valued growth stocks, by raising the risk-free discount rate.

Here’s the long-term ratio of US value stock performance to US growth stock performance, with both a long-term view on top and a 3-year view on the bottom. Whenever the line is going up, it means value stocks (as defined by Russell) are outperforming, and whenever the line is going down, it means growth stocks (as defined by Russell) are outperforming:

![Value vs Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-value-vs-growth.png)

After an already long stretch of growth stock outperformance from 2007 through 2019, the pandemic lockdowns triggered an acceleration in growth stock outperformance in the first half of 2020 as tech companies (which are often growth-oriented) did well while physical (which are often value-oriented) companies suffered.

Starting in autumn 2020, however, value equities started to catch a bid, and outperformed into spring 2021 as long-duration Treasury yields rose as well. However, by spring 2021, Treasury yields rolled over, and value stocks began a temporary period of underperformance vs growth stocks. This looks like it may have bottomed with a higher-low in September 2021, with value stocks starting another leg up relative to growth stocks.

## Uranium Note

After going parabolic for a period of time, uranium has taken a breather:

![Uranium Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-uranium.png)

The Sprott Physical Uranium Trust (SRUUF) has reached a local period of investor exhaustion, with investors no longer sustaining a high premium over net asset value. The premium has been in the -1.5% to +1.5% range for much of the past couple of weeks. If the premium remains low or goes negative, it means Sprott can’t buy more uranium, and can’t keep the supply squeeze going.

As of Friday, the premium closed at over 4%, so the trade is not necessarily finished yet.

Overall, I am neutral on uranium at this time, after having been bullish since October 2020 and re-iterating my bullish view a month ago in early September 2021. The long-term prospects remain favorable, as it remains somewhat below the cost of most new production, and there is less uranium produced by mines than is consumed by utilities each year. The intermediate-term prospects, on the other hand, are not something I have much of a view on unless Sprott’s premium to NAV remains positive and Sprott initiates another round of purchases.

I continue to like uranium as a small and rather uncorrelated portfolio position.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin reached over $52k as a local top on September 6th, but then corrected to around $40k and has since bounced sharply from there:

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-btc-chart.png)The bearish view is that bitcoin has put a big double-top or bull trap in place, and could go down considerably from here.

The bullish view is that bitcoin has put in a higher low ($40k in September 2021 compared to the $29k lows of July 2021), for continued strength late into this year and then into 2022.

I have no strong view for its performance in the near term, but if it breaks over $53k, that’s a bullish price confirmation of new local higher-highs. On the other hand, a sustained drop below $40k would be a break through what has become a massive support level in terms of both the price chart and on-chain accumulation volume (cost basis). Overall, I remain structurally bullish on a multi-year timeframe, since going long in April 2020.

One of the bullish catalysts for bitcoin relates to exchange supply. Since inception of the original bitcoin/crypto exchanges, the number of bitcoins on exchanges has steadily increased… until March 2020 when it peaked at around 3.1 million coins (out of a fully-diluted total of 21 million coins). It has been in a decline most of the time since then, as shown by the orange line below:

![BTC Exchange Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-btc-exchange-balance.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

Since March 2020, the number of bitcoins on exchanges has been in its longest downtrend ever, at 18 months of ongoing low totals on exchanges (currently there are fewer than 2.5 million bitcoins on exchanges). This has never happened before in any prior bull or bear market, which places bitcoin in unprecedented territory and potentially sets up a structural supply crunch, which is bullish.

This is happening for a few reasons. The number of new bitcoins generated per year is decreasing, due to block subsidy halvings, and has reached an inflection point in terms of structural exchange balances. By the end of this year, 90% of all bitcoin issuance from inception through the final bitcoin mined in 2140, will have already been mined. Meanwhile, the culture among bitcoin investors has shifted to some extent towards dollar-cost averaging and treating their bitcoins like a savings account rather than a speculative trading vehicle, which persistently removes bitcoins from exchanges into cold storage, resistant to selling.

The Lightning Network, the open source second layer that consists of Bitcoin base layer smart contracts for faster payments, continues its period of rapid growth:

![Lightning Capacity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-lightning-capacity.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/lightning-network-capacity/)*

El Salvador’s president has been tweeting out stats for Chivo Wallet, which is El Savador’s government implementation of a combined dollar and Bitcoin/Lightning mobile wallet. I don’t fully trust the stats but the claim is that there are over 2.7 million users within the first month (roughly 40% of El Salvador’s population), and that the system is processing over $1 million in remittance payments per day.

To the extent that these numbers are accurate, it means that Chivo Wallet has taken about 5% of El Salvador’s remittance market in the first month of launch, and is used for a lot of domestic payments. Major international companies like McDonalds and Starbucks now accept Bitcoin/Lightning payments in El Salvador, since doing so is part of the country’s new legal tender law. External data providers indeed show that Chivo Wallet is currently the most popular app in El Salvador for both Google and Apple app platforms.

In other news, China outright banned cryptocurrencies again (which they have done multiple times since 2013), while Twitter launched an integration with the Lightning Network that allows users to send tips to each other using Bitcoin/Lightning. The implementation is still being rolled out; tip receivers use custodial Strike wallets and are limited to a couple countries, while senders can use any Lightning wallet including open-source non-custodial implementations from anywhere.

As a last bit of news, Fed chairman Jerome Powell was asked in front of Congress if he had any intention to ban cryptocurrencies in the US, and he said no. He also re-iterated a view that stablecoins need to be regulated.

Meanwhile, SEC chairman Gary Gensler has been cracking down on the broad crypto space, to apply existing securities laws against many of them as unregistered securities. Crypto projects that raised capital and used that capital for the development of the project, are all at high risk of being considered unregistered securities since they pass the [Howey Test](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/howey-test.asp). Bitcoin, since it raised no capital and has no central development organization, doesn’t pass the Howey Test, and is considered a commodity rather than security under applicable securities laws.

Basically, broad crypto-related companies face a lot of legal challenges at the moment, while bitcoin-only companies are generally in the clear in the US under the current legal environment.

This topic of regulation came up in an [interview between Raoul Pal and Michael Saylor](https://www.realvision.com/shows/raoul-pal-adventures-in-crypto/videos/taking-the-sec-very-seriously), which covered some of the legal perspectives in the second half. Pal is a major ether bull (and broadly bullish on the crypto ecosystem as a whole) while Saylor is a major bitcoin bull in particular. In that second half, Saylor describes how, given his position at the head of a public company and with general counsel to consult with, securities laws are pretty clear and that many cryptos (outside of bitcoin and a few others) are at risk of further regulation.

## Ethereum Note

Similar to bitcoin, ether has been in a choppy price environment and has a supply squeeze going on in the background. There are now over 7.8 million ether locked up in the one-way Ethereum 2.0 staking contract. So, ether balances on exchanges continue to decline, much like bitcoin. All else equal, that’s bullish for price in the intermediate-term.

The volume of non-fungible token art on Ethereum is pretty flat, after the big August 2021 spike:

![OpenSea Volume](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-opensea.png)

*Chart Source: [Dune Analytics](https://dune.xyz/rchen8/opensea)*

Overall, the price action of ether will probably correlate with bitcoin for the final quarter of 2021 and into 2022. A key risk for Ethereum’s ecosystem is that Ethereum itself passes the Howey Test as a security (but at this stage could potentially be grandfathered in as no longer a security), and many of the projects that run on Ethereum pass the Howey Test (and are at risk of being considered securities). Unlike the Bitcoin ecosystem, there are pretty easy attack surfaces (development organizations) for regulators to go after for many of those projects.

Recently, the fifth-largest DeFi project on Ethereum, known as Compound, suffered a bug that gave out tens of millions of dollars worth of tokens to a subset of users from Compound’s comptroller contract. Any time you hear about a “DeFi hack”, it’s actually not a hack in the traditional sense; it’s a poorly-written smart contract with loopholes in it that someone exploited or that behaved erratically on its own. Software frequently has bugs; and DeFi smart contracts happen to involve a lot of money, with public attack surfaces.

Although Compound is an open-source project, it is tightly associated with Compound Labs, a VC-backed centralized firm. That’s been an ongoing criticism of this portion of the crypto ecosystem; most decentralized finance protocols have clearly centralized hubs behind them. Bitcoin proponents often criticize these as being “decentralized in name only”, rather than being truly decentralized with no top-down control like is the case with the Bitcoin protocol.

The founder/CEO of Compound Labs put out a threat involving the IRS to encourage people to give Compound’s funds back:

![Compound Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-compound-ceo.png)

There are a few angles of interest there. The first is that some/many of the recipients could be non-US citizens/residents, for which the IRS has little relevance. A second is that if users received it, it’s already taxable, and if they give it back, it’s potentially still taxable and that just makes it even more complicated for them. A third is that Mr. Leshner criticized other DeFi projects for involving real-world legal frameworks and not respecting the concept of “code is law”, but then seemingly stepped back from that when it happened to his own project. Here was him from 3.5 months ago:

![Compound CEO Contradiction](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-compound-ceo-2.png)

“Code is law” refers to the idea that however a smart contract executes (even if different from how it was intended due to a bug), then that outcome simply is what it is. The argument from that angle is that blockchain-based decentralized systems are meant to be strictly self-sufficient, not relying on courts for arbitration (and indeed, not being arbitrable even if attempted), and not being reversible by centralized third parties. Otherwise, as the argument continues, these smart contract projects are not really decentralized in the first place, are they?

In practice, code is generally not law within the altcoin smart contract ecosystem. One of Ethereum’s first smart contracts in 2016 raised $150 million and [was almost immediately exploited](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_DAO_(organization)) with the funds taken by exploiters. The Ethereum base layer was centralized enough, however, that the lead developers performed a hard fork to a new chain where that exploit never happened, like using a time machine to get the funds back. The original chain where code is law and the exploit remained in effect, became Ethereum Classic, and the forked chain to reverse the exploit became the consensus Ethereum chain. So, code was not law for what became the main Ethereum chain; lead developers were able to reverse it on the base layer and the community was willing to fork into that new reality. But defenders of Ethereum correctly point out that this was early on in Ethereum’s lifecycle. More recently and relevantly, many DeFi exploits have occurred since then, and the owners of those partially-centralized “decentralized finance” protocols often reach out to exchanges or threaten legal action to try to get the funds back, or sometimes provide job offers or otherwise try to coax the people to give the funds back voluntarily.

In this case, the CEO of Compound Labs seemed to take the stance that code is law when discussing another project, with the argument that courts and politicians shouldn’t be involved in what are supposed to be decentralized smart-contract projects. But 3.5 months later when his protocol lost funds due to a bug, his immediate reaction was to threaten to bring a government agency into the matter, desperate to get the funds back even though that’s simply how the code executed. With that course of action not particularly well-received (and without much legal recourse anyway), he shifted to relying on goodwill to coax recipients to voluntarily give the funds back. Community outreach makes sense within a “code is law” framework.

Often, DeFi protocols experience bugs that result in users losing funds. Sometimes, like in this case, a subset of them accidentally are given funds from someone else (in this case, Compound’s comptroller contract).

It’s not hard to see why the SEC has their eye on this space; many developers and VC-backed companies raise capital via tokens (many of which are likely unregistered securities), and then perform development with risk of loss for users from bugs. Billions of dollars get locked up on what are basically partially-centralized beta-mode projects.

Regulators have not kept up with fast-moving blockchain development, so there has been a period of asymmetry where token-based companies have had less oversight than traditional companies. Depending on one’s point of view, some people expect traditional companies to increasingly go the blockchain route and be less-regulated, while others expect blockchain companies to become more regulated like traditional companies are.

## Financial Sector Update

Long-duration sovereign bond yields are rising in many markets, so it’s time to revisit the financial sector.

![10 Year Treasury Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-10-year-yield.png)

After correcting down for about four months, the 10-year Treasury has been on the rise again for over two months, including a pretty sharp move recently. The Delta virus wave is receding, and energy shortages combined with rent prices are contributing to rather high inflation for a longer period than most people with a deflationist view thought would happen by this point in time.

Banks make money from borrowing at low rates and lending at higher rates, so a steepening yield curve (the difference between long-duration sovereign bond yields and short-duration sovereign bond yields) affects their profitability quite a bit.

The bullish case for the financial sector is that the yield curve will likely continue to steepen, that we’re still early-cycle as far as lending is concerned for this credit cycle, that banks are stuffed full of cash, and that bank valuations are reasonable compared to equities in general while they pay out good dividends as well.

## Final Thoughts

A lot of people rightly say that the “stock market is expensive”. While technically correct, that’s a rather incomplete statement.

By many metrics, the S&P 500 is indeed very expensive based on the cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio, price/sales ratio, dividend yield, the Q ratio, or market capitalization to GDP. The combination of passive indexing, the dollar cycle, and other factors, have piled record amounts of capital into US large growth stocks, and by extension, the broad S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100.

However, after a long period of US tech stock outperformance, many value sectors such as healthcare, energy, financials, and industrials are at average or below-average valuations, even for ones that have significantly improved their balance sheets and other fundamental areas in recent years. Looking at those sectors internationally, including for hidden tech stocks in those sectors like Sberbank which is disguised as just a bank, they’re even cheaper. They are generally dismissed as value traps, even if their earnings are growing and their balance sheets are strengthening, and they are priced at valuations where growth isn’t even necessary to provide decent returns.

So basically, investors are saying, “I don’t want to own energy companies (ESG concerns), banks (credit risk), healthcare (regulation risk), most things outside of the United States in general (jurisdictional risk), or basically anything that underperformed during the past decade”, and then finding that nothing that meets their increasingly small circle of possible investments mainly revolving around tech and consumer discretionary companies, is cheap.

Well, when everyone is piled into a specific subset of the market, of course that subset isn’t cheap.

My next public newsletter will likely be a revisit of that topic.

For example, EOG Resources (EOG) has substantially strengthened its balance sheet in recent years, and trades at a historically attractive valuation:

![EOG FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-eog-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

![EOG YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-3-eog-ychart.png)

Many investors don’t want to own energy companies, but are happy to fill their car up with gasoline, and happy to turn on their lights or plug in their EV to an electrical grid that is in most places powered in part by coal or natural gas.

For a variety of reasons at the moment, there are multi-commodity energy shortages across multiple countries. For the energy sector as a whole, the investment and political narratives have gotten ahead of the engineering capabilities in terms of solar and wind technology to replace these energy sources. There haven’t yet been enough electrical energy storage breakthroughs, electrical grid upgrades, or material science improvements, to replace fossil fuels for multiple purposes, even though fossil fuel companies were priced as though they will be phased out in a few years.

And for the record I’m an environmentalist; part of why I majored in electrical engineering 16 years ago was due to an interest in clean energy. However, a political/investor goal without a realistic and specific *technical* plan to reach that goal, usually doesn’t work out very well, and the public initiatives around this subject have been more about managing appearances than actually getting full solutions into place.

While I do still like some expensive tech stocks, I continue to prefer US and international value stocks, and particularly ones that also have decent scores for quality in terms of balance sheet strength and management. And I think many investors under-appreciate the jurisdictional risk that is present in the US over the next 5-10 years.
