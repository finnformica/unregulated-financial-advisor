## Macro View

**US Business Cycle**

Traditional measures of the economy continue to look mixed.

The Atlanta Fed’s estimator tool continues to suggest healthy GDP growth here in Q3:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-gdpnow.png)

And yet the aggregate assessment of leading indicators continues to flash a mild recession, which it has been doing for the better part of three years now without any NBER-defined recession having occurred. This index includes weekly manufacturing hours, weekly jobless claims, various indices of new orders, building permits, a leading credit index, interest rate spreads, consumer expectations of business conditions, and stock prices.

![Conference Board LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-lei.png)

![Lebowski Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-lebowski-meme.png)

The point I’ve been emphasizing for a while is that amid fiscal dominance, the economy functions differently than under monetary dominance, which is what everyone is trained to analyze. The baseline runs hotter, and those who are on the receiving side of deficits tend to be fine, while those who are not tend to suffer unless they’re able to position for it.

The private sector in isolation is indeed pretty weak by many metrics. The housing sector and commercial real estate sector have both pretty much stalled, ISM manufacturing is weak, bank lending is tepid, etc.

However, the 6-7% of GDP fiscal deficits (~$2 trillion nominally) serve as a continuous background stimulus. This flows to retirees via Social Security who spend it into the consumer economy, flows to the health care sector via Medicare and Medicaid (including healthcare employees who spend it into the non-healthcare economy), flows to Defense (including soldiers and defense employees who spend it into the non-defense economy), and interest expense (which flows to individuals and financial institutions, some of which gets spent into the non-financial economy).

The $300+ billion in new annualized tariff revenue reduces that slightly (which is why I monitor it pretty closely), but only by around 15%, which is why the overall effect remains.

Due to that fiscal dominance environment, I’ve been more focused on the labor market. If the labor market remains in decent shape along with the deficits, then it tends to override the other weak variables. It’s like Thanos and his Infinity Stones (deficits and labor market) beating the Avengers (basically every other indicator). And for a while, the labor market has been fine.

Well, these past two weeks, the labor market flashed a warning. Initial jobless claims (which are one of the fastest pulse checks we have available) suddenly spiked to a 3+ year high:

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-initial-claims.png)

Now, that’s still a reasonably low number. Roughly 300,000 is the level that would flash more of a red light, whereas this is just a yellow light. Thanos took a punch, maybe lost a stone, but otherwise is still winning for now. But it’s worth watching more closely.

**Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity in the US is drawing down as the Treasury refills its cash account, as expected. I discussed this in the prior report:

> Domestic liquidity currently remains rangebound. The overnight reverse repo facility is mostly drained (which puts liquidity back into the system), the Treasury General Account is up to $590 billion as it refills (which pulls liquidity out of the system), and the Fed continues to perform gradual quantitative tightening (which pulls liquidity out of the system.
>
> If the Treasury General Account reaches its $850 billion fully-filled target by the end of September, then it’ll likely drop liquidity to the lower end of its multi-year range.
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> -August 31, 2025

Since that report, the TGA refilled by another $82 billion. When this goes up, it sucks liquidity out of the financial sector:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-tga.png)

Overnight reverse repos are basically tapped out and thus cannot offset that TGA refill:

![RRP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-rrp.png)

As a result, overall liquidity is gradually headed down toward (but not yet at) the lower end of its 3-year range:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity remains decent, as the weaker dollar and decent Chinese credit indicators are collectively good for global liquidity:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-dxy.png)

Thus, it’s not surprising that foreign equities overall are outperforming US equities this year, as we have more internal liquidity headwinds that aren’t necessarily spilling out to the rest of the world.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin lately has been recovering from the sell-off it had throughout the second half of August:

![BTC Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-btc.png)

I think this consolidation was healthy. During the summer, bitcoin treasury companies got to rather frothy valuations. Their high multiples of net asset value assigned to them by the market incentivized them to rapidly issue new equity and/or debt to buy more bitcoin, but as the multiples compress, they’ve been slowing their purchases.

Strategy is now down to a 1.48x market value to net asset value (mNAV) ratio, while Metaplanet is down to an unusually low 1.31x. Metaplanet had an 8x mNAV multiple back in June:

![Metaplanet mNAV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-15-metaplanet-mnav.png)

This represents a *lot* of froth coming out of the market. Metaplanet in particular is a stock that I’ve been avoiding for valuation reasons, but that I like fundamentally and could see myself going long at some point.

![Metaplanet Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-metaplanet-tweet.png)

As it gets cheap here, I’m starting to keep an eye on it as a potential buy. Not quite yet, though, because there’s no particular catalyst in place for a bottom, so it’s potentially still a falling knife.

For those interested in going deeper on the subject of bitcoin treasury companies, I had a discussion/debate with Andy Constan on the topic [here](https://youtu.be/BbgR5ceiqL4).

## A Tale of Two Sectors

The S&P 500 has touched new highs recently, and remains at rather high valuation levels:

![SPY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-spy-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Under the surface, of course, sectors have deviated considerably. In many cases, this comes down to whether they are more beneficially effected by loose fiscal policy or more harmed by tight monetary policy, and whether they’re greatly affected by tariffs or not.

If we look over the long run, the consumer discretionary sector has outperformed financials by more than 3x:

![XLF vs XLY Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-xlf-vs-xly-longterm.png)

But year-to-date, financials are winning by nearly 2x:

![XLF vs XLY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-xlf-vs-xly.png)

Banks and insurers are impacted by interest rates and other aspects of the economy, but aren’t in the direct crosshairs from tariffs. Consumer discretionary companies, on the other hand, have been near the epicenter of the tariffs.

Lately I’ve been watching Lululemon. There’s an old Peter Lynch quote about buying what you know, and indeed I do wear some Lululemon workout attire and enjoy the products. So, the fact that their stock is an absolute dumpster fire (technical term) got my attention:

![LULU FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-lulu-fastgraph.png)

Their stock price completely round-tripped the entire post-pandemic performance, even though their earnings remain elevated compared to that starting point. This is because valuation compression has been so severe. They have a strong balance sheet, so this could become an interesting value opportunity at some point. I’m not buying yet, though.

![LULU YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-lulu-ychart.png)

This particular company has a one-two punch. For starters, they were already running into some headwinds from intense competition. They’ve historically been seen as overpriced, and that makes room for newer entrants with similar products at lower price levels. Additionally, their product offerings have been failing to spark joy among the youngest buyers as of late, and their executive team has acknowledged this. However, the second punch was tariffs. That’s what turned the stock price from stagnant to free-fall.

Seeking Alpha summarizes:

> And the fallout from lululemon’s (NASDAQ:LULU) FY25 guidance continues to weigh on the stock with shares limping into Friday’s open down by double-digits and looking at 5-year low at the open.
>
> The company slashed its profit and sales outlook for the year due to tariff headwinds, and soft U.S. sales, both of which were worse than the market anticipated. Additionally, tariffs are expected to shave 300 basis points off gross margins with the bulk coming from the end of the de minimis exemption, “a risk that we believe investors were unaware of,” Needham’s Tom Nikic observed as two-thirds of the company’s e-commerce orders ship from Canada.
>
> But while tariffs will take a bite out of future profits, the company has a bigger problem. U.S. consumers, specifically the company’s key Millennial and Gen Z demographic, aren’t particularly interested anymore in what the company is selling, specifically its lounge and social wear lineup.
>
> “Our lounge and social product offerings have become stale and have not been resonating with guests,” McDonald acknowledged, adding that lululemon (NASDAQ:[LULU](https://seekingalpha.com/symbol/LULU#hasComeFromMpArticle=false#source=section%3Amain_content%7Cbutton%3Abody_link%7Cfirst_level_url%3Anews "lululemon athletica inc.")) has become too predictable in casual offerings and missed opportunities to create new trends, a deficiency that has plagued the company for several years.
>
> –[Seeking Alpha News Editor](https://seekingalpha.com/news/4492697-lululemon-faces-tariff-shock-and-fading-consumer-buzz), September 5, 2025

There are a handful of variables at play here. Mainly, if you look at a given large U.S. apparel company, some substantial percentage of their sales will be to Americans, and some other percentage will go everywhere else. Some companies have a greater share of sales abroad, whereas others are more America-centric. Those in the former camp are better-positioned to weather tariff impacts because only a portion of their sales are affected by them. Lululemon, on the other hand, happens to be in the latter camp; the great majority of its sales are in the United States, and thus a greater share of its sales are impacted by these new tariffs.

So am I buying the stock? No. But it has my attention. At some point as the dust settles, I’ll be looking to see if a combination of product/personnel shifts start to stabilize market share, and we’ll see what happens with tariffs when they are assessed by the Supreme Court later this year. At the moment it risks being a value trap, but it could start to show signs of a value opportunity, given that it has a solid balance sheet to work from here.

More broadly, this is a lesson in conservative valuation. Nike (NKE) has had a multi-year collapse in both growth and stock price, with tariffs just being the latest hit:

![NKE FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-nke-fastgraph.png)

Back in 2021, I remember watching this in real time wondering why the market was paying 48x earnings for an apparel company. It wasn’t as though it were some hot start-up; it was a 50-year old business. One of the popular justifications at the time were that it’s more than apparel; it’s a lifestyle. People literally collect Nike shoes, and so it was argued to have a wider economic moat. While that’s a fair point, it clearly wasn’t enough to justify such nosebleed valuations.

Interestingly, it still has a price/earnings ratio about 3x as high as Lululemon, despite weaker growth:

![NKE YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-nke-ychart.png)

Nike at current prices still doesn’t particularly interest me, whereas Lululemon is one I’m at least keeping an eye on for now.

In contrast to all of this, many financials continue to do well. I emphasized banks in my prior report, so here’s an insurance example:

![TRV FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-trv-fastgraph.png)

Travelers (TRV) is just chugging along right at its average earnings multiple. Their bond portfolio took a hit during 2022-2023, but they’re not prone to bank runs or anything like that, so they’ve been able to gradually absorb the hit and move on.

It comes down to individual details and execution, but overall I continue to find plenty of reasonable long-term opportunities in the financial sector. They’re not in the crosshairs of tariffs, AI tools can help them save costs on the backend (i.e. more productive per employee), and large fiscal deficits help keep them nominally supported.

## Final Thoughts: Gold’s Sustained Breakout (so far)

In my prior report, I flagged the fact that gold was nudging up to a new high, potentially breaking out of the consolidation it has been in since April.

I went back and forth with the pro and con arguments for the sustainability of the breakout.

**Pros:** It was touching new highs, poking above the psychologically important $3,500/oz level, and the multi-decade structural changes are in its favor.

**Cons:** The breakout was still within the margin of error of the high end of its consolidation range, and the price was still structurally overbought in terms of the monthly relative strength indicator and similar measures of sentiment.

Ultimately I sided with the pro argument, saying that due to the structural changes happening with developed market fiscal environments and a globally shifting central bank policy of leaning a bit more into gold, that’s the side I want to err on despite the frothiness:

> The good news lately is that gold is back to the top end of that consolidation range, and has touched above the psychologically important $3,500/oz range. Additionally, the increasing consensus around a dovish Fed is beneficial for the price, all else being equal.
>
> The bad news is that it’s not really a true breakout yet, more like a test, and it’s still structurally overbought on the monthly chart:
>
> ![Gold Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-stockcharts.png)
>
> Now, because I think we’re going through major long-term changes (the end of a four-decade structurally declining interest rate cycle, a global shift in sovereign interest from US Treasuries to gold around the margins in an increasingly multi-polar world, and persistent US fiscal dominance which fuels the attractiveness of hard assets), gold remaining in overbought status for unusually long periods of time is not unreasonable.
>
> That’s why since autumn 2018 when I added precious metals to the newsletter portfolio, I’ve happily erred on staying long even through periods where I expect intermediate-term weakness.
>
> Money Metals visualizes it well in their [quarterly newsletter](https://www.moneymetals.com/uploads/content/MoneyMetalsInsider_Summer2025.pdf). The dollar is no longer expanding as a share of global reserves, and gold is no longer falling, in what is likely the bottom of a trend that basically began after WWII:
>
> ![Money Metals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-money-metals.png)
>
> After all these 5 months of price chop, I am increasingly uncertain as to whether to expect a breakout or more consolidation. Given my prior comments, I continue to be structurally long, and at the very least I am less negative on the intermediate-term prospects for the metal than I was in April.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

Since then, the breakout has sustained, adding another $160-$170/oz onto the price:

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-gold-price.png)

![Gold Monthly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-14-gold-monthly.png)

I continue to have some reservations around gold’s monthly RSI and overall frothiness, but also continue to view it as an attractive long-term hold within the context of a diversified portfolio, given the “monetary reset” that the global financial system is gradually undergoing, not in the sensationalist near-term sense, but in the realistic longer-run sense as U.S. fiscal dominance continues to play out and as the world becomes more multi-polar over time.
