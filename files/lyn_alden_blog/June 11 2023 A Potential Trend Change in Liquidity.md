## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

The Fed’s balance sheet increased last week for the first time since March:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-balance-sheet.png)

This was because, although they continued to do quantitative tightening (letting some of their securities mature off their balance sheet and thus reduce bank reserves in the system), they have been on a trend of raising their bank term funding program “BTFP” loans:

![BTFP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-btfp.png)

The BTFP is the facility that the Fed put in place during the March bank liquidity crises. Banks can get collateralized loans against their securities from the Fed, and while it comes at a high interest rate compared to average deposit rates, the banks can use their collateral at face value for this type of loan even if the collateral has unrealized losses on it. For a while its usage flattened, but lately it has been increasing again.

Back in my April 30th report, I brought attention to the fact that when the Fed makes emergency loans, they historically are unable to fully wind those down unless they increase their permanent balance sheet:

> During 2008 and 2020, the Fed’s initiation of loans (red line below) ended up marking a change in the Fed’s permanent balance sheet (blue line below). In other words, during emergencies the Fed made loans (a provision of temporary liquidity), but had to turn to outright buying securities and providing permanent liquidity as well. Those permanent purchases of securities is what allowed the Fed to eventually wind down their loans.
>
> ![Fed Loans and Securities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-fed-loans-and-securities.png)
>
> My base case is that the Fed will be unable to permanently wind down their current loans unless they cease quantitative tightening and perhaps turn to a bit of quantitative easing as well. There are some paths that could prove me wrong on that, such as the Treasury issuing a ton of T-bills and sucking money out of reverse repos, and if those events start to happen I’ll be reporting on them and shifting my base case as appropriate. Outside of those specific pathways, they’re running into a pretty tight liquidity situation.
>
> -April 30th, 2023 Premium Report

So far, that observation continues to be relevant. The Fed’s total loans have only come down slightly since they spiked in March, and the BTFP in particular has resumed its rise. Their main hope to end lending without increasing their permanent balance sheet would be to draw some of the $2.1 trillion in liquidity out of reverse repos and back into the rest of the system, but that’s barely been happening at all. Reverse repos have been basically flat year-to-date, since participants can earn high yields with the Federal Reserve as the counterparty. Money market funds are the primary users of the reverse repo facility.

Overall domestic net liquidity continues to be flat (and actually mildly up lately), due to the Fed’s March 2023 loan liquidity injection as well as how quickly the Treasury General Account had to draw down during the final weeks of the debt ceiling showdown:

![Domestic Liquidity Indicator](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-domestic-liquidity-indicator.png)

This has been the sideways trend that I’ve been talking about since January.

> Going forward, this sideways liquidity situation is likely set to continue for the next few months. The Fed is still reducing its balance sheet (negative for liquidity) while the debt ceiling issue is likely to result in lower Treasury General Account balances (ironically positive for liquidity) until the debt ceiling is resolved, as described in the next section.
>
> When the debt ceiling is raised and the Treasury Department begins refilling its Treasury General Account with more bond issuance later this year, then that would likely be very negative for liquidity, assuming the Fed is still reducing their balance sheet at that point. That would be a negative double-whammy for liquidity.
>
> -January 23, 2023 premium report

Now, this trend appears likely to change for the worse because the debt ceiling has been lifted. The Treasury General Account plans to increase their cash account to $425 billion with a lot of Treasury bill issuance by the end of June, and then gradually raise it from there up to $600 billion by the end of September. The red line below shows their rough projection:

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-tga.png)

When the TGA rises sharply without QE by the Fed, it’s usually not good for risk assets. It sucks liquidity out of the financial system.

For example, when they drained the TGA into the financial system in early 2017 and held it low for the rest of the year, stocks had an extremely strong year. Then when they sucked liquidity back out of the financial system and refilled the TGA in early 2018 (and that was a lot of money back then), the stock rally snapped and entered a more volatile period for the rest of the year.

The big TGA rise in 2020 was accompanied by *enormous* quantitative easing, so it was a net liquidity injection despite the TGA rise. Stocks did great from that low point. And the big drawdown of the TGA in 2021 was a massive injection of liquidity back into the market which was also great for stocks.

The big TGA rise in early 2022 was not accompanied by QE (the Fed balance sheet was relatively flat and starting to draw down a bit), and so it sucked a lot of liquidity back out of the financial system, and stocks sharply fell for the entire first half of that year.

The big drawdown in the TGA starting in the second half of 2022 and continuing into early 2023 helped the stock market bottom in autumn 2022 and hold up decently from there. This TGA drawdown pushed liquidity back into the financial system while the Fed was still removing liquidity, resulting in flat liquidity conditions and decent stock conditions. The Fed’s March 2023 response to banking systems also pushed some temporary liquidity into the system, which was useful for risk assets in general.

Well, now they’re about to sharply raise the TGA again and while the Fed is still doing QT, which is negative for liquidity in the financial system (unless it all comes out of reverse repos, which is unlikely). It’s also happening at a time when the volatility index is at its lowest point since before the pandemic.

![VIX](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-vix.png)

I don’t know what’s going to happen, but I don’t love the risk/reward outlook for the next few months when the TGA is about to sharply rise and the volatility index is extremely complacent.

With volatility this low, it’s worth considering some hedging. I’ll be adding a small hedge in my No Limits portfolio as described in the portfolio updates section.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The large fiscal deficit is an inflationary and stimulatory force on the economy. The Fed’s higher interest rates increases the interest expense of the federal government, which widens their deficit, and so they’re ironically pouring that into the economy like a stimulus. Meanwhile, the higher rates also put downward pressure on asset prices and lending activity, which hurts tax revenue due to how financialized the economy is. As a result, the deficit began re-widening over the past year:

![U.S. Fiscal Deficit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-deficit.png)

On the other hand, those same higher interest rates are putting pressure on portions of the private sector, which is disinflationary and recessionary. The purchasing manager’s index for example continues to show sub-50 (contraction) conditions with a gradual slide lower:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-ism.png)

Small/medium banks are pretty liquidity constrained, and slowing down their lending. Commercial real estate is pressured. There is weakness around the margins in the U.S. labor market.

![Quintanilla Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-quintanilla-tweet.png)

Atlanta Fed forecasts mild growth for Q2 (which is for the April through June period). The next couple of quarters after that are a risk period, in my view.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-gdpnow.png)

The economy has been in a sideways malaise, with neither a recovery nor an outright recession. This seems to be in large part due to the aforementioned mix between what the tighter monetary policy does to fiscal deficits and the private sector.

In decades past, the federal debt and deficit were lower as a percentage of GDP. So, higher rates didn’t affect the public sector much, and hurt the private sector. Therefore, higher rates put a brake on things.

However, now that the federal debt and deficit are quite a large percentage of GDP, higher rates meaningfully add to the already-large deficit, and pour more deficit spending into the economy at a scale really affects things. As a result, higher rates are inflationary from a public sector perspective but still disinflationary from a private sector perspective, and the overall result is less clear-cut than it used to be.

As we look ahead towards a tighter liquidity situation, the disinflationary/recession side probably has more strength than the inflationary/stimulatory side for the next couple quarters. However, if it gets to the point where the Fed is forced to step in and help with liquidity again, then inflation could take back over. We’re not there yet but that’s the base case outlook that I’m working with at the moment. Continued disinflation for now, and then another round of inflation when we have the next period of re-liquification and economic re-acceleration (e.g. rising PMIs).

**SEC Lawsuits**

The U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission came out swinging on the crypto industry over the past couple of weeks, with dual lawsuits against the world’s two largest cryptocurrency exchanges.

First they filed a lawsuit against Binance, which is *by far* the world’s largest exchange, and which primarily operates internationally in dozens of jurisdictions. A day later, they filed a lawsuit against Coinbase (COIN), which is the biggest onshore U.S. regulated exchange, and a publicly traded company on the NASDAQ that serves both retail and institutional clients.

The lawsuit against Coinbase is pretty specific. The SEC alleges that Coinbase sells unregistered securities to the public, including popular tokens like Solana and Cardano that it has called out as unregistered securities (among many others). The lawsuit alleges that Coinbase serves as an exchange, broker, and clearing agency for unregistered securities. Additionally, while they don’t name Ethereum as a security per se (nor do they argue that it’s not), they argue that providing staking services is a security, and Coinbase further violates securities law by providing unregistered staking services for Ethereum and other proof-of-stake coins. Overall, this is a rather existential lawsuit against Coinbase, since it challenges most of their business model.

The lawsuit against Binance is more severe, and as such, the SEC has requested a temporary restraining order against them. The SEC alleges that in addition to selling unregistered securities to the public like Coinbase does, Binance also 1) issues its own unregistered security (BNB) and co-mingles customer funds with their own funds (which is what FTX did). Binance exists offshore but the SEC alleges that they didn’t filter Americans from interacting with it, and alleges that their U.S. division isn’t sufficiently disconnected from their global division, which gives them the authority to go after it since it impacts Americans.

In other words, while Coinbase is considered to be “likely safe” but “operating illegally”, Binance is argued to be both “potentially unsafe” *and* “operating illegally”. The SEC is worried that Coinbase sells you unregistered securities, but is less worried that they don’t handle customer funds safely. Meanwhile, the SEC is worried that Binance sells you unregistered securities while also having a potentially fraudulent backend accounting system and risk of outright default or fraud to their customers. The Binance lawsuit is a bigger deal overall, but the Coinbase lawsuit is a bigger deal for the American cryptocurrency industry.

In response to these actions, Robinhood announced that they would drop Solana, Cardano, and Polygon from their trading platform since they were explicitly listed by the SEC in these lawsuits as being unregistered securities. And going in the other direction Crypto.com (which operates out of Singapore) announced they were pulling out of the U.S. institutional market for the moment due to lack of demand. Crypto.com has similar practices as Binance and thus faces some potential lawsuits ahead.

Basically, unless the SEC ends up losing these cases, crypto exchanges that wish to stay in the U.S. need to be more conservative with what assets they list on their platforms, while others can choose to move further offshore and remove themselves more completely from U.S. customers while listing any assets that they want.

**Howey Test 101**

The Howey Test has been around since the 1940s and represents the de-facto test to determine whether something is a security.

I would argue that we’re in need of reform to make registering a security a more streamlined and inexpensive process in the 21st century to take into account technological changes that have occurred, but nonetheless the overall description of what constitutes a security is pretty straightforward.

Put simply, the Howey Test says that a security is:

1. An investment of money
2. into a common enterprise
3. with the expectation of profit
4. to be derived from the efforts of others.

A company’s stock is a clear-cut security, for example. Investors provide startup capital (1) into the company as a common enterprise (2) because they expect the value of the stock to appreciate and/or for the company to pay dividends (3) based on the work of the directors, executives, and employees of that company (4). The same is true for bondholders that lent money to that company, and so bonds are also securities.

Many cryptocurrencies are highly centralized. They raised capital with an initial coin offering and sold those initial pre-mined coins to early investors that provided capital (1) via a foundation or company as a legal entity that represents a common enterprise (2) with specific statements or plans by founders to increase the value of those tokens (3) based on development efforts of programmers that are employed or funded by the foundation or company, using proceeds that came from those early investors (4).

In contrast, Bitcoin is not a security, and Gary Gensler as the head of the SEC has said this multiple times. Satoshi Nakamoto raised no capital (1) and had no legal structure or common enterprise (2), which invalidates it as a security. He just put open source code out there for free, and all bitcoins were generated by users using their computers to process transactions and earn block rewards. He continued to work on it voluntarily to refine certain details, but it was functional from the start from his own efforts.

In fact, no proof-of-work coins were named in either the Binance or the Coinbase lawsuit, whether it’s Bitcoin, Litecoin, Dogecoin, Monero, or others. It’s the proof-of-stake or similar coins that were identified as securities, since they generally were launched with initial coin offerings by centralized entities and used those proceeds to further develop their networks from unfinished to finished projects.

**The Ethical Issues**

In normal venture capital investing, founders and investors make very long term bets on the success of the company. While private markets do exist, they’re not very liquid, and they occur between accredited participants. Most exit liquidity and gains for the founders and investors come from the startup company being acquired by another business (after careful financial review and auditing by professionals) or by growing enough to go public (and on average it’s about eight years from founding to going public, if they make it that far). In other words, their finances are tied to the longer-term fundamentals of the startup.

When a company does go public, it has to file all sorts of disclosures to explain what the company does, what the income and balance sheet look like, who owns it, and what the risks are, and the disclosures have to pass legal scrutiny.

However, many centrally-created cryptocurrencies gave early coins to founders, raised capital from investors, and then after a couple years got their coins listed on cryptocurrency exchanges and sold to the retail public, without disclosure filings that are normally associated with securities. The founders and venture capitalists in many cases walk away rich, and then the project goes on to crash and stagnate, leaving retail investors with the losses. The fortunes of these token VCs are very much separated from the long-term outcome of the project. I covered this back in a [December 2022 public article](https://www.lynalden.com/defi-problems/).

**A Note on Ethereum**

Ethereum has been one of the better-performing cryptocurrencies since inception, and has been the second largest cryptocurrency by market capitalization for a long time.

Gensler has said that when Ethereum was launched, it was likely a security (they raised capital into a common enterprise, with a plan to increase the value of the pre-mined coins through development work paid for by the initial capital). Whether it’s still a security is not quite as clear as many other coins and thus would probably take more work by the SEC to prove. There are a few development hubs rather than just one, and the Commodities Futures Trading Commission has considered it a commodity alongside Bitcoin for years, although they’ve admitted it’s less clear than for Bitcoin.

Thus far, Ethereum has not been explicitly named as a security in the SEC lawsuits, although providing staking services for Ethereum has been named a security.

However, much of what Ethereum is used for, is securities. This chart shows Ethereum gas fees by category over time:

![Ethereum Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-11-ethereum-gas.jpg)

-Initial coin offerings (ICOs) were among the early use cases, and this usage is basically security issuance by definition. Most of them were unregistered as such and some of them have lost major lawsuits for that reason. The industry subsequently shifted towards doing ICOs overseas and *then* listing them on U.S. exchanges, rather than doing the ICOs within the United States. But then that still means the exchange itself is selling unregistered securities.

-Stablecoins became the second big narrative, but eventually the high fees caused a lot of stablecoins (especially those used for actual payments and savings) to shift to cheaper and more centralized blockchains like Tron.

-The third big narrative for Ethereum was Decentralized Finance or “DeFi” which refer to pseudo-decentralized exchanges and pseudo-decentralized leveraging/liquidity providers that cryptocurrencies are traded and leveraged on. Many of the tokens related to DeFi are securities, so this is mainly about the trading and leveraging of (unregistered) securities.

-The fourth big narrative for Ethereum was non-fungible tokens or “NFTs”, mainly in the form of digital collectibles. Physical collectibles like trading cards are generally not considered securities, and most digital collectibles are probably not either, although in the digital realm there are certain features for some NFTs that can potentially make them securities (e.g. certain royalty payments programmatically attached to them and so forth).

I think tokenizing certain types of assets or securities is a concept that has legs to it, but it’s a complex mix of technology and regulation. Some jurisdictions like Singapore are embracing the concept while many others are not. Thus far, the vast majority of tokenized assets have been low-quality (e.g. not Apple stock in security token form, but rather some unregistered security with a speculative use-case). I described this problem two and a half years ago in a [public article](https://www.lynalden.com/ethereum-analysis/):

> One of my concerns, when reviewing the biggest use cases for decentralized apps, is that a lot of the use-case is circular and speculative.
>
> Ethereum is heavily used for decentralized exchanges of crypto tokens, crypto stablecoins that serve as liquid units of account for trading crypto tokens, and lending and earning interest on crypto tokens which is a practice that serves as a liquidity/borrowing source for traders of crypto tokens. To a lesser extent, it is also used for gamified ways to earn or trade various crypto tokens.
>
> So, it’s a big operating system powered by crypto tokens, for the purpose of moving around… crypto tokens.
>
> A healthy banking system in the real world would consist of people depositing money, and the banks making various loans for mortgages and for business financing, to generate real-world utility.
>
> A speculation-based banking system, on the other hand, would consist of a bunch of banks taking deposit money, and then lending to speculators in the nearby stock market, along with technology providers that make this easier, and then what those speculators are trading mostly consists of shares of those banks, shares of those tech companies, and shares of the stock exchange, resulting in a big circular speculative party. The biggest use case so far for Ethereum is a decentralized version of that circular speculation-based system.
>
> There are games that are fun and collectible on their own, but in large part, Ethereum is currently mainly about decentralized finance and speculative trading.

The risk to Ethereum is that it has thus far had to continually reinvent its narrative to maintain the momentum, and so rather than accumulating multiple use-cases in a more sustainable way or keeping the same use-case from cycle to cycle, it tends to partially disregard the prior use-case and move onto the next one since each one tends to be circular and speculative and based on regulatory arbitrage. This process risks being exhausted at some point.

## Final Thoughts: China’s Airplane Ambitions

In my prior two reports, I covered how China has rapidly accelerated its auto exports to become the #1 or #2 country by auto exports within a very short period of time.

China has the most electricity production of any country, operates the biggest industrial base, is the world’s biggest commodity importer, and makes more steel than the rest of the world combined. They’ve now reached the next stage of making and exporting larger industrial products at scale, such as full automobiles.

Within the past couple of weeks, China has officially entered the commercial airplane market. The [Comac C919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comac_C919) is a narrow-body airplane with 150-160+ seats and a 3,000 nautical mile range. Comac was founded in 2008, began production of the C919 in 2011, rolled out the first prototype in 2015, and performed the first test flight in 2017.

On May 28, 2023, they officially entered service for passenger flight in China. Going forward, as they build out their commercial aerospace capabilities a bit more, China will likely need to import fewer Boeings and Airbuses than they historically have done. Over the next decade, I think China could become a sizable airplane export market, competing with Airbus and Boeing, at least in developing countries (i.e. we won’t see Comacs flying around in the United States or Europe).

**Floating the Idea of a Naval Agreement**

The U.S. Navy’s fifth fleet covers the Persian Gulf and surrounding area. They have joint agreements with a number of navies in that region to provide protection, with Iran and Iranian proxies historically being the main threat that they counter. A large portion of global oil comes through the Persian Gulf, and so a large swath of the world takes an interest in keeping that area peaceful.

A couple weeks ago, the U.A.E. [dropped out of this agreement](https://www.wsj.com/articles/u-a-e-says-it-exited-u-s-led-naval-force-fdbe23c9), having expressed disappointment with the U.S.-led coalition.

And then a couple days after that, Iran announced that it would join a [joint naval force agreement](https://www.reuters.com/world/middle-east/iran-says-form-naval-alliance-with-gulf-states-ensure-regional-stability-2023-06-03/) with several surrounding countries including the U.A.E., Saudi Arabia, Oman, Iraq, India, and others. The agreement was reportedly facilitated by China. I remain somewhat skeptical of this news item until I see more official announcements by other countries involved, but it does come amid improving relations between these countries.

Meanwhile, the U.A.E. also [re-affirmed a commitment](https://twitter.com/OFMUAE/status/1664682093442367516) to expand and deepen its cooperation with BRICS and “Friend of BRICS” nations. BRIC began in 2006, South Africa was added to form BRICS in 2010, and last year China proposed to broaden it further. This year, BRICS held their annual summit in South Africa a week and a half ago. Back in April, as this summit was being organized, [19 nations submitted bids to join the organization](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-04-24/brics-draws-membership-requests-from-19-nations-before-summit), including Iran, Saudi Arabia, the U.A.E., Argentina, Egypt, Indonesia, and others.

Back in March, China [brokered a truce](https://www.politico.eu/article/china-broker-deal-iran-saudi-arabia-diplomatic-relations-sheikh-nimr-al-nimr-missile-attacks/) between Saudi Arabia and Iran, for which they would re-open diplomatic relations with each other. Saudi Arabia and Iran have been long-standing antagonists and in particular had cut off their diplomatic relations for seven years until March 2023, but China is an increasingly important trade partner with both of them, and has been pushing forward the concept of a unified Asia, or at least an Asia that doesn’t antagonize each other and that operates in a more transactional manner.

China already has pretty good relations with Russia, Saudi Arabia, Iran, and many other countries throughout Asia. The China/India relationship is historically cold, but India and Russia cooperate rather heavily with a long-term plan of growing trade between them, and both China and India want oil to be able to flow safely out of the Persian Gulf and across the Indian Ocean. The Saudi Arabia and Iran relationship is a centerpiece of antagonism in the Middle East, but is showing signs of thawing. To the extent that some of these specific relations can improve slightly, China will have found itself with a rather powerful bloc.

Analysts such as Peter Zeihan that focus heavily on geography argue that as the United States pulls back its naval protection from the rest of the world, countries such as China that have come to rely on this protection will become more vulnerable to attacks on their supply lines, their energy security, and so forth. However, to the extent that China, India, Iran, Saudi Arabia, and a handful of other powerful Asian countries are roughly on the same page in terms of trade and naval agreements between China and the Persian Gulf, then a large portion of that argument can be addressed.

Additionally, given China’s massive industrial base, they have considerable ship-making capacity to draw from to ramp up their naval projection capabilities between themselves and the Persian Gulf, if they were to decide that this investment is required. It could serve as a domestic jobs stimulus as well, if they go that route.

I continue to view China’s activities with other BRICS nations and Middle Eastern nations to be under-reported in U.S. media. They continue to build a number of diplomatic bridges, at a time when they are also climbing up the export value chain, as the largest trading partner with the majority of countries in the world.

China’s domestic economy is not exactly booming at the moment, mainly due to lack of domestic demand and too much focus on exports, and they continue to have real estate valuation problems and a demographics cliff ahead of them. It remains to be seen to what extent China will be able to turn the corner from an export-led economy to a more balanced consumption/export economy over time. However, their overall energy production and industrial output, along with an uptick in diplomatic successes, is giving them some degree of geopolitical momentum.
