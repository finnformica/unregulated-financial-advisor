## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

The U.S. domestic liquidity situation continues to be flat. The government is focusing on issuing debt on the short end of the duration spectrum, which is currently the most expensive part of the yield curve but is the least disruptive for liquidity.

By emphasizing T-bill issuance rather than T-note or T-bond issuance, they are successfully draining money from reverse repos:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-domestic-liquidity.png)

Equities have become rather expensive in recent months, with quite a lot of enthusiasm built in despite sluggish economic indicators and flat liquidity.

In general, I view most of the mega-cap stocks as being overbought at the moment, especially compared to the expected returns from T-bills and T-notes. So far, stocks have pushed up higher than I would have guessed, and the overall risk/reward doesn’t seem attractive.

The recent price rise of Apple (AAPL), for example, has been entirely valuation-based amid sluggish fundamentals:

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-aapl-fastgraph.png)

The dollar index has been in a rather firm downtrend since autumn 2022, which tends to be decent for domestic and global liquidity. My base case is to expect a continued decline, but due to the high weighting of the euro within the dollar index basket, I don’t have very high conviction. The bigger story for the 2020s decade will likely be what happens with major emerging market currencies.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-dxy.png)

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index notched another decline in June, and the year-over-year figure remains at reessionary levels:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-lei-yoy.png)

> “The US LEI fell again in June, fueled by gloomier consumer expectations, weaker new orders, an increased number of initial claims for unemployment, and a reduction in housing construction,” said Justyna Zabinska-La Monica, Senior Manager, Business Cycle Indicators, at The Conference Board**.** “The Leading Index has been in decline for fifteen months—the longest streak of consecutive decreases since 2007-08, during the runup to the Great Recession. Taken together, June’s data suggests economic activity will continue to decelerate in the months ahead. We forecast that the US economy is likely to be in recession from Q3 2023 to Q1 2024. Elevated prices, tighter monetary policy, harder-to-get credit, and reduced government spending are poised to dampen economic growth further.”
>
> –[The Conference Board, July 20th](https://www.conference-board.org/topics/us-leading-indicators)

Continued jobless claims historically reach over 4 million during a recession, and greater than 5 million or 6 million during a severe recession. During this cycle, they hit a low of 1.2 million last year, rose to nearly 2 million earlier this year as the economy slowed down, cooled back down to 1.6 million, and are now rising again. If this follows the normal seasonal pattern (rather than get recessionary), we should see it decline again in August:

![Continuing Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-nsa-continuing-claims.png)

Freight companies have been having considerable stress lately. Most notably, Yellow Corporation (YELL), one of the largest transportation companies in the country, [is on the verge of running out of cash and entering bankruptcy](https://www.nytimes.com/2023/06/27/business/yellow-trucking-bailout-teamsters.html). Due to the inability to pay employee benefits, upwards of 22,000 employees are expected to go on strike. Yellow owes considerable debt, including over $700 million to the federal government as part of an aid package it received the last time the company was in trouble.

Broadly, the freight transportation index is down year-on-year, and is lower than it was four years ago, representing a prolonged stagnation:

![Freight Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-freight-index.png)

**Oil Update**

The oil price continues to chop along, rather than making any lower lows recently:

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-wti-crude.png)

Many investors assume that the price of oil has to crash during a recession. Keep in mind that the recessions of 2009 and 2020 were pretty unusual in that regard. What they had in common was that a lot of new supply came online in the years prior to the recession, and then the recession was unusually severe. If we go back to other recessions, like the 2001 recession, the price of oil often just chops around in a range through it.

As a general rule, global energy usage just keeps going up. Even as energy usage in the developed world stagnates, it is more than offset by the growth of the developing world:![Energy Consumption](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-energy-consumption.png)

I showed in my prior report that U.S. oil production is likely to peak out later this year:

![Shale Oil](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-shale.png)

Over the past year, a number of oil sources came online to help with the oil price spike. The U.S. rapidly drew down its strategic petroleum reserve, and oil from Iran entered the global market at scale due to their increasing connections with China. However, I don’t think that’ll be repeated next year, and U.S. oil production is likely to peak out for a period of time.

Some of the historically best executing energy majors like Exxon Mobil (XOM) can be purchased for under 12x forward earnings:

![XOM FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-xom-fastgraph.png)

Some of the historically weaker majors like BP (BP) can be purchased for under 7x forward earnings:

![BP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-bp-fastgraph.png)

After being a lousy investment during the 2010s decade, I expect oil producers with low decline rates (e.g. ex-shale) to be good total return investments this decade. They’re inexpensive, they’re under-owned, and many of them have used this recent period of high oil prices to deleverage their balance sheets.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin continues to chop along in a range around $30k.

As a base case, I view it as a “don’t overthink it” trade when it comes to the next few years. Bitcoin is showing all the same types of on-chain indicators as it showed during prior bear-to-bull market transitions. I tend to show different indicators in each of my reports and they all point in the same direction:

![Bitcoin Charts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-bitcoin-charts.png)

Recently, bitcoin’s monthly MACD has turned positive as well. The price is above the 10-month moving average, and the moving average has an upward slope.

![BTC Monthly](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-btc-monthly.png)

Plus, the next supply flow halving is expected to be in April next year. Every four years, the Bitcoin network is programmed to halve the number of new coins that are created for each block, which means there is less daily new issuance for the market to absorb. Currently, about 900 new bitcoins are produced per day, so at an average price of $30,000 per coin that’s $27 million in daily selling pressure. Some miners hold some of the coins they generate, so realistically the daily selling pressure is a bit lower than that. After the halving next year, only 450 bitcoins will be produced per day.

I rarely have a firm view on the next six months of bitcoin price action, but I continue to be bullish for the intermediate and longer term. Building a reasonably-sized starter position and then [dollar-cost averaging into it](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) from there tends to be the easiest way to accumulate without trying to be a trading wizard. I don’t recommend leverage, and I do recommend having position size that makes sense relative to your volatility tolerance.

I have a book coming out by early September called *Broken Money* that explores the history of monetary technologies, including of course a significant section on the Bitcoin network and adjacent technologies.

**SEC v Ripple**

The Securities and Exchange Commission has been involved in a multi-year lawsuit against Ripple Labs, for alleging selling their XRP token as an unregistered security.

In a recent court ruling, a judge sided partially with Ripple. Specifically, it was determined that Ripple indeed violated security laws with its initial sales to institutions, but that secondary sales to the retail public were not unregistered securities. This caused a rally in XRP, various cryptocurrencies, and shares of publicly-traded exchanges like Coinbase (COIN).

However, based on recent filings, the SEC has argued that it was the wrong decision by the judge and seems likely to appeal the decision to a higher court. Here’s a cleaned up summary of a [recent SEC filing from July 21st](https://assets.bwbx.io/documents/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/raAgcBtaEU24/v0), in which they argue that the recent court ruling on *SEC v Ripple* should not be used as a reason to dismiss the charges that the SEC is bringing against Terraform Labs (the creators of the infamous Luna token).

> *[The court decision of SEC v Ripple]* correctly concludes that defendants’ extensive public statements promoting XRP created in institutional buyers reasonable expectations of profits from Ripple’s efforts. The same result should have followed directly with respect to retail buyers too. Instead, *SEC v Ripple* creates an artificial distinction between the expectations of sophisticated institutional and retail investors, improperly transforms Howey’s reasonable investor inquiry into a subjective one, and turns on its head the reasoning underlying Howey and other cases.
>
> First, what an investor reasonably expects does not depend on whether the investor buys into the investment directly from or communicates directly with the promoter. The promoter’s choice of using intermediaries to sell does not alter reasonable expectations—those who buy indirectly have the same reasonable expectations. […]
>
> Second, *SEC v Ripple* erroneously creates two different “reasonable investor” standards—one for institutional and another for retail investors—but creating any such subjective dichotomy is contrary to Howey. […]
>
> Third, *SEC v Ripple* misconstrues the “efforts of others” analysis in other ways. This prong is meant to identify “whether the efforts made by those other than the investor are the undeniably significant ones, those essential managerial efforts which affect the failure or success of the enterprise.”
>
> -SEC filing, July 21st

## Bank Earnings Update

The past week has been filled with Q2 bank reports, which gives an idea of what’s happening with their deposits, their earnings, their delinquency rates, and so forth.

JPMorgan Chase (JPM), the largest bank in the United States, reported a blow-out quarter, with a significant earnings beat.

Bank of America (BAC) and Wells Fargo (WFC) had similarly good results, with nice beats and decent outlooks. They’re the second and fourth largest banks in the country. Citigroup (C) was a slight outlier, with a small earnings miss, but nothing that spooked the market.

U.S. Bank (USB), the fifth largest bank in the country, also beat earnings expectations but gave a mild outlook for the full-year 2023. The market liked the result, especially compared to the valuation that it was placing on the company, and so shares had a big surge. Notably, U.S. Bank has more deposits now than they had when the March 2023 banking liquidity crisis occurred.

Truist Financial (TFC), the sixth largest bank, came in light on earnings, and its deposits were flat-to-down. There was nothing particularly bad in the quarter from the banking side, but so far their merger is not giving the synergy cost savings that they expected, which continues to be a drag on an otherwise very cheap stock that operates in the part of the country with the best demographics (the southeast).

A common theme among most bank results was that loan delinquencies remain low, but are rising. Banks have been setting aside more money to cover loan losses, which is reflected in their earnings. In addition, their net interest margins for Q2 were unsurprisingly worse than Q1.

Looking at the month of July, the top seven bank stocks are up anywhere between 2% and 16%, inclusive of any dividends that some of them may have paid. Citi is lagging, while U.S. Bank has surprised to the upside.

![Bank July Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-bank-july-returns.png)

**Valuation Check-Up**

There’s a good chance that the bottom is in for large bank stocks, although I don’t necessarily expect stellar returns until we finish the current loan delinquency cycle. For smaller banks, I’m more cautious. It requires a lot of analysis on any small bank stock these days to be confident in its stability.

U.S. Bank is currently trading at just under 10x expected 2023 earnings, which is about a 25% discount to its historical average valuation. For this type of market environment, I think that’s rather reasonable:

![USB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-usb-fastgraph.png)

Meanwhile, its price to tangible book is 2.2x, which is 15% below its average of 2.6x.

![USB Price to Book](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-usb-price-to-book.png)

It pays a 5% dividend yield, but the dividend is likely to be roughly flat until it gets past the current loan delinquency cycle.

**Commercial Real Estate Exposure**

Most large banks have very little commercial real estate lending exposure, which is one of the main troubled areas of the economy. For example, Truist has only 8.9% of its loans in commercial real estate, and only 18% of those are in office buildings, which means just 1.6% of the loan book has office building exposure. And of that 1.6%, there is generally a buffer on the initial loan-to-value ratio, so a project can struggle quite a bit before it would severely impair the loan. U.S. Bank has similar numbers. Most commercial real estate lending is concentrated in smaller regional banks.

Truist has a weighted average maturity of three years for its office building loans. In general, commercial real estate loans are a lot shorter duration than residential loans. This is why commercial real estate owners (particularly office building owners) are in a lot of trouble; their debt is maturing and is getting refinanced at *much* higher rates at a time when occupancy rates are low due to the structural uptick in work-from-home arrangements.

Inversely, certain companies like Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) locked in an average fixed-rate debt maturity profile of 20 years when interest rates were low, which makes for a much better risk/reward setup. That’s the situation an asset-heavy business wants to be in amid this environment.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-epd-fastgraph.png)

**Senior Loan Officer Surveys**

In aggregate, banks have been tightening lending standards over the past year. The current level of tightness is typically associated with a recession. Notably, although the rate of change is likely topping out, it’s not until it’s below zero again that banks in aggregate are loosening credit standards.

![Senior Loan Officer Survey](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-loan-officer-surveys.png)

**Aggregate Deposits**

Total U.S. bank deposits, which are updated weekly, have been generally rising since the week of April 26th. The Fed’s ongoing QT is a dampener for deposits, but the drawdown in the reverse repo facility and the usage of Fed loans has more than offset it during these three months.

![Bank Deposits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-bank-deposits.png)

Overall, large banks have a mixed outlook. Their deposit base is fine, although they have higher deposit interest expenses on average. They have an uptick in loan delinquencies from a low base, but are allocating money to cover those losses as normal. They’re trading at below-average valuations, which is rather fair given the high degree of uncertainty as we looking over the next year.

## Final Thoughts: CVS Attempting a Bottom?

I’m long both Cigna (CI) and CVS Health (CVS), and between the two Cigna has certainly been the better performer.

Cigna is a health insurer and pharmacy benefit manager combined. CVS is a health insurer, pharmacy benefit manager, and retail pharmacy/convenience/healthcare center combined. Over time, CVS is transitioning more of its square footage in its stores to on-site basic medical care.

However, the stock is now back near its 2019 low valuation levels, with signs of a momentum shift. It’s definitely one to watch.

![CVS FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-cvs-fastgraph.png)

It’s a funny analysis if you compare this CVS FASTGraph to the Apple FASTGraph near the beginning of this report. Both stocks have had stagnant fundamentals for two years (orange/blue lines going sideways), and during that time, Apple’s stock price surged higher while CVS’s dropped hard. Apple is trading at nearly 4x as high of a price/earnings ratio as CVS at the moment. Now, I do think Apple should have a higher earnings multiple, maybe even twice as high, but four seems excessive and the power of sentiment on these stocks has been impressive.

Ever since its Aetna acquisition, CVS had been in a period of financial consolidation and simplification, focused on debt reduction, share buybacks, and resumed dividend growth. This changed a bit over the past year when they acquired Signify Health and Oak Street Health for a combined price of over $18 billion. This has been in contrast to the prior period of debt reduction and share buybacks to make a leaner structure, and the acquisitions were not cheaply priced.

![CVS Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-23-cvs-debt.png)

In my view, the market has been underwhelmed by these recent acquisitions, and so I think the correction in price was partly warranted. CVS already has a lot of complexity in their business, and so directing cash flow towards organic growth, dividends, share repurchases, and debt reduction would be the simpler path going forward than adding yet more external businesses under their already enormous umbrella. In addition, the pharmacy benefits business has been facing some regulatory scrutiny as of late.

However, I expect the company to once again move back to a strategy of consolidation, integration, and organic growth, now that these additional moving parts have been added, and thus I like the risk/reward ratio of CVS within the context of a diversified portfolio.
