## Macro View

**Tariff Updates**

As of this morning, news has come out that the US and China are de-escalating their recent trade war escalation, and have agreed to a loose framework. President Trump and President Xi might meet later this week at the APEC Summit in South Korea, which could help solidify it. However, China’s side has been less forthcoming about the tangibility of this framework than the US side has.

In 2024, approximately 15% of China’s exports went directly to the United States, with some additional (hard to quantify) percentage going through other countries to the United States. So far in 2025 amid the tariff wars, China’s exports are still near their all-time highs, but the ratio that goes to the US has decreased and the ratio that goes elsewhere has increased. Additionally, China has flexed its muscles on rare earths, which is a key vulnerability for the United States. In China’s own economy, they continue to have major spare capacity and a disinflationary environment.

In parallel, the US Supreme Court is set to hear the case about tariffs starting on November 5th. President Trump has relied on emergency powers to impose the tariffs, and two court levels have ruled against him on that so far as it escalated its way through the court system (i.e. arguing that the breadth and length of the tariffs he has imposed falls outside of the scope of emergency powers and executive orders, and thus should be set by Congress). It’ll be up to the Supreme Court to decide the final outcome. Polymarket betting participants current place 41% odds of the Supreme Court ruling in Trump’s favor, but the volume is pretty low to draw any major conclusions from.

**US Business Cycle**

The US economy continues to operate under a two-speed system, with large fiscal deficits stimulating several areas and tight monetary policy weighing on others.

In general on the consumer side, those who are older and wealthier are doing fine, while those who are younger and/or on the lower end of the income spectrum are struggling. On the business side, everything in the AI orbit continues to expand rapidly, while real estate and certain other areas are struggling.

The total labor market is slowing to a crawl at sub-1% year-over-year growth, but jobless claims remain low, indicating little acute stress as of yet.

Some of the anomalies show up in purchasing manager’s indices. Let’s look at manufacturing, for example, both the activity itself and the price portion of it:

![Manufacturing PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-manufacturing.png)

We are in a period of prolonged slump in manufacturing PMI, where it hasn’t totally collapsed as it does in a recession, but it has spent years chopping mostly below 50, which means mild contraction in manufacturing activity. Here in 2025, the “prices paid” portion spiked despite the PMI remaining low, which is a stagflationary aspect associated with tariffs.

Services are typically less cyclical than manufacturing, but service companies have to buy goods as well. Industries like homebuilders and utilities are services, for example, but they have to buy a lot of physical goods, and many of those are tariffed. As a result, there is a rare spike in prices paid by services companies despite weakening service activity:

![PMI Services](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-services-annotated.png)

So far, most of the tariff costs have been absorbed by the companies as they compete to retain market share, but in the coming months my base case is that those costs will start to trickle out more in the form of mild price increases.

**Liquidity**

There is some pretty big liquidity news to report, but it’s not the sensationalist stuff that has been posted around social media lately.

The Fed has been shrinking its balance sheet from excess levels since mid-2022. Occasionally it would cause some issues, but the Treasury Department would offset that with more T-bill issuance to pull excess liquidity from the reverse repo facility instead, and put that back into the financial system. Between the Fed and the Treasury, they kept domestic liquidity almost completely flat.

Now, the reverse repo facility is drained, and the Fed’s balance sheet is nearing levels that are tight enough to cause some issues. Mostly these issues briefly pop up at the end of a quarter, because financial institutions perform “window dressing” to temporarily make their balance sheet look ideal in a snapshot for their quarterly report.

The Fed has a standing repo facility now, so that if financial institutions need cash in exchange for pledging safe collateral, it’s available to them. That facility has been used a few times during these quarter-end window-dressing periods.

However, since mid-September and continuing into late October, the Fed’s repo facility has been repeatedly used even without it just being the end of a quarter:

![Repo Activity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-repo.png)

Additionally, the secured overnight financing rate (aka SOFR) has mildly spiked as well, relative to the rate of interest paid by the Fed on bank reserves. This indicates mild liquidity shortages in the private collateralized borrowing market between financial institutions:

![SOFR and IORB](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-sofr-and-iorb.png)

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-sofr-iorb.png)

All of this is to say that the Fed’s quantitative tightening is starting to reach a persistent level of liquidity shortages in the domestic banking system.

Then, in mid-October, Fed Chairman Powell acknowledged these signs of liquidity strain and said the Fed may soon end its balance sheet reduction. Reuters [reports](https://www.reuters.com/business/feds-powell-say-end-balance-sheet-drawdown-may-be-nearing-2025-10-14/):

> Oct 14 (Reuters) – Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell said on Tuesday the end of the central bank’s long-running effort to shrink the size of its holdings, widely known as quantitative tightening, or QT, may be coming into view.
>
> Given the central bank’s long-running goal of leaving enough liquidity in the financial system to allow for firm control of short-term rates and normal money market volatility, Powell said “we may approach that point in coming months, and we are closely monitoring a wide range of indicators” to know if that has happened.
>
> As a signal the end stage is getting closer, “some signs have begun to emerge that liquidity conditions are gradually tightening, including a general firming of repo rates along with more noticeable but temporary pressures on selected dates,” Powell said at a gathering held by the National Association for Business Economics in Philadelphia.

If we look at bank cash as a percentage of total bank assets, it currently stands at around $3 trillion, or around 12.6% of total bank assets:

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-cash.png)

![Bank Cash Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-cash-share.png)

Years ago, the New York Fed put out a [research report](https://www.newyorkfed.org/medialibrary/media/research/staff_reports/sr1019.pdf) concluding that within the current regulatory framework, 8-10% bank cash as a percentage of total bank assets is the transition point between “scarce reserves” and “ample reserves”, and 12-13% bank cash as a percentage of total bank assets is the transition point between “ample reserves” and “abundant reserves”. The Fed is aiming to get down from an abundant reserve environment to an ample reserve environment, and so that 12-13% level is their approximate target which they’ve now reached. Back in 2019 they went all the way down to the scarce reserves level and triggered a big repo spike, and they’re aiming for a smoother landing than that this time.

March 2023 provided a bit of a curve ball with the regional bank crisis, partially because cash drained out of smaller banks faster than larger banks. There was still sufficient total liquidity, but it was unevenly distributed. When looking at banks as a whole, the liquidity situation seemed fine, but when it was separated into large and small banks, the issue was more clear:

![Large vs Small Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-large-small-bank-cash.png)

However, since that issue was mostly related to regional banks with specific issues (a high rate of uninsured deposits and a high rate of unrealizes losses on Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities relative to their total bank capital), that problem was isolated. The Fed and Treasury briefly provided extra liquidity to put out the fire, but then the Fed was able to gradually resume its balance sheet tightening because total reserves in the system were still sufficient.

Now we are finally getting to the point where *overall* reserves are reaching “ample” rather than “abundant” levels, and so liquidity strains are popping up more persistently and the Fed is discussing an end to its balance sheet reduction.

Back in 2024, I expected us to reach this level by 2025. However, the Fed instead slowed their rate of balance sheet reduction (quantitative tightening), which let them extend the time of performing their balance sheet reduction without actually changing the level that they’d ultimately get it to.

Once they slowed it, I’ve been pointing toward late 2025 or 2026 as the time for liquidity strains and the end of the Fed’s balance sheet reduction, and we’re right on track:

> Domestic liquidity conditions continue to be flat, as they have been for three years since mid-2022:
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-5-11-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> This flat liquidity situation will likely to persist into Q4 2025. They’ve already slowed their rate of quantitative tightening twice, which extends the time they can continue to do it. By 2026, the Fed will likely shift toward persistent balance sheet expansion, albeit at a mild pace. At that point, they’ll most likely be accumulating Treasuries but will continue to let mortgage-backed securities mature off their balance sheet.
>
> -May 11, 2025

> Overall bank cash levels remain above prior acute liquidity events, but in Q4 2025 they will likely start to enter somewhat tight territory.
>
> ![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bank-cash-levels.png)
>
> My expectation continues to be that the Fed will end quantitative tightening in 2026, and possibly move toward gradual balance sheet increases (which they won’t call QE, similar to their balance sheet expansion from October 2019 to March 2020).
>
> So, domestic liquidity looks like it’ll be flat-to-down for a while, potentially running into lower thresholds, and when it does bottom it’ll be a pretty structural event that moves us up out of a multi-year range.
>
> -August 31, 2025

So, all of this is playing out as expected. The financial system is indeed running into tight liquidity conditions here in Q4 2025, the Fed will end its balance sheet reduction in the coming months, and then we’ll see their timeline toward gradually expanding it in the months that follow from there.

It won’t be a large stimulus, but rather will be a mild pro-liquidity event that will mark a multi-year pivot in the balance sheet from shrinking to neutral and then back toward gradually expanding.

**Global Liquidity**

Global liquidity continues to be decent despite these temporary frictions in the US domestic financial system.

-China has plenty of spare industrial capacity, which gives them a disinflationary trend. This means that significant amounts of monetary and fiscal stimulus can be applied before it would cause noticeable price inflation. And indeed they’re in an easing cycle.

-Japan elected a new prime minister, Sanae Takaichi. The fact that she is the country’s first female prime minister and the fact that she is on the conservative end of Japan’s political spectrum have both made global news, but for the purposes of this report, the notable fact is that she is in favor of substantial fiscal deficits for certain purposes (such as expanded military spending) and a firm continuation of Abenomics. Overall, her policies are likely to add mildly to global liquidity.

The US and Japan continue to emphasize fiscal policy, and more recently China and Europe have partially woken up to more fiscal policy (China for economic stimulus, Europe for defense spending), and so there continues to be a rather coordinated global pro-liquidity move now and for the intermediate future.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin continues to consolidate, and I still view the bullish cycle to be intact. I have no firm view about the short term.

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

As bitcoin becomes larger and more integrated into the existing financial system, the type of cycles it goes through change. And many macro cycles were prolonged in this cycle, from the inverted/flat yield curve to the very long period of mid-low purchasing manager’s indices (PMIs). As I’ve covered a lot, the loose fiscal policy and tight monetary policy have been kind of like holding down both the gas and brake on a car at the same time, creating a rather unusual macro environment. It’s not surprising that bitcoin’s cycle is prolonged and compressed as well, given that it historically trades in line with these other boom/bust liquidity cycles and economic cycles.

As the Fed balance sheet moves from gradually contracting to expanding, and as the Fed brings down rates a bit in an environment that continues to have loose fiscal policy in the US and elsewhere, I expect bitcoin will catch a bid for its next leg higher.

## Banks and Private Credit

Given that this report focused heavily on liquidity, it seems prudent to discuss banking more broadly.

The latest talking point about banks is their high rate of lending to non-deposit financial institutions or NDFIs. Broadly speaking, a NDFI is any financial institution that does not accept deposits and thus is not regulated like a bank. This can include hedge funds, pension funds, insurance companies, private credit, private equity, and various credit intermediaries (entities that borrow from non-deposit sources and make loans with that capital).

The total amount of loans by US banks to NDFIs has reached $1.7 trillion:

![Loans to NDFIs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-loans-to-ndfi.png)

To put that in context, total bank assets are around $24.5 trillion, and so loans to NDFIs make up about 7% of total bank assets:

![Loans to NDFIs Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-loans-to-ndfi-share.png)

As those chart show, there was a big $250 billion jump right at the start of 2025. This is because US federal bank regulators finalized [a new set of reporting requirements](https://www.mayerbrown.com/en/insights/publications/2024/06/us-banking-regulators-finalize-nonbank-lending-reporting-requirements) for US bank about loans to NDFIs, which went into effect at the start of 2025. These requirements also expanded the definition of NDFIs. So, that vertical jump wasn’t a bunch of new NDFI loans being created; it was previously unreported ones being reported. Nonetheless, even without that jump, the growth has been significant.

The growth in bank loans to NDFIs is partially due to a regulatory mismatch. Banks are the most regulated of financial entities, especially ever since the global financial crisis of 2008, whereas non-bank lenders or “shadow banks” have less stringent regulations. Thus, banks have played it safe with conservative lending standards, and in part have lent to NDFIs who can then lend a bit more flexibly, providing a buffer between the bank and the end-borrower.

Now, when we talk about NDFIs or concerns about them, most people aren’t talking about insurance companies, pensions, or hedge funds in this context. They’re talking about the lenders. Primarily they refer to private credit, and this can also be expanded to include private equity.

People often think of “shadow banks” or “private credit” as an inherently spooky thing, but they’re not really. In many ways, they are less extreme than a normal bank in theory. A normal bank operates with massive leverage, and uses liquid liabilities (deposits) to make illiquid loans, which can be quite dangerous. They take your liquid savings, or your payroll account if you’re a business, and lend it out to various borrowers. Private credit, however, uses investor money to make loans, and generally the private credit fund tells the investors up front that their funds may not be very liquid during times of stress. In other words, private credit uses illiquid liabilities (investor/institutional savings and bank loans) to make illiquid loans, which is quite fair in and of itself. And when a private credit fund blows up, it’s way less harmful than when a bank blows up, since it’s generally not Mom-and-Pop deposits in peril, or the payroll checking account of a startup.

So, the theoretical basis for private credit is sound. In practice, however, there are indeed some issues. Since private credit funds are often filling a role that banks can’t or won’t do, private credit lending activities tend to be higher on the risk spectrum, although they will vary wildly in terms of the types of risk they take on. Additionally, the whole private credit market is less transparent than the regulated banking environment. This combination of higher risk and opaqueness means that it’s not too hard for a micro-bubble to form and pop, causing some damage without a lot of warning.

The reason I say “micro-bubble” is because the raw numbers are pretty constrained. [According to Moody’s](https://www.moodys.com/web/en/us/insights/data-stories/breakdown-of-banks-annual-reporting-on-private-credit.html), US banks have lent about $300 billion to private credit funds. They also lent $285 billion to private equity, and have an additional $340 billion in credit facilities that are available to those borrowers but that haven’t been tapped yet, and thus haven’t been put at risk yet.

In context, US banks have $24.5 trillion in assets and $2.5 trillion in equity capital. Even in an extreme event where 50% of all loans to private credit funds default, that’s a $150 billion hit to banks.

There are cracks in private equity and credit. That much is true. And the weakening lower-income consumer segment is contributing to that. But the majority of the pain is or will be felt by the investors in those types of funds that face problems. It takes quite a lot of damage in a private credit fund for them to not only fail their investors but also outright default on their loans to the bank. And then as shown by the numbers above, even a massive default wave by private equity and credit is absorbable by banks in aggregate (although surely some over-exposed banks could be in trouble).

When people see big numbers in lending, and warnings around excess, they often think about the global financial crisis. However, the global financial crisis, aka the subprime mortgage crisis, was focused on residential real estate lending, which is the biggest bank lending category of all. Total mortgages outstanding were $10.8 trillion in 2008, and total bank assets were only $12.2 trillion. Those mortgage liabilities were held partially by banks, and partially by NDFIs. A major default cycle in residential real estate mortgages was a true kill shot for the US banking system, which was why it was such a crisis at the time.

The global financial crisis was the absolute peak in the private sector debt bubble. Banks only had 3% of their assets in cash. Since then, the debt has rotated from the private sector to the public sector, especially as a share of GDP. Banks have much higher ratios of cash and Treasuries now than they did going into 2008.

![Debt to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-debt-to-gdp.png)

To the extent that banks have problem areas today, they are mainly micro-issues. In other words, some industries are indeed hurting (commercial real estate, certain types of private equity and private credit, etc), but that is mostly a problem for them and their investors, and not a large enough issue to cripple the US banking system as a whole.

For example, we can take this tweet:

![Private Credit Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-loans-to-private-credit.png)

Seems like a problem, right?

Let’s start with the biggest: Wells Fargo. They have $1.74 trillion in assets, of which their $59.7 billion in private credit exposure represents 3.4% of their total assets. If half of all of that defaulted on them tomorrow, it’d be a ~$30 billion hit, which would represent a 1.7% loss of their total assets, and a moderate dent in their total equity capital.

Next we can look at Truist, since I’m an investor in that one. Truist has $535 billion in assets, of which $19.5 billion is made up of loans to private credit. That represents 3.6% of their total assets, and if half of it were to all default tomorrow, it wouldn’t feel great but it would represent only a 1.8% reduction in their assets and a moderate dent to their total equity capital.

The list goes on.

At this point, a bear might acknowledge that any individual problem area is pretty small, but all of the problem areas together are pretty big. If there would be a massive crash in commercial real estate, combined with a massive crash in private equity and private credit, then that could damage banks. And of course, that’s true.

But even that is containable. The first line of damage hits investors in those types of funds. The equity holders in commercial real estate, the equity holders in private equity, the investors in various funds that focus on these areas, are who would feel the pain first as their investments are marked down and in some cases zeroed out. Many loans by banks are highly collateralized, meaning it takes a massive loss of value to even partially breach the amount they’ve lent.

The second line of damage if it gets bad enough hits the banks via defaults on the loans they’ve made, but that’s where these ratios matter. Their exposure to these troubled areas collectively represents a rather manageable share of their total assets and total equity capital.

Here’s a chart of cash and Treasuries held by banks as a percentage their total assets:

![Bank Safe Assets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-bank-safe-assets.png)

In the run-up to the global financial crisis, this figure dropped to just 13%. This meant that only 13% of bank assets were held in nominally risk-free assets, and the other 87% was exposed to things that had a realistic chance of default.

Fast-forward to today, banks have 32% of their assets in nominally risk-free assets, nearly a record high, and only 68% in assets that have a realistic chance of default. And those 68% of assets are made with pretty tight lending standards today.

As the regional bank crisis of March 2023 showed, banks can indeed choke even on Treasuries in extreme scenarios, but that only applied to banks with very unique circumstances. They had huge deposit growth, mainly among flighty depositors above the $250k FDIC insurance limit, and heavily invested in long-duration Treasuries. Facing the most rapid rate hiking cycle in history, a handful of those banks like Silicon Valley Bank had their deposits rug-pulled and thus ironically faced insolvency even as they held default-free assets. The rest of the banking system was fine.

Most well-diversified banks in the US are sound. They hold considerable cash and Treasuries, already survived the rapid rate-hiking cycle, and they hold a diverse array of loans that for the most part have pretty low default rates even in adverse scenarios.

So many people are looking for the next private sector bubble, and while small-to-medium ones will indeed pop up from time to time, the biggest bubble is up at the sovereign level: the federal debts and deficits. And that’s a bubble that ends in fire (debasement and inflation), not ice (defaults).

Having doubled in less than two years, it now has the most overbought monthly RSI in history:

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-26-gold-chart.png)

In the tactical sense, the current levels concern me, and I think risk management is in order. It’s not a bad time to check your allocations and considering rebalancing. We’re at levels for which a 6-month, 12-month, or longer correction/consolidation would not surprise me at all.

In a structural strategic/sense, however, I still don’t view gold as being fundamentally overvalued. The world is becoming more multi-polar, with gold as the largest neutral reserve asset and price-settling mechanism available. As major global shifts like this occur, it’s natural for things to get quite extreme, and the entire gold market cap is still a rather small share of global assets.

Thus, my current approach for precious metals is one of caution rather than one of outright avoidance.
