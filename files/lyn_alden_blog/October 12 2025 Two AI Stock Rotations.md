## Macro View

**Tariff Updates**

Tariff news has been pretty slow lately, so I haven’t had to include a section on them for a few reports now.

However, on Friday there was an escalation. President Trump posted that after months of relatively good relations, China has escalated tensions, and so he might not have the upcoming in-person meeting he was planning to have with President Xi, and might massively increase tariffs.

![Trump Post 1](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-trump-post-1.png)

After markets closed for the day, President Trump posted again, this time escalating further with plans to add 100% tariffs on top of existing tariffs, and tighten some export controls.

![Trump Post 2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-trump-post-2.png)

Right after both posts, stocks fell, but notably also the dollar index fell. While each individual data point only goes so far, it’s interesting that the knee jerk reaction by markets to an escalation between the US and China was to sell the dollar rather than move toward it as a safe haven.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-dxy.png)

Since primary markets were closed for the weekend by that second post, the pain came out in cryptocurrencies, which trade 24/7. Various funds that expect to wake up to major losses on stocks/bonds on Monday without being able to change any stock/bond positions in the meantime, can immediately sell down crypto assets to raise capital and de-risk their overall exposure.

As the carnage continued, and pundits called for the possibility of a “Black Monday” in equity markets, Trump walked back a small portion of the escalation that evening, saying that he still might meet with China’s President Xi. Basically, he re-inserted enough ambiguity that markets could relax a little bit, and they did.

Here on Sunday, the situation has de-escalated further. China put out a statement saying that their rare earth export controls were in response to a series of actions that the US has done over the past month, rather than intentional escalation on their part. They also clarified that export restrictions are not outright export bans, and that most civilian uses will still be approved.

After that, President Trump posted on Sunday, strongly de-escalating tensions for the moment.

![Trump Post 3](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-trump-post-3.png)

Crypto markets popped up on the news, but this remains an ongoing headline risk to markets.

China has a stronger set of cards than many US officials seem to have expected, and that’s a topic I’ve been writing about for a while. China has an enormous industrial base, and generates an enormous amount of electricity to power it, and all of that is very expensive and time-consuming to replicate here in the United States. Even if we do replicate it here, it’ll come with higher operating expenses. Additionally, China has a huge market share of rare earth elements (which are environmentally damaging to produce and refine), which the US military and other industries rely on. In the meantime, China is the largest trading partner with the vast majority of countries.

As part of their escalation, China further restricted the availability of rare earth exports (but as they clarified, not outright banned in most cases), and has played hardball with commodity producers to buy commodities with pricing mechanisms that are more tied to their market than the US market, and potentially in yuan instead of dollars. A week and a half ago, various reports came out that China put a halt to iron purchases (and they’re by far the biggest buyer in the world) to force BHP to sell iron on China’s terms. (See [Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/commentary/breakingviews/chinas-high-stake-bhp-standoff-can-also-be-brief-2025-10-02/), [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-30/china-bans-all-bhp-iron-ore-cargoes-as-pricing-dispute-deepens).)

Some recent reports are saying China was successful at that, but I haven’t confirmed it enough with sources I trust. Sources like [Discovery Alert](https://discoveryalert.com.au/news/currency-shift-global-iron-ore-markets-2025/) are now reporting:

> BHP Billiton has struck a groundbreaking agreement with Chinese buyers to settle 30% of its iron ore spot trading in Chinese yuan (RMB) instead of US dollars. Set to begin in Q4 2025, this pivotal arrangement marks a significant departure from traditional commodity trading practices and signals a potential power shift in the global iron ore price trends.
>
> The deal represents more than just a simple currency change. According to industry sources, it requires “the integration of domestic bank loans, cross-border payment systems, and exchange rate hedging to form a closed loop for the whole process.” This complex financial architecture underscores the strategic importance of the arrangement for both parties.

China has already grown the market for yuan-priced commodities about 10x from 2020 through 2024, as [highlighted by Reuters](https://www.reuters.com/commentary/breakingviews/chinas-high-stake-bhp-standoff-can-also-be-brief-2025-10-02/). I expect China to continue to be successful at realigning more commodity pricing around itself, at least when it comes to its own trade. It’s a more multi-polar world now. However, this ongoing negotiation still needs more serious reporting to understand properly.

Risk has been rapidly re-inserted into the market, although since this is a very headline-driven market, part of it has evaporated as quickly as it came. I expect additional trade disputes to pop up in the future, and for China to be harder to reckon with than the China-hawks tend to assume.

**US Business Cycle**

Some economic data is delayed due to the ongoing government shutdown.

Initial jobless claims are still within their normal range, after briefly touching multi-year highs a month ago.

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-initial-claims.png)

ISM [reported](https://www.ismworld.org/supply-management-news-and-reports/reports/ism-pmi-reports/pmi/september/) that US manufacturing activity contracted for the seventh consecutive month in September. However, prices are still rising, as tariffs and fiscal deficits contribute some inflationary (and in this case, stagflationary) pressure. Notably, ISM [service](https://www.ismworld.org/supply-management-news-and-reports/reports/ism-pmi-reports/services/september/) prices are also rising sharply.

Jeffrey Kleintop, the former Chief Global Investment Strategist at Charles Schwab, posted a useful chart showing that with a six-month lag, consumer price inflation tends to follow ISM prices.

![Jeffrey Kleintop Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-jeffrey-kleintop-tweet.png)

We continue to be in a two-speed economy. Large fiscal deficits act as a stimulatory tailwind for individuals and businesses on the receiving side of them, while tight monetary policy and tariffs act as headwinds for those on the wrong side of them.

## Digital Assets Note

During my report from two weeks ago, I briefly discussed bitcoin’s sharp correction from $118k to $109k and reiterated that I think the bullish cycle remains intact:

> Many people on social media are asking if the cycle is over, and in my view the answer is no. There are no major signs of speculative froth in the bitcoin market, and what little signs there were of froth on the margins (high premiums for bitcoin treasury companies and a sharp rise in altcoin treasury companies) have decreased considerably in recent weeks.
>
> ![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-bitcoin-mvrv.png)
>
> Bitcoin has to shake tourists off from time to time. When it goes up significantly, some people are willing to overleverage it or overpay for leverage on it, and companies that buy and lever up any other altcoin also get rewarded by the market, which is unsustainable. It makes sense for a period of pain to wash out those attempts at free money, which can build the next foundation for another leg higher.
>
> ![DeNiro Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-9-28-deniro-meme.png)
>
> -September 28th, 2025 premium report

Literally that Sunday night, bitcoin started to rally. About a week later, in early October, it touched new all-time highs at over $126k.

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-btc-chart.png)

This past Friday night, after President Trump posted about escalations with China, the crypto market crashed. As I mentioned in the macro section, cryptocurrencies are the only thing that trade 24/7, so sudden news tends to force market participants to express their views or de-risking actions there. When leverage is involved, it can become quite dangerous. Even a brief crash can trigger margin calls and wipe out traders entirely. Some leveraged traders are forced to sell at that point, causing an illiquid spike down and causing more to liquidate.

Total liquidations peaked at $19.5 billion, setting a new record. This was posted near the peak:

![Crypto Liquidations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-ash-crypto-tweet.png)

Bitcoin itself is fine; it had a brief illiquid flash-crash and then remained in the trading range it was already in during the prior weeks. There still may be some weakness ahead, but I don’t view anything as fundamentally changed.

However, altcoins in many cases got killed. Many of them have low liquidity, so when illiquid trading conditions and leverage wipe them out, they’re unlikely to recover quickly if at all. Many crypto traders lost seven-figure or eight-figure paper fortunes, and coins that ostensibly had billion-dollar market caps flash-crashed to zero.

![Gum Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-gum-tweet.png)

![OG General Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-og-general-tweet.png)

I continue to be bullish on unlevered bitcoin for the intermediate term and the long term, although I have no firm view on the short term.

I’m also bullish for the long term on the highest-quality bitcoin treasury companies that use modest amounts of non-callable leverage, since they don’t get liquidated on a flash-crash like this. It’s important to buy them at fairly low multiples to net asset value.

In contrast, I continue to be bearish on most of the ex-bitcoin crypto space over the long run, other than stablecoins and certain other types of tokenized real-world assets.

**Moderately Bullish on ACN**

There’s a mismatch currently in the AI space. The chip designers and makers are profitably raking in the product sales, and the hyperscale cloud companies are profitably raking in the hosting fees, but the AI companies like OpenAI that sell their services to people and businesses are operating unprofitably, funded by venture capital funds with the hope of future profits.

Additionally, a recent MIT study found that the majority of enterprise deployments of AI are not living up to the hype. About 5% of deployments result in new revenue and profits, while the other 95% fail to show new revenue and profits. This is despite the fact that AI has been wildly popular at the consumer level.

[Fortune reports](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/):

> But for 95% of companies in the dataset, generative AI implementation is falling short. “The 95% failure rate for enterprise AI solutions represents the clearest manifestation of the GenAI Divide,” the report states. The core issue? Not the quality of the AI models, but the “learning gap” for both tools and organizations. While executives often blame regulation or model performance, MIT’s research points to flawed enterprise integration. Generic tools like ChatGPT excel for individuals because of their flexibility, but they stall in enterprise use since they don’t learn from or adapt to workflows, Challapally explained.

In other words, the research suggests that the tools are useful but they’re not being applied efficiently in those larger-scale environments compared to the individual level. It is of course much easier to tailor tech to boost productivity on a person-by-person basis than to roll out a solution to supposedly make tons of employees more productive.

Additionally, a significant amount of AI deployments are focused on business frontends, while the backend seems to be where the greatest gains are made at the moment. Fortune continued:

> The data also reveals a misalignment in resource allocation. More than half of generative AI budgets are devoted to sales and marketing tools, yet MIT found the biggest ROI in back-office automation—eliminating business process outsourcing, cutting external agency costs, and streamlining operations.

I’ve made a similar point to that as well, arguing that AI tools will help make the back-ends of banks and insurers more efficient, cutting costs:

> It comes down to individual details and execution, but overall I continue to find plenty of reasonable long-term opportunities in the financial sector. They’re not in the crosshairs of tariffs, AI tools can help them save costs on the backend (i.e. more productive per employee), and large fiscal deficits help keep them nominally supported.
>
> -September 14, 2025 premium report

Different studies may find different results, of course. But I’ve seen enough evidence to notice the overall trend.

So, if chipmakers are expensive, AI companies aren’t profitable, and the majority of business implementations of AI fail to accrue positive results, then where can one invest? One possible answer is to invest in companies that help other companies implement AI for tangible results, since that area still has a long way to go.

One such example is Accenture (ACN):

![ACN FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-acn-fastgraph.png)

Accenture is a global professional services company. They get contracted by governments and corporations to help them with a blend of technology implementation and management consulting. These days, that includes implementing AI strategies and technologies, and re-orienting a business around this new tech. From their 2024 10-k filing:

> We help our clients use technology to drive enterprise-wide transformation, which includes:
>
> -building their digital core—such as moving them to the cloud, leveraging data and AI, and embedding security across the enterprise
>
> -optimizing their operations—such as helping our clients digitize faster, access digital talent and reduce costs as well as through digitizing engineering and manufacturing; and
>
> -accelerating their revenue growth—such as through using technology and creativity to create personalized connections, experiences and targeted sales at scale, leveraging data and AI, transforming content supply chains and marketing and commerce models and helping create new digital services and business models.
>
> Our clients turn to us to help them drive reinvention with our unique combination of services across Strategy & Consulting, Technology, Operations, Industry X and Song. Our strategists and deep industry, functional, customer and technology consultants work hand-in-hand with our clients and across services to shape and deliver these reinventions.
>
> At the same time, we see AI as the new digital. Like digital, AI is both a technology and a new way of working, and its full value will only come from strategies built on both productivity and growth. And we believe it will be used in every part of the enterprise. We also believe the introduction of generative AI signifies a transformative era that is set to drive growth for us and our clients.
>
> -Accenture 2024 10-k

The company employs 779,000 people and made nearly $70 billion in revenue over the past year.

![ACN YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-acn-ychart.png)

The company’s recent results have been strong, but they’ve had some lighter forecasts lately as they seek to realign their business more around AI. As such, they’ll be doing an $865 million restructuring, including downsizing some employees (which comes with severance pay as a cost) and other one-time changes. In addition, they lost some government contracts which spooked the market, but government contracts account for only about 8% of their revenue (and it’s not just the US government). During this time of heightened uncertainty, the stock price dropped considerably.

However, I would argue that their stock price has been overvalued for years, which is why I avoided it. This drop brings them below their historical average valuation for the first time since the pandemic crash of 2020, and back to some semblance of fair value. So, I am interested in the stock for the first time.

If the company can grow earnings at 7-8% per year, while paying a 2.5% dividend yield along the way, that’s pretty solid. A dividend-adjusted PEG ratio of 2x on that would imply a price/earnings ratio of around 20x or less, and their current multiple has dropped just below that.

Additionally, the price to free cash flow ratio is more attractive (around 15x) because they earn more free cash flow than net income in most years.

![ACN FCF FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-acn-fcf-fastgraph.png)

They have a rock-solid balance sheet, with an AA- credit rating, and more cash than debt.

The company does, of course, have some risks. Some fear that their services will be disrupted by AI, although I don’t see that happening on an investable time horizon (5-10 years). A more likely concern is that their restructuring could fail to accelerate profits, and the valuation could sink further (i.e. if a 20x earnings multiple is possible, a 15x multiple is also possible). In addition, the company is one of many that are based in Ireland for its tax haven purposes, and that arrangement could become politically targeted at some point in this age of growing nationalism/protectionism and fiscal dominance.

Overall, I have become moderately bullish on ACN. I’m not sure if this is the bottom of the sell-off or not, and thus I am not aggressively committing to the position, but I consider it suitable to put capital into as part of a first tranche. I will then monitor it and see how it is going to see if it warrants additional capital.

**Moderately Bullish on PYPL**

PayPal is a large global payments processing company that owns its namesake brand, as well as Venmo and Braintree. Venmo provides consumer peer-to-peer social payments, while Braintree provides backend payment solutions for companies, and PayPal itself provides branded checkout solutions.

![PYPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-pypl-fastgraph.png)

PayPal has gone through the growing pains of transitioning from a growth stock to a value stock. The question now is can they be a high-quality value stock, or will they become a value trap?

A pretty firm stock price bottom seems to be in. In autumn 2023, they dipped to around $50/share and an earnings multiple of around 10x. Around that time, they got a new CEO, Alex Chriss, who is implementing new strategies to turn the company around.

The main challenge with PayPal is that they have plenty of competition from other payment solutions. The good news is that their solutions tend to be pretty sticky due to high switching costs. For example, PayPal handles about a third of my website payment volume, and hell will probably freeze over before I change that due to all of the technical headaches that would ensue. It takes something pretty massive to switch payment processors once you’ve implemented a solution, if it’s tied into your software stack in some meaningful way.

A key initiative at PayPal currently is that they are integrating Venmo, PayPal, and Braintree together more. In other words, they have these three big networks, so it should be easy to pay with Venmo to any merchant that uses PayPal on the receiver side, but those sorts of integrations haven’t historically been prioritized enough. As they tie their systems together and extend globally, it opens up some growth vectors.

PayPal is ultimately a value-oriented cash flowing business, so we shouldn’t expect major growth. The ideal scenario is to enjoy moderate growth while giving excess cash back to shareholders in the form of dividends, buybacks, or balance sheet improvements. They currently do that primarily through share buybacks.

The balance sheet is great, the earnings multiple is around 15x, and with new management and transformation plans in place, I’m moderately bullish and believe that it warrants a starter position.

## Final Thoughts: Soaring Precious Metals

Last week, gold touched over $4,000 for the first time and silver flirted with its all-time high of around $50.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-gold-chart.png)

![Silver Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-10-12-silver-chart.png)

We’re at the stage where separating timeframes is important. I’ve been bullish and long on precious metals since 2018, when gold was $1,500 and silver was $15. Notably, the newsletter portfolio began in September 2018 (the oldest of my model portfolios), and then for the [first update](https://www.lynalden.com/november-2018-newsletter/) in November 2018, I added precious metals to it.

After a substantial bull run, I became [more cautious](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2020-newsletter/) on gold in mid-2020, citing near-term headwinds but re-iterating my long-term bullishness. Gold chopped sideways for the next 2.5 years all the way until late 2023. By early 2024, it started taking off, and ran all the way into 2025.

In April 2025, I highlighted some overbought concerns with gold again, and it consolidated sideways for the next 4-5 months into August, with me providing frequent updates within my “final thoughts” sections about the ongoing consolidation.

In August 2025, I saw signs that it was breaking out, but was somewhat conflicted. It was still overbought, and yet the structural trends were strong. I’m a contrarian value-investor at heart, and so overbought well-performing things ironically tend to rub me the wrong way intuitively, but that’s a tendency I am conscious of and try to override when I’m highly confident that it’s part of a structural trend. So, I erred with the structural trends in that August report:

> The good news lately is that gold is back to the top end of that consolidation range, and has touched above the psychologically important $3,500/oz range. Additionally, the increasing consensus around a dovish Fed is beneficial for the price, all else being equal.
>
> The bad news is that it’s not really a true breakout yet, more like a test, and it’s still structurally overbought on the monthly chart:
>
> ![Gold Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-stockcharts.png)
>
> Now, because I think we’re going through major long-term changes (the end of a four-decade structurally declining interest rate cycle, a global shift in sovereign interest from US Treasuries to gold around the margins in an increasingly multi-polar world, and persistent US fiscal dominance which fuels the attractiveness of hard assets), gold remaining in overbought status for unusually long periods of time is not unreasonable.
>
> That’s why since autumn 2018 when I added precious metals to the newsletter portfolio, I’ve happily erred on staying long even through periods where I expect intermediate-term weakness.
>
> Money Metals visualizes it well in their [quarterly newsletter](https://www.moneymetals.com/uploads/content/MoneyMetalsInsider_Summer2025.pdf). The dollar is no longer expanding as a share of global reserves, and gold is no longer falling, in what is likely the bottom of a trend that basically began after WWII:
>
> ![Money Metals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-money-metals.png)
>
> After all these 5 months of price chop, I am increasingly uncertain as to whether to expect a breakout or more consolidation. Given my prior comments, I continue to be structurally long, and at the very least I am less negative on the intermediate-term prospects for the metal than I was in April.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

Since then, we’ve been in breakout territory, with a fast jump from $3,300 to $4,000 for gold, with other precious metals along for the ride.

I’m once again reaching a similar view that I had back in April: that I’m still long-term bullish, but my 6-month expectations have greatly diminished, after such a sizable run.
