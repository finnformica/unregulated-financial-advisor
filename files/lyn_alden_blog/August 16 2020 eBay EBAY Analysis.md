# eBay Inc (EBAY) Deep Dive Analysis

*August 16, 2020*![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report focuses on macro updates, discusses the latest portfolio changes, and then analyzes a household-name internet company that is in the process of consolidation.

As a housekeeping note, I published a [new public article](https://www.lynalden.com/tax-shift/) last week on how certain tax and healthcare changes have affected the U.S. stock market and U.S. economy in certain ways, and released a [free newsletter issue](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2020-newsletter/) today that went into detail on the Treasury market and precious metals.

## U.S. Business Cycle Update

We’re at a crossroads in the United States, and this time period is quite sensitive in terms of having run off the “fiscal cliff”.

As I’ll show in the economic data later in this report, there is a disconnect between employment levels and consumer spending levels, and that disconnect is due to a large amount of government transfer payments that filled the income gap that the spike in unemployment caused. However, many of those government payments stopped in recent weeks, and are in limbo from political gridlock.

Whether you’re in favor of or against any of the particular fiscal proposals by Republicans or Democrats, the numbers themselves are in the economic data. When fiscal spending shuts off for a while, things start looking quite a bit different from an investment perspective than they do when fiscal spending is on, as I’ll outline later.

I’ve previously described the current environment (and likely the environment over the next several years) as a “fiscal heavy” environment, meaning that economic data will be quite heavily tied to fiscal spending or lack thereof (with a lag), and as we get either aggressive spending moments or gridlocks, we’re likely to see ebbs and flows between the various indicators that this fiscal spending affects.

There will likely be a recurring trade-off between economic data and currency devaluation, as fiscal spending patterns ebb and flow over time.

**COVID-19 Update**

In my report from two weeks ago, new daily cases of COVID-19 in the United States were starting to level off, but the death rate was still going up. That’s because deaths happen with a lag. I wrote in that report:

> Last month, when we had rising new cases but not yet a rise in deaths, I wrote that we need to look for a potential increase in deaths by mid/late July, and that’s what we got.
>
> This month, we can probably say the opposite: with daily new cases no longer increasing, the daily death rate also looks set to level out in the coming weeks as well. Hopefully that happens.

Fortunately, it did happen. Deaths have started to go flat:

![COVID-19 Data](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-covid-19.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

Europe and East Asia are coming in with much lower case and death rates per capita than the U.S. currently, so we are still “running hot” relative to many other countries. In terms of reported U.S. daily death numbers, the numbers are equivalent to a 9/11 terrorist event happening every three days.

However, the rate of change is moving in the right direction now, both in the overall U.S. data and in the specific southern states that were leading this second wave.

Brazil has a been a hot spot, and they are seeing some leveling out in their data as well. India remains among the last big countries where daily cases and deaths are still marching up week after week. Their economic data has also been weaker than I would have guessed when this all started.

**Jobless Claims Update**

Jobless claims continue to be one of my preferred indicators because they come out weekly, and that is quite important in such an extreme macro environment compared to having to wait a full month to see a reported change.

We have improving news here. After a troublesome rise in July, when a big wave of PPP loans were maturing (at which point, small businesses could begin downsizing employees and still have those federal loans be forgiven), jobless claims are back on the downtrend in August:

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The bad news continues to be that, even at this reduced level compared to the April peak, weekly initial jobless claims and the total number of continued jobless claims, are both far higher than the peak level reached in the global financial crisis in 2009.

If we dig into the report, and look at self-employed people and other groups receiving unemployment insurance, the total number of people receiving unemployment insurance is over 28 million, which is a few million off its peak but still very high in absolute terms.

There are temporary job losses caused by the virus itself, such as in restaurants, airlines, hotels, concerts, and those sorts of businesses operating at sharply reduced capacities, which is snapping back to varying degrees as they go from completely closed to partial operation.

But aside from that, there are permanent job losses caused by the credit cycle playing out, as many companies seek to reduce costs and streamline operations. In some cases, this is due to bankrupt small restaurants and other hard-hit types of businesses that aren’t making it through this. In other cases, it is the natural result of marginal businesses having to change things, such as Ford cutting a big chunk of its white collar workforce.

In that sense, there continues to be a high job churn rate, with jobs being created and destroyed. Job losses are moving up the income ladder into more white collar work, whereas the initial unemployment shock mainly focused on blue collar service work.

**The U.S. Business Cycle Heatmap**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Notice how much retail sales stand out from everything else at the moment:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

* Exports of Goods and Services
* Imports of Goods and Services

* 10 Year Minus 3 Month Yield Curve
* Consumer Prices, All Urban Consumers, All Items in City Average

The second quarter of 2020 was likely the economic bottom point, but it’ll take a lot of growth just to get back to even in some of these indicators, with employment likely taking quite a while, as the pandemic accelerated some technological trends that were already in place.

Real GDP, industrial production, exports, imports, and employee payrolls, are all way down. Construction spending is down from recent levels, but held up rather well in comparison to most of the other indicators. The big outlier is retail sales, which dipped briefly but swiftly bounced back to  new all-time highs.

The reason for this is due to government transfer payments. Unlike every other recession in the modern era, the government handed out a massive stimulus directly to consumers and businesses to more than offset the loss of employment income so far. Compared to the global financial crisis, the government transfer numbers aren’t even close.

Here is a chart of annualized rate of government transfer payments:

![Government Transfer Payments](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-transfer-payments.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This included $1,200 one-time stimulus checks to most people (employed and unemployed), $600/week in extra federal employment benefits for unemployed people (which is on top of their normal state unemployment benefits), an expansion of unemployment benefits to include self-employed people that lost income (which they normally don’t cover), over $500 billion into small businesses in the form of loans that mostly turn into grants via repayment forgiveness, corporate bailouts, hospital payments, and other types of aid.

As a result, national personal income went up rather than down this year, so far. This chart shows personal income and retail sales in this crisis and in the previous crisis from a decade ago:

![Personal Income and Retail Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-retail-sales-personal-income.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

At the moment, we have a slow recovery in employment and broad economic activity, but specifically a V-shaped recovery in personal incomes, personal expenditures, and retail sales, due to government aid replacing that lost employment income.

Over the next year, we’ll see if this dips back down eventually, and causes a more normal recessionary event, or if transfer payments keep incomes from declining. Successful investing in this environment unfortunately is quite tied to accurately predicting how much fiscal spending happens or doesn’t happen, in terms of offsetting the employment shock.

Not everyone made a higher income, though. Many owners of hard-hit small businesses lost income because PPP loans/grants did not fully replace their bottom-line owner profit. Some high-payed employees lost income, because for example if someone lost a $250,000/year job, all of these extra unemployment benefits didn’t replace that.

However, a large percentage of people among the middle class, working class, and poor made more money than they otherwise would have over these past four months due to the government aid. Many people kept their existing jobs, and also got that extra $1,200 stimulus check. Other people lost their jobs, but made more per week from combined unemployment aid and the $1,200 stimulus check. Many small businesses were barely impacted, but still got PPP loans that turn into grants because they met the requirements, so free money dropped onto their bottom line.

And much of that is shut off at the moment. No more stimulus checks, no more extra federal unemployment benefits, and no more rounds of PPP loans/grants. If that status persists for months, it would start looking more like a typical (but bigger) recession.

That is why, the timing and magnitude of the next round of fiscal stimulus is important to monitor from an investment perspective. Whether we agree with it or not, depending on what form it takes, it does affect outcomes like inflation/deflation, consumer spending, the number of bankruptcies, the results of cyclical businesses, and so forth.

The Conference Board consumer confidence index is showing that, despite the rapid fall from high levels, consumer confidence has so far remained well above the trough levels that normal recessions (with less unemployment and less GDP contraction) normally reach:

![Consumer Confidence](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-consumer-confidence.png)

*Chart Source: [Advisor Perspectives](https://www.advisorperspectives.com/dshort/updates/2020/07/28/consumer-confidence-down-in-july)*

So far, that is again because a large amount of fiscal spending has buffered how most people are feeling this recession. There’s a lot more room to go down if fiscal spending subsides, and the system’s insolvency starts to play itself out.

As I covered in my recent [Current Macro Themes piece](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-themes-2020-8-2/), the fact that this recession is different than normal is to be somewhat expected, based on where we are in the long-term debt cycle. There is so much debt in the system including at the sovereign level, and so much wealth concentration, that there is inevitably more intervention than a typical deleveraging event, and the challenge is that it can manifest itself in different ways and on uncertain timelines, partly based on politics.

The resolution of a long-term debt cycle (described in detail in the Themes piece) is a highly complex and challenging event, generally with big political and cultural changes, and plays out for quite a while. The general outcome almost inevitably involves a lot of fiscal spending and currency devaluation, forced by civil unrest and debt-driven deflationary forces.

My base case is that the next round of fiscal spending will happen prior to the election, because very few people in power want to be the ones viewed as having held back free money to people and see this consumer confidence level go down.

Trump is saying he wants to send out more stimulus checks and business aid but that Democrats are holding things up by wanting to bail out financially imprudent states. Democrats are saying they want to help the people in a bigger way but Republicans are holding it up because they want to prioritize their wealthy corporate buddies. There are multiple ways like this to spin it into a blame game as the election draws near, and they will.

The percentage of politicians outright calling for little or no fiscal spending out of a sense of fiscal restraint in principle, is small. However, the types of spending they want to do, and the magnitude of it, has a big spectrum of differing opinions.

The next few months is a very bumpy and uncertain period, and nothing is for certain.

## eBay Inc (EBAY) Deep-Dive Analysis

eBay Inc (EBAY) is this week’s pick for a deep dive analysis.

Most people are familiar with it. It’s a large e-commerce company with a $40 billion market capitalization that focuses on consumer-to-consumer and business-to-consumer transactions.

They’ve had a nice stock run-up in 2020 but this was fully warranted in my view, given recent changes to the business and the fact their business benefited from COVID-19 rather than being harmed by it. Lately there was a bit of a pullback, letting off some of the overbought sentiment:

![eBay Stock Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-ebay-price.png)

eBay was founded 25 years ago in the mid-1990’s, and absolutely crushed the S&P 500 since inception.

Lately, however, they have been in an unfocused and low-growing period, but that is rapidly changing.

**Acquisitions and Divestitures**

eBay made a series of acquisitions over the years, and many of them were highly successful.

They bought payment company Paypal in 2002, and spun it off in 2015 as a public company that by then was far larger than eBay itself.

They bought communications company Skype in 2005, and sold it several years later, and now it’s owned by Microsoft.

They bought online ticket company StubHub in 2007 for a few hundred million dollars, and sold it earlier this year for about $4 billion, to European online ticket company Viagogo. eBay really lucked out here with the timing, because sporting events around the world were crushed shortly after this due to COVID-19, and that deeply affected StubHub.

The big recent news is that eBay announced the sale of its online classified business to Adevinta, a Norwegian online classifieds business. The deal was worth $9.2 billion, including $2.5 billion in cash and the remaining in Adevinta stock. Adevinta’s stock price has gone up since the announcement, so eBay’s current gain for the sale is well north of $10 billion on paper. This one hasn’t closed yet but is well agreed upon, with a high chance of closing in early 2021.

Adevinta focuses on online classifieds as its core business, and expects to have $150+ million/year cost synergies (not buzzword ones, but tangible ones) which should unlock a bit of additional value from this, and eBay’s new stake in Adevinta stock will let them participate in this. So, eBay’s classifieds business simply makes more sense with Adevinta than with eBay and the sale seems to be a smart one.

Back in early 2019, Elliott Management, a well-known activist fund, took a position in eBay and suggested selling their Stubhub and classifieds businesses, and gave specific valuations that they estimated them to be were worth. This was because eBay was in a period of relative stock price stagnation, as investors valued the company at a lower multiple, and growth was sluggish. eBay ended up selling those businesses within the ranges for what Elliott Management estimated them to be worth, and hit Elliott Management’s stock price target for doing so. Well played!

So now the question is, as eBay goes forward as a streamlined namesake marketplace business, is it still attractive, or has all of the value been unlocked? I lean towards the former, as the stock seems to be in an attractive place now.

**Management and Performance**

eBay management has been… mixed. There’s more to say here than for most companies.

On one hand, their performance since inception has been incredible. Even some of their unfocused acquisitions ended up being extremely lucrative by the time they were divested. The company earns high returns on invested capital and a high profit margin.

On the other hand, their core marketplace business has stagnated with slow growth, and needs revitalizing. I think Elliott Management’s activist prodding of the company made a lot of sense at that time, and it worked out exactly how they expected by unlocking a lot of value and re-focusing the business.

Beyond that, it gets weird.

This year, several former high-ranking eBay employees [were arrested](https://www.justice.gov/usao-ma/pr/six-former-ebay-employees-charged-aggressive-cyberstalking-campaign-targeting-natick) for cyber-stalking a couple that had a blog/newsletter that was critical of eBay. The alleged events happened in 2019. From the link:

> BOSTON – Six former employees of eBay, Inc. have been charged with leading a cyberstalking campaign targeting the editor and publisher of a newsletter that eBay executives viewed as critical of the company. The alleged harassment included sending the couple anonymous, threatening messages, disturbing deliveries – including a box of live cockroaches, a funeral wreath and a bloody pig mask – and conducting covert surveillance of the victims.

eBay’s then-CEO David Wenig left the company shortly after the alleged cyberstalking events happened in 2019, although the cyberstalking was not public yet, and he cited his disagreements with the Elliott Management divestiture plan as the catalyst for his moving on. eBay revealed in June 2020 [via a press release](https://www.ebayinc.com/stories/news/ebay-inc-issues-statement-regarding-indictments-of-previously-terminated-employees/) that an internal investigation found that Wenig’s communications to other executives about the topic were inappropriate. From the link:

> The Company noted that the internal investigation also examined what role, if any, the Company’s CEO at the time of the incident, Devin Wenig, may have had in this matter. The internal investigation found that, while Mr. Wenig’s communications were inappropriate, there was no evidence that he knew in advance about or authorized the actions that were later directed toward the blogger and her husband. However, as the Company previously announced, there were a number of considerations leading to his departure from the Company.

After Wenig left the company, there was an interim CEO, and then in late April 2020, the interim CEO was replaced with Jamie Iannone, who is the current CEO.

Jamie Iannone worked at eBay from 2001 to 2009 up to a vice president role, and then left the company and pursued roles at Walmart. Specifically, he was CEO of SamsClub.com (owned by Walmart), and then moved up to COO for e-commerce at Walmart.

Walmart has performed well in ecommerce in recent years. In an era where many physical retailers are being devastated, some of them like Walmart (WMT), Target (TGT), Home Depot (HD), and Best Buy (BBY) have kept up by becoming omnichannel retailers, with both physical locations and major ecommerce websites.

In my view, there are good signs that Iannone, with his extensive background at both eBay and Walmart, is the right CEO for the job here. In his 3.5 month tenure so far, he finished the existing consolidation plan by coming to a successful agreement to sell the classifieds business, and has the right priorities in my view. He acknowledged the issues with eBay’s past, and hopefully can improve the culture of the company as it moves forward as a more streamlined business that focuses on its namesake online marketplace.

Iannone and the rest of eBay’s leadership currently have a specific set of written targets. Specifically, they target to have a long-term average of $3.5 billion in cash and short-term investments (tons of liquidity), gross debt-to-EBITDA of below 3x, net deb-to-EBITDA of below 1.5x, and to try to grow steadily while returning a ton of cash to shareholders in the form of dividends and share buybacks.

These targets make perfect sense for a mature cash cow business like eBay. Combined with their recent divestitures of non-core businesses, I think eBay makes a lot of sense as a relative value stock here.

As of the end of Q2, eBay has $5.3 billion in cash and $8.7 billion in debt. That’s a net debt position of $3.4 billion, or $3.9 billion if we include about $500 million of short-term debt that they have on the sheet as well.

![eBay Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-ebay-balance-sheet.png)

The company generates about $2.5 billion in free cash flow per year or more, so its debt to free cash flow ratio is great. When it completes the sale of its classifieds business, it’ll get another one-time $2.5 billion cash injection, offset by lower incoming free cash flows due to no longer having that business. They will also have several billion dollars worth of Adevinta stock that they can sell over time to divest and raise more cash.

The overall balance sheet is strong. They have plenty of flexibility to use the current cash level and incoming cash to reduce debt, reduce share count, pay dividends, and potentially make targeted acquisitions that improve its namesake marketplace.

Going forward, the important thing to watch is how eBay’s online marketplace performs, now that it has the full attention of the company, and under new management. Although it’s a high-margin cash cow business, it has been growing very slowly in recent years, and totally flat over the past year, until it had a temporary surge in Q2:

![eBay Stagnation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-ebay-stagnation.png)

*Chart Source: eBay Q2 2020 Earnings Presentation*

I expect that current management will be able to slowly grow this business in a sustainable way, but there is wide variability in regards to how this will play out over time, and therein lies the key risk.

**Valuation Analysis**

eBay had a stock price surge recently. However, compared to many other well-functioning pandemic-resistant businesses like the Apple and Microsoft charts I showed earlier, eBay doesn’t show signs of being notably overvalued:

![eBay FastGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-ebay-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

It’s not a screaming buy, but among many of the alternatives I see in the stock market, and compared to cash and bonds, this one looks quite reasonable as a blend of quality and value.

The earnings multiple is in the mid-to-high teens, which makes sense with high single digit earnings growth and a small dividend (with plenty of liquidity and not much criticality), and is roughly in line with its historic average. In the chart above, I only go back to the point after Paypal was spun off, because before then the combined company had significantly different characteristics that make a direct comparison improper.

Consensus analysts expectations are currently for GAAP EPS of $3.04 in 2020, $3.34 in 2021, and $3.95 in 2022.

Here’s a base-case analysis:

![eBay StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-8-16-ebay-stockdelver.png)

*Source: [StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/stockdelver-book/)*

By this estimate, eBay is fairly-valued.

The price could reasonably be a lot higher if investors are willing to accept 7-8% returns rather than 10% returns. Alternatively, the price could be a lot lower if 4% overall top-line growth ends up being too much and the company fails to maintain market share against competitors, and goes on to stagnate and/or shrink.

There is a moderate amount of sensitivity here, but not as much as highly-valued growth stocks.

Once eBay closes the classifieds business sale by early 2021, investors should also be mindful that eBay has a decent possibility of eventually being acquired by a bigger company, since it’s quite streamlined now rather than a collection of separate businesses. This is an upside potential, since an acquisition would likely come with a substantial valuation premium.

**Company Risks**

*Competition Risk*

To put it lightly, eBay faces significant competition from other online marketplaces, such as Amazon, Etsy, and Craigslist.

eBay’s marketplace has a strong network effect and brand recognition, but it has been outpaced in recent years by strong competitors. Amazon began including marketplace sellers and now competes basically with everyone and everything, and Etsy comes from a vintage/crafts angle, so eBay has to work hard to remain relevant. Craigslist and other classifieds businesses compete to some extent with eBay’s namesake marketplace, as many items can either be sold to someone locally or shipped to a long-distance buyer.

eCommerce is a growing pie, and there is plenty of room for eBay to succeed. However, they have to maintain and grow the desirability for both buyers and sellers to use their marketplace compared to some of the alternatives. I’m optimistic that eBay’s current management can do this well, but time will tell.

*Payments Risk*

eBay recently shifted to using its own payments solution, which gives buyers more ways to pay (cards, Paypal, Apple Pay, Google Pay, etc). While this is great, there is implementation risk of any major change like this. For example, some sellers were reporting an inability to do partial refunds with the new system.

Shortcomings like that have to be improved, and quickly. With a focused business model now, there’s no reason why they shouldn’t be able to make this a top-tier solution.

*Macro Risk*

eBay is quite resistant to economic cycles. On one hand, recessions can reduce some types of discretionary transactions on its marketplace, but on the other hand, recessions can cause more people to sell their items or want to seek deals by buying other peoples’ pre-owned items. “Selling your extra stuff on eBay,” is a well known way to raise money if needed. I know someone that routinely buys his cars from eBay and fixes them up, and saves a ton of money over the years by doing so.

Like other companies, however, eBay faces risks from corporate tax changes and other macro/political changes. A higher corporate tax rate would of course reduce net income, and thus reduce the stock price assuming a static earnings multiple. I think the possibility of higher corporate taxes in the United States within the 2020’s decade is significant.

*Sentiment Risk*

The market is frequently trading with a wholesale “risk on or risk off” sentiment recently.

When we get a “vaccine breakthrough” news day, or “we’re making progress on fiscal stimulus” news day, the market often has a sell-off in tech names that have recently performed well, and a bidding up of beaten-down value stocks that are more economically sensitive. And then when we get a “bad economic report” news day or a “fiscal stimulus is gridlocked” news day, we tend to have tech stocks bid up and sensitive value stocks sold. eBay has generally traded with the risk-off crowd, since it’s an online marketplace that is benefiting from COVID-19.

So, even if eBay executes well and is at a reasonable valuation, it could easily underperform for a year or two based simply on investor sentiment preferring to shift into higher-risk stocks and sell some of the recent winners. Other parts of my portfolio could benefit from that, but eBay might very well not.

In the fullness of time, eBay stock should do well from current levels if they execute well on growing their core business and paying a high shareholder yield, but that’s inherently a long-term outcome.

In other words, there may be points in time where eBay becomes a “pound the table buy” rather than a “well, it’s okay and probably better than many alternatives” buy. As it currently stands compared to many of the alternative places to put money, eBay’s current long-term risk/reward profile appears relatively attractive within the context of a diversified portfolio.

## Summary

The U.S. economy, and more broadly the global economy, is entering a challenging period. Bonds offer incredibly low yields, many stocks are extremely expensive, and there remains a big solvency question over the next couple years as high unemployment and fiscal stimulus are the two competing forces that affect the ability of consumers and businesses to pay bills and buy things.

I prefer not to invest in high-risk and high-reward companies that could either go bust or soar higher based strictly on bailouts and stimulus. I’d rather invest in some of the stronger companies in their respective industries, because the probability that they will weather tough times is very high.

Based on my analysis, eBay is about fairly-valued in terms of having a decent likelihood of providing good equity returns over the next 5+ years. Although it’s not notably undervalued, its valuation is reasonable given its recession-resistant business, high liquidity, and new CEO, with a recently-streamlined business model that allows executives to focus on supporting and growing its namesake marketplace against strong competition in the still-growing ecommerce industry.
