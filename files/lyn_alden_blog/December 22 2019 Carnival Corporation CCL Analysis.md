# Carnival Corporation Deep-Dive Analysis

*Published December 22, 2019*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This issue takes a look at the status of the U.S. business cycle, and then explores a high-yielding cyclical stock that has been beaten down lately, but may be starting to break out.

## U.S. Business Cycle Status

This quarter has been about valuation expansion of stocks along with flat economic performance. S&P 500 earnings have been flat this year and this quarter while economic indicators are relatively weak, but the stock market has reached new all time highs and has become rather euphoric.

The tariff rollback with China and the Fed injecting liquidity into the markets have both likely been strong tailwinds for equity prices. Market sentiment has been very positive.

CNN’s fear and greed index highlights the situation well, since it’s back near its peak greed level:

![CNN Fear Greed Index December 2019](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-cnn-fear-greed-index.png)

*Chart Source: CNN*

The most important economic metric I am watching at this point is the 4-week moving average of initial jobless claims, which comes out every week. Labor costs are increasing, and corporations have already pulled a lot of levers to increase profitability and have bought back as many shares each year as possible, so cuts to labor costs by corporations are a serious option at this point.

The U.S. is currently at a 50-year low point for initial jobless claims per week. This metric tends to bottom and turn up before recessions:

![Initial Claims Long Term](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-initial-claims-longterm.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Zooming in on the same chart, we can see that the 4-week moving average has potentially bottomed. During this decade-long economic expansion, this is the first time that initial jobless claims have stopped decreasing for over a year:

![Initial Claims December 19](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-initial-claims-dec19.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Now, it’s possible that we could still see another leg lower in initial jobless claims, but being data-dependent at the moment suggests a higher probability that the initial jobless claims bottom is in.

And if this starts rising rather than remaining relatively flat, we could start seeing more serious economic cycle risks as we head a couple quarters into 2020. I’ll be sure to keep updating readers on this indicator as it goes up or down, along with my monthly business cycle heat map of broader data.

A specific data point I happen to follow, which I discussed a while ago on the free newsletter, is heavy truck sales. Unlike some of the broader core indicators I track, this one is rather niche, but it’s useful because it’s a leading indicator rather than a coincident indicator. We just got a new data point last week showing that heavy truck sales dropped off a cliff in October and November:

![Heavy Truck Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-heavy-truck-sales.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This could be like 2000 or 2007, presaging the end of the business cycle within a year or so, or it could be a fake-out like 1995 or 2016, where the economy faltered but was able to get back up and keep pushing higher without a recession due to stimulus and other factors. But, not good at the moment.

## Carnival Corporation (CCL) Deep Dive Analysis

This report’s focus stock is Carnival Corporation (ticker: CCL), which is a relatively aggressive stock idea.

Carnival is the largest cruise line operator in the world, and besides its primary Carnival brand, it owns a variety of other brands including Holland America Line, Princess Cruises, Costa Cruises, P&O Cruises, AIDA, and Seabourn. The company has outperformed the S&P 500 from as far back as 1990, but with more volatility:

![Carnival Historical Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-ccl-historical-returns.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

However, the company has underperformed the S&P 500 over the past 3-year, 5-year, and 10-year periods due to its big sell-off over the past 2 years.

At this relatively beaten down point with indications of stabilization, I am interested.

**Company Overview**

Here is how the company summarizes itself in its annual filing:

> We are the world’s largest leisure travel company and among the most profitable and financially strong in the cruise and vacation industries. We are also the largest cruise company, carrying nearly half of global cruise guests, and a leading provider of vacations to all major cruise destinations throughout the world. With operations in North America, Australia, Europe and Asia, we operate a portfolio of leading global, regional and national cruise brands that sell tailored cruise products, services and vacation experiences on 104 cruise ships to the world’s most desirable destinations.

Carnival earned $18.9 billion USD in revenue in 2018. Of that, $10.1 billion came from North America, $6.0 billion came from Europe, $2.5 billion came from Australia and Asia, and the small remainder came from elsewhere. Their market capitalization is currently around $34 billion.

The two other leading cruise stocks in order of size are Royal Caribbean (RCL) and Norwegian Cruise Lines (NCLH). Each of these major brands owns a variety of smaller brands that target various niches or regions, and together the three companies pretty much dominate the global cruise industry.

Each cruise operator is known for a certain brand image. Carnival tends to be the relaxed budget-friendly one with among the lowest costs. Royal Caribbean and Norwegian are known for being slightly more upscale. Each company also owns higher-end luxury brands though, like Carnival’s Seabourn brand and Norwegian’s Oceana brand.

For a while, Royal Caribbean has had the biggest ships, but Carnival is in the process of preparing a large set of new ships that match Royal Caribbean’s largest ships in size, which closes that particular gap.

Frankly, all three cruise operators interest me at the moment and appear to be decent long-term values. I selected Carnival for its balance sheet strength, deeper value, and higher dividend yield. As the largest operator, it has cost advantages over its competitors and has a very diversified worldwide business.

Carnival has a relatively strong A- credit rating, while Royal Caribbean is on the bottom end of investment-grade with just BBB-. Carnival has a much higher interest coverage ratio and a much lower debt/income ratio, which gives it a stronger financial position.

Carnival has a 4.0% dividend yield while Royal Caribbean has a 2.4% dividend yield and Norwegian has no dividend. Royal Caribbean and Norwegian have a higher growth rate than Carnival, but the difference is less significant when leverage is adjusted for.

Carnival is trading at a P/E ratio of 11.4 and an EV/EBITDA ratio of 6.6, while Royal Caribbean is trading at a P/E ratio of 13.8 and an EV/EBITDA of 8.2.

In hindsight, perhaps I could have added Royal Caribbean to the newsletter model portfolio and Carnival to the Fortress Income portfolio (due to its higher yield and stronger balance sheet in exchange for lower growth potential), but I have gone with Carnival for both for now.

**Management and Company Metrics**

Arnold Donald has been a board member of Carnival since 2001, and became the CEO in 2013 after the company experienced a series of problems which are mentioned in the risk section below. By all accounts he has been a successful CEO during his tenure so far.

Due to his long tenure on the board and six-year tenure as CEO, Donald has a rather large $30 million position in Carnival stock. However, he was a seller of the stock for many years. This is normal; his compensation was largely in shares so he occasionally had to cash out. It’s often said that company executives and directors can sell for any variety of reasons, but they only *buy* for one reason: bullishness. After years of selling, Donald began buying shares in December 2018 and June 2019 at a cost basis in the high-$40’s, not far from where the price is now at around $50.

One thing I really like about Donald’s leadership is his focus on improving the company’s return on invested capital metric (ROIC). Multiple studies show that ROIC is one of the most important metrics that a company can focus on for valuation improvement, and he is aware of that. Under his tenure, he made it a public goal for the company to reverse the company’s deteriorating ROIC and eventually achieve double-digit ROIC and he has steadily been able to climb up in that metric.

![Carnival ROIC ROE ROA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-ccl-roic-roe-roa.png)

*Chart Source: Y Charts*

Current ROIC across the whole cruise industry has been below my normal threshold, but because the operators enjoy an oligopoly-like status, they are still able to dig narrow economic moats around their branded and differentiated business models, which gives them some degree of resilience and outperformance potential.

**Growth Plans**

The interesting thing about the cruise industry is that it’s a lot smaller than people may realize, with very low penetration rates. Among developed markets, only about 3% of the population takes a cruise in a given year.

The market capitalization of the entire publicly-traded global cruise industry is less than, say, the market capitalization of the 3M Company (MMM) or Cisco Systems (CSCO), and less than a tenth of the market capitalization of a titan like Apple (AAPL).

Arnold Donald recently gave an interview in Chief Executive magazine, and he had this to say when asked about potential disruption to the cruise industry:

> **We’ve seen disruption in the hotel and transportation industries with Airbnb, Uber and Lyft. How concerned are you about disruption in the cruise industry?**
>
> The beauty of the cruise industry is that it’s tiny. We’ll maybe sail as an industry 30 million people this year. There are 1.4 billion travelers a year, with over half a billion vacationers a year on the planet. The city of Orlando attracts something like 70 million tourists a year. Las Vegas sees 40-something million. Venice does 24 million or 25 million The entire global cruise industry is the size of one major tourism city, and not even that in some cases. So, we’re so tiny. Therefore, when you say disruption we’re not anywhere near a mature industry. We’re underpenetrated in every market in the world. We’re capacity-constrained because there are only so many shipyards. The most the industry can grow in a year is 7 percent off a small base. It will be tough for anything that involves being on the ocean, that requires a vessel, to be instantaneously disruptive because there’s limited shipbuilding capacity.

People that do cruises tend to be loyal to the concept, and keep going on more. There are pros and cons to cruises compared to alternate forms of vacation, but one of the main advantages is the convenience; people can hop aboard a ship (without taking a flight in many cases) and see multiple destinations without the hassle of managing multiple hotels, locations, flights, car rentals, sightseeing itineraries, and so forth.

In addition, Asia is a large source of potential future cruise passengers. As highly populous nations in Asia enter the middle and upper classes with disposable income for vacations, they are an ideal source of growth in the cruise industry, and all of the major operators already have cruise lines operating in Asia.

Analysts on average currently expect the next year to be relatively flat, but for high-single-digit annual EPS growth to resume after that, which along with the high dividend yield and low valuation could make for a compelling investment from current levels.

*The Second Dividend*

Carnival investors also get an extra perk. If you show proof that you own at least 100 shares of Carnival stock, you get an on-board credit of $50-$250 every time you take a cruise on most of Carnival’s brands. It’s $50 for cruises of less than one week, $100 for cruises between one and two weeks, and $250 for longer cruises. You have to contact customer service and show proof of stock ownership a few weeks in advance of the cruise.

The other cruise lines have very similar benefits, but because Carnival has the cheapest share price, the ratio of benefits to required stock ownership is the best. Naturally, if you are loyal to one cruise brand over another, you might consider owning that company’s stock to receive the benefit instead.

If you happen to be a regular cruiser, this can be a great offer, especially for Carnival. At about $50/share today, it requires about a $5,000 holding in Carnival stock to get the benefit, which is easy to do in a six-figure or seven-figure diversified portfolio. If you already happen to take a one-week cruise each year and start earning a $100 credit each time, for example, that’s a 2% return on your $5,000 investment per year on top of the normal rate of return that comes from stock appreciation and dividends.

If you cruise more regularly or take longer cruises, that benefit can ramp up dramatically. On the other hand, for people who don’t take cruises or don’t cruise regularly, they won’t get much of a benefit. It’s certainly worth being aware of at least; statistically there are probably a couple dozen cruisers in this research service out of the hundreds of members.

Basically, Carnival stock pays two dividends. One consists of cash, and the other consists of free drinks.

If the company achieves only 4% average annual revenue growth over the next decade, maintains steady margins, reduces its share count by an average of 2.5% per year, pays its 4% dividend, and is eventually sold for a P/E ratio of just 12, it can comfortably achieve 10% annualized returns. If the company enjoys faster top line growth, or the investor has opportunities to sell the stock at a higher earnings multiple (the average over the past 20 years was 17.8 compared to less than 11.5 today), the return could be significantly better.

**Risks**

Carnival has a limited economic moat, strong balance sheet, and low valuation, but it is a volatile stock and should be considered on the riskier side of the equity spectrum.

*Cyclical Business*

Cruises are the epitome of consumer discretionary companies. For hotel companies, at least they have a combination of vacationers and business travelers. For cruise operators, it’s all about vacationers. If the economy turns down, fewer people take cruises and prices decline across the industry, which squeezes profit margins.

In addition, these companies have significant operational leverage. They have significant fixed capital invested in their fleet of ships, so small declines in revenue can result in very large decreases in income. Carnival had to briefly eliminate the stock’s dividend during the global financial crisis, for example.

To some extent, Carnival has already experienced a partial recession over the past year. Europe’s significant economic slowdown materially impacted Carnival’s operations in that region, and the current U.S. administration banned cruise lines going to Cuba, all of which hit Carnival’s profitability and growth. However, if the U.S. enters a recession in the next couple of years, Carnival and other cruise operators are likely to take yet another hit.

The mitigating factor is that Carnival has survived multiple recessions in the past and the stock has already arguably priced in a recession. In addition, their geographic diversification generally gives them a buffer. Investors can further mitigate this cyclical aspect by dollar-cost averaging into positions, so that your cost basis averages down during broad market sell-offs.

The current drawdown from all-time highs is large, but if history is of any guide, there’s certainly a possibility for another leg lower before this business cycle is completed. Being a highly discretionary stock, Carnival’s drawdowns from all-time highs tend to lead the broader S&P 500. Prior to the past two recessions, Carnival stock was down 20-30% while the S&P 500 is still hitting its final new highs for the cycle:

![CCL Historical Drawdowns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2019-12-ccl-historical-drawdowns.png)

*Public Disasters*

In addition to a variety of smaller problems that inevitably occur to cruise ships from time to time, Carnival had two very bad and very public problems in 2012 and 2013 which helped prolong their woes from the previous cycle downturn.

In 2012, a ship from their Costa brand capsized due to human error off the coast of Italy and 32 passengers died. The captain left the ship prematurely while hundreds of living passengers were still aboard, and he ended up being charged with manslaughter for multiple reasons for his role throughout this disaster.

Then in 2013, a ship from Carnival’s namesake brand had an engine room fire that was automatically extinguished, but resulted in a loss of the ship’s power and a problem with the ship’s sanitation system, which resulted in human sewage infecting the ship. The ship with all of the passengers aboard was stranded for many days with daily media coverage, and the media nicknamed it the “poop cruise”. This incident hurt the company’s bookings and likely negatively-impacted the whole cruise industry.

These two events were so problematic that the longtime billionaire CEO was replaced by the current CEO, Arnold Donald, in 2013 to help turn the company back around.

Incidents like this are unpredictable, which is why keeping the stock to a small position, or investing more broadly in two or three cruise stocks rather than just one, can mitigate risk.

Two days ago on Friday, ironically when Carnival reported great quarterly results and saw its stock price go up by more than 7%, it was widely reported that two Carnival ships collided with each other in Mexico. One was stationary, and the other one reportedly was knocked into it due to winds or currents. There were no major injuries, and the ships only have some surface damage, but it nonetheless made headlines.

*Fuel Costs*

Carnival’s most variable cost is fuel. When the price of oil was low in 2016, their fuel costs for the year were $915 million, and then two years later in 2018, their annual fuel costs were $1.619 billion, representing a 77% increase. In comparison, their food costs were almost perfectly flat at $1 billion per year during that period.

Considering that the company makes about $3 billion per year in net income, these fluctuating fuel costs are substantial. To some extent, cruise operators can pass these fluctuations on to passengers, but a sustained rise in energy prices could pressure the industry.

Going forward, Carnival has largely switched over to LNG-powered ships to save money and for environmental benefits.

**Conclusion**

Personally speaking, I plan to start dollar-cost averaging into Carnival stock as part of a diversified portfolio. Despite a partial recovery, the stock is still down 30% from its all-time highs from less than 2 years ago.

It’s risky and cyclical enough, especially given the current late-cycle economic indicators, that I wouldn’t want to go “all in” at the current price in a big way. There is frankly a substantial probability that we’ll see new lows before new highs. However, with the stock already having priced in a recession, I’m willing to start building a small position from current levels.

If a recession comes much later than expected after having priced one in, there is a lot of upside potential. On the other hand, if a recession does come to U.S. shores in 2020 or 2021, Carnival’s strong balance sheet and wide geographic diversification should give the company significant defense to get through the rough seas and may provide further buying opportunities.
