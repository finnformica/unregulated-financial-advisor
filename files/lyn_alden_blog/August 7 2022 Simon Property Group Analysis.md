## Macro View

The US economy and much of the global economy continue to decelerate, however not all areas are equally affected.

The following macro sections provide a breakdown of some of the divergences in different data sets.

**US Business Cycle Analysis**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Rather than describing the economy in narratives or qualitative terms, or cherry pick data to fit a particularly bullish or bearish view, it quantifies a broad section of data.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 and Q4 of 2021 had decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation. Q1 and Q2 of 2022 had negative real GDP growth (although still positive year-over-year) with even hotter inflation:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Heat Map Indicators Referenced:**

* Real Gross Domestic Product
* Industrial Production
* Advance Retail Sales, Excluding Food Services
* Total Construction Spending
* Capital Goods Orders, Nondefense, Excluding Aircraft
* Exports of Goods and Services
* Imports of Goods and Services

* 10 Year Minus 3 Month Yield Curve
* Consumer Prices, All Urban Consumers, All Items in City Average

Note that many of these are nominal figures. Retail sales, construction spending, capital goods orders, exports, imports, are all measured in nominal dollars, and thus have a significant inflation component. Retail sales growth at 7.73% year-over-year, construction spending at 8.25%, and capital goods orders at 8.59% all seem like fine numbers, until we factor in 9% CPI, which puts those three indicators slightly negative in inflation-adjusted terms.

The US has had two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth so far this year. Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow estimator is forecasting an annualized real growth rate of 1.4% for Q3. During the start of the prior two quarters, the GDPNow calculator also had a positive expectation, but in both instances it turned negative by the end of the quarter.

**US Jobs Update**

Jobs continue to be cited as the main argument for why the US is not in a recession yet, despite the weakness in real GDP.

On Friday in particular, the monthly US jobs report came out *way* higher than market expectations. However, this came with certain caveats.

However, the red line shows that the labor force participation rate remains on the weaker side. And specifically, it has been weakening since March 2022.

The weekly number of initial jobless claims also began to increase in March 2022:

![Job Openings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-job-openings.png)

In my view, the preponderance of evidence continues to point to weaker labor conditions since March 2022 in rate of change terms, although we’d need to see more data to confirm this trend. Nonfarm payrolls are the outlier strong data point.

I think it’s currently debatable whether the US is in a recession. Regardless, I would describe current conditions as being an economic malaise, or an engineered slowdown to deal with supply-side limitations. Some industries like restaurants and flights continue to bounce back, while other industries like manufacturing and technology have weakened considerably.

Zooming in on travel for a second, the TSA numbers continue to recover over time:

*Source: TSA*

Thus far, 2022 US flight passenger figures are noticeably better than 2021 figures, and of course way better than the disastrous 2020 figures. However, it looks like it could take another couple years to get back to 2019 levels. Remote meetings have eliminated the need for some of the more tedious/unnecessary types of business travel, but other than that, we should see most travel return to normal eventually including vacations and business/networking trips.

Many countries are recovering pretty well in terms of tourism. Asia continues to be the laggard in that regard, however, as some of them have more strict borders in general, and there are far fewer Chinese tourists leaving China to visit other countries in the region.

Japan in particular tends to stand out in this way. Due to still-strict tourism limits in the wake of COVID-19, its number of tourist arrivals looks something like the price of LUNA:

## International Report: Debt Assessments

Rather than publishing this year’s international report in one big PDF, I am dividing it up into premium reports this summer to analyze each of the major areas separately.

This section will focus on areas of debt in the world. To start with, here’s the heatmap of various country debt levels for the countries I cover, as of the end of 2021:

![BIS Debts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-bis-debts.png)

Many countries actually went down slightly since this time last year, because nominal GDP recovered (with a large inflationary component) for many countries on the list. There were some exceptions like Thailand, however, that saw considerable increases in debt as a percentage of GDP between the end of 2020 and the end of 2021.

Japan, Italy, Singapore, Spain, and the United States are the troubling areas in terms of sovereign debt, at least out of this sample of countries. Out of this list, Singapore at least has a massive sovereign wealth fund (over 3x as large as their GDP) which more than offsets that, and so for practical purposes I would not consider them to have a true sovereign debt problem.

Switzerland, Australia, Canada, South Korea, the Netherlands, and Norway have concerning levels of household debts, largely attributed to their very expensive and desirable real estate markets. Most of this debt is backed by collateral- the houses. But the challenge with that is it means that maintaining high house prices is an important piece of household solvency for those countries.

Sweden, France, Singapore, China, Switzerland, Canada, Japan, and a few other countries have high levels of corporate debt. In some instances this is mainly because the corporate sector is a large share of GDP, whereas in others it’s because debt levels truly are a problem. For some areas, the data can be misleading. Japanese corporations, for example, have nearly as much cash as debt in aggregate, so their *net debts* are rather low overall.

For emerging markets, which tend to have lower total debts relative to GDP, the more important figures tend to be their external hard currency debts. From a macro perspective, the main difference between a developed country and a developing country, is whether its government and corporations need to borrow significantly in foreign currencies or whether they can issue the entirety of their debt in their country’s own currency. This chart shows the dollar and euro debts for major emerging markets, compared to their foreign-exchange reserves, as a share of GDP:

![EM Reserves and Debts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-emerging-markets-reserves-debts.png)

Chile, Turkey, and Argentina have some of the worst ratios in this regard, and the latter two are suffering from ongoing currency crises. Indonesia and Mexico have some vulnerability in this regard as well.

Russia’s numbers are complicated by the freezing of some of their reserves. They went into war with one of the most fortress-like financial positions of any country, but that’s become somewhat threatened by import sanctions and frozen reserves, as well as the various costs of waging war.

The general trend we see is that emerging Asia tends to be pretty strong financially as a region, with significant reserves and modest external debt levels relative to their economic size. Elsewhere, Brazil and Mexico are in decent shape, but have more financial risk overall, with Mexico benefiting from its economic ties to the US and Brazil being well-positioned for a decade of more scarce commodities.

**Chinese Economic Snapshot**

China’s credit impulse has decidedly turned up:

![China Credit Impulse](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-china-credit-impulse.png)

*Chart Source: [MacroMicro](https://en.macromicro.me/charts/35559/china-credit-impulse-index)*

China’s economic cycle is out of sync with the US and Europe, in the sense that China already had a big deceleration of economic activity and may be turning up.

However, this remains complicated due to China’s ongoing zero-Covid policy. Various cities continue to experience lockdowns whenever a handful of cases are reported. Their economy is seemingly treading water rather than getting back into growth mode.

And then we can throw geopolitical risk into the mix as well. China is currently conducting live-fire military drills around Taiwan, in response to the visit by US Speaker Pelosi. They’re reportedly doing these drills at six locations all around the island.

China could trigger sanctions or other issues if they get more aggressive, although my base case doesn’t assume that. An invasion of Taiwan is always a nonzero possibility, and a slightly less aggressive approach is merely to perform an air+sea blockade of the island, starve it out, and thus force certain concessions. Taiwan is almost 100% reliant on energy imports, and then of course its semiconductor exports are critical for its economy and the global economy.

The United States has been using an approach of deliberate ambiguity for decades; the US State Department does not support Taiwanese independence, nor do they support China unliterally changing the current relationship with Taiwan to force a reunification. Nonetheless, the US State Department acknowledges cultural and economic similarities between the US and Taiwan, as well as economic ties. Taiwan has its own currency, government, passports, and military.

Freedom House, a US-based nonprofit that researches and advocates for democracy around the world, assigns Taiwan a score of 94/100 for freedom, and assigns China a score of only 9/100. For reference, Freedom House assigns its own host nation, the United States, a decent (but not great) score of 83/100. It’s not hard to see why the majority of Taiwanese people do not want to reunify with the mainland.

Meanwhile, the sanctions on Russia have been a significant catalyst for China’s currency being used as a regional reserve currency. There has been a [big uptick](https://www.reuters.com/markets/rates-bonds/russians-rush-buy-chinese-yuan-otkritie-bank-says-2022-07-07/) in Russians’ buying of yuan compared to many other currencies. Some Indian firms have been [buying Russian commodities in yuan](https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/exclusive-how-an-indian-cement-maker-bought-russian-coal-using-yuan-2022-07-07/). BHP Group, the large Australian miner, [sold iron to a Chinese company in yuan](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-china-baowu-bhp/bhp-completes-first-yuan-based-iron-ore-sale-to-chinas-baosteel-idUSKBN22O0LZ).

The ability of China to denominate its commodity purchases in yuan instead of dollars, and for various entities in the region to hold some more yuan or make purchases with yuan, strengthens their network effect somewhat, and reduces their probability of ever having a major dollar shortage and commodity-import-related currency crisis.

My published view since 2020 is that we’re headed towards a more multi-polar currency world. When the Bretton Woods system was established, the US economy was over 40% of global GDP, but now it has fallen to around half that, as global recovery and mean reversion have taken shape. But on the other hand, China also isn’t really big enough (let alone open enough) to be the sole reserve currency issuer either. So, I think the situation continues to point towards more diverse payment systems and currency reserves overall.

And along those lines, the president of Turkey recently met with Putin in Russia, and upon his return, [announced](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-08-06/turkish-banks-are-adopting-russian-payments-system-erdogan-says) that five Turkish banks have adopted Russia’s Mir payments system, and would be using it to pay some of their commodity bills in rubles. Payment and reserve diversification across Eurasia is a big theme to keep an eye on.

## Digital Assets Note

Things remain rather quiet in most of crypto-land. Coinbase had probably the biggest news of the past week.

For one, Coinbase announced that they’ll be partnering with BlackRock, so that institutional users of BlackRock’s Aladdin investment management platform will be able to buy bitcoin through Coinbase Prime. For the moment this will only apply to bitcoin, but I assume it’ll be extended to ether at some point.

Secondly, Coinbase announced a partnership with Meta Platforms to expand NFT displaying capabilities. Instagram users will be able to connect a digital wallet and display their various NFTs, and this’ll be rolling out globally.

In the past couple reports, I’ve been monitoring Tether’s market cap as a sign of digital asset ecosystem liquidity. During the Terra/LUNA crash and then again during the 3AC liquidation, billions of tethers were redeemed for dollars as entities pulled liquidity out of the whole ecosystem.

For the first time since then, Tether has begun seeing (mild) net inflows, which is a positive sign:

**Bitcoin Update**

Bitcoin has been gradually making a series of higher highs and higher lows from its June bottom.

Most indicators still point to the price of bitcoin being a pretty deep value range. For example, the Puell multiple which looks at the value of coin issuance relative to its past year trend, is bouncing off of its historical capitulation zone:

![Puell Multiple](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-puell-multiple.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/puell-multiple/)*

Although I am bullish as part of a diversified portfolio with a 3-5 year view, I don’t have a strong conviction about bitcoin’s price over the next 6-12 months. Normally these types of capitulations lead to very good returns over the subsequent year, but we’re still in a macro situation that involves the Fed tightening monetary policy into a decelerating economy. The good news, as far as historical analogues go, is that bitcoin does tend to bottom before the purchasing manager’s index does, so it wouldn’t be surprising for bitcoin to have bottomed even though the economy likely has not.

For this reason, I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) into bitcoin as being the best way to get exposure for most people that want some.

A new bitcoin-tangential project called [Keet](https://keet.io/) caught my eye. It is based on the new Holepunch platform. Holepunch/Keet is backed by Bitfinex (one of the largest crypto exchanges), and represents a pretty interesting step forward in peer-to-peer communication.

Holepunch is a platform that will allow developers to build peer-to-peer apps. Keet is the first of such applications- it’s an encrypted peer-to-peer messaging app that allows for high-quality video and large file sharing, with Lightning and USDT payments built in. The app doesn’t go through any centralized servers. Right now it’s in alpha closed-source development, and is expected to be open sourced by the end of this calendar year.

Holepunch is a different technical approach to what Impervious is doing with their upcoming browser, but with a similar goal. These types of applications basically represent “Web 3” but without various tokens attached to them, and rather are just peer-to-peer applications associated with the Bitcoin network in various ways.

**Ethereum Update**

There is a big dichotomy in Ethereum right now between low chain usage and high price speculation.

On one hand, the chain usage is low in the sense that NFT volumes, DeFi trading volumes, and transaction fees are way down. With fees so low at the moment, there isn’t much incentive for smart contract users to shift over to the cheaper competitors like Solana and Avalanche, which has helped Ethereum retain smart contract market share in this bear market.

![Ethereum Fees](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-eth-fees.png)

On the other hand, the price of ether has a lot of interest lately due to the upcoming Merge plan for September 2022, referring to the time when Ethereum developers intend to switch the network over to proof-of-stake.

This is a time of heightened technical risk, akin to replacing an engine in a car while it’s still being driven. If it goes smoothly, I wouldn’t be surprised to see a temporary price spike, as the market prices out the risk of major problems. If it does not go smoothly, then that could of course be quite bad for price.

Some Ethereum miners are planning to keep mining the proof-of-work chain, and create a split similar to what happened years ago with Ethereum Classic. Stablecoin providers can basically pick which chain will win because they can only allow redemptions on one chain, and so they wreck the DeFi system of whichever side of the split that they don’t endorse. Assuming that there is no major technical problem, it’s pretty clear that they will pick the proof-of-stake chain, and so the potential split version shouldn’t be of much economic importance.

Overall, the Merge is the biggest news in the space over the next coupe months. With the Coinbase/BlackRock integration announcement, then assuming the Merge goes well, I expect to see some interest from BlackRock in it, along with marketing of it around pro-ESG narratives.

This could certainly include an uptick in marketing against Bitcoin’s proof-of-work consensus, although I think such marketing efforts would be [misguided](https://dergigi.com/2021/01/14/bitcoin-is-time/).

## Simon Property Group Analysis

Simon Property Group (SPG) is a real estate investment trust that owns a collection of premium malls and retail outlets. The majority of their properties are in the United States but they also have a set of assets throughout Asia and Europe.

As one would expect, their stock price collapsed during the pandemic:

![SPG FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-spg-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Their stock price then bounced back in 2021 during the liquidity/stimulus boom, only to roll over sharply again in 2022. I view this as a pretty good entry point, and a better risk-adjusted entry part than what investors could buy back in the depths of 2020, since there was far more risk then.

It’s actually pretty remarkable how well SPG’s portfolio of malls and outlets held up during 2020. They had to cut their dividend, much like 2009, but they didn’t have to eliminate it completely. SPG maintains a strong A- credit rating from S&P. In fact, they acquired some bankrupt retailers during the crisis.

This REIT usually sees its dividend yield spike during recessions. It’s currently offering a higher-than-normal yield. Maybe that’s another data point that we are indeed back in recession. YCharts’ data is a bit murky here (the dividend yield is 6.57%, not 7.42%, assuming the current payout continues), but nonetheless it’s a mostly-accurate historical visual:

![SPG DIvidend Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-spg-dividend-yield.png)

**Cyclical Risks and Rate Risks**

SPG’s occupancy rate at of the end of Q2 2022 was 93.9%, which compares favorably to the 91.8% occupancy rate that they had as of the end of Q2 2021.

That being said, if the economy weakens significantly enough, and/or if mortgage and bond rates go high enough, SPG could run into revenue and cost pressures.

The good news is that mortgage rates are rolling over for the moment, after a huge spike upward earlier this year. Residential mortgages are down nearly a full point from their June highs, and although commercial rates are different, the whole long-duration rate complex is pretty much moving in the same direction.

One way to hedge interest rate risk is by continuing to be long energy. If inflation has another leg up, it would probably be in part because energy prices are going up again. I’d be uncomfortable owning SPG by itself, but much more comfortable when I own it with both recession-resistant stocks (eg healthcare) and pro-inflation stocks (eg oil and gas producers).

**The E-Commerce Risk**

Aside from general recessionary conditions (a cyclical risk), the more structural risk for brick-and-mortar real estate is e-commerce. Amazon and other online vendors continue to take market share from physical retailers.

However, over 85% of retail sales still happen at physical stores. E-commerce has grown up to about 15% of retail sales from nearly nothing two decades ago. This penetration rate spiked during the COVID-19 lockdowns, and since then has been subsiding somewhat:

![E-Commerce Penetration](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-ecommerce.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As the dust settles, I assume this trend will eventually start to grind higher again. If we look at countries with higher-than-normal ecommerce penetration, mainly in Asia, we can see for example that China has 25% ecommerce penetration and South Korea has nearly 30%. I expect the US to probably get over 20% eventually.

However, the defense against that in the brick-and-mortar industry is to own destination properties. This means attractive high-end malls, with restaurants and other properties that people want to spend time at. As the recent bounce-back in travel and restaurants has shown, people want to get out again. SPG has some of the best properties to defend against digital encroachment.

Of course, SPG is a value investment; we shouldn’t expect major growth out of it. However, I think there is a discrepancy between its equity pricing and its bond pricing. Debt markets do not seem very concerned about SPG in this environment, while equity valuation has gone down considerably.

My base case is for SPG to spend the next few years grinding back up to $150/share, while paying out a 6.5% dividend yield along the way. However, I think it’s best kept as a modest position size.

## Focusing on Dividends

My base case is that the ratio of US stock market capitalization to US GDP has reached a peak that probably won’t be surpassed for a long time. I think that 200% figure was the high water mark for quite a while:

![Wilshire to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-wilshire-to-gdp.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

That peak was reached in the aftermath of record fiscal stimulus, zero interest rates, and cheap commodity prices. I don’t think we’ll see that set of circumstances for a long time.

That doesn’t mean that the S&P 500 or the Wilshire 5000 can’t make new highs. They certainly could, especially in nominal terms (both them and GDP have a significant inflationary component each year, especially in today’s environment). However, the value of the stock market relative to the output of the economy has, in my view, reached a pretty hard-to-match level. It was peak financialization.

It think we could see a period of sideways-ish action in stock prices, especially when measured in inflation-adjusted terms.

What does that mean? Well generally speaking, it means I want yield. I want to be very selective with stocks that don’t pay a dividend, and want to put most of my capital to work in companies that pay me to own them and can provide decent returns even in a long-term stock market malaise.

At the risk of being a broken record, I continue to like Bank of America (BAC) and a number of other banks after this recent sell-off:

![BAC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-bac-fastgraph.png)

I expect ongoing economic stagflation, but not major loan losses from the big banks, and the valuation is attractive. Banks have far less risk on their books than they had during the run-up to the 2008 crisis.

A recent OPEC meeting [noted](https://twitter.com/JavierBlas/status/1554814293396393989) that OPEC nations have “severely limited availability of excess capacity”. Many countries such as Japan still have basically no tourist arrivals- meaning less jet fuel usage than pre-pandemic. China’s lockdowns and real estate deleveraging are also taming their oil consumption. Long story short, I remain structurally bullish on the energy sector despite being near-term agnostic due to the Fed’s attempt to rein in demand.

## Final Thoughts

The surprisingly high nonfarm payrolls report from Friday re-adjusted market perceptions of Fed policy a bit.

Notably, the report increased market consensus of the Fed’s September meeting to being a 75 basis point hike rather than a 50 basis point hike, and the 2-year Treasury note jumped up to a yield of 3.24%. The 2-year yield tends to represent forward expectations of what the Fed’s interest rate will look like in the months ahead.

![Fed Funds Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-Fed-Funds.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

As long as the job market and financial conditions remain somewhat resilient, we can expect more Fed rate hiking. They’re actively trying to weaken the job market.

However, as discussed earlier in this report, there are some conflicting signals for the job market; with the nonfarm report being a notable outlier, the majority of the signals suggest that the job market is already softening. Meanwhile, the 10yr-2yr yield curve is already inverted, and the 10yr-3mo curve is likely to be inverted after the next Fed hike.

Overall, I continue to view the macro environment as being in a cyclically disinflationary period, amid an inflationary decade. This makes managing a portfolio dependent on one’s time frame; things I expect to do well for the next 5+ years could very well be weak over the next 6-12 months. When in doubt, I always stick with my longer-run timeframe, although I try to rebalance around the margins counter-cyclically.

Either way, this continues to be an environment to make sure you know what you own, and to ensure that whatever you own has an attractive combination of scarcity and/or utility associated with it.
