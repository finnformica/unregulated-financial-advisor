**Inflation Models**

“Pent up demand” is a mixed thing.

If someone used to eat at a restaurant once a week, but skipped doing it for the better part of a year, it’s unlikely that they’ll eat out twice per week for a full year. Some of their restaurant meals were simply lost forever, in other words, and were replaced by grocery meals which are cheaper and less labor intensive.

On the other hand, there can certainly be a surge in travel, dining, events, and all sorts of things when people feel more safe to travel and it’s less of a hassle to do so, when more things are open normally, and when quarantines are no longer commonplace.

Plus, US broad money supply increased 25% year-over-year. Many other countries increased their broad money by 10-15% as well. At the same time, there has been pressure on various supply chains.

Commodity prices ex-energy have gone up a lot recently. Soybean prices are at six-year highs. Copper is at eight-year highs. Iron is at nine-year highs. Beef is at all-time highs, and double its price from two years ago.

Shipping costs have spiked:

![Shipping Costs](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-shipping.png)

*Chart Source: [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-01-13/world-economy-s-fragile-rebound-boxed-in-amid-shipping-logjams)*

Energy is still historically cheap due to structural oversupply, but WTI crude is up about 25% in the past three months, so it’s following along directionally.

Nordea recently published a case for reflation. One of their charts, for example, shows a good likelihood of higher core CPI a few months from now, based on a leading indicator:

![Nordea Inflation Model](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-nordea-model.png)*Chart Source: [Nordea](https://corporate.nordea.com/article/62953/week-ahead-b-1-1-7-blues)*

Jeffrey Gundlach of DoubleLine Capital recently did a variety of media appearances and said that their internal inflation model is pointing to a 2.5%-3% inflation level by around May 2021. As of December 2020, the official CPI basket was about 1.3% year over year and core CPI was about 1.6% year over year.

There’s a saying that goes, “all models are wrong, but some are useful.” That’s the correct way to use models. We’ll see what happens with some of these leading indicators and models during the next two quarters, but overall it appears that increasing the broad money supply by a double-digit rate naturally has some upward effects on prices…

**The US Business Cycle Heatmap**

If we take a broader view of the US economy and break it down part by part, we see some divergences. The economic bottom was in Q2 2020, but from there, some areas have bounced back more than others. Retail sales, capital spending, and construction spending, have been the strong areas. Jobs have been the weakest area.

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than cherry pick good or bad data points to fit a narrative:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

## Bonds, Dollar, and Gold Note

Since August 2020, there has been a steady upward move in US Treasury yields. The 10-year was at 0.93% two weeks ago, jumped sharply to 1.15%, and has since cooled off several basis points. Despite this rise, the yield is still below the prevailing inflation rate, let alone still below what some of the inflation models are pointing to by mid-2021.

![Treasury Yields](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-treasury-yields.png)

The Federal Reserve is buying about $80 billion worth of Treasuries per month, which is quite a bit, but compared to the massive issuance of Treasuries likely coming in 2021, it’s actually not that much. There is a ton of Treasury bond supply, and prices are adjusting accordingly. And now with US sovereign bonds yielding a much higher rate than many other developed country counterparts, we’re seeing some renewed foreign interest in buying some after they haven’t bought almost any in 2020 on net.

This chart shows Treasury issuance minus the Fed’s purchases of Treasuries:

![Net Treasury Issuance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-net-issuance.png)

*Chart Source: [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-12-22/treasuries-seven-year-rally-imperiled-by-a-2021-duration-glut)*

On that chart, 2018 and 2019 were years of quantitative tightening and a strengthening dollar, while 2020 was the year of massive QE, helping the dollar to weaken. If they stick with their current trajectory, 2021 could have a period of dollar consolidation or temporary strengthening, front-loaded here in Q1.

In other words, this is potentially going to result in a relative monetary tightening, because the rate of central bank monetization (huge) is temporarily being outpaced by Treasury issuance (even more huge). If Treasury issuance is a lot higher than the rate of central bank Treasury purchases, it means the large difference has to be purchased by the market. That sucks dollars out of the economy, since those entities hand over existing dollars in order to buy Treasuries. If there aren’t enough buyers at any point, yields will go higher until it’s enough to draws in buyers again.

Whether it’s dollar bullish or more neutral/bearish depends on who ends up buying the Treasuries. If US banks buy a lot of them, it can remain dollar bearish. If there is a strong uptick in foreign demand to buy them, it can create a neutral or bullish quarter for the dollar.

And this is rather front-loaded. The Treasury plans to issue over $1.1 trillion in net Treasuries here in Q1 2021, which is nearly double the amount they issued in Q4 2020. In Q4 2020, they issued about $620 billion, and the Fed purchased about $240 billion, leaving the private market to absorb about $380 billion. If the Treasury issues $1.1 trillion in Q1 2021 and the Fed still only buys $240 billion, that’s $860 billion in Treasuries for the private market to buy with existing dollars.

As a side effect, the dollar has found a period of stability and is bouncing here over the past week, and consequently putting downward pressure on risk assets. Over the past year, for example, the S&P 500 and dollar have been tightly inversely correlated:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-dollar.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Basically, the Fed doesn’t seem to want to dovishly front-run things here. In recent months, they’re testing the market’s appetite for holding their balance sheet accumulation rate steady in the face of massive Treasury issuance, to see how readily the market absorbs the rest of the issuance.

In a severe situation, we could see something like what happened in Q4 2018 when quantitative tightening was putting pressure on equity valuations, and yet Fed Chairman Powell said they would be on “autopilot” as it relates to quantitative tightening. In that event, the S&P 500 fell 20% from peak to trough, and the high yield credit market completely froze for six weeks, causing Powell to reverse course and become more dovish.

With payrolls turning down in December, and initial jobless claims turning up recently, the Fed’s mandate towards maximum employment is being pressured. At the same time, with bubbles forming in various asset classes, the Fed seems to be fishing for a pain point in markets and economic indicators, to see how tight they can be before something forces them otherwise. My expectation is to see some turbulence for the next couple months.

This is why I like to keep a nonzero position in cash and cash-equivalents, because we can get these air pockets of tightening that potentially result in corrections and buying opportunities. And as I’ll show in the portfolio update section, I’m increasing my cash-equivalent allocation a bit here.

*Takeaway:* I remain long-term bearish on the dollar and bullish on international equities and commodities, but in the nearer-term I’m tactically dollar neutral-to-bullish and risk-asset neutral-to-bearish in Q1 2021. I think the Fed is going to be forced to blink and buy more Treasuries at some point, but at the moment, the near-term risk/reward ratio in many asset classes besides cash-equivalents skews to the downside.

So, investors have to make sure their time horizon for their investments is defined. I don’t make sweeping changes on long-term positions for near-term issues, but I’ll tighten down the defense a bit here, sell some excesses, and raise some liquidity around the margins.

The next Fed FOMC meeting is January 27th, and the one after that is March 17th, so we’ll see if they announce any changes based on the recent lackluster data in jobs.

Gold’s biggest correlation is with 10-year real yields, inversely.

These real yields are calculated as the 10-year nominal Treasury yield minus the 10-year inflation breakeven rate that the TIPS market is currently pricing in. This chart shows the price of gold in red and the 10-year real yield in blue over the past five years:

I expect in the years ahead to see real rates pierce down to new lows, with an inflation spike and/or yield curve control, but with the Fed currently fishing for a pain point and letting yields rise, there’s no immediate catalyst to drive real yields lower.

Lastly, if I compare gold to my M2-per-capita model, it’s fairly-priced. Going forward, M2 is likely rising at a 10%+ annualized pace, and gold is likely to follow along, but with plenty of near-term uncertainty depending on real rates. Investors should manage position sizes appropriately. Although I consider it to be prudent to have a nonzero position in Bitcoin, it’s a very volatile asset and investors should weight their position size according to their personal risk model and conviction level regarding the protocol’s network effect.

## US Bancorp Deep Dive Analysis

With inflation indicators picking up, the yield curve steepening, the Fed not aggressively keeping a lid on the long end of the Treasury rate curve, and a relatively high likelihood of more fiscal stimulus in 2021, US bank stocks still look solid here.

**A Steepening Yield Curve**

My base case, after the initial election results in early November 2020, was that it looked like we’d have a Biden/Harris presidency and a red Senate. However, with the victory by the Democrats in Georgia’s January 5th bonus Senate election, the situation now is that Democrats will control the Presidency, Senate, and House for the next two years.

The Senate control is narrow, however, with 50 Republican-leaning senators, 50 Democrat-leaning senators, and Kamala Harris as the tiebreaker vote.

For those who are unfamiliar with US politics, in addition to the US vice president’s role as the second-highest ranking member of the executive branch, they are also involved with the legislative branch as the President of the Senate. They normally can’t vote in the Senate, but if there is a tie, they get to cast the tiebreaking vote. This effectively gives Democrats 51-to-50 Senate control.

However, a handful of Democrat senators are more conservative-leaning, like Joe Manchin from West Virginia. So, Democrats don’t have a full runway to do the most progressive policies that some of the left-leaning members would prefer. There’s still going to be political drama and negotiations about what sorts of bills can get through the Senate.

If indeed we get significant fiscal stimulus, and the Fed lets rates run a bit higher (and both of those are still “ifs” rather than certainties), then the yield curve steepening could persist well into 2021.

All else being equal, banks make more money with steeper yield curves, because they borrow at the short end of the curve and lend at the long end of the curve, generally speaking. Some bank models are more correlated to the curve (generally the smaller or more traditional banks), while others have less connection with it (generally larger investment banks).

At this point in the business cycle where we’re coming out of the depths of a recession, the yield curve tends to steepen. Here’s the 10 year minus 3 month spread:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-yield-curve-10-3.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The research firm and advisory CrossBorder Capital has a liquidity model that is suggesting a steeper curve as well:

![Liquidity and Curves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-yield-curve-liquidity.png)

My long-term scenario eventually sees the Fed capping the yield curve below the inflation rate (like they did in the 1940s, the only other time in history where fiscal debts and deficits were this big as a percentage of GDP), but unless or until that happens, the yield curve can potentially steepen.

Even when capped, the yield curve would be positive; it’s just that most or all of the yield curve would remain submerged below the prevailing inflation rate while the broad money supply grows quickly.

**US Bank Overview**

US Bancorp (USB) is the bank holding company of US Bank, which is the fifth largest bank in the United States. Their footprint of branches ranges from the west coast through to the center of the country. They don’t have much physical presence in the southeast or northeast.

More broadly, the federal government is a big customer for them as well. US Bancorp provides payment processing for millions of small federal purchases via government-issued credit cards. Despite the name “US Bank”, however, the company is not in any sort of privileged position for this role; they have to win multi-year contracts from time to time in order to retain that revenue source.

US Bancorp has historically been one of the best-managed banks in the United States, with industry-leading return on equity. This chart shows the return on equity of the largest banks in the country over time, with US Bancorp in pink consistently at or near the top:

![US Bank ROE](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-bank-roe.png)

US Bancorp, as the fifth largest bank in the country, actually has a higher credit rating than any of the four larger banks. In addition, the weighted average FICO score of their borrowers is 794, which is very high.

Warren Buffett’s Berkshire Hathaway is a large holder of US Bancorp shares, and while they have sold or trimmed many of their shares for other banks, US Bancorp is one of the main banks along with Bank of America (BAC) that they’ve held onto and didn’t trim.

**Latest News**

Like all banks, US Bancorp took a big hit to earnings back in Q2 2020, as they set aside a massive provision for expected loan losses, which are trickling in during recent quarters. In other words, banks had to front run the loan losses by setting aside a ton of earnings to cover a reasonable percentage of them ahead of time.

And unlike 2008, the US banking system was well-capitalized going into this recession, with a high allocation to cash and Treasuries. Along with massive fiscal stimulus, that’s why we’ve seen no large bank failures so far, despite a much larger economic shock than in 2008. Out of all the large banks, only Wells Fargo (WFC) had to cut their dividend, and that was in large part due to internal mismanagement.

Like all banks, US Bancorp is benefiting from US fiscal spending. As the broad money supply went up rapidly over the past year, so did US Bancorp deposits. Their Q3 2020 deposits were 15% higher than their  Q3 2019 deposits, which gives them more funds with which to earn an interest rate spread.

**Dividends and Share Buybacks**

Like many banks, US Bancorp had to cut its dividend during the Great Financial Crisis. However, unlike many large bank peers, they didn’t face much permanent capital destruction, since they were among the more conservatively-managed banks at the time. They were one of the banks that was able to bounce back to full health.

Since 2010, US Bancorp has been raising its dividend every year, and buying back shares at a pace of about 2% per year.

![USB Shares and Dividends](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-usb-shares-dividends.png)

All banks were forced by regulators to halt share repurchases and retain capital in 2020 due to the pandemic crisis. However, banks that pass stress tests are now allowed to buy shares again. US Bancorp announced a $3 billion share buyback plan in December 2020 that is beginning here in January 2021. They’re holding the quarterly dividend flat for the moment.

**Valuation Analysis**

US Bancorp is trading for around 14x consensus 2021 analyst earnings, and pays a solid dividend yield of nearly 3.5%.

![USB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-usb-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Years ago, US Treasuries used to pay higher yields than many stock dividend yields, including for US Bancorp. However, as Treasury yields have gone down over time, US Bancorp now pays a dividend yield that is far higher than the 10 year Treasury. This chart shows the spread between US Bancorp’s dividend yield and the 10 year Treasury yield:

![USB Yield Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-usb-yield-spread.png)

And in terms of the price/book ratio and the cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio, the stock is cheaper than average over the past decade:

![USB Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-usb-valuation.png)

Overall, I consider many healthy US banks, including US Bancorp, to be among the better risk-adjusted investments out there in this environment. A pullback here in Q1 wouldn’t be surprising if we see general market turbulence, but fundamentally, the stock looks solid.

**Company Risks**

US Bancorp has a variety of risks, in line with the rest of the global banking sector.

*Credit Risk*

The global banking system faces considerable uncertainty. A solvency crisis takes a while to play out, even as the liquidity crisis and market bottom can happen pretty early. As the economy likely improves in 2021, many businesses will still be defaulting on obligations. However, as previously described, US Bancorp and other banks set aside a ton of reserves during 2020 to handle a large amount of loan losses through this year.

We’ll see over the next year how bad some of the solvency problems get. Plus, it partly depends on fiscal stimulus and bailouts, which makes it inherently impossible to predict for sure. If solvency problems are not as bad as feared, then it means banks over-allocated funds for the solvency issues, and they’ll get to take those back. If solvency problems are worse than feared, then the expected uptick in earnings for banks like US Bancorp will likely need to be revised down as they set more money aside to absorb defaults.

*Interest Rate Risk*

One of the biggest risks for banks around the world is interest rate risk. Unlike credit risk which hits hard and fast, interest rate risk slowly bleeds the banks of profitability over time. Banks don’t do well in zero-rate or negative-rate environments, and tend to prefer steeper yield curves. European and Japanese banks have been absolutely crushed by low rate policy for a very long time.

The United States offers better prospects for this than their European and Japanese peers.

A steeper yield curve seems likely at this time, unless or until the Fed changes their policy to be more accommodative. However, should the yield curve stagnate or turn down instead, I like gold as a hedge. The gold price is strongly inversely correlated to real yields. Gold does better if real rates stay low. Banks do better if nominal yields go up and the yield curve has a positive slope.

Gold and bank stocks can both do well together if inflation becomes high, the broad money supply is growing quickly, and the yield curve is positive but submerged below the inflation rate. So, between the two, gold and bank stocks can hedge certain outcomes inverse to each other, while also benefiting from certain situations together.

*Disruption Risk*

Investors are concerned with all sorts of disruptive risks for established bank companies. Fintech companies ranging from PayPal (PYPL) to smaller peers have made payments easier, and now cryptocurrencies and decentralized finance applications can further disrupt established banking channels. And then on top of that, some investors are worried about the prospect for central bank digital currencies to go around the commercial banking system and compete with them.

A simple way to hedge these disruptive risks is to have a nonzero Bitcoin position. I like a banks+Bitcoin combo at this time, within a diversified portfolio. If decentralized finance and tokenization massively disrupts the profitability of established banks quicker than almost anyone expects (rather than those banks gradually adopting to the tech as needed), then Bitcoin and perhaps Ethereum as well are asymmetric plays on that outcome. If banks continue to do reasonably well from beaten-down valuation levels, then they can continue to be solid dividend payers and participate in a potential growth-to-value equity rotation in the context of a cyclical recovery.

## Summary: Rampant Speculation

The strong performance of risk assets throughout 2020 was in large part a result of rapid increases in the broad money supply and a decline in the dollar, which improved liquidity conditions.

However, with the Fed holding their Treasury security accumulation at a relatively steady $80 billion per month rate as the US is expected to issue a massive amount of Treasuries in Q1 2021, this can very well reduce liquidity and temporarily strengthen the dollar or at least stem its decline. There is a ton of speculative fervor in markets at a time when the dollar is finding a local bottom and seemingly bouncing up a bit or consolidating.

While my longer-term views are unchanged, I’m tactically defensive and somewhat dollar neutral-to-bullish at this time, until either the dollar move fades or the Fed shifts monetary policy again.

The equity put/call ratio had its lowest day in ten years last week:

![Equity Put-Call ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-1-17-put-call.png)

On a rolling 10-day average basis, the equity put/call ratio reached its lowest point over the past month, from the beginning of the dataset in 2006. Basically, investors are buying a ton of bullish calls compared to bearish/hedging puts, which tends to be a contrarian signal that the market is locally overbought. However, it seems to be certain segments of the market rather than the market more broadly.
