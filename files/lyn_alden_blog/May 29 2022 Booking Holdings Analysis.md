## Macro View

The US and European economies continue to show signs of economic deceleration.

The relationship between an economic deceleration and an outright recession is that a deceleration is a reduction in the real growth rate from a positive level (e.g. from 5% annualized growth to 2% annualized growth), whereas a recession is an outright reduction in economic activity, meaning negative real growth. In other words, a deceleration can lead to a recession, but is not synonymous with one. A deceleration that gets deep enough and slips into negative territory becomes a recession.

**Treasury Yields**

In my prior two premium reports over the past month, I became increasingly constructive towards Treasuries, which is rather different than my baseline view of things.

As an update to that, Treasury yields continue to show signs of having topped for a while, meaning their prices show signs of having bottomed.

![Treasury Yields](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-treasury-yields.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

With deceleration in economic growth clearly apparent in the US, Europe, and elsewhere, the market may have already priced in “peak Fed tightening” even though the Fed itself is still catching up to that expected tightening. In other words, the Fed still has further to go in terms of rate increases, but the market’s perception of how far they will get with those rate increases over the next 12-18 months may have already hit its high note for the cycle.

Do I like Treasuries for the long run? No; their yields are lower than the inflation rate, and I expect the 2020s decade to most likely be rather inflationary.

However, I do like them as a 12-month trade as a disinflation hedge in a portfolio that is otherwise geared towards hard assets overall.

Broad money supply growth is also weakening, as the Fed has ended QE, is going to start QT, and as fiscal stimulus has faded. The red line shows broad money supply, while the blue line shows its year-over-year percent change:

![M2 Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-m2-growth.png)

In the long run, there is so much debt in the system that it’s nearly impossible to “normalize” monetary policy, referring to a situation where they are able to bring interest rates higher than the inflation rate for a prolonged period of time while also avoiding any central bank balance sheet expansion.

However, that doesn’t mean they can’t tighten policy for periods of time, and test the various bounds of the system to see where it might make them reverse course.

**Dollar Index**

The dollar index is usually a decent position to hold during periods of economic deceleration, and it has been over the past year.

When central banks are tightening monetary policy, what they are doing is trying to temporarily harden the unit of account that prices of things are denominated in, and along with it, to harden the debts relative to the assets. In other words, there are trillions upon trillions worth of debts in dollars, euro, yen, and other major currencies, and although those currencies weaken persistently over time relative to hard assets, there are counter-trend periods where those currencies strengthen against most hard assets instead, as policymakers try to reign in inflation and speculation.

This is especially true for the US dollar, because there is over $13.4 trillion in offshore debt that is known to be denominated in dollars. Contrary to what might be intuitive, most of this $13.4 trillion is owed to entities in China, Europe, and Japan, with only a minority of it owed to US entities, despite the fact that it’s denominated in dollars.

So, when the Fed tightens monetary policy, it puts a lot of pressure on entities, especially throughout emerging markets, that have a lot of dollar-denominated debt. Particularly, it hurts countries that have a lot of net dollar debt, meaning countries whose dollar debts in aggregate are much higher than their foreign exchange reserves (e.g. Turkey). It also, however, hurts its own asset prices because it leads the foreign sector to reduce its Treasury purchases.

Official data on foreign Treasury security holdings comes out with a two-month lag. The latest data shows that the foreign sector collectively reduced its Treasury security holdings by $97 billion between February 2022 and March 2022, from $7.710 trillion to $7.613 trillion. Most of this came from Japanese reductions.

However, we can also look at Fed custody data, which comes out weekly but only represents about 40% of the foreign-held portion of the Treasury market. It generally gives a good real-time directional signal for what’s happening, which is later confirmed in the next two months by the broader Treasury data.

This weekly dataset shows a pretty strong inverse correlation between dollar strength and foreign Treasury holdings. When the dollar gets strong, the foreign sector generally gets squeezed, reduces its Treasury purchases, or outright sells Treasuries in order to get dollars:

![Dollar vs Treasuries](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-dollar-vs-Treasuries.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

We see a similar (albeit not as clean) inverse correlation between the dollar and US stocks. A sharply rising dollar is generally a headwind against stock performance:

![Dollar vs S&P 500](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-dollar-vs-sp500.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The dollar has had numerous strengths vs the euro and yen over the past few months.

The Euro Area mismanaged its energy policy and so they are struggling with much higher prices and bigger risks of shortages than the US, although the situation is not exactly great in the US either. In addition, if the ECB doesn’t manage the spreads between southern European debt (e.g. Italy) and northern European debt (e.g. Germany), then they can run into a currency crisis and even a potential Eurozone breakup. Therefore, their ability to tighten monetary policy is limited, despite whatever the inflation number happens to be at a given time.

Meanwhile, Japan has capped its 10-year yield at 0.25% via yield curve control with an unlimited bid to buy JGBs to maintain that yield peg. This was lower than it needed to be and led to a sharp yen devaluation in a rather short period of time.

However, now that the market may be pricing in peak Fed tightening, we are starting to see some cracks in dollar strength. The dollar index is among the most overbought it has been on the weekly and monthly charts, and is starting to show signs of reversal just when rates also touched what it is at least a local peak.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-dxy.png)

In addition, there are some currencies like the Brazilian real that are up against the dollar year-to-date, despite the fact that the dollar is up significantly vs the euro and the yen. So this dollar bull run hasn’t had quite the breadth that some of the prior major dollar bull runs have had.

I’m currently neutral on the dollar, but looking for signs of follow-through on the downside. Moreover, I’m also watching specific currency pairs like USDBRL, USDCNY, and various other pairs that are outside of the typical USDEUR and USDJPY pairs that dominate the dollar index.

**Energy Update**

Oil prices continue to hover near cycle highs. This is an area I’ve been rather bullish on despite the increasingly clear data on economic deceleration. This is because the high prices are mainly a side effect of undersupply.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-wti-crude.png)

There was an intense period of price oscillation during the first two months after Russia invaded Ukraine, with both higher lows and lower highs, and then over the past month it broke out of that oscillation to continue grinding higher for now.

This is despite the fact that the US is actively drawing down its Strategic Petroleum Reserve in order to reduce shortages and alleviate high prices. And it’s despite the fact that China, the world’s biggest importer of oil, has had a sizable chunk of its population in strict lockdowns for the past few months and thus is running its economy below its potential level.

![Big 4 Energy Storage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-big-4-storage.png)

*Chart Source: [@James56487175](https://twitter.com/James56487175)*

While oil prices may be temporarily suppressed if US and Europe go into an outright recession (due to demand destruction), I continue to be structurally bullish on oil, gas, their producers, and their pipeline businesses for several years to come. There continues to be a lack of significant capex to bring more supply online, despite the high prices.

Oil futures continue to be cheaply priced. The current oil price is $115, while the Dec 2025 contract is only $73. I view these types of longer-dated contracts as being decent risk/reward opportunities.

The market still seems to think this is quite transitory and war-related, when the capex and supply data continue to show significant underinvestment. These catalysts of course added some upside volatility to prices, but the structural lack of sufficient new supply is the longer-lasting issue.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin continues to chop along in a rather tight trading range after having dropped earlier this month:

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-btc-chart.png)

*Chart Source: Look Into Bitcoin*

The challenge is that all four of these things are historically true:

Firstly, as I’ve described a number of times, bitcoin tends to do neutral-to-poorly when economic growth and global liquidity are decelerating. In other words, bitcoin’s big bull runs tend to occur in rising PMI environments, while it tends to be flat or down in declining PMI environments.

Secondly, bitcoin tends to bottom in price well before the PMI cycle bottoms. Bitcoin’s top aligns with PMI tops rather closely, but it tends to bottom before PMI and then chop along for a while until PMI starts to turn up.

Thirdly, we’ve never had an inflationary PMI downcycle in bitcoin’s history (or in modern developed market history, frankly). This current one is the first.

Fourthly, bitcoin is already rather washed out by several on-chain metrics such as Reserve Risk, with data that has historically marked a bottom:

![BTC Reserve Risk](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-btc-reserve-risk.png)

*Chart Source: Glassnode*

I remain uncertain regarding bitcoin’s price over the next six months, but bullish on it with a 3-5 year view.

I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the best way to get exposure to bitcoin for most people that want some as part of their asset mix.

For altcoins, I continue to not view them as investable for significant amounts of capital. I explained in my recent article “[Digital Alchemy](https://www.lynalden.com/digital-alchemy/)” how the vast majority of altcoins have trouble maintaining an upward price trend relative to bitcoin for more than one or two bull cycles, are not as decentralized as they claim to be, and in my view are best analyzed like equities rather than protocols due to having rather centralized development teams and marketers.

**Oslo Freedom Forum**

I’m writing this report from Norway, where I was invited to speak on two panels at the Oslo Freedom Forum, which is an annual event hosted by the Human Rights Foundation.

The majority of the forum involves activists, Nobel laureates, policymakers, artists, and journalists gathering to present topics on human rights issues, primarily regarding authoritarian and semi-authoritarian places of the world. These include people who push for political rights, religious rights, gender rights, sexual rights, and so forth in unfree parts of the world. Dozens of the speakers have faced actual violence, including jail, torture, exile, and assassination attempts on foreign soil by operatives from China, Russia, Iran, Venezuela, and other countries.

Some of them are also critical of certain practices in western democracies, of course. It could be related to war, to surveillance, or to a host of other things.

A portion of the forum (specifically the part about financial freedom) was about bitcoin-related topics this year, since the parent organization (the Human Rights Foundation) uses bitcoin as one of its empowerment tools throughout high-inflation and authoritarian countries. This is due to its properties related to self-custody, censorship-resistant transactions, portability, and lack of supply inflation (in exchange for high volatility as a nascent asset, and a technical learning curve), which makes it a useful tool in areas where bank accounts are unilaterally frozen without the rule of law.

In other words, similar to how VPNs and TOR can make it easier to send and receive information in hostile computer networks, the bitcoin network can make make it easier to send, receive, and store value in hostile financial networks. It’s a tool, available for people who need it.

So, I was asked to speak on panels about bitcoin scaling and bitcoin energy usage. Other panels on the subject by focused specifically on interviewing activists who use bitcoin, whereas mine were the more technical variety.

Also discussed at the forum was the use of stablecoins, which I’ve been optimistic on as well (as long as they’re the collateralized type). In many developing countries with currency crises, dollar-denominated bank deposits get forcibly converted to local currency and sharply devalued. So, even people who do try to take steps to protect themselves from currency devaluation, often get disrupted in their attempts if they rely on the local banking system. Due to this and other reasons, dollar-based stablecoins are rather popular in a number of high-inflation countries. Stablecoins are centralized and do therefore come with various counterparty risks, but if that central custodian is outside of the country that is experiencing the currency crisis, then many people view holding it as being safer than holding cash in their local banks.

Basically, the combination of cryptography and money is pretty powerful, in various ways. Regulators in many countries aren’t thrilled about it, especially in some of the less free places of the world, but the Pandora’s Box of global peer-to-peer money is basically open now, and it’s hard to force it back closed. Anyone with an internet connection and a bit of a learning curve has access to digital liquid value that they can self-custody, transport, or send peer-to-peer. It’s like having money in the cloud, except the cloud itself is also decentralized.

It’s worth keeping in mind that although most of the *capital* in crypto is from the US, Europe, Japan, Singapore, and places like that (since that’s where the vast majority of the capital of all types is), the *per-capita adoption* is far higher in developing countries. Nineteen out of twenty top countries by per-capita crypto adoption are developing countries, and only one (the US) is a developed country.

![Crypto Adoption Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-5-29-crypto-adoption.png)

*Chart Source: [Chainalysis](https://blog.chainalysis.com/reports/2021-global-crypto-adoption-index/)*

The use case tends to be more obvious to people in developing countries, whereas people in developed countries who have trouble looking out past their own situation often wonder what problems things like bitcoin and stablecoins could possibly solve.

**Norway Parliament Discussion**

During this trip, I was invited to speak within the Norwegian Parliament building to a number of elected politicians and their advisors, as part of a 12-person group to discuss the use-cases of bitcoin and stablecoins as it relates to global human rights. It was frankly not something I expected to be doing, but it’s not something I would decline to do when invited either.

Earlier this month, Norway’s Red Party (a small communist party) tried to ban bitcoin mining within Norway, and was joined by the Green Party and the Socialist Left Party, but they couldn’t get agreement from the other parties and thus their proposal didn’t pass. Some members of other political parties subsequently became interested in learning more about bitcoin and cryptocurrencies, especially since the Oslo Freedom Forum, which has been hosted in the city for 14 years, makes use of it.

A portion of the 12-person group that visited Parliament consisted of west Africans. Some of them, for example, were involved in protests against excessive police violence in Nigeria, had their bank accounts frozen because of it, and so they turned to using bitcoin for funding, which the Nigerian government has been unable to prevent even though they have severed crypto exchanges from their banking system. Nigerians also face persistent inflation problems with their local currency, so bitcoin has enjoyed a rather high adoption rate there in general in recent years for both savings and censorship-resistant payments and storage.

Similarly, one member of the group was from Venezuela, and he used bitcoin mining to gain portable value and leave the country and its hyper-inflation, and now sends money back to his relatives that are still there. Police confiscated his mining rigs which is what led him to leave, but they were unable to identify or confiscate his bitcoins, which he was able to travel away with. He then co-founded a company in Canada, where he lives now.

A woman from Togo, who is currently in exile due to being a democracy activist, described how hard it is for Togolese diaspora to send money back home to friends and family if they are on certain government lists and thus liable having their bank accounts frozen. At times they’ve had to smuggle physical cash in. They eventually found bitcoin and stablecoins to be useful tools to make this easier.

Myself, Nic Carter (VC investor in crypto-related companies), and Darin Feinstein (chairman of the world’s biggest publicly-traded bitcoin mining company) were also asked to attend to answer questions regarding energy usage in the network, since that was the relevant part for the country’s prior attempt at a mining ban. Jack Mallers (founder of Strike which runs a payments service on top of the Lightning network) was also there, as was Alex Gladstein of the Human Rights Foundation, and Ola Snove representing the bitcoin-related division within Aker, one of Norway’s biggest companies.

The politicians asked good questions around bitcoin’s usefulness for human rights activities, its energy usage, the potential for illicit transactions, and so forth.

A few days prior to this, I was also invited to speak virtually at an event held by Canada’s Office of the Superintendent of Financial Institutions (OSFI), which is the government agency responsible for regulating banks and insurance companies in the country. I gave a description of the process and answered regulator questions regarding Proof-of-Work vs Proof-of-Stake, and other details around bitcoin’s energy usage.

It certainly was an interesting week on this front, and I learned a lot in the process since it was outside of my normal activity. Many policymakers and regulators are interested in learning about the nuances of how these networks function.

I combined this Oslo trip with a vacation (specifically, I flew up to Norway’s arctic circle region with some fellow forum attendees and [hiked some mountains that overlook the ocean](https://twitter.com/LynAldenContact/status/1530183070044790786)) so I’ve fallen behind on email. Catching up on that will be a priority next week.

## Booking Holdings Stock Analysis

I’m bullish on a number of travel stocks, although I plan for a bumpy ride along the way.

**Risk Analysis**

There is a wide range of uncertainty for Booking Holdings. International travel is bouncing back, but that trend could eventually stall for any number of reasons including an unexpected wave of the virus or other factors.

In addition, weakening consumer income and high energy costs are both big concerns for air travel.

Therefore, I would be cautious about owning the stock without also being long oil and gas in various ways, and without defensive assets that would hold up well in a recession.

I’m not too concerned about competitors, although in any industry there is market share risk. Booking Holdings has highly diverse and well-known brands, so the biggest variable will be about the travel industry itself.

**Related Companies**

Mexico has three publicly-traded airport stocks, and each of which owns a number of airports within a large Mexican region. OMAB has a large and spread out number of airports, including at Monterrey.

## Final Thoughts

With the Federal Reserve continuing to tighten monetary policy into an already-weakening US and European economic situation, it still pays to be defensive overall. That doesn’t mean to be 100% in cash or take no risk, but to be cautious with return expectations and positioning this year. We’ll probably get bear market rallies along the way (and indeed we’re probably in the midst of one now), but until the facts start to suggest otherwise, the economic situation, corporate earnings environment and the liquidity situation don’t look great.

Treasuries, cash, gold, healthcare stocks, energy pipelines, and energy producers are some of my favorite assets to hold in this environment. US economic activity is decelerating, while the market may have reached peak hawkishness on its assessment of the Fed’s ability to hike rates over the next year.

For the longer run, I am also constructive towards bitcoin, Latin American equities, Southeast Asian equities, homebuilders, industrials, and select tech stocks. These all have more near-term uncertainty as we continue through a complex macro environment, but that I think look attractive fundamentally with a 3-5 year view.
