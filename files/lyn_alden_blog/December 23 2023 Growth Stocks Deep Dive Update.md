## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Both domestic liquidity and general asset prices have had an upward tilt since the Treasury’s late-October announcement that they would issue a lot more T-bills relative to T-bonds than expected (which is pro-liquidity because it pulls money out of the reverse repo facility):

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-domestic-liquidity.png)

Global liquidity (measured here as the dollar-denominated broad money supply of eight major currency blocs) is rising in absolute terms as well, although its year-over-year metric is running into tough base effects from 12 months ago:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-global-m2.png)

The Fed has come out more dovish than consensus expectations lately. Powell previously indicated that the Fed was not discussing rate cuts, but other Fed members said they *were* discussing rate cuts, and the updated dot plot by the Fed pencils in rate cuts for 2024. So within a span of a few weeks, their messaging flip-flopped rather significantly.

Another small (but funny) item positively impacting liquidity is that banks are actively arbitraging the Fed. In March 2023 in response to the collapse of Silicon Valley Bank, the Fed and Treasury introduced a facility called the Bank Term Funding Program that allows banks to use Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities as collateral for dollar loans. The generous part is that the collateral would count for 100% of its face value rather than market value (and due to the sharp rise in interest rates, all of these securities had lower market values than face values). In recent months, the effective rate on the BTFP facility has fallen below the interest rate that the Fed pays on bank reserves, and so banks are borrowing from that Fed facility and then re-depositing that money at the Fed to earn a positive risk-free spread. The loser on the other side of the trade is the Fed, and since the Fed’s profits (or lack thereof) go back to the Treasury, the ultimate loser is the taxpayer. Unless renewed, this facility expires 12 months after its creation date in March 2024, and that would be negative for domestic liquidity to the tune of over $130 billion. This chart is via [Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-12-21/demand-for-fed-s-bank-term-funding-facility-grows-to-record-high):

**Economic Indicators**

The Conference Board’s November [data](https://www.conference-board.org/topics/us-leading-indicators) came out on December 21st, and pointed toward ongoing weakness in absolute terms, but also ongoing stabilization in rate-of-change terms:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-lei.png)

Their index consists of a broad set of economic data and financial indicators, and has been suggesting recession for more than a year now, with no actual recession materializing.

The argument that I’ve been making is that some of these economic indicators are thrown off by the unusually wide divergence between fiscal policy and monetary policy. Very large fiscal deficits are acting as a stimulating force for many sectors of the economy that are not sensitive to interest rates, while the Fed’s tight monetary policy is acting as a recessionary drag on sectors that are sensitive to interest rates.

So in other words, travel is booming while commercial real estate is firmly in recession, and there are a lot of sectors that have a big divergence like that. The United States in general continues to have weak production but strong consumption.

As I covered in the prior report, Europe is showing more firm recession indicators across the board (weak production *and* consumption), while China has strong production but weak consumption, in part because it has loose monetary policy and tighter fiscal policy, which is the opposite of the United States.

The gap between the United States and Europe is showing up in various ways, but one of the more striking charts I’ve seen lately is the diverge between CCC-rated junk debt [as displayed by FT](https://www.ft.com/content/6ce48223-4b5f-4ebf-a259-1832f4cdade2). **Red Sea Closure**

Houthi rebels in Yemen have punched above their weight in 2023.

On October 31st of this year, Houthi rebels shot a ballistic missile at Israel that was intercepted by Israel’s Arrow system while it was in low Earth orbit above the 100km Karman line. This therefore became the first recorded instance of active space combat. There have been various tests of this sort of thing, but this represented the first instance of it between hostile groups. Therefore it will go down in recorded history that Earth’s first known instance of space combat was initiated not by the United States, China, or Russia, but by Houthi rebels in Yemen.

The Houthis have continued this type of pressure throughout November and December, by attacking ships in the Red Sea. Here in December, it has become bad enough that virtually all ships are re-routing around South Africa on the longer path instead of going through the shorter Suez Canal and the Red Sea path. This adds costs and time to global shipments, burns additional fuel, and economically harms Egypt that normally relies on its Suez Canal fees as an important part of its national income. So while the Shia Muslim Houthi rebel groups are primarily against Israel in these actions, it’s Sunni Muslim Egypt that is actually bearing the brunt of the economic fall-out. The rest of the fall-out is spread across the world less directly.

In recent days, the United States defense secretary announced a coalition of a dozen nations called Operation Prosperity Guardian that will deploy naval forces into the region to quell Houthi attacks, with the goal of making the Red Sea safe for transit again. Aside from Bahrain and Seychelles, most of the coalition forces are North American and European. China, Egypt, Saudi Arabia, and BRICS nations in general are not participating, although the United States has said that there are currently eight other participants that chose not to be named publicly.

As of now, it is unclear how quickly or effectively this military coalition will be able to restore safety and confidence for commercial vessels to transit through the Red Sea. Meanwhile, the United States remains at record low levels of oil in its Strategic Petroleum Reserve, and oil traders in aggregate are the most net short on oil than they’ve been in years. It’s not a setup that I would want to short, personally. And all else being equal, this represents a supply disruption (extra ship-hours and fuel are tied up in longer shipping routes) and thus puts mild upward pressure on global inflation amid what has otherwise been a disinflationary cycle.

**ETHBTC Update:**

The Ethereum-to-Bitcoin “ETHBTC” ratio briefly dropped below 0.05 for the first time in years, representing a new cycle low. Bitcoin has rallied harder than Ethereum this year, which has reduced the ETHBTC ratio sharply.

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-ethbtc.png)

Sentiment on social media is getting rather consensus-negative and loud about Ethereum, so I wouldn’t be surprised to see a bounce in the ratio. Long-term, my base case is bearish on the ratio. So far, Ethereum has joined the list of coins that have failed to make a higher-high against bitcoin in the cryptocurrency bull cycle after their initial launch cycle.

At least for this year, Ethereum has found itself in a bit of a narrative void between Bitcoin and Solana. Bitcoin maximizes for security and decentralization, and sacrifices most other variables including speed, throughput, and programmability on the base layer. Ethereum was designed to be a more programmable version of Bitcoin, but in exchange for way more complexity, attack surface, higher node requirements, constant developer changes of the core ruleset, and thus more centralization overall.

And then Solana came along and pushed all those variables harder. Solana can handle way more throughput and thus offers lower fees, but it’s more centralized than Ethereum. Solana fell badly in 2022 because it was caught up more closely with the FTX collapse than most other cryptocurrencies, but has recovered sharply in 2023.

For investors in retail stocks, it has long been known that being in the middle is usually not ideal. You want to be the best luxury retailer, or the most brutally efficient and cheapest discount retailer. You don’t want to be the middle-of-the-road retailer. Ethereum proponents find themselves in kind of that middle ground at the moment. And to make their argument harder, Ethereum is scaling through layers, and each of those layers is arguably as centralized as Solana is.

Since Bitcoin is meant to be money, a layered design approach makes sense, meaning that higher throughput capacities need to be on higher layers like Lightning or Liquid rather than the base layer. And Lightning in particular has a highly decentralized design (there isn’t even just one Lightning network, and there is no way to measure how many transactions are occurring across the entirety of the network) and serves as connective tissue for multiple different scaling layers.

Ethereum has pursued a layered approach as well, but for them it’s less clear that this is the right design path because the high programmability/velocity of the network requires a lot of composability in order to be useful. If smart contract activity is split and siloed across multiple different second-layer solutions (as it is now), then that reduces network effects and liquidity. Solana instead just puts everything on one high-throughput base layer where all composability and liquidity is maintained together, but at the cost of greater base layer centralization.

A similar thing happened in recent years between Ethereum and Tron for stablecoins. Most small stablecoin payments (the kind used for actual medium-of-exchange or as liquid savings by people in developing market savings) shifted over to Tron where fees were cheaper. The stablecoin issuer is centralized anyway, so people didn’t really care about the fact that Tron is more centralized than Ethereum.

Overall, I don’t really have any firm opinion about Ethereum or Solana price and they are not assets that I particularly recommend investing in for the long run, but I continue to track major activity and competition among all top-ten cryptocurrencies.

**Inscriptions Across Blockchains**

Early in 2023, programmers found a way to use Bitcoin’s 2017 Segwit and 2021 Taproot soft fork updates to put large images and other data types into the signature portion of each Bitcoin block. This allows for putting more data into each block than was intended by the developers, up to four megabytes total in some edge cases. These are called “inscriptions”, and the ownership of them is referred to as “Ordinal Theory”.

Speculators have since used that opportunity to put NFTs and various “BRC-20” token types onto the Bitcoin blockchain.

Proponents of this phenomenon argue that it brings more speculators/traders from other blockchains to bitcoin, accelerates wallet development, and gives miners more fee revenue. Detractors of this phenomenon argue that it adds needless speculation to the Bitcoin blockchain, and that the additional fees make the user experience worse for people trying to use it to send money.

My view is closer to the second camp since I’m a fan of utility rather than speculation, but since I expect Bitcoin fees to be high in the long run due to higher adoption and fixed block space anyway, from my perspective this just brings forward the fee pressure and forces developers and users to adapt to that situation. In addition, there’s no easy way to reverse this, and fees are what allow market participants price what is most valuable to them over the long run.

In practice, what this means is that Bitcoin users should be mindful about when to use the main chain and when to use layer-two solutions. Sending small transactions on the main chain, including withdrawing multiple small amounts to self-custody from exchanges and brokers, is not optimal in a high-fee environment. They can get “stuck” in the address that you withdraw them to, due to fees being high relative to the principal amount in order to move them again in the future. Instead, whenever possible it’s better to batch smaller transactions into fewer larger transactions when using the main chain, and plan ahead for the likelihood of higher fees.

This inscription phenomenon has spilled over to other blockchains as well. Basically, it’s a purposely inefficient way to introduce so-called “scarce” collectibles. The inefficiency is a *feature rather than a bug* when it comes to launching new assets in this context, from the perspective of the people participating in them. In recent days, [95% of transactions](https://twitter.com/etherscan/status/1737431914850734453) on major EVM chains have been inscriptions.

Bitcoin has remained 100% functional amid all of the inscription usage, and its only significant consequence has been higher fees. Some blockchains, however, have buckled under the stress test. The TON blockchain lost most of its transaction throughput on [December 5th](https://twitter.com/moo9000/status/1732751363249934640) for a period of time due to a spike in inscription activity. Arbitrum, a layer two platform on top of Ethereum, briefly went down on [December 15th](https://twitter.com/hildobby_/status/1735710373192335637) due to a spike of inscription activity. Others have faced similar pressure.

My base case expectation is that inscription activity will die down eventually, like prior NFT cycles did, but we’ll see. The underbelly of the price action is pretty ugly and unsustainable from what I’ve seen: creators of inscriptions and BRC-20 tokens pump up the price and then try to dump them. In general, illiquid and/or non-fungible assets are *way* more vulnerable to intentional price manipulation than liquid and fungible assets. But these activities tend to gain momentum until they exhaust demand, and the late entrants end up being the ones holding the bag.

**Argentinian News**

Javier Milei is now the president of Argentina. He’s the first self-described anarcho-capitalist head of state, and among the first Austrian economist heads of state, especially for a G20 country.

One of the things that people in his school of thought believe is that money should be determined by the market rather than issued by the state as a monopoly. And as part of this view, he has vowed to close Argentina’s central bank, where inflation is currently 160% year-over-year.

Normally when countries face extreme currency problems, they officially or unofficially become dollarized, meaning they use the dollar as the primary form of money. Sometimes the government decides it, and sometimes the people decide it and the government can’t really do much about it because their currency is wrecked. This is indeed mostly a result of market forces- people gravitate toward the most globally liquid/salable currency, which in the current era is the dollar, and they accelerate that trend in times of local currency stress. Even in countries where the currency is bad but still holding up somewhat, like Egypt, there are generally black markets for physical dollars. Those black markets could be for physical yuan, euros, yen, or francs, but usually they’re not. Usually they’re for dollars, or at least that’s where the vast majority of demand and liquidity is.

This past week, Milei’s administration released a series of economic reforms that are too exhaustive to go into here. The notable one for digital assets in general is that they’ve formally allowed for currency competition; people can make contracts in any units they want. This makes it easier to dollarize (including via stablecoins), and also can affect assets like bitcoin.

Diana Mondino, Argentina’s foreign minster in the Milei administration, [wrote on Twitter](https://twitter.com/DianaMondino/status/1737874320322424984) two days ago:

> We ratify and confirm that in Argentina contracts can be agreed in Bitcoin.
>
> And also any other crypto and/or species such as kilos of steer or liters of milk. Art 766. – Obligation of the debtor. The debtor must deliver the corresponding amount of the designated currency, whether the currency is legal tender in the Republic or not.

Back in August of this year during Milei’s campaign, Mondino [wrote](https://twitter.com/DianaMondino/status/1691139808607744006) that “Argentina will soon be a Bitcoin haven.”

This is similar but slightly different to El Salvador so far. El Salvador has been a formally dollarized country for a while, and since 2021 formally recognizes both dollars and bitcoin to be legal tender in the country. Milei’s administration in Argentina takes a different philosophical approach of discarding the concept of legal tender in practice. But they kind of point toward the same end result anyway.

Realistically, most people won’t make bitcoin-denominated contracts any time soon. People generally use whatever the largest and most liquid money is for that (e.g. the dollar), and that takes time to shift. But the more real effects are that multiple frictions are being taken down around these types of assets.

Back in 2022, amid heavy demand for digital assets, some of Argentina’s major banks were stepping up efforts to offer them to customers, but Argentina’s government [banned banks](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-05-05/argentina-slams-brake-on-crypto-banning-purchases-through-banks) from offering them to customers. They cited the typical headline reasons of volatility, cybersecurity, and money laundering, but really it was about trying to slow the flight out of the their failing currency. And then in 2023, they went a step further by [also banning](https://decrypt.co/139068/argentinas-central-bank-clamps-down-on-bitcoin) payment apps from offering digital assets to customers.

Those sorts of efforts tend to be futile, especially for an educated and well-connected population such as what Argentina has, because they can go around these centralized blockages through peer-to-peer trading and other means. Under this new administration, those types of policies are being reversed, which makes it easier for Argentines to do what many of them were already doing: access the globally-competitive monies that they want to, whether they be dollars, bitcoins, or whatever else.

**Nigerian News**

Back in February 2021, Nigeria’s central bank banned banks from sending money to cryptocurrency exchanges. They cited the usual reasons, but really they had persistent double-digit inflation for more than a decade, and tech-savvy people there were rapidly adopting stablecoins, bitcoins, etc.

They then launched their central bank digital currency, tried to clamp down on physical cash, etc. But the CBDC had (and still has) persistently low adoption, and stablecoins and bitcoin have high adoption. According to Chainalysis, Nigeria has the second highest adoption in the world, and is #1 for peer-to-peer trading volume, which is how they got around the banking ban. As I described in my [November newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/november-2023-newsletter/), this technology is hard to stop because people can bring it through ports of entry with infinite value density, can trade peer-to-peer, can acquire bitcoin or stablecoins from foreigners by doing freelance online work or through remittances, etc.

Usually, countries with both 1) bad currency problems and 2) a rather tech-savvy and well-connected population, tend to be the leading adopters of this stuff. Nigeria indeed has persistently bad currency problems, and has a substantial tech scene despite its low GDP per capita, which made it a ripe jurisdiction for high adoption.

Yesterday, after almost three years of the ineffective ban being in place, Nigeria’s central bank [announced](https://www.reuters.com/world/africa/nigerian-central-bank-lifts-ban-crypto-trading-2023-12-23/) the end of the ban, and instead will pursue a path of acceptance and regulation.

Unless a country locks itself down like North Korea, it’s not really feasible for it to outright ban these technologies with any sort of real effectiveness. Countries that do usually end up capitulating and reversing course after some time.

## Growth Stock Opportunities

In my prior report, I provided updates for a number of value stocks that I found to be interesting after their period of correction or consolidation in 2023. Many of them have been underperformers, so the objective was to sort through them and see how they look going forward.

This final report of the year covers growth stocks, with a look at pockets of likely overvaluation, and pockets of good risk/reward. Many of them have been outperformers, so the objective is to see if they’re still reasonably valued or not.

A key metric I use for profitable large-cap growth stocks is the price/earnings/growth or “PEG” ratio. For example, if a stock is trading at 20x earnings and has estimated earnings growth of 15% per year going forward, then its PEG ratio is 20/15 = 13.3.

I modify the typical ratio a bit by adding a dividend adjustment. Specifically, I add the dividend yield to the earnings growth rate. This is because a stock growing at 10% per year and paying a 2% dividend that can be reinvested into more shares, is quantitatively similar to a stock growing at 12% per year that pays no dividend.

I generally look for dividend-adjusted PEG ratios below 2x, although I also consider balance sheet strength and other metrics as well.

**Visa and Fiserv**

Visa (V) is up 12% since I emphasized it in my October 1, 2023 report, but it continues to be reasonably valued in my view. They have strong network effects and strong growth, but face some tail risks of technological disruption in the years ahead either from CBDCs on one side or things like Bitcoin on the other side.

![V FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-v-fastgraph.png)

Fiserv (FI) is up 36% since I emphasized it in my May 1, 2022 report, which is about double the return of the S&P 500 since that date. Fiserv provides financial technology to banks and other financial institutions, as well as payment processing services for large and small merchants. After a major acquisition, period of overvaluation and subsequent consolidation, they are back on a stock appreciation trajectory.

![FI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-fi-fastgraph.png)

Both businesses have similar forward EPS growth estimates, but Visa trades at a notably higher valuation. This is largely due to balance sheet differences. If value is measured in terms of EV/EBIT or similar metrics that take into account debt, their valuation ratios are more comparable.

-Visa is a much larger company, worth over $500 billion in market capitalization, and has virtually no net debt. They could issue tens of billions of dollars to debt and buy back shares or make an acquisition if they wanted to; they’re almost entirely equity-based currently.

-Fiserv is a smaller $80 billion company with about 1/6th of Visa’s income, and has over $20 billion in net debt (much of it being acquisition-related). The debt likely makes sense, because it’s at low rates and comes due gradually over the next decade. But in order to avoid refinancing at notably higher interest rates, a decent chunk of income will need to go toward net debt reduction.

![V vs FI Balance Sheets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-v-fi-balance-sheets.png)

So, both companies look interesting, but Visa does warrant its higher earnings multiple between the two. Visa is currently trading at around 28x price/earnings, which compared to its 14% expected EPS growth rate, puts it at a price/earnings/growth “PEG” ratio of 2x, and thus near the top end of my acceptable growth valuation range. I often look for stocks with a PEG ratio of 2x or less, and Visa’s fortress balance sheet does warrant allowance for the high end of that range. Fiserv’s PEG ratio is lower at 1.2x, but is about the same overall valuation when the debt is considered.

I continue to hold both of these for the long run unless or until either something changes my thesis, or they become notably overvalued.

**HDFC Bank**

India remains a strong geography for core economic growth, and the economy overall is under-financialized compared to many other developing countries. In other words, households still have pretty low usage levels of credit. As a result, the financial sector is growing particularly fast there as they catch up to typical middle-income country levels of financialization.

HDFC Bank (HDB) has a lot of characteristics of an attractive buy-and-hold stock. It’s India’s largest bank, and it offers a wide swath of different financial services across the country. The PEG ratio is well under 1.5x, but they’ve faced some company-specific challenges in recent years that warrants a discounted valuation. Throughout 2021 and into part of 2022, they were dealing with some tech infrastructure problems. And here in 2023, they are and will continue to be working through this year’s big acquisition of their parent company.

![HDB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-hdb-fastgraph.png)

I continue to view HDFC Bank as a long-term buy and hold investment, and in general a structural bet on the ongoing real economic growth of India and its financial sector specifically.

**Mega Caps**

Some of the mega-cap stocks like Apple (AAPL), Microsoft (MSFT), and Tesla (TSLA) have expensive PEG ratios, while others like Alphabet (GOOGL) and Meta (META) do not.

Apple for example is basically a value stock at growth-stock valuations now:

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-aapl-fastgraph.png)

Meanwhile Alphabet’s PEG ratio is pretty low, and their balance sheet is stronger than Apple’s:

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-googl-fastgraph.png)

Nvidia (NVDA) is kind of a special case because the growth is so explosive and hard to measure. In fact, Nvidia’s forward price/earnings ratio is lower than Costco’s. If consensus analysts are correct (a big “if”), then Nvidia’s current price is only about a 20x multiple of its earnings in two years.

![NVDA FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-nvda-fastgraph.png)

AI might be overhyped in some ways, but the enormous demand for GPUs that it is contributing to is likely not. I spent a few days fooling around with [generative art](https://www.lynalden.com/the-four-monies/) and that certainly is massively GPU-intensive, and it’s rapidly getting better. Code generation, text generation, image generation, performing simple tasks, and other such activities will likely grow in demand and require enormous numbers of GPUs.

Analysts keep referring to the “Magnificent 7” as one big trade, but each one is different. I view Tesla as being overvalued, Apple as being expensive compared to its mediocre growth rate, Microsoft as richly priced at the moment but still strong long term, Alphabet as reasonable, Amazon as reasonable, Meta as reasonable, and Nvidia as reasonable with a big variance band. Ironically, some of the most expensive growth stocks I find are outside of that headline group, such as Costco and Cadence.

## Final Thoughts: Consumer Staples

I didn’t cover many consumer staples stocks in either the value or the growth reports recently. Many of them are too expensive to be value stocks, but have growth and dividend characteristics that don’t really make them growth stocks either.

For the past few years, I’ve considered most of them to be mildly overvalued. However, weight loss drug Ozempic has seemingly spooked investors away from many food-related consumer staples companies. Ozempic is the current hit weight loss drug, or more broadly a craving-reducing drug. It’s very expensive to keep taking, and it has side effects in many people, so it is not as though it’s some ubiquitous miracle cure that is going to get most people off of junk food, even if it is effective for many people.

A number of food-focused consumer staples companies like Pepsico (PEP) have seen their share prices drop in 2023 amid what has otherwise been a strong stock market.

![PEP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-12-23-pep-fastgraph.png)

I don’t necessarily view many food-related consumer staple stocks as strong buys at the current time, but many of them have fallen out of the overvaluation orbit and back down toward a more sensible valuation. For the first time in a few years, I find some of them interesting as portfolio diversifiers, and am willing to gradually add small tranches to them.
