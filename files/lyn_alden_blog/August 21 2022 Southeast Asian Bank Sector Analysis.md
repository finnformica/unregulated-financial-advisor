## Macro View

The global economy continues to decelerate, with major trouble in Europe, and softness in the United States and China.

**US Business Cycle Analysis**

The Atlanta Fed GDPNow estimator is currently suggesting a 1.8% annualized real GDP growth rate for Q3, 2022. This is better than the prior two quarters, but weak in absolute terms.

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-atlanta-fed.png)

*Chart Source: [Atlanta Fed](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow)*

Meanwhile, the S&P 500’s recent rally hit some resistance just under the arbitrary but widely-watched 200-day moving average, and is at risk of rolling into negative momentum:

![SPX](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-spx.png)

The US banking system is well-capitalized and quite safe a from 2008-style insolvency crisis, but with the Fed tightening monetary policy into an already-slowing economy, they are basically engineering a consumer and industrial recession, or nearly so. How deep and long of a recession remains an open question, based in part on fiscal and monetary policy decisions.

The broad money supply, and bank deposits specifically, have not increased since the start of the year. This is the longest stretch of flat bank deposits and flat broad money supply in over a decade, and it’s not surprising that this coincided with two consecutive quarters of negative real GDP growth.

![Bank Deposits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-bank-deposits.png)

**US Inflation and Monetary Policy Update**

Headline consumer price inflation reached a local top in June, with the July numbers (reported here in August) coming in slightly down thanks to the correction in energy prices. Here’s the zoomed-out and zoomed-in charts for some context:

![Consumer Price Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Back in late 2021, I wrote that I expected inflation to reach a local peak in rate-of-change terms by late Q1 or early Q2 of 2022. As the Russian war began, I had to push that back a couple months to late Q2 or early Q3 in my reports, and it seems that we’ve arrived at that point.

Rent and owner’s equivalent rent are still going up in year-over-year terms because they operate on a big lag from actual rent and house prices, but several other areas of the CPI calculation have rolled over in year-over-year terms. That doesn’t mean most prices will go back to where they were before this bout of inflation, but it means they probably won’t keep going up at the same pace as they have been lately.

However, this is in part due to slowing demand, from tightening of both fiscal and monetary policy. Global supply-side energy shortages persist with no resolution in sight due to insufficient capital expenditures, and thus I view inflation to likely be a recurring story for the 2020s decade even as we see relief from it for periods of time. Whenever policymakers around the world try to re-stimulate for another growth cycle, they are likely to run into energy supply challenges.

Despite strong signs of cooling inflation in the near-term, the 2-year Treasury yield hasn’t quite rolled over yet. Upward momentum has stalled, but there is no clear rollover yet. Along with Eurodollar futures, the 2-year Treasury yield (blue line below) tends to be a good proxy for market expectations around Fed policy (red line below).

![2 Year Treasury vs Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-2yr-vs-fed-funds.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The market is nearly evenly split about whether the Fed will raise by 75 basis points or 50 basis points at the September meeting, with the probability slightly pointing towards 50 basis points. They also continue to let securities mature off their balance sheet, resulting in slow quantitative tightening.

**European Energy Crisis**

Europe continues to face extremely high energy prices and risk of outright shortages. A number of factories have had to [shut down or reduce operations](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-08-17/norsk-hydro-shuts-aluminum-plant-as-europe-s-power-woes-deepen), due to uncontrollably high energy costs, which means Europe is basically being de-industrialized until they can fix their energy supply problems.

Bloomberg published a [good summary](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-08-19/germany-risks-a-factory-exodus-as-energy-prices-bite-hard) of the woes facing Europe’s economy as a result of the extreme energy pricing that they are experiencing.

> Europe’s industrial heartland faces a potential exodus as manufacturers of German car parts, chemicals and steel struggle to absorb power prices that rocket to new highs almost every day.

![Germany Power Prices](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-germany-power.png)

The core issue of course is their natural gas prices, which broke out in late 2021 but then were kicked up another notch once Russia invaded Ukraine:

![Dutch Natural Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-dutch-natural-gas.png)

Eventually this parabola will break, but the question is what will the cost of that be in terms of demand destruction and supply-side energy infrastructure capex. Europe continues to be in a very dangerous place economically, with high debt, little structural growth, and significant energy shortages.

## International Report: Currency Fundamentals

A country’s currency devaluation risk is determined by multiple factors.

-A structurally positive trade balance is usually supportive of currency, whereas a structural trade deficit suggests a heightened risk of currency devaluation.

-Positive real rates can attract foreign pools of capital to want to hold their currency, strengthening it. Deeply negative real rates can lead foreign pools of capital to want to sell that currency, weakening it.

-A large amount of reserves relative to GDP represents a lot of ammo that can be used to defend the currency if necessary.

-A country with a lot of foreign obligations (e.g. dollar-denominated debt) is at heightened risk of default and/or major currency devaluation.

-A country with an impaired economy, out-of-control fiscal deficits, or other stability problems, is at risk of extreme inflation and currency devaluation.

-Capital controls can slow down the market pricing of a country’s currency, but the consequences still assert themselves over time.

-Some wealthy countries with huge current account surpluses (e.g. Switzerland, Norway, and Singapore) purposely maintain low inflation-adjusted rates and engage in persistent one-way currency intervention to keep their currencies from strengthening too much.

-The world reserve currency has its own unique dynamics, due to how much global debt is denominated in it and how it serves as the axiom that most other central banks manage their currencies around (either trying to prevent their currencies from devaluing too quickly too much against the dollar, or trying to prevent their currencies from strengthening too much against the dollar in order to maintain mercantilist practices).

Here’s a table of countries that shows their benchmark short-term interest rate, their official inflation, and the spread between the difference, as of mid-2022:

![Rates and Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-rates-inflation.png)

Turkey breaks the auto-generated color scheme of the “Spread” column, since it’s so deeply negative that it is the only number that can be red or orange. Even other deeply negative real rates countries are merely yellow, due to how far of an outlier Turkey is in this regard.

Brazil in particular and a handful of other emerging market countries generally, currently sit at the top of the real rate spectrum, in part because they can raise rates (their debt levels are low enough to do so without blowing up the economy), and in part because they have to in order to prevent major devaluations (they have a lot less leeway than developed markets when it comes to currency stability).

Direction also matters, which is not visible on this chart. For example, Russia is shown with a deeply negative rate, but it’s an evolving situation. Earlier this year, they raised rates as high as 20% until they stabilized their currency, and now their inflation rate is falling, and they’ve been easing policy.

Here’s a table of countries that shows how much foreign-exchange reserves they have relative to GDP, as of mid-2022:

![FX Reserves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-reserves.png)

We can divide this table into a few different groups.

-Most diversified developed market countries have low reserves, in part because their liabilities are denominated in their own currency and thus they need fewer reserves to stabilize their currency in times of crisis.

-A handful of smaller and arguably mercantilist developed market countries have enormous reserves, in part due to economic success and in part because they use one-way currency interventions to build reserves rather than letting their currencies strengthen. Some of them also have huge sovereign wealth funds which are not included here.

-Emerging markets in general have to maintain higher reserves, and some of them like Thailand and Russia go further on this than most.

-A handful of large emerging markets, namely Turkey and Argentina, have low reserves, and unsurprising tend to be the ones with active currency crises.

For major emerging markets, we also need to compare their reserves to their external hard-currency debt for the full context. I have data on fewer countries for this, but here’s my chart on that (which I also had in my prior report). This data is from mid-2022:

![EM Reserves and Debts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-7-emerging-markets-reserves-debts.png)

Lastly, here’s a table of countries’ current accounts as a percentage of their GDP. Unlike for the other tables here, this is for the full year 2021, rather than mid-2022:

![Current Account to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-current-account-gdp.png)

Here in 2022, most of their current accounts are similar to how they were at the end of the prior full year, but some of them have changed significantly. Germany for example has quickly given up its previously-structural current account surplus as its energy imports spiked and its industrial exports became more constrained.

I am not a currency trader, and these types of fundamental data collections are not for trading currencies over a short or intermediate-term time period. Instead, they are to identify major risk areas to potentially avoid when it comes to long-term foreign equity investing.

I view low-reserves emerging markets and European developed countries to have concerning currency risks. Meanwhile, I view high-reserve emerging markets and energy-secure developed countries to have safer currencies, in general.

**Ethereum Update**

The Ethereum ecosystem has had some recent drama.

A popular mixer on the Ethereum network called Tornado Cash was sanctioned by the US Treasury Department. It still operates, but is illegal for Americans to use, and large portions of the network have censored it even for many non-Americans. This opened a can of worms and has wider implications for the broad digital asset ecosystem and cryptography/privacy in general.

Tornado Cash is a smart contract that lets people put in their assets, mix them around with the assets of many other users, and then withdraw an equivalent amount of assets. Various people or firms trying to use on-chain analysis to track the assets of a given individual will find that the trail goes cold once it reaches Tornado Cash, since each trail that goes in splits into multiple possible outgoing directions.

Many Ethereum users use Tornado Cash for normal privacy reasons. They don’t want people and corporations being able to use on-chain analysis to track their full history of transactions, see every NFT they buy, and to know their entire crypto net worth. It’s normal for people to want privacy for most things, especially from other private sector entities.

However, when various entities exploit poorly-written DeFi smart contracts and take the funds, they also often run the money through Tornado Cash. This is money laundering, and is a minority use-case, but a significant one.

The US Treasury Department decided that stopping groups from exploiting DeFi contracts and cashing out the funds, was more important than network-wide privacy, so they sanctioned Tornado Cash. This is not surprising, given that the Treasury goes against bank privacy wherever it can as well; it wants to see everyone’s transactions and everyone’s net worth, to whatever extent is possible. This move, however, makes it easier for individuals and corporations to also track peoples’ crypto transactions and net worth. Since it uses an address model rather than a UTXO model, Ethereum is not particularly well-designed for privacy to begin with, and Tornado Cash was what patched that issue. Without the use of Tornado Cash, many Ethereum users are generally quite transparent on the network.

Due to the complexity of doing so, most Ethereum users do not run their own node, but rather rely on third-party node operators like Infura and Alchemy. Both Infura and Alchemy blocked access to Tornado Cash once it was sanctioned, and so did the big stablecoin issuers, which brought up major questions about the centralizing risks of the network (and issues I have brought up in some of my Ethereum-critical public articles).

In addition, with Ethereum’s transition to a proof-of-stake consensus model less than a month away, it opened up a round of questions about the ability of the entire network to potentially be censored at the protocol level. Lido, Coinbase, Kraken, and Binance alone control over half of all staked ETH. If they, and a small handful of other known regulated financial entities, are told to censor any part of the network, they would probably have to comply.

![ETH Stakers](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-eth-stakers.png)

*Chart Source: [Glassnode](https://glassnode.com/)*

Right now, Tornado Cash is still accessible for the relatively few Ethereum power users that are running their own nodes (although for Americans it is illegal to do so). A test for the proof-of-stake version of Ethereum will be to see if Tornado Cash gets completely censored at the protocol level and thus inaccessible even to those power users, or if it manages to avoid that fate for now.

After Tornado Cash was sanctioned, someone sent individual amounts of 0.1 ETH from Tornado Cash to dozens of famous public Ethereum holders, including many Ethereum influencers, the CEO of Coinbase, and broad celebrities like Jimmy Fallon and Shaquille O’Neal that have identifiable Ethereum accounts. A number of DeFi protocols then locked some of those users out of using their services, which shows some of the fragility, lack of privacy, and lack of fungibility within the ecosystem.

Overall, the switch of Ethereum to using a proof-of-stake consensus algorithm should make its inflation rate go down (which is good for price, assuming consistent demand), but risks letting the network be more easily censored at a foundational level than it currently is (which, if it occurs, could reduce demand over time and call in question the purpose of the project).

Within the bitcoin community, at least based on multiple connections I have, multiple groups are taking this shot across the bow on Ethereum by the US Treasury seriously, and ensuring that their own systems are hardened as much as possible, running threat analysis scenarios on their own ecosystems, and accelerating some of the anti-censorship and decentralization-enhancing technologies that have been in development for the past few years.

One of the surprising results of the situation is that a Tornado Cash open source developer was arrested, and multiple developers had their GitHub accounts shut down. Historically, the development of open source software itself, including privacy software, has been protected as speech (whereas actually using it to commit crimes is of course a different story). It’s like how a firearm manufacturer is not directly responsible for a murder with that firearm, or how a vehicle builder is not directly responsible for a drunk driving death. As the story develops we may find more connections with the particular arrested developer and laundering entities, but nonetheless, the initial set of facts around the arrest currently calls back to the debate on encryption from three decades ago and makes it relevant and public again.

In the early 1990s, the US government didn’t want end-to-end encryption to be commonplace, and wanted backdoors that they could access. And it ended up being one of those battles that the US government lost. Indeed, end-to-end encryption paved the way for why we trust the security of doing credit card payments online, as inventors shared their innovations globally.

![Encryption History](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-encryption.png)

*Source: [Cryptologie](https://www.cryptologie.net/article/487/a-history-of-end-to-end-encryption-and-the-death-of-pgp/)*

Software backdoors are one of the vulnerabilities that can be exploited by hackers, and the US government itself [keeps storing citizens’ data poorly and getting hacked by Chinese entities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Office_of_Personnel_Management_data_breach). And of course, many corporations keep getting their databases hacked, resulting in hundreds of millions of people having their sensitive personal data spilled online.

This topic is worth monitoring over time in a legal sense as it relates to digital asset ecosystem risk. While many governments have accepted that digital assets are here to stay in some form, the concept of *private* digital transactions is one that they seem willing to push back hard against.

## Southeast Asian Bank Analysis

Bank stocks are a rather direct way to bet on the growth of a country’s real economy.

Things like tech stocks can decouple from a country’s real economy for significant periods of time, but because banks are making loans, holding securities, and surrounded by currency risk, they more directly correlate to what’s happening in the economy. To offset that, they tend to trade at low valuations and be pretty diversified, with exposure to some combination of large businesses, small businesses, consumers, payments, and wealth management, which is why a bet on a country’s largest bank or group of largest banks tends to be a pretty direct bet on the economy as a whole.

Fintech companies can threaten that model to some extent, but many of them ride on top of banking rails, and many large banks end up acquiring and incorporating fintech companies within their operations.

For this reason, I am rather bullish with a 5+ year view on the large banks of some of the countries that I’m optimistic towards. This includes DBS Group (DBSDY) of Singapore and HDFC Bank (HDB) of India, among others.

I’ve covered these two in detail before, but I am updating and re-emphasizing my long-term bullish view on them.

**DBS Group**

DBS is one of Singapore’s big three banks, and has multinational exposure to the surrounding region including China, India, Malaysia, Indonesia, and elsewhere.

This broad geographic exposure presents risks, but also makes it a source of curated southeast Asian emerging market exposure. Investors can buy a developed market bank that nonetheless has significant emerging market operations. This is the dollar ADR price:

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

The stock’s valuation is currently below its historical average, with an attractive above-average dividend yield as well.

![DBSDY Dividend Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-dbsdy-dividend-yield.png)

Here’s a breakdown of DBS’s geographic and industry diversification:

DBS Group and Singapore in general have been accommodative towards the digital assets industry. For example, DBS Group offers digital asset custody services, institutional digital assets trading, and plans to offer retail digital assets trading.

The bull case for Singapore as a whole is that it’s a well-run city-state that serves as a significant commerce hub for the region. In particular, as Hong Kong is more fully absorbed into Chinese control, Singapore stands as the primary financial hub of the region outside of Chinese control, but one that still does business with China.

Singapore has a persistent current account surplus and hugely positive net international investment position, which results in strong underlying currency fundamentals. Indeed, the Singapore dollar has appreciated vs the US dollar over the past four decades. However, Singapore engages in currency interventions to prevent its currency from appreciating too much.

**HDFC Bank**

HDFC Bank is a large diversified bank in India, with thousands of physical branches and a robust mobile offering.

![HDB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-hdb-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

The bank comes at a higher valuation and with a much lower dividend yield than is typically associated with developed market banks, but it has a very strong track record of growth, and it’s currently mildly below its historical average valuation level by a number of metrics. I think the growth runway over the next decade continues to be attractive.

The bank is highly diversified among commercial and retail clients:

![HDB Diversification](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-hdb-diversification.png)

*Chart Source:* [*2022 Investor Presentation*](https://www.hdfcbank.com/personal/about-us/investor-relations#/overview-footer1)

HDFC Bank of India is planning to merge with related company HDFC Ltd, which engages in diversified financial services. HDFC Ltd holds a significant stake in HDFC Bank already, and after the merger, HDFC Bank will be operating as a singular company. This will consolidate the brand, streamline the ownership structure, and generate significant cross-selling opportunities of existing customers of both organizations. The merger has been approved by the Reserve Bank of India.

India has persistently been one of the fastest-growing countries over the past four decades:

![IMF India Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-imf-growth.png)

*Chart Source: [IMF](https://www.imf.org/external/datamapper/NGDP_RPCH@WEO/OEMDC/ADVEC/WEOWORLD)*

Despite this growth, India’s GDP per capita is still only about $2k USD. This is about one-fifth of the GDP per capita of China, which itself is less than one-sixth of the GDP per capita of the United States. Even growing to just $4k or $6k GDP per capita (still only 40% or 60% of China’s current figures) would represent tremendous absolute growth for a country of 1.4 billion people (and a lot of energy demand, as well).

With a median age of only 29 and still-strong population growth, India is on the verge of surpassing China as the world’s most populated country. Their population growth is slowing (as is the case for most of the world), but should remain solid for the next couple decades. Most of the southeast Asian region in general, other than Thailand, continues to have decent population growth rates that are higher than those of developed countries:

![Population Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-southeast-asia-population-growth.png)

India is a significant energy importer, which remains a risk from the perspective of a long-term energy bull such as myself. However, India has strong ties with Russia (much to the West’s chagrin these days), and it has significantly ramped up its purchases of discounted Russian energy products. This is a trend that I expect to continue, as Russia turns east towards China and India, while Europe figures out how to diversify its energy mix away from Russia and towards other sources.

## Final Thoughts

For the most part, I continue to have a risk-off outlook.

As long as the Federal Reserve continues to tighten monetary policy into an economic slowdown, there isn’t a strong reason to put one’s neck out there and take on excess risk.

That being said, as a long-term investor that doesn’t flip into and out of positions very frequently, I maintain somewhat of a barbell approach. The defensive side of my portfolio consists of cash, Treasuries, gold, energy pipelines, healthcare stocks, real estate, and diversified dividend-paying companies. The more aggressive side consists of bitcoin, a small number of select growth stocks and/or deep-value cyclical stocks, and emerging market exposures, with an emphasis on southeast Asia and Brazil/Mexico.

To continue with this report’s international bank theme, I’ll include one more chart here as an update for Brazil’s large bank:

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-8-21-itub-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

This one is riskier than both DBS and HDFC (as can be seen by the lousy historical performance), and thus requires cautious position sizing, but overall I continue to be bullish on Brazilian assets generally, including Itau specifically. It remains a very out-of-favor and generally ignored area by global investors.

This past decade represented a long commodity bear market which was hard on Brazil, but I continue to expect the 2020s decade to be better for commodities generally, which should benefit the country as long as they can avoid major policy missteps.
