## Macro View

I consider the first half of 2023 to be a potential dead-zone for the macroeconomic outlook, meaning that many risk assets are likely to chop along in a volatile sideways pattern. As investors debate about the probability of a U.S. recession, Fed actions, the debt ceiling, and inflation, it remains a potential up-and-down mess until greater clarity enters the market.

**Domestic Liquidity Update**

My preferred measure of domestic liquidity consists of the Fed’s balance sheet minus the Fed’s reverse repo activity minus the Treasury’s cash account. This combination, popularized by other macro researchers, is a good representation of how much capital is getting pushed into or out of the actual financial system.

In recent years, liquidity has been a key driving force for asset prices. Over the past month, equities did better than would be expected from liquidity, which suggests that they may be ahead of themselves here:

![Domestic Liquidity Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-domestic-liquidity-annotated.png)

*My base case as it relates to liquidity continues to be the following:*

One) The Fed is continuing to reduce their balance sheet month after month for as long as they can do so without breaking anything, which is negative for liquidity. Here’s the Fed’s balance sheet:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-fed-balance-sheet.png)

Two) The Treasury cash account is volatile, up and down in various months, but will likely have a downward trend until the U.S. debt ceiling impasse is resolved. When the federal government hits its debt ceiling, the Treasury begins taking “extraordinary measures” to keep funding the government without issuing more debt, and one of the key extraordinary measures is to draw down their cash account (which is *good* for liquidity). Weekly tax receipts and weekly spending are unpredictable and add a lot of volatility to this (e.g. the cash account was sharply up over the past two weeks), but the debt ceiling and other major issues dictate the directional trends. Here’s the Treasury cash account:

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-tga.png)

Three) Reverse repos may have peaked and are gradually rolling over, which is neutral for liquidity, and would be a net positive for liquidity if it begins to decline more significantly. Here’s reverse repo usage:

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-reverse-repos.png)

The summary of these three items is that, at least from a liquidity perspective, I expect risk assets to be choppy and approximately sideways in the first half of 2023, unless or until I see a catalyst that suggests otherwise. By the end of the first half of 2023, the debt ceiling impasse will likely be resolved (which may or may not include a temporary nominal default; such an outcome is not completely out of the question in such a divided political situation), and from that point the U.S. Treasury will likely have an upward bias for its cash account as it refills it back to target levels, which is negative for liquidity when it occurs.

If the debt ceiling is resolved early, then the Treasury cash account can roughly stay where it is (it’s currently around their target level) rather than drawing down, and the Fed balance sheet and reverse repo activity will be the primary movers of domestic liquidity.

**US Business Cycle Analysis**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Rather than describing the economy in narratives or qualitative terms, or cherry pick data to fit a particularly bullish or bearish view, it quantifies a broad section of data.

The economic cycle peaked in year-over-year rate-of-change terms in Q2 2021, and since then has been decelerating but still generally positive.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-economic-heatmap.png)

The table starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the table, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

Note that many of these are **nominal** figures. Retail sales, construction spending, capital goods orders, exports, imports, are all measured in nominal dollars, and thus have a significant inflation component. Retail sales growth at 5.17% year-over-year, construction spending at 7.66%, and capital goods orders at 5.13% all seem like fine numbers, until we factor in 6.42% CPI, which puts those three indicators approximately flat in inflation-adjusted terms. The economy, in many aspects, is operating at stall speed.

In my view, the difference between nominal and real (inflation-adjusted) figures is confusing a lot of analysts since it makes things harder to analyze. For stock prices, things like nominal earnings and revenues are what matter. But for measuring the rate-of-change of an economy, the real figures are more useful. Most data, such as PMI data and the yield curve suggests that the sluggish real numbers are giving a better insight into how the economy is performing.

**Bitcoin Ordinal NFTs**

A developer recently made use of the 2017 Segwit and 2021 Taproot soft fork updates for Bitcoin in a way that was not originally intended by those updates: to let people inscribe NFTs directly into the Bitcoin blockchain, referred to as “digital artifacts”.

Historically, the Bitcoin network allows people to put in small amounts of arbitrary data into the blockchain, in addition to normal transaction data. Most of the space in a given block is used to process bitcoin transactions, but some of it can be used for other purposes. A big purpose has been to attach other blockchains onto Bitcoin. One of the early examples of this was the Omni protocol, which is a platform on top of Bitcoin that the Tether stablecoin was originally issued on, starting back in 2014. Similarly, any blockchain can insert regular checkpoints or similar proofs into the Bitcoin blockchain in order to inherit some of its non-recursive security.

This chart shows the popularity of affixing other protocols onto the Bitcoin network via OP\_RETURN, which was an activity that peaked a few years ago in a bubble-like fashion:

![OP Return Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-op-return.png)

*Chart Source: [Transaction Fee Info](https://transactionfee.info/charts/output-opreturn/)*

More recently, [Babylon Chain](https://babylonchain.io/) created a system for proof-of-stake blockchains to more efficiently place checkpoints into the Bitcoin blockchain, which lets them make use of Bitcoin’s immutability. I met the co-founder of that project, Stanford professor David Tse at a Princeton University event back in November that we both spoke at, and had the opportunity to discuss with him afterward on some of the details.

Jumping back to the present as it relates to NFTs: pictures, songs, videos, files, and games are just strings of data. Segwit and Taproot inadvertently created a path and an incentive to insert very large amounts of arbitrary data into the signature section of a block as a single transaction, which comes with a 75% fee discount relative to the rest of the block, and is meant to be used for transaction signatures. This means that people can now stuff a picture of a bored ape or whatever else into the Bitcoin blockchain with a transaction, if they are willing to pay the fees to the miners to do it.

Most NFTs don’t actually store the image on a blockchain, because the image is too big. Instead, they are basically “receipts” that point to NFTs on some external server. All else being equal, NFTs are considered by enthusiasts to be more valuable if they can be inserted directly into the blockchain, since a high-quality decentralized blockchain is basically the most immutable digital real estate in the world. This is why many expensive NFTs are low-resolution images. Low-resolution images like cryptopunks can be stuffed directly into a blockchain whereas a more complex piece of art generally cannot be.

Well, with Ordinal Theory, Bitcoin now hosts very large NFTs, up to four megabytes in size per block (since the signature portion of the block can expand beyond Bitcoin’s hard limit of 1 megabyte for the main transaction space per block). And these NFTs can arguably be considered the “highest quality” type of NFTs by collectors: large images inserted directly into the most immutable blockchain in the world. Once inserted into the blockchain, they become distributed into nodes all around the world. This has caused quite a debate in the Bitcoin community.

**Against:** Some bitcoin proponents consider this to be a form of spam. It increases the bandwidth and storage requirements of running a node, at least up to a point, and may result in higher average transaction costs due to more demand for block space. In their view, Bitcoin does use some filtering to keep out spam already, and updating the filters to exclude this use case to optimize for the monetary use case would not really be a form of blockchain censorship. Some of them have argued that a soft fork should be considered to filter out these NFTs.

**For:** Other bitcoin proponents consider this to be a novel new use case that brings in more users, and a way to generate higher fees for miners when fees are otherwise low. As long as people are paying the required transaction fees and following the existing coded rules of the protocol (which Ordinal NFTs are), they argue that it’s not spam. From their perspective, to try to filter them out would be a form of blockchain censorship and is not a good precedent. Furthermore, if the Bitcoin blockchain becomes more persistently in-demand and transaction fees become higher later this decade, it should price out most frivolous use-cases anyway.

I discussed the prospect for an uptick in non-monetary use-cases of Bitcoin’s block space in an article almost two years ago:

> Block space in bitcoin’s blockchain is just information; it doesn’t all have to be used purely for payment transactions. Messages can be and have been included in various transactions within blocks, including by Satoshi Nakamoto in the genesis block.
>
> A given block on the bitcoin blockchain, as an immutable distributed public ledger, can be thought of as virtual real estate, and only 144 blocks are available per day on average. Most uses are for settling bitcoin payments, but the space within a transaction can be used for other purposes too, because any information you put there becomes permanent and publicly-available.
>
> There’s potentially a use-case for that, because you can put something there as a matter of public record, as an arbiter of truth that is stored on countless devices around the world that can never be changed once it’s buried under more blocks.
>
> Over time, various services have been willing to pay transaction fees to secure messages inside of the bitcoin blockchain. Veriblock, for example, has a service that allows weaker blockchains to “inherit” bitcoin’s security by using the OP Return operator. Veriblock and similar solutions accounted for a double-digit percentage of Bitcoin’s ongoing transactions for a couple years, although in recent years this practice has tapered off.
>
> Overall, non-transactional messages are no longer a big portion of bitcoin’s block space usage, but in the future this could conceivably pop up again if new use-cases are identified. Many of the previous use-cases have migrated to become ethereum tokens and trading around in that space instead.
>
> -Lyn Alden, March 2021, [Bitcoin: Fee-Based Security Modeling](https://www.lynalden.com/bitcoin-security-modeling/)

In most ways, I consider Ordinal NFTs to be a non-issue, but worth watching. I’m not interested in buying or selling Ordinal NFTs, and my base case is that Bitcoin block space will eventually become persistently full anyway. Putting images and other content on the Bitcoin blockchain was possible before, but now it’s easier to do and is being done more, since it can be done in large bursts and has NFT tracking associated with it. This does, however, show why developers need to be very cautious with updates: changes to the protocol can be used in ways that were not necessarily expected when designed.

The biggest risk with Ordinal NFTs is that their popularity can increase the possibility of a specific attack vector occurring: a covert operations team on behalf of a government agency or some other group that is opposed to the existence of Bitcoin could, if they lack ethics, secretly inscribe illegal child imagery into the Bitcoin blockchain, draw media attention to that fact, and then push for regulation that attempts to ban people from running Bitcoin nodes because node runners are now technically hosting illegal child imagery.

There are ways for people to prune their node of non-transaction data, but still, that’s an attack vector that was technically possible before, but now has a higher probability of occurring in a more targeted and deliberate manner. The expense of the block space makes use cases for this type of thing very unlikely, unless someone is deliberately trying to attack the network.

Images are just strings of binary data, and can be left unseen unless specifically rendered by a program that renders data of a certain format (e.g. JPEG). Nonetheless, I expect a number of attempted crackdowns by governments on Bitcoin this decade, and this particular avenue of attack has just opened up a bit more, when modeling the situation in terms of adversarial thinking.

**Nostr**

A number of Bitcoin developers created a new open source protocol called Nostr a few years ago, which stands for “Notes and Other Stuff Translated by Relays”. [Here’s a good overview](https://nostr-resources.com/).

It had basically no adoption for a while, but starting in 2022, it suddenly began to achieve significant momentum. Jack Dorsey in particular (co-founder of both Twitter and Square) became a huge advocate for it and really lit a fire under its adoption. The past couple months have seen an explosion of adoption. In this chart, a “pubkey” basically represents a username:

![Nostr Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-nostr-growth.png)

*Chart Source: [nostr.io](https://nostr.io/)*

Currently, social media networks are siloed. Twitter is a closed system, Facebook is a closed system, Telegram is a closed system, etc. Nostr, however, is an underlying open source protocol that anyone can build a client for, and clients that build on it are interoperable with each other.

The closest analogy is to compare it to email. People who use Gmail, Yahoo, Hotmail, AOL, and various individual webmail services, can all send emails to each other regardless of which service they use. This is because they are all built on the same underlying set of protocols, and are not closed/siloed systems. Similarly, Nostr is an underlying open source social media publishing protocol that people can build clients for. People have made Twitter-clone clients, Telegram-clone clients, desktop clients, and mobile clients for Nostr, and they are all compatible with each other.

![Nostr Example](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-nostr-example.png)

To use Nostr, you can use one of the clients (the iOS app called Damus is one of the most popular), and generate a private and public key pair. You keep the private key secret, and the public key is like your username that you share with other people.

There is no central server (or “relay”), and instead many individual users run relays. Users can pick which relays they connect to, and relay operators can ban certain users from their relay. Users publish material to multiple relays, and relays transmit and store that material, with discretion by relay operators about which material they choose to transmit and store on their particular relay.

Unlike Bitcoin, there is no global consensus on the Nostr network. As a result, users and relay operators can sculpt their experience to however broad or narrow of a subset of the network they wish to experience.

The combination of public-key cryptography and distributed servers means that no central entity can censor anyone or ban anyone. Individual relay operators can choose to exclude things, and if users want to exclude certain things they can stick with those more exclusive relays. Some relays can operate for free and other ones can charge a fee.

It also means that some of the user experience is a bit clunky. If a user doesn’t connect to good relays, the speed will be very slow and some data will be incomplete, like seeing posts but being unable to see the posts that they are in response to. When I connected to the network with my second Nostr client as part of my testing, I could see all the same posts and direct messages as my first client, but “likes” didn’t translate over. Users have to be careful about which clients to trust- if you insert your private key into a scammy client, they can use it to capture your cross-client Nostr digital identity.

My analysis is very positive on Nostr so far, overall. It’s purposely simple at the protocol level, and yet elegantly hits what seems to be the right spot in terms of user responsibilities and server responsibilities. I expect that some of the clunky aspects will be smoothed out over time; at the moment the ecosystem is basically devoid of capital with a handful of people working on it, and yet it’s already quite impressive as it is, and for what it does.

Notably, Nostr is an example of something that is decentralized, and yet doesn’t sit atop a blockchain or try to achieve a global consensus on the state of the network. It does much of what “Web3” developers aim to do, but without a new token attached. All of this development happened with basically no funding.

Instead, Nostr makes use of the Bitcoin/Lightning network, while not being built on it. Users can easily tip each other with Lightning transactions or pay premium relay operators with Lightning payments in order to use those relays instead of the various free ones. In other words, Nostr makes use of the fact that a decentralized internet-native monetary network exists, rather than try to recreate an inferior and network-specific money of its own. If Nostr continues to catch on at scale, it would be an accelerant to the size of the Lightning network, all else being equal.

Meanwhile, the Lightning network has been continuing to grow through the bear market anyway:

![Lightning Capacity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-8-lightning-capacity.png)

There was a data error with an LND node that a lot of the public charts get their Lightning data from between mid-October and mid-December that resulted in flat numbers being reported even though the network was still growing. I reached out to Lightning Labs back in December to inquire about this, so they reached out to the LND node operator that was causing the issue for these various charts, and they subsequently fixed the node issue to resume reporting, although that temporary period remains flat due to lack of data.

## Barbell Risk Management

After a strong January, many risk assets are stretched to the upside in the near-term sense. In my view, there is a good probability of many asset classes consolidating for a period of time.

At the same time, many risk assets are still in long sideways consolidations, at cheap valuations. Many bank stocks are not much higher than they were five years ago, and many oil stocks are not much higher than they were 15 years ago, despite solid earnings and stronger balance sheets.

Therefore, it becomes a matter of timeframe management. I continue to use a general barbell strategy, with an emphasis on defense but the willingness to take select risks and absorb any volatility that might come their way.

**Defensive Assets: Cash, T-bills, Gold, Defensive Value Equities**

Short-duration Treasuries, including through ETFs like the SPDR 1-3 Month T-Bill ETF (BIL) provide some of the best defensive capabilities for investors looking to reduce portfolio volatility within a given year. They pay way higher yields than most bank accounts, and don’t have the volatility of long-duration bonds.

For a bit more volatility, the iShares 1-3 Year Treasury Bond ETF (SHY) is also pretty defensive overall, due to its relatively short duration.

Money market funds are also pretty attractive here due to their safe high yields, and have been making use of the Fed’s reverse repo facility.

Normally, when the 2-year Treasury yield crosses under the Fed Funds rate, it means the Fed is close to being finished with its rate hiking cycle.

The big exception was the late 1970s and early 1980s under Chairman Paul Volcker, as he jacked up interest rates to record levels in the United States to fight inflation, and purposely caused a recession in doing so.

Gold will likely correct or consolidate if the U.S. dollar index has another sharp rally, but otherwise tends to benefit in slowing economic environments. It continues to be rather cheap and out-of-favor, even as it rallied in recent months. I’m neutral on it at the current time but rather bullish longer-term.

## Final Thoughts: The Fed’s Operating Loss

I discussed the Fed’s loss of profitability in some prior premium reports after it started happening in Q4 2022, but went ahead and decided to write a full [public article](https://www.lynalden.com/broke-federal-reserve/) on the topic this week. It’s one of those “jargony” topics that benefits from analysis in plain English.

In short, the Fed raised interest rates so quickly that its interest rate on its liabilities (bank reserves and reverse repos, in particular) now exceed its interest rate on its assets (mostly Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities of considerable duration). The Fed used to send the majority of its profits to the U.S. Treasury as remittances, but now it’s operating at a loss, and so is no longer sending money to the U.S. Treasury. Furthermore, even if the Fed becomes profitable again in the future, it’ll have to pay itself back for these current losses before sending any more money to the U.S. Treasury.

As long as the Fed holds interest rates around these levels or higher, this means that 1) the U.S. federal government just lost a $100+ billion annual income source from the Fed’s remittances and 2) this money is pouring into the financial sector instead.

For multiple reasons including balance sheet strength and high interest on reserve balances, I continue to be more constructive on the U.S. banking sector than the market consensus seems to be, despite my rather bearish outlook for real economic growth.

Over the next five years, I am pretty bearish on the U.S. public financial situation, and pretty bullish on value stocks (especially energy producers and transporters), banks in general, select emerging markets, energy, gold, and bitcoin.

Over the next one year, however, I continue to see a heightened degree of uncertainty and a high likelihood of choppy, volatile markets as the U.S. flirts with recession.
