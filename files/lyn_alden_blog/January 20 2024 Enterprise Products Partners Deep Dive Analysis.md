## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity conditions continue to be constructive, mainly as the rate of the drainage of the reverse repo facility to finance the Treasury’s T-bill issuance (which is good for liquidity) matches or exceeds the Fed’s rate of balance sheet reduction (which is bad for liquidity).

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-domestic-liquidity.png)

Over the past three months, the reverse repo facility has drained a bit less than $200 billion per month back into the financial system. If that rate continues, the facility will be drained by mid-April. Federal Reserve officials have already discussed the possibility of slowing down their balance sheet reduction to prepare for when the reverse repo facility gets low, because at that point there would be more risk of a liquidity problem.

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-reverse-repos.png)

Large banks have been the primary beneficiaries of this liquidity effect compared to small banks, especially over the past six months. This following chart shows the cash levels of large banks in blue and small banks in red:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-bank-cash.png)

This is the same chart, but is re-denominated to be in terms of cash as a percentage of total bank assets:

![Bank Cash Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-bank-cash-ratio.png)

The following chart shows global liquidity, which in this context is the global broad money supply (from the top several currency blocs) denominated in dollars:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-global-m2.png)

After falling throughout November and December, the dollar index has been rising in January (i.e. the dollar is strengthening relative to other major currencies), which puts some downward pressure on global liquidity because it’s the primary denominator that debts and contracts use as their unit of account.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-dxy.png)

Overall, I expect flattish liquidity conditions to continue domestically and globally over the next quarter or two.

**U.S. Economic Data**

With the ongoing divergence between very loose fiscal policy and tight monetary policy, along with the post-pandemic environment that still has some ripple effects from the pandemic period, the United States continues to experience an unusual business cycle.

The U.S. manufacturing sector has been contracting for 14 months according to the purchasing manager’s index (sub-50 means contracting), which is the longest since at least 1960 without the formation of an official broad U.S. recession:

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-pmi.png)

The yield curve between the 10-year Treasury and the 3-month Treasury has been inverted for the longest since at least 1960 without the formation of an official broad U.S. recession:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-yield-curve.png)

The United States has never entered a recession where the fiscal deficit as a percentage of GDP is larger than it is now, at least since 1960. The deficit is a significant stimulatory variable in nominal terms:

![Fiscal Deficit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-deficit.png)

The Federal Reserve has never had an annual operating loss since at least 1960, but is now running at a structural loss. This doesn’t materially affect the Fed itself, but rather it increases the size of the fiscal deficit, since the Fed is no longer remitting profits to the Treasury:

![Fed Remittances](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-fed-remittances.png)

What happens when an unstoppable force hits an immovable object? We’re going to find out, likely by the end of this year.

To the extent that this analogy represents fiscal policy vs monetary policy, fiscal policy almost always wins over monetary policy when they directly collide.

Does that mean we can’t have a recession? No. But it means if we do have a recession, it’ll likely look different than a typical recession of the past several decades. It’ll likely have less of an unemployment spike and likely won’t be as disinflationary, because in many ways it’s more like an emerging market style of recession.

When everyone is looking for one type of bubble to pop, it’s usually another that does instead.

Many people are wondering when house prices and stock prices will crash, and when the unemployment rate will spike, and when will the major banks face a huge wave of defaults. In other words, they’re wondering when another 2008-style major disinflationary recession will occur, and they implicitly expect the numerator (asset prices) to sharply fall back down toward the denominator (the currency).

Instead, I think the biggest bubble is in the fiscal situation, which means the sovereign bonds and the currency are what are in a bubble. Or, another way of putting it is that it could be a scenario where the denominator (the supply of money and money-equivalents) gradually catches up to the numerator (asset prices). We already experienced a wash-out of sovereign bonds starting in 2021 through most of 2023, and we had a wave of four-decade high inflation. However, even as those issues have subsided, the government is still running growing structural deficits as far as the eye can see, which is a background inflationary stimulus that pushes against other disinflationary variables and keeps a lot of asset prices elevated in nominal terms.

In other words, I think it’s best thought of as a slow-motion [crack-up boom](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/c/crackup-boom.asp). It’s not as severe as what emerging markets sometimes experience, but it’s the milder form that a developed market can experience due to spiraling fiscal deficits.

Since there is so much dollar-denominated debt globally, there remains persistent inflexible demand for dollars, which helps reduce or delay the type of full-on crack-up boom that a weaker currency could face in this kind of outlier fiscal situation. More and more dollars and dollar-equivalents (e.g. T-bills) are coming to market, but rather than being rejected with lack of demand (which would be extremely inflationary), they are being absorbed by the insatiable demand for dollars that all of the dollar-denominated debt represents (which tampers down inflation as long as that absorption occurs).

What I mainly want to hold in this scenario are equities that have variable revenue but mostly fixed costs. As the nominal supply of money and money-like assets increase, it feeds into the revenue of these types of equities, while they have fixed amounts of long-term debt and have well-controlled operating expenses.

An example is a company like Fiserv (FI), which has rising revenue and net income but low-rate debt termed out over the next decade:

![FI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-fi-fastgraph.png)

In addition, I find it useful to retain a portfolio slice of commodity exposures, and to hold debasement-resistant monies like gold and bitcoin.

**Copper: The Global Tiebreaker**

China and Europe are not running the same fiscal playbook as the United States, and they have much softer economies overall. They are contributing to global disinflationary pressures as households and businesses tighten their belts, which helps keep commodity demand low and consequently commodity prices low.

In the United States, the key question is whether some of these cyclical indicators will start to roll up again, or if they will head deeper into recession. And right now there are still conflicting signals on that.

A “tiebreaker” that I like to use is the copper-to-gold ratio, as sort of a checksum on global economic acceleration or deacceleration. Copper is heavily associated with capital spending cycles, meaning it’s used widely in construction and manufacturing. The copper-to-gold ratio tends to correlate very strongly with the purchasing manager’s index, but it filters out a lot of country-specific information. As of now, the ratio continues to head cyclically *down*, with lower highs and lower lows, indicating ongoing economic weakness of materially-intensive sectors on a worldwide basis:

![Copper-Gold Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-copper-gold.png)

**Bitcoin Spot ETFs Overview**

Eleven different bitcoin spot ETFs launched on January 11th, including one by the largest asset manager in the world, BlackRock. Included in these eleven ETFs is the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC) that converted into an ETF and began trading on a major stock exchange rather than over the counter.

Due to the nonexistence of a simple spot ETF until now, many large pools of capital didn’t have a tradeable security suitable to their needs if they wanted pure bitcoin exposure as a portfolio slice. The Grayscale Bitcoin Trust was a trust that traded over-the-counter and its price could deviate from its net asset value due to the lack of a redemption feature. MicroStrategy (MSTR) is a software company that has a big bitcoin holding, but that has financial leverage and can also deviate from its net asset value. Bitcoin futures ETFs have underperformed the spot price of bitcoin since their launch due to roll costs. Now that these bitcoin spot ETFs have launched and are accessible on major exchanges, these pools of capital have access to a security with low fees and high liquidity and that directly correlates with the bitcoin price without major overshoots or undershoots.

Over time, the availability of these ETFs should be constructive for bitcoin’s price, as various diversified portfolios can more easily incorporate a nonzero amount of bitcoin into their allocations. Many advisors that were not allowed to recommend bitcoin before, now can. Large institutions that previously disliked bitcoin now aggressively market in favor of it, because now they have a way to capture some fees on it.

To quantify the impact of the ETFs, there are currently about 900 new bitcoin generated per day, which at $40k/coin equals $36 million worth of new bitcoin generated per day. Some of these are retained by miners, but others are sold into the market. In April, the pre-programmed supply halving will reduce the number of new coins to 450 per day or $18 million at $40k/coin. If the price rises to $80k/coin, that brings it back up to $36 million worth of new bitcoin being generated each day post-halving. If the ETFs can maintain even $50+ million per day in net inflows for a while as large previously-untapped pools of capital build positions, then that can take more coins off the market than are generated per day.

However, there are some platforms like Vanguard that have specifically excluded the bitcoin ETFs from being accessible to their clients due to not fitting with their investment philosophy, even though they include things like triple-leveraged short Nasdaq ETFs. So there still are some pools of capital that lack access to them for ideological reasons.

And overall, large pools of capital do not change investment strategies quickly. A lot of people thought that bitcoin spot ETFs would be a catalyst for the next bitcoin bull market, but I view the likely causation as being reversed: more funds will flow into the bitcoin spot ETFs *due* to the rising bitcoin prices rather than bitcoin spot ETF inflows being the primary *cause* of rising bitcoin prices. ETFs in this sense are mostly amplifiers and accelerators.

**Differences Between the ETFs**

The ETFs are similar to each other in general.

-Most of them have relatively low fees, and some of them are waiving fees for the initial period of asset accumulation to try to get ahead out of the gate.

-Most of them custody their coins at Coinbase, with the biggest exception being Fidelity that custodies them in their own custody solution. So, if you hold large amounts of the ETFs, you could consider splitting your exposure up between the Fidelity ETF and one of the others to spread out tail risks related to custody hacks.

ETFs benefit from liquidity network effects, meaning that all else being equal, winners tend to keep winning for a long time. Liquidity in this context refers to average daily trading volume for the ETF itself as well as the volume of any option contracts that are built around it.

People naturally gravitate toward the top few ETFs in their category, and institutions specifically seek the most liquid ones because with their size, they need to do so.

As an example, BlackRock has offered the longstanding iShares MSCI Emerging Markets ETF since 2003. It has an expense ratio of 0.70% and offers exposure to large and mid-sized emerging market companies. But then, in 2012, they launched the iShares Core MSCI Emerging Markets ETF in 2012. It has an expense ratio of 0.09%, and yet it also holds more companies overall and includes large, mid, and small companies. IEMG has outperformed EEM since inception, mainly due to the cumulative difference in fees:

![EEM vs IEMG Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-eem-vs-iemg-performance.png)

Why didn’t iShares just lower the fee on EEM rather than launch a parallel cheaper product? It was because the original product had high liquidity, and some people were locked into it due to the tax effects as well. So they kept milking that product for fees, and instead launched a new ETF with lower fees so that they don’t get undercut by another ETF provider. That way, they have one expensive ETF catering to the trading crowd, and one cheap ETF catering to the buy-and-hold crowd.

IEMG quickly caught up and surpassed EEM in terms of assets under management:

![EEM vs IEMG AUM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-eem-vs-iemg-aum.png)

And yet IEMG is still below EEM in terms of daily trading volume (both in terms of number of shares per day and in total dollar amount per day), and the depth of liquidity on EEM’s option market blows IEMG’s option market away:

![EEM vs IEMG Volume](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-eem-vs-iemg-volume.png)

The same thing happened with the longstanding SPDR Gold Trust (GLD) and the SPDR Gold MiniShares Trust (GLDM). Between them, GLD is the highly liquid trading vehicle in terms of both shares and options, whereas GLDM is the cheaper buy-and-hold offering by the same provider.

And so far, the same thing is playing out in bitcoin. Grayscale has by far the largest ETF in terms of assets under management and existing volume. So, they only reduced their expense ratio from 2% to 1.5%, which is extraordinarily high. A lot of people have capital gains built up in GBTC, and so selling in favor of a lower-cost ETF would result in a big capital gains tax. Therefore, Grayscale will milk the fees for a long time even as other providers offer much lower expense ratios that are typically 0.30% or less. Capital should rotate to those cheaper ones over time, but it will take time.

Personally, I like the Fidelity Wise Origin Bitcoin Fund (FBTC) out of the bunch. Fidelity has been mining bitcoin for nearly a decade, and has been custodying and publishing research on bitcoin for many years. As an institution, they have been miles ahead of others in this category. Their ETF has among the best starting liquidity profiles.

Apart from that, the iShares one and the Ark one are likely to maintain decent liquidity, with iShares being highly likely to have good liquidity and staying power.

If you have GBTC with big capital gains in a taxable account, be careful of selling it without considering the tax consequences first. If you have GBTC with no major capital gains, or if you have it in a retirement account, then there’s a strong case to sell it in favor of one of the leading lower-cost ETFs operated by iShares, Fidelity, or Ark.

**ETFs vs Real Bitcoin**

The main advantage of the ETFs is that they are convenient. You can buy them within an existing brokerage account, including retirement accounts, in order to have some custodial bitcoin price exposure as a slice in a portfolio.

The main disadvantages of the ETFs are twofold. The first is that you pay an ongoing expense ratio, indefinitely. This is in contrast to real bitcoin where you typically pay a one-time fee or spread to buy it but then don’t pay any more fees on it. For example, Fidelity’s ETF charges 0.25% per year indefinitely, whereas Swan Bitcoin charges 0.99% one time upon purchase. Fidelity comes out ahead if you hold for fewer than four years (assuming constant bitcoin price), and after that Swan comes out ahead. If bitcoin goes up in price, then Fidelity’s fees keep growing in dollar terms, and so in terms of the number of dollars paid in fees, Swan could come out ahead sooner.

The second and arguably more important difference is that if you later decide you’d like to directly custody bitcoin yourself or use it for any of its monetary properties, it’ll be a taxable event if you sell the ETF and buy real bitcoin in place of it. This could be a huge tax hit if bitcoin goes up multi-fold during your holding period.

Therefore, for investors that anticipate that they may one day want to directly hold bitcoin or use it, [buying the real thing is ideal](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/). However, the ETFs can be a useful substitute within the context of certain types of accounts for convenience, especially retirement accounts.

## Enterprise Products Partners Analysis

With asset prices in general being very rangebound, inexpensive dividend-paying assets continue to be an attractive option. And my benchmark example for that continues to be Enterprise Products Partners (EPD). Oftentimes, the best places to put new capital is into investments that are already in your portfolio.

Enterprise operates over 50,000 miles of pipelines as well as large amounts of other key infrastructure including natural gas processing plants, storage facilities, and deep water docks.

![Why EPD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-why-epd.png)

*Source: December 2023  EPD Investor Presentation*

Enterprise has 25 consecutive years of annual distribution increases, and they have the highest credit rating among all pipeline operators in the country. They currently yield 7.7%, so if they are able to raise the distribution and other per-share growth metrics by 3% per year on average going forward, they can deliver 10.7% annualized returns at a constant equity valuation. And this is with minimal direct commodity price exposure; it’s mostly a toll-road type of business.

Enterprise transports crude, natural gas, natural gas liquids, and petrochemicals. When people think of oil they mainly think of gasoline in their cars, but in reality everything from the plastic on your monitor or phone as you read this, to the Tylenol you take if you have a headache, to the fertilizer used to grow your lunch, is made from products that Enterprise transports around.

![EPD Products](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-epd-products.png)

Like many publicly-traded partnerships, Enterprise’s early growth period involved constant equity issuance along with constant debt issuance. Whereas many other partnerships eventually blew up during the industry downturns in 2015/2016 and 2020, Enterprise instead transitioned gradually toward a more self-financing model.

-They eliminated 50% incentive distribution rights, and then collapsed the whole general partner into a flatter structure.

-They began slowing down their distribution growth compared to their cash flow growth, to reduce the payout ratio and thus be able to finance more of their own projects internally from spare cashflow.

This chart shows the cash flow in gray, and the part that is paid out in distributions in blue. The multiple has improved substantially in recent years. And they no longer have to grow distributions faster than cash flows because they have reached their target payout ratio by this point.

![EPD Distributions](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-epd-distributions.png)

This chart shows their shares/units outstanding. As the company reduced their payout ratio, they no longer needed to issue more shares:

![EPD Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-epd-shares.png)

The company still issues some debt, but only when it makes economic sense for them to do so. Their projects typically give them 12% ROI, and they historically have been able to borrow at sub-5%, which means borrowing is lucrative for equity holders. Going forward, borrowing costs are likely to remain higher, but their debt is mostly fixed-rate, with an average yield of 4.6% and an average duration of 19.3 years.

![Enterprise Products Partners](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-epd-balance-sheet.png)

Enterprise’s distribution yield is currently 3.37% higher than the yield on a 10-year Treasury. In addition, Enterprise’s distribution grows each year. Despite higher 10-year Treasury yields today, Enterprise’s distribution yield spread over the 10-year Treasury yield remains moderately above its two decade average, meaning that on this metric Enterprise remains inexpensive:

![EPD Distribution vs 10-Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-epd-dividend-premium.png)

Overall, I view 3.37% as a very reasonable equity risk premium, and that only counts the distribution rather than all distributable cash flow per share. With the combination of higher yield and annual growth, Enterprise unitholders can expect to earn more than double the returns of Treasuries in a relatively low risk package, albeit with more volatility and tail risk than Treasuries along the way.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-1-21-epd-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

**Enterprise Risks**

As a large infrastructure business, Enterprise is vulnerable to major changes in regulation, and its assets can be disrupted by natural catastrophes or terrorist attacks.

Over the next few years, the United States’ ability to export natural gas liquids to Europe and other parts of the world is a key driver of new infrastructure projects. If the United States becomes more protective and restricts exports, it could materially harm the business interests of many of these infrastructure operators.

Over the long term, Enterprise’s business would stagnate if U.S. energy production stagnates. However, at under 11x earnings and geared toward sending a lot of its cash flow back to unitholders, Enterprise is currently priced for no growth, even as it grows mildly.

Enterprise is a publicly-traded partnership rather than a corporation, and so it issues a K-1 during tax season. This comes with tax advantages for U.S. investors but requires filling out an additional form during tax season. Non-US investors face more challenges with publicly-traded partnerships like Enterprise because they face distribution withholding taxes, and thus should talk to their tax specialist before investing. I believe this is a reason why MLP-structured midstream businesses tend to trade at a discount to similar midstream businesses that are structured as corporations.

I view Enterprise as the ideal compliment to growth assets. Many tech stocks or hard monies do not pay a yield, but instead appreciate in price over time. A mix of assets like Enterprise that pay regular high income, and growth assets or store-of-value assets that appreciate over time, helps investors meet income needs while also participating in growth and/or protecting against long-run monetary debasement.
