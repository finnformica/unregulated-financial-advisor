## Macro View

Ongoing macro analysis continues to be a balance between liquidity interventions and recession probabilities.

Problems in leveraged commercial real estate and unprofitable tech firms (including private equity) continue to mount. These are the most sensitive sectors to interest rates.

On the other hand, the recent upturn in liquidity, and ongoing strength in sectors like travel, restaurants, and defensives (healthcare, utilities, consumer staples, and profitable tech) continue to hold up well.

**Domestic Liquidity Update**

Overall domestic liquidity remains moderate for now, and likely for the next couple of months. This domestic liquidity index consists of the Federal Reserve balance sheet, minus the Treasury General Account, minus reverse repos.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Federal Reserve balance sheet portion of domestic liquidity (shown below) inched down last week, after two massive upward weeks due to loan activities.

My base case is to see it trend sideways for a while, meaning the balance sheet will likely remain above the recent low point, but not screaming higher right away unless some further crisis happens that necessitates it. On a longer timeline, I expect it’ll need to head up again, but not necessarily in the next couple months.

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-fed-balance-sheet.png)

So far, this outcome is in line with what I wrote in my prior report:

> We’ve exited an uncertain market regime, and entered another uncertain market regime, but one with different characteristics. The Fed balance sheet is likely to trend sideways-ish for months, unless or until some big catalyst forces it higher. They don’t want to flood the system with liquidity but they want to keep the wheels from falling off.
>
> -March 19th, 2023 premium report

The Treasury General Account portion of domestic liquidity had a big drawdown last week, and is now getting rather low. When the TGA draws down, it’s pro-liquidity for the financial system, all else being equal.

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-tga.png)

I expect that here in April it will be a net increase for the TGA due to tax season, but after that, the trend is generally down until the debt ceiling is raised. And due to how poorly most assets performed in 2022, capital gains taxes payable in April for the 2022 tax year are likely to be very weak.

When the Treasury can’t issue debt due to a debt ceiling limit, it has to turn towards extraordinary measures. They start with low-impact things like drawing down their cash balance and temporarily not reinvesting federal employee retirement Treasury securities. When those approaches get mostly tapped out, they turn towards discretionary/partial government shutdowns. And if that situation remains in place long enough, they eventually have to choose between missing some Social Security or Medicare payments, or defaulting on some Treasuries, which is basically “DEFCON 1” and is what can bring the debt ceiling debate down to the emergency hour, with Congress and the President scrambling to resolve it on a weekend.

Once the debt ceiling is resolved, and the Treasury presumably wants to **refill** their cash account up to their ~$500 billion target, that would be a major liquidity drain. That continues to be a storm cloud on the horizon for the second half of 2023, and could be a significant enough catalyst to stop the Federal Reserve’s quantitative tightening, if it’s not already stopped by then due to banking system liquidity issues.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Purchasing managers indices are pointing upward in March, from recessionary levels.

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-pmi.png)

The more-reliable but slower-updating ISM numbers were mildly up for February and aren’t available yet for March:

![ISM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-ism.png)

The six-month September 2019 to February 2020 period continues to be worth being familiar with in terms of market and economic dynamics. In that period, the Federal Reserve ran the banking system into a liquidity problem, stopped reducing its balance sheet, began trending its balance sheet sideways, and various economic indicators were bouncing up from near-recession levels. It continued that way until COVID lockdowns struck in March 2020.

You can see the bounce in PMI on the charts above in late 2019. And for the Federal Reserve balance sheet, here is what I wrote in my previous report:

> What does this mean? To start with, I think the period from September 2019 to February 2020 is important to be familiar with. Prior to September 2019, the U.S. economy growth rate was decelerating, and the Fed was performing quantitative tightening to reduce its balance sheet. When the repo spike happened in mid-September 2019, their plans changed.
>
> From September 2019 to February 2020, the Fed was managing the liquidity problem in the banking system (which they had caused via their quantitative tightening) by 1) participating in the repo market and then by 2) buying T-bills. They refused to call it quantitative easing at the time, because the goal wasn’t to be stimulative for markets or the economy; it was to provide the minimum amount of liquidity to keep things from seizing up. So the balance sheet went up a bit and then mostly sideways.
>
> ![Repo Spike Period](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-repo-spike-period.png)
>
> -March 19, 2023 premium report

Overall, I think we are in a similar dynamic now. Liquidity and economic indicators are likely to trend sideways-ish for a bit. As we look out further, perhaps into the second half of 2023, a U.S. recession is a significant possibility depending on what policymakers do.

They also made a set of Chinese refinery stake acquisitions, and overall have tightened their energy partnership with China.

When analyzing non-petrodollar energy and trade agreements, analysis tends to be very polarized.

On one hand, there’s been no shortage of “*imminent death to the dollar*” headlines over the past couple decades, every time China or some other country makes a currency move of this sort. On the other hand, there are a lot of “*there’s absolutely no change for the dollar’s reserve status on the horizon and anyone who says otherwise is a conspiracy doomer*” takes as well.

I think the reality is pretty clearly in the middle. Decades ago, China was small compared to what it is now. It’s now the #1 or #2 economy by most metrics, including the largest importer of energy and commodities, and the largest trading partner with the majority of countries in the world. And multiple countries have an incentive to diversify their payments and foreign reserves to minimize their vulnerability to sanctions or asset seizures by any single country.

Around the margins, it seems clear that we’re shifting towards a more multi-polar world in terms of regional powers and currency agreements. China still has capital controls, which makes their currency/bonds broadly unattractive as a reserve asset. However, due to their economic size and trading relationships, they have a significant ability to present an alternative payment and intermediate-term savings ecosystem for various “BRICS” or “BRIICSS” countries. In addition, foreign central banks have been consistently increasing their gold tonnage ever since 2009, which was a reversal of a multi-decade trend from the 1970s to 2009 of dwindling gold tonnage held by central banks.

What this suggests is that around the margins, the percentage of U.S. federal debt held by the foreign sector is likely to decrease. It’s already been on the decline since 2013, as U.S. federal debt kept increasing and foreign holdings of it went more sideways:

![Debt Holdings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-debt-holdings.png)

The red line in the chart above includes both foreign central banks and foreign private pools of capital. Foreign central banks have outright stopped increasing U.S. Treasury holdings for much of the past decade, while foreign private sources still accumulate, just not as quickly as they used to.

In the coming years, we will likely see the Federal Reserve return as a buyer of U.S. Treasuries, due to insufficient balance sheet capacity elsewhere to absorb the ongoing Treasury debt issuance ($1.5 trillion net new issuance per year, and climbing). In the intermediate term, the debt ceiling prevents net new issuance for the next few months, and domestic non-bank entities have some balance sheet space to absorb some more.

It’s pretty remarkable that interest rates are nearly 5% and the dollar index is fairly strong, and yet gold is knocking on the doors of all-time highs in dollar terms.

**Crypto Crackdowns**

From 2013 to 2017, the U.S. Justice Department and the FDIC engaged in something called “[Operation Chokepoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Choke_Point)” where they de-banked a lot of “legal-yet-undesirable” industries that are often scammy, crime-adjacent, involve the moving of money, or are associated with so-called vice.

This included a broad range of industries like gun/ammo sales, payday lenders, coin dealers, pawn shops, tobacco sales, telemarketing, credit/debt repair services, dating services, escort services, pornography businesses, racist organizations, and so forth. Obviously those are not all similar businesses, but what they share in common is that they were all among those that were targeted to varying degrees.

The operation received some valid criticism for being an overreach: rather than the Legislative Branch passing laws against these types of (mostly legal) businesses, the Executive Branch unliterally and quietly pressured banks to de-bank them even if they couldn’t charge them with committing any actual crimes. Most people don’t lose sleep about shady businesses being de-banked, but more broadly it brings up concerns about the precedent and implications for what else this type of informal action may be used for in the future.

The chairman of the FDIC from 2012 to 2018 was Martin Gruenberg. Beginning in January 2023, he became chairman of the FDIC again. In recent months, there has been an uptick in pressure for banks to de-bank cryptocurrency companies, in what many have called “Operation Chokepoint 2.0”.

In many cases it involves cryptocurrency companies themselves being de-banked, but in some cases it also involves blocking outgoing transactions from peoples’ accounts to cryptocurrency exchanges. Sometimes the bank says it is completely voluntary, while other times they point out that regulators are putting pressure on them.

![Gromen BTC Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-gromen-btc-tweet.png)

In some ironic ways, this type of action is almost an advertisement for bitcoin. The bank tells its customers what they’re not allowed to spend their money on, and what types of payments they’re not allowed to accept. It’s a reminder to people that their deposits in their bank are not really theirs; they’re an IOU with conditions attached.

This has occurred alongside the recent news of the CFTC going after Binance, and the SEC going after Justin Sun of TRON for securities law violations and issuing a Wells notice to Coinbase (COIN).

Gary Gensler, chairman of the SEC, recently put “[deal with it](https://knowyourmeme.com/memes/deal-with-it)” sunglasses (a trope in online meme culture) onto his Twitter profile picture. This is his actual Twitter avatar now, which he switched to amid the various SEC enforcement actions:

![Gary Gensler](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-gensler.jpg)

It’s worth reading Gary Gensler’s [February 2023 interview with New York Magazine](https://nymag.com/intelligencer/2023/02/gary-gensler-on-meeting-with-sbf-and-his-crypto-crackdown.html) in full. Here’s a relevant section:

![Gensler New York](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-gensler-new-york.png)

On one hand, most of this is not surprising. The majority of cryptocurrency companies are engaged in selling what are most likely unregistered securities. It’s a very scammy industry in aggregate, with a lot of pump-and-dump schemes, companies operating in legal gray zones or offshore, and so forth. If I was running a bank, that’s not exactly the category of companies I’d cater to either. Bitcoins are officially not securities, but most other digital assets do seem to pass the Howey Test.

On the other hand, the net has been wide. A person or business being de-banked for merely accepting bitcoins as payment is quite different than being de-banked as a company that sells unregistered securities to the public and has been under CFTC/SEC pressure. However, even the former category (de-banking companies for merely accepting bitcoin as payment) falls in line with prior Choke Point 1.0 action against coin stores, money transfer businesses, and so forth.

Governments around the world have been gradually tightening rules around alternative monies for years. In 2020, for example, Germany reduced the amount of gold that can be legally purchased without identification from 10,000 euros to 2,000 euros worth. Germans can buy all sorts of things for over 2,000 euros without showing identification, but not gold. This is because gold can be stored or moved in large amounts for payments and savings, and they want to be able to surveil its ownership and movement. Nigeria cut off cryptocurrency companies from its banking system last year in order to try to protect its inflationary currency (13% annualized inflation over the past decade), and this year their central bank reduced the amount of physical cash that can be withdrawn in general.

These responses (capital controls and pressures or bans on alternative money) happened all the time in the 1940s and the decades around that period. The combination of high debt and high inflation generally leads to savers looking for the exists, as governments undergo financial repression and try to add frictions to the exits. All else being equal, it’s a lot less costly to simply cut things off from the banking system than to ban them outright, because for example there are 4,000 banks in the U.S. (who will tend to comply with any pressure) but 330 million people (which means tons of enforcement points).

> High public debt often produces the drama of default and restructuring. But debt is also reduced through financial repression, a tax on bondholders and savers via negative or belowmarket real interest rates. After WWII, capital controls and regulatory restrictions created a captive audience for government debt, limiting tax-base erosion. Financial repression is most successful in liquidating debt when accompanied by inflation. For the advanced economies, real interest rates were negative ½ of the time during 1945–1980. Average annual interest expense savings for a 12—country sample range from about 1 to 5 percent of GDP for the full 1945–1980 period. We suggest that, once again, financial repression may be part of the toolkit deployed to cope with the most recent surge in public debt in advanced economies.
>
> –[The Liquidation of Government Debt](https://www.imf.org/en/Publications/WP/Issues/2016/12/31/The-Liquidation-of-Government-Debt-42610), IMF Working paper 2015/007

![Nope Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-nope.png)

![Yield Curve Control](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-ycc.png)

There are many peer-to-peer methods to buy bitcoin and similar assets, and those services might increase their market share in the years ahead if bank offramp restrictions increase. Paxful, for example, operates in dozens of countries around the world and has thousands of sellers, millions of buyers, and hundreds of different payment methods depending on the region.

Nigeria has some of the highest levels of cryptocurrency adoption in the world despite the Nigerian banking system being cut off from cryptocurrency exchanges, because they trade peer-to-peer through those sorts of methods. In the West we have recently had more currency comfort, whereas if people experience 13% annualized inflation for a decade like they have in Nigeria, they become more assertive with finding ways out of that system.

**Insurance Companies**

One boring-but-profitable subsector out there continues to be insurance stocks. As the question of “are we going to have a recession later this year?” continues to be unclear (but leaning towards “yes” in my view), the risk/reward of a lot of cyclical stocks continue to be challenging to analyze, while insurance is pretty recession-resistant.

Banks make money by taking in deposits and then lending money out, and earning a spread. Many of them have been vulnerable lately because at any time, people can pull their deposits out and go elsewhere, including into higher-yielding money markets or T-bills, or just gravitate towards the top-ten banks.

Insurance companies, on the other hand, make money by taking in premiums and paying out claims, and investing the time delay between the two. Many insurance companies barely make any profit on claims vs premiums, but at any given time, they hold a large “float” of money, and they conservatively invest that money into various income-producing assets. Most of them are bonds, but around the margins they invest in equities too.

Insurance companies, relying on premiums rather than deposits, are less vulnerable to a quick loss of their funding. They do have unrealized losses on their bond portfolios from the sharp rise in interest rates, but as bonds mature at face value and get reinvested, the average interest rate on the portfolio improves. Low-rate environments are rough for insurers and high-rate environments are good for insurers, although similar to banks, a rapid transition from low-to-high can be a challenge.

The diversified property and casualty insurance company, Traveler’s (TRV), represents a stock that analysts are bullish on, and that in terms of valuation trades in-line with its historical average.

![TRV FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-trv-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

The stock has an earnings yield of 7.5%. Virtually all of that goes towards dividends and buybacks, since it’s a mature business that grows its revenue organically at-or-slightly-above the inflation rate, while returning cash to shareholders. Their dividend yield is a little over 2%, and their 10-year average annual dividend growth is about 7%. Recessions don’t historically materially affect their business.

## Final Thoughts: Charles Schwab’s Risk

Charles Schwab (SCHW) is one of the largest brokerages in the United States, and it has its own moderately large bank as well.

And… the company currently has negative tangible equity, if their hold-to-maturity assets are marked to market. It’s not a stock I hold in any portfolios, although I do use their brokerage services.

This chart shows their tangible book value, without taking into account their hold-to-maturity unrealized losses (which if marked-to-market would drive the number negative):

![SCHW Tangible Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-2-schw-tangible-book.png)

When we look at Silicon Valley Bank and why it collapsed, we can see two big risk factors.

The first risk factor was that its unrealized losses on its security holdings exceeded its capital, meaning it was insolvent. Overall, the bank exposed itself to a lot of duration risk compared to other banks, and those losses exceeded their capital. If they could have held to maturity, they might have been fine, but they couldn’t because of the second risk factor.

The second risk factor was that they mostly served large business accounts, and so the vast majority of deposits were not FDIC insured. Those types of deposits are quick to rush out if there’s any sign of a problem. If deposits didn’t flee, the bank could have held its losses until maturity, get their full principle back, and thus become solvent. But they were rug-pulled by their depositors before then. The combination of both of those risk factors combined into a kill shot for the bank.

Several other banks have one risk factor but not both. Citi (C) for example has a large percentage of uninsured deposits due to its focus on serving businesses. However, it has mild unrealized securities losses, firmly positive capital, and it’s a global systemically important bank with extra requirements and an implicit bailout guarantee, so deposits are flowing towards it rather than out of it. While it’s not quite up there with JPMorgan Chase (JPM) in terms of safety, Citi is one of the safer banks right now.

Schwab has the opposite risk factor than Citi, and overall it’s the more dangerous risk factor. Much like Silicon Valley Bank, Schwab owns a lot of long duration securities, and their unrealized losses exceed their capital. They’re technically insolvent now. But as for the other risk factor, they’re in a low risk category. Less than 20% of their deposits are uninsured, which reduces flight risk. The market doesn’t seem worried about it; Schwab stock is trading at 14x earnings. And because they are big and have a diversified deposit base (not just a bunch of tech startups in California), their chance of a deposit run is further reduced.

But isn’t it unsettling? Among the biggest brokers in the United States is mark-to-market insolvent, and most likely wouldn’t be able to withstand a bank run on its own, in the unlikely event that one were to happen.

Fortunately, Schwab has multiple external sources of liquidity if needed, including the Federal Reserve’s BTFP, which can give them cash loans in return for using their securities as collateral at face value. So, with external help, they can deal with a bank run. Such external facilities do come at a much higher cost of capital than deposits, however, and thus would threaten Schwab’s profitability and potential solvency if they are used in significant amounts.

When analyzing Schwab stock, I think its current valuation is rich in terms of risk/reward. Although depositors should be fine, the company itself is vulnerable to a tail risk that could impair its market capitalization, and even if it doesn’t face that, regulators could tell them to raise capital (halt dividends and share buybacks, or dilute equity). Even the current shift by consumers towards money market funds is likely to hurt Schwab’s profitability over time.

When analyzing Schwab as a broker, I’m less concerned. Investors have $500k worth of SIPC insurance on securities. And cash depositors have FDIC insurance up to $250k. As a large entity, Schwab buys additional insurance from Lloyds of London and others, and so has an extra $600 million of firm-wide insurance on customer securities in case of insolvency. Schwab deals with trillions of dollars worth of securities. I personally have securities held at Schwab, and am not removing them. However, for new capital inflows, I am prioritizing my other brokerage Fidelity for diversification, to limit the overall amount of uninsured exposure I have to any one brokerage, especially one with negative mark-to-market tangible equity.

I’m somewhat optimistic from a value perspective on several large diversified banks currently, and concerned about small/vulnerable banks. As a vulnerable bank, shorting Schwab with an upside stop at $60 might not be a bad hedge for investors that are otherwise long large diversified banks. I don’t have a firmly bearish view on Schwab, but if ongoing systemic issues threaten an otherwise bullish position on big banks, then Schwab’s equity value is likely to be punished more. I also don’t recommend shorting stocks unless one is experienced and willing to actively monitor that type of complex portfolio.

**Zooming Out**

This ultimately goes back to my 1940s analogue thesis. A central bank can’t rapidly raise interest rates from zero to mid-to-high single digits in a very high debt environment without causing 1) way larger public deficits due to increased interest expense (which is ironically inflationary), 2) a lot of quietly insolvent banks who made the sin of investing in… Treasuries, and 3) the implicit need to backstop any large entity with printed money if it runs into problems, which is what happened with banks recently.

![Nope Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-19-nope.png)

A lot of analysts think that the Federal Reserve can just tighten as much as it wants to quell inflation, as though this was the 1970s with low debt and bank-driven inflation. They just see a simple algorithm that they’ve been conditioned towards for decades: “If inflation is high and unemployment is low, then tighten.”

However, since the 2020s resemble the 1940s (high debt and fiscal-driven inflation), it’s not that simple.

The Federal Reserve now has to manage system-wide bank liquidity along with inflation. They want to suppress private-sector lending to a below-trend level, while still providing adequate liquidity to markets, banks, and the U.S. Treasury Department. This process can suppress inflation and demand for a period of time, but is ultimately temporary in nature. In the long run, they’re most likely going to be pulled towards inflating large swaths of debt away, whether they like it or not.

Their current tight policy approach, while they do it, is good for our positions in cash-equivalents (T-bills and money markets) as a form of defense against recession and financial crisis. However, due to their inability to fully tighten and maintain positive real interest rates in such a high debt environment, it continues to favor pro-inflation positions (dividend stocks, commodities, and hard monies) over the long term, with appropriate position sizes.

In other words, we’re likely looking at a period of continued disinflation over the next several months, but with more waves of inflation on the horizon. Various parts of a portfolio can reflect those different timeframes.
