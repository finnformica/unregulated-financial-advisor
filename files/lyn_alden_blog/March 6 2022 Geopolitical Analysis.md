## Russia/Ukraine Events

Since February 24th, Russia has been launching a large-scale invasion of Ukraine.

It would not have surprised many, including myself, to have seen *some* military action by Russia in the Donbas region of eastern Ukraine. However, what Russia has done is far outside of my base case expectations, and represents the triggering of a major tail risk. Russia has launched a large-scale assault across all of Ukraine, with bombings of military facilities across the country including the western-most portions of Ukraine, along with a military push towards the capital, Kyiv. It’s an aggressive sovereign war where they are taking cities and territory, rather than a border dispute.

So far Russia has focused on military rather than civilian targets in Ukraine, but with any military engagement of this size, civilians inevitably become casualties as well. Some civilians have taken up arms to defend their territory, and thus the line between combatant and civilian isn’t always clear, either. At the moment it is hard to determine the number of causalities, but here is Wikipedia’s ongoing update from multiple conflicting sources of military and civilian causalities:

In addition, it is widely estimated that well over a million people have fled into central/western Europe, and Poland in particular.

Putin’s stated goal is to demilitarize Ukraine- to destroy a large portion of its military capability. However, Putin has also said that Ukraine’s statehood is a fiction, and has said that if Ukraine’s leadership “continues doing what they are doing”, they risk their statehood.

**Background Context**

The North Atlantic Treaty Organization or “NATO” is an alliance of 30 countries including the United States and much of Europe, established in 1949, that agree to mutual defense.

The cornerstone of NATO is Article 5, which states that if any NATO country is attacked, every single NATO member country will respond in their defense. It acts as a major deterrent because NATO countries collectively account for around 60% of global military spending and thus should have a decisive advantage over any attacker or alliance of attackers. The only time Article 5 was invoked in seven decades of the NATO alliance so far was in the aftermath of 9/11, which was in response to suicide bombers and terrorist organizations rather than sovereign entities.

Vladimir Putin has viewed NATO as a threat for a long time. With no mountain range or other naturally defensive features between itself and Europe, Russia has indeed been invaded from the west a number of times over the centuries. In recent decades, NATO has been expanding its member states from the west into former Soviet areas, because those countries wanted to join the defense alliance.

Similar to how the US does not want Russian military assets anywhere near its borders (e.g. the infamous Cuban Missile Crisis), Russia does not want US-aligned or NATO military assets in its border states either. Here’s one of the problems from Putin’s point of view in map form, with NATO expanding eastward due to countries joining the alliance:

![NATO Map](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-6-nato-map.jpg)

*Map Source: [BBC](https://www.bbc.com/news/world-europe-18023383)*

Additionally, the broad region formally known as [Kievan Rus’](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27) is the ethnic origin of what we currently know as Russia, Ukraine, and Belarus. What complicates matters is that the modern post-Soviet borders between Ukraine and Russia, despite being sovereign lines, don’t perfectly match up with the demographics. For example, Crimea is primarily made up of people who are [ethnically and linguistically Russian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demographics_of_Crimea). The Donbas region has a more mixed split between Ukraine and Russian ethnicity/language, and for a while it has been contested locally by separatist forces even though it is within Ukraine sovereign territory.

Polls conducted by domestic and foreign sources prior to this war have generally found that the majority of Russians support Putin and have a somewhat favorable view of attacking Ukraine to ensure it doesn’t join NATO. They have a less favorable view, however, of trying to occupy/re-unite Ukraine. This poll was from just prior to the attack:

As a former KGB officer, Putin has long had pro-Soviet viewpoints. Nearly two decades ago, he referred to the collapse of the Soviet Union as the greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the 20th century.

> “First and foremost it is worth acknowledging that the demise of the Soviet Union was the greatest geopolitical catastrophe of the century,” Putin said. “As for the Russian people, it became a genuine tragedy. Tens of millions of our fellow citizens and countrymen found themselves beyond the fringes of Russian territory.
>
> –[NBC, 2005](https://www.nbcnews.com/id/wbna7632057)

So, when we analyze the situation as objectively as possible, it’s understandable why Russia would feel pressured from NATO, and even why they might go as far as to dispute some of the existing borders with Ukraine around the margins. However, an attack by Russia across the *whole of Ukraine* and a challenge to its statehood is far outside of any reasonable ethical framework, which is why the international response has been so significant, especially compared to the 2014 annexation of Crimea.

Russia going after Kyiv is very different in terms of magnitude than Russia going after Crimea. It’s an unjust attack from any reasonable interpretation.

## Financial Sanctions and Frozen Reserves

In response to the attack, the international community has sanctioned Russia in multiple ways. This includes North America, Europe, Japan, and many others (but not every country, and notably, not China). Russia is also being partially cut off from the SWIFT system, which without access makes settling international transactions much more labor intensive. Many forms of payment by everyday Russian citizens have been heavily disrupted.

However, the world has not yet formally cut off its imports of Russian commodities, because quite simply, the global math doesn’t work without them. Europe gets more than 40% of its natural gas from Russia, as well as a big chunk of its oil and coal. If Russia outright shuts off the gas pipelines to Europe, many of the lights literally go out (and the manufacturing, and the heat, and the air conditioning, etc). The question then becomes which region goes insolvent first; Russia without its energy export revenue, or Europe without its energy.

And as for nickel to make EV batteries, or platinum for hydrolysis? Well, Russia is one of the top three nickel producers and one of the top three platinum producers in the world. Russia is also one of the top three makers of nuclear reactors for nuclear electrical power as well. So, that’s a problem across the board.

And for food, Russia is the single biggest fertilizer exporter and wheat exporter globally. That’s another problem, and perhaps a more immediate one if things get worse. Russia has been limiting fertilizer exports.

People who seem sure of how this will turn out, are probably the least informed. People who understand the commodity dynamics and other details, are understandably less-sure. It’s very complex.

Probably the largest international reaction overall to Russia’s invasion is the freezing of Russia’s international reserves. Going into this war, Russia had $630 billion in international reserves, which basically represents decades of accumulated and saved trade surpluses. This includes $130+ billion in gold held in their own jurisdiction, which they still have. The other nearly $500 billion or so consists of fiat currency reserves, generally either foreign deposits or debt securities.

Of that nearly $500 billion, it appears that somewhere around $400+ billion has now been frozen by international sources, mainly in Europe. However, something like $70-$80 billion was associated with China, which are not frozen. So between Russia’s gold and yuan assets, they have somewhere around $200 billion in workable reserves compared to the $630 billion they had a couple weeks ago.

This means that unless or until Russia gets to unfreeze those reserves, this war has cost over $400 billion for Russia from frozen reserves alone, which is nearly a quarter of their annual GDP, and over 5x what they spend annually on their military.

The global ripple effects from this are still unclear. Russian entities may default on global counterparties, financially impairing those entities. Whenever this much money is frozen or disrupted, there are bound to be liquidity and solvency issues popping up in many areas that we don’t necessarily expect.

**Putin’s Potential Miscalculations**

Prior to the war, Russia had a trade surplus, low debt levels, huge FX reserves, and was benefitting from being a key commodity producer during a structural commodity bull market. They had a massive nuclear deterrent to discourage any potential attacks even from larger economies such as China or the United States. Their large corporations were rather well-run and well-capitalized. Putin had majority support among Russians, according to domestic and international polls.

And then they attacked a foreign country and got their own economy heavily sanctioned and maybe two-thirds of their reserves frozen.

This begs the question- how much did Putin anticipate? Did he make an irrational decision out of pro-Soviet obsessions, or is he midway through a well-thought-out plan? He has spent at least a decade trying to make Russia as sanction-resistant as possible, including building huge international reserves, emphasizing gold more than most other countries, and selling Russia’s US Treasuries which would be first in line for sanctions. It would be foolish not to assume that he has thought things through, although it does appear that several things are probably not going as he anticipated.

I’m a financial analyst and former engineer/manager rather than a military analyst, so apart from obvious humanitarian concerns, I can only realistically look at the likely outcomes of this situation through the lenses of money and energy, which are topics I cover in depth. I won’t be an armchair military strategist as many financial analysts have seemingly become lately. I do research what military/geopolitical experts are saying about the situation, from multiple perspectives, and can incorporate that research into my analysis.

It remains to be seen, at least by me, to what extent Vladimir Putin may have miscalculated with this attack. It’s certainly not looking good for Russia from a financial standpoint, but it’s not over yet.

-Perhaps Putin underestimated the morale of the Ukrainian military and civilian population to fight back. Despite having a much smaller military budget (Ukraine’s military has a smaller annual budget than the New York Police Department, for reference), Ukraine isn’t folding to Russia easily. Plus, Ukrainians are being supplied lethal arms by many countries, and many civilians are taking up arms. The longer this war goes on, the more logistically expensive it becomes for Russia to keep the pressure on Ukraine. One could easily imagine that if this drags out, domestic support for the war within Russia will reduce as Russian savings and investments are crushed for the sake of a foreign war, and they have trouble importing things. Even if Russia successfully conquers Kyiv, it could take longer, be more expensive, and/or be less decisive, than Putin anticipated.

-Perhaps Putin didn’t expect Europe to freeze Russia’s FX reserves. Russia has already de-dollarized by getting rid of its Treasuries (likely anticipating a US freeze), and holds an unusually large amount of gold, but it also holds hundreds of billions of euro-denominated assets. Did he expect to have hundreds of billions worth of frozen European and IMF reserves? It seems unlikely; that’s a huge cost. Russia is risking complete financial ruin for a war on the other side of their border, when they already have a serious nuclear deterrent against any potential NATO aggression on their own borders. Why do this, other than madness? Isn’t Russia *less* secure now, than before?

On the other hand, this isn’t over yet. The reserves of a large nuclear military power have been frozen, and that nuclear military power supplies key commodities to the countries that froze their reserves. That’s a problem, and not nearly as clean of a “win” yet as western media wants to portray the situation. Depending on how far Putin is willing to go, and how far he planned this out, and to what extent he can maintain leadership in his country in this time of hardship for Russians, he could eventually force the country’s reserves to be unfrozen by holding certain critical exports “hostage”.

Could Putin capture Kyiv, and then negotiate a ceasefire and pullback? Terms could include NATO formally agreeing not to include Ukraine within its alliance, Russia getting its reserve assets unfrozen, and Russia agreeing to pull back out of Ukraine and to continue exporting critical commodities to Europe and the rest of the world to keep their lights on.

Basically, the game theory is complex here, and it’s hard to know what certain political leaders are thinking or will do if things get really ugly. Right now it’s an arm-wrestle militarily to have an upper hand in ceasefire negotiations, and Russia’s financial crisis is kicking in faster than Europe’s commodity crisis. But that could change quickly, and a commodity crisis can be as bad as a financial crisis.

## The 2020s/1940s Comparison Just Got Closer

For the past couple of years, I have been making the comparison that the 2010s were a lot like the 1930s, and the 2020s are shaping up to be a lot like the 1940s, in terms of the macro landscape in general and fiscal/monetary policy in particular.

This aligns with the long-term debt cycle (quantitative analysis) and the 4th Turning generational theory (qualitative/social analysis).

The long-term debt cycle, observed by Ray Dalio and others, refers to the practice among highly-indebted sovereign nations to inevitably engage in financial repression when they run out of room, with high debt levels and zero interest rates. They end up holding interest rates below the inflation rate for a long period of time, and monetizing debt, as needed.

The 4th Turning generational theory, combined with similar historical observations, refers to a historical/demographic analysis by Neil Howe and others, which shows that human societies tend to make big changes in roughly 50-100 year increments; existing legacy institutions start to break down and give rise to increasing levels of wealth concentration and reactive populism, until new institutions are constructed and the generational cycle starts anew.

I’ve also been looking for a commodity bull market, energy shortage, and generally a decade where “real stuff” matters, which is another similarity to that era.

This whole 2020s/1940s comparison unfortunately got a lot more clear now that there is kinetic war in Europe, and more broadly, financial war between one of the world’s most important commodity exporters and the rest of the world.

If China supports Russia even passively, they can form a powerful economic/military alliance. China is a manufacturing powerhouse and the world’s largest commodity importer. Russia is a major commodity exporter, with strong aerospace capabilities. Already, Russia and China are outlining new pipelines that, if completed, would redirect gas that was once destined to Europe, towards China instead.

> The Russian gas giant signed a contract to design the Soyuz Vostok pipeline across Mongolia toward China, Gazprom said in a statement. If Russia reaches a new supply agreement with China, Soyuz Vostok will carry as much as 50 billion cubic meters of natural gas per year to the Asian nation.

That could ease Gazprom’s reliance on the European continent, currently the single-largest buyer of Russian gas.
>
> –[Bloomberg, February 28th](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-02-28/gazprom-paves-way-to-new-china-gas-deal-as-sanctions-hit-russia)

That’s basically the Boolean variable. If China supports Russia in any multiple of areas to a serious degree over the long run even without direct military assistance, the West faces a serious loss of control. China could buy Russian commodities and accelerate usage of their [Cross-Broder Interbank Payment System (CIPS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-Border_Interbank_Payment_System), which goes around dollar/SWIFT payment channels. China’s new central bank digital currency can also go around SWIFT. This event may accelerate China’s ability to buy commodities directly with yuan, as China likely has stronger negotiating power with Russia now.

China is the largest trading partner for the majority of countries in the world, more so than the US now, and is a natural ally with Russia against the US and NATO, and can give Russia a monetary export outlet for their oil, gas, and other commodities (at a discount that benefits China). China has the most installed industrial capacity, and the most electricity generation and consumption to support that industrial capacity, but they need to import commodities. China could buy up the various Russian joint ventures that European oil majors recently exited from in Russia, including mainly large energy deposits worth tens of billions of dollars. China, then, would have more exposure to commodity reserves (purchased at a discount) to alleviate its inherent need to import commodities.

“Western companies sell long-term commodity deposits to Chinese companies at a steep discount” is not the headline most of us want to hear, but is what may happen in this scenario.

## Asset Class Review

COVID-19 accelerated a number of trends, including remote work, migration out of the most heavily-populated areas, peak globalization, and policies around debt monetization and fiscal support.

The Russia/Ukraine conflict, and more broadly the sanctions and freezing of reserves along with export restrictions for commodities, could easily accelerate a number of trends as well, including diversified non-SWIFT payment channels, a shift in international reserve investment practices, persistent commodity scarcity and inflation, and global bifurcation or partial de-globalization of supply chains.

This requires a re-assessment of every asset class. I’ve already been emphasizing trends along these lines, but recent events crystalize it and make things more specific.

**Gold: Established Sovereign Asset**

The world has about $15 trillion USD-equivalents of official international sovereign reserves. This represents stored-up trade surpluses that are held by central banks, that can be used to collateralize and support their fiat currencies. Of this, less than $2.5 trillion consists of gold, and the rest consists of various fiat-denominated assets, mainly cash deposits and debt securities. Even a large portion of the gold assets are custodied abroad; many countries let the US or UK hold their official gold reserves for them in New York and London respectively.

As the world recently found out, almost all of that can be frozen by other countries. The WSJ recently published an article called “[If Russian Currency Reserves Aren’t Really Money, the World is In for a Shock](https://www.wsj.com/articles/if-currency-reserves-arent-really-money-the-world-is-in-for-a-shock-11646311306)“.

Although they are often used interchangeably, one way to differentiate between “currency” and “money” is that currency is an asset for you that is a liability of some other entity (such as a commercial bank or central bank or government treasury), while money is an asset for you that is not anyone else’s liability (such as gold historically, or potentially bitcoin). Equities, debts, and currencies are all liabilities.

When we further realize that we are in the later stages of a long-term debt cycle, where there is too much debt in the system for most developed countries to maintain positive inflation-adjusted interest rates on their currencies and sovereign debt, these currency/bond fiat assets become even less attractive as sovereign reserves, because they lose purchasing power over time in addition to being freezable. That leaves basically gold as the only asset currently big enough to be a true sovereign reserve asset, and bitcoin as a smaller, more volatile, up-and-coming contender.

Basically, countries hold the bulk of their international reserves (decades of accumulated trade surpluses) in foreign fiat assets (currencies and bonds) that 1) devalue over time and have negative real yields now and 2) can be frozen. A number of countries, particularly those that are not very fond of the West, are likely re-evaluating some of their reserve practices at the moment.

Gold has a decent fundamental and technical setup here. It looks like a cup-and-handle pattern that could roll up to new highs:

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-6-gold-chart.png)

I’ve been looking at gold favorably again for the past couple months and reporting on it. Gold tends to do better than stocks when economic growth is decelerating (e.g. falling purchasing managers indices). The war and the widespread realization among sovereigns that most countries’ reserves are entirely their own reserves, likely gives it an added kicker. It’ll probably have periodic corrections after becoming periodically overbought, but I like it here for the next year or two overall.

**Bitcoin: Contender Sovereign Asset**

Bitcoin is a distributed ledger with a supply cap, and more decentralization and security than any other blockchain. It’s only thirteen years old, but it has been a promising thirteen years, despite multiple challenges to it.

![Bitcoin Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-6-btcusd.png)

It’s still too small and volatile for most sovereign entities to want to hold it, but it can serve well as “people’s money”. Ukrainians facing ATM freezes and such? Having self-custodied bitcoin or gold helps with that (and Ukrainians are ranked #4 on the crypto adoption index, meaning they have a very large per-capita holding of bitcoins and crypto compared to other countries). Russians facing a tanking currency and stock market? Well, gold and bitcoin are both at all-time highs in ruble terms, so people holding them have held up well compared to most of their peers (and Russia is #19 on the crypto adoption index). People living in any of the dozens of countries (representing roughly a billion people) that are currently experiencing official double-digit inflation? Again, things like gold and bitcoin are an option for them to try to protect their savings, especially because buying foreign stock indices is not always as easy to do, and those assets can be confiscated/frozen by their governments in a currency crisis anyway. Not everyone can afford a house, which is the most common form of inflation protection.

Along with all of the advantages, one of the persistent challenges to bitcoin is that it’s not particularly private, and so individual addresses can be tracked and blacklisted in various ways. The surface way to block transactions is to prevent centralized exchanges from converting specific bitcoins back into fiat currency. The deeper way to block transactions is to convince or force 51% of bitcoin miners to censor certain entities directly on the blockchain.

The defense against those two censorship methods respectively are to 1) find vendors or exchanges or entities anywhere in the world that will accept bitcoin for goods or services or other assets (as it doesn’t necessarily need to be converted back into fiat to be spent) and 2) maintain bitcoin miners in one’s own jurisdiction to reduce the probability of 51% of miners coming under a specific multi-country alliance and blocking transactions from foreign adversaries.

Additionally, there are ways to make bitcoin transactions more private, but they are not perfect. There are other blockchains that are inherently more private, but they are less firmly auditable in terms of supply than bitcoin (which is the reason that bitcoin was not designed to be maximally private; everything is a trade-off). Much of the innovation on bitcoin happens in secondary layers. For example, Liquid is a federated sidechain that runs on bitcoin and has more privacy features and faster transactions.

Bitcoin continues to be an evolving asset that benefits from structural adoption but still faces a number of risks and significant volatility. I remain bullish on it with a multi-year view.

**Energy: Everyone Needs It**

The West has not yet cut off its commodity imports from Russia, because it critically needs them. However, many companies are avoiding the purchase Russian commodities for resale because they are not 100% sure of the legality of their product once they have it, and they don’t want to be stuck with products they can’t sell. In addition, Russia is moving to halt some of its commodity exports. It remains a fluid situation. Urals oil is trading at a massive discount to Brent oil.

Here is a chart of European natural gas prices. This is basically a “Weimar” chart:

![European Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-6-european-gas.png)

Energy prices at levels like this tend to trigger stagflationary recessions. Meanwhile, WTI crude is up to $125 per barrel.

If we look at global energy stocks, however, it doesn’t look like a war yet. They are collectively just around the top of their long-term bear market trading range:

This is because the market is assuming lower energy prices in the future. For example, December 2025 oil futures are only at $72/barrel. Energy stocks are priced not just on current oil prices, but long-term expectations of future prices. The market is completely treating this as a transitory spike in oil prices.

And maybe it is transitory… for a while. I don’t know what oil prices will do in any six-month timeframe. Will oil peak at $$125, cause a global recession, comes back down to $65 as demand is destroyed, and then grind its way back up to $175 by 2028? Maybe. What if oil instead spikes to $200+, and then comes back down to $100, and then grind up to $300+ by 2028? Who knows? There are some serious potential shortages out there.

I’m inclined to fade short-term extreme price spikes, but take the bullish side of long-term prices. In other words, I was [calling for structurally higher energy prices](https://www.lynalden.com/oil-and-gas/) for this decade over the past couple years prior to any wars breaking out, simply from supply shortages alone. Europe in particular was already having a severe energy shortage during the second half of 2021. War just adds fuel to the fire; it makes the 2020s/1940s macro comparison even more blatant. I continue to like energy producers and energy transporters here, with a 5+ year holding period.

Analysts are fading forward earnings from oil companies, and so stocks like Suncor (SU) are trading at less than 10x forward earnings:

A world that focuses more on LNG and less on pipelines theoretically has a smaller price gap between regional natural gas prices, similar to how oil has a relatively small price gap for most places around the world.

Their market is crashing from valuation levels that were already cheap, so for those who want some risk, there’s your potentially oversold play:

Poland, which is classified as an emerging market, sends out about 3% of its exports to Russia and 2% to Ukraine, and gets about 5% of its imports from Russia and 1% from Ukraine. So, that’s not a huge deal. However as part of its imports, Poland specifically gets about half of its natural gas from Russia, like many European countries do. That specific part is a huge deal, if things get ugly.

I don’t think this is the time to buy the Polish market *yet*, but it’s on my close watch list. That’s one of the cleanest war plays out there, in my view. Or perhaps more appropriately, a peace play- an investment for when things are showing signs of potentially having bottomed in terms of fundamentals and technicals, with war de-escalating.

Overall, I continue to overweight value-oriented and commodity-oriented companies, while still maintaining growth exposure to some degree in targeted areas or companies.

## Final Thoughts

Some of the best investments that someone can make are outside of their portfolio. They can invest in their personal lives, their family, and their community.

Maintaining a healthy stockpile of everyday goods might be among the best-performing risk-adjusted investments during the 2020s decade. Just like how people should have a liquid emergency fund of cash, people should have an emergency fund of supplies as well.

That doesn’t mean going full-on “prepper” but it means being responsible and not relying on an extraordinarily complex society functioning 100% smoothly all of the time. To do so would be naïve. That’s especially true during this decade, where war, global supply chain problems, and cyberattacks can impact us when we least expect it, even if only indirectly.

Many of us have parents or grandparents that went through hard times and were prone to saving and stockpiling for the rest of their lives due to those experiences, whereas many of us have had it easy for our whole lives and assume that whatever we need will be available. We can go to a store and have an endless array of options ready to buy, with shortages being very rare. But as the pandemic lockdown period of 2020 showed, things like paper towels and toilet paper can disappear from store shelves quickly. That type of scenario could easily get worse, and involve food and energy more. And then maybe it gets better, and then worse again. These things tend to be nonlinear.

Make sure that your pantry has a decent stockpile of nonperishable foods, along with water, paper products, bath products, first aid, and anything else you envision needing. If you have the space for it, these assets are the best version of “cash” around- they’re inflation-adjusting goods that you can consume over time and that are broadly valuable to other people as well. A few thousand dollars can buy your family a big reserve.

Have some paper cash on hand, along with harder self-custodial monetary goods ideally. Keep your car with a decent amount of gasoline most of the time, rather than letting it run down to 1/16th of a tank before filing it up. Consider having a backup generator for your home. At the very last, have some small $25 phone/laptop chargers that can store several phone batteries worth of electricity, and maintain their charge for months if unused. I love them for traveling with anyway, let alone as a backup source of electricity domestically.

The supply chain system is not built for everyone deciding to hoard at once, and not based on the possibility of disruptions occurring ever, which is the problem. The global system is very fragile, based on lean manufacturing and just-in-time delivery, with low inventories to maximize returns on invested capital. It assumes persistent global harmony and lack of war or other severe geopolitical problems.

To counteract this, it’s good practice for households to be mild hoarders all the time, meaning that they have a reasonable stockpile at their home for things they need, rather than rushing out all at the same time to buy things and finding the shelves empty. Pretend you’re a family in the 1940s. It couldn’t hurt.

In a good scenario, this practice of reasonable stockpiling could end up being unimportant, and yet still would likely be a better investment than cash when considering inflation levels and interest rates over the next several years. Are paper towels and sardine cans going to get cheaper? Probably not, right? So it’s probably better than cash in a bank yielding near-zero. In a bad scenario, this practice could be critical, keeping your family safe and comfortable during challenging periods.

In the meantime, my thoughts go out to any readers who have been directly impacted by these series of events. I know I have some Ukrainian and Russian subscribers, so hopefully you are safe.
