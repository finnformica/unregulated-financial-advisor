## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity reached a cycle low around tax season in mid-April, but has since bounced back up a bit:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-domestic-liquidity.png)

The three components of this are the Fed’s balance sheet (up means more liquidity), reverse repos (down means more liquidity) and the Treasury general account (down means more liquidity).

-The Fed’s balance sheet continues to go down, which is negative for liquidity, although they will be slowing the pace of balance sheet reduction in June.

-The reverse repo facility has been flat, which is neutral for liquidity.

-The Treasury general account hit a cycle peak of $941 billion during tax season, but has since drained back down t0 $725 billion, which put some liquidity back into the financial system and is in line with the Treasury Department’s long-term expected level for that account.

We can also look at the ratio of banks’ cash to total assets:

![Bank Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bank-liquidity.png)

Before the global financial crisis, banks were extremely levered and had low liquidity. Various fiscal and monetary policy measures re-capitalized and re-liquified them during and after the crisis, and set regulations for them to maintain higher capital and liquidity levels. Whenever the Fed does quantitative tightening for a while and reduces system-wide liquidity, it eventually stops when they hit a liquidity problem in banks or in Treasury securities.

In September 2019, the repo market between big banks had a liquidity problem (and repos were used as a funding source for pools of capital to buy Treasuries), so the Fed stepped in with emergency repo support and then a few weeks later went back to structural balance sheet growth via purchasing T-bills. This repo spike mainly related to big banks and is circled in the chart above.

In March 2023, several regional banks were underwater on their Treasury/agency securities positions, and liquidity was low again, so the Fed and the Treasury teamed up to offer the Bank Term Funding Program. This is also circled in the chart above, and is more related to regional/community banks rather than the big money-center banks. Various pools of liquidity drained back into the banking system (Treasury general account briefly, and then reverse repos more permanently), and bank liquidity has remained higher than that level ever since.

I expect the rangebound liquidity situation to continue for several more months. But if the Treasury shifts to a higher ratio of T-bill issuance again, as they did in Q4 2023, then that could pull more liquidity out of reverse repos and back into the system.

In 2025 there will be two significant catalysts for liquidity to watch. The first is that the New York Fed is projecting their balance sheet decline to end and start moving back up that year. The second is that the debt ceiling limit, which is currently suspended, gets reinstated in January 2025. Down the line this potentially leads to another debt ceiling battle. During debt ceiling battles, if it gets gridlocked long enough, the Treasury is forced to drain some or all of their cash balance into the financial system to keep paying their bills without issuing new debt.

**Global Liquidity Indicators**

Global liquidity continues to be in a two-year rangebound state. This dollar-denominated measure of global broad money supply is mainly determined from the combination of 1) how much money supply growth is happening globally and 2) how strong the dollar is relative to the other major currencies:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-global-m2.png)

This metric hit a cycle low in late 2022, as the dollar index spiked. That’s when overall U.S. domestic liquidity switched from going down to going sideways (reverse repos and the Treasury general account began going down, offsetting the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet reduction). I expect global liquidity to break out to the upside in 2025, although I continue to watch it for a potential earlier break-out here in 2024 due to the dollar index topping and rolling over earlier.

The dollar index is determined by a number of factors, including the strength of the U.S. economy, measures of U.S. inflation, and the market’s anticipation of interest rate cuts, holds, or hikes based on that combination of economic strength and inflation. I’m neutral about the dollar index with low conviction in either direction for the remainder of 2024. But when we get to a point where the Fed’s balance sheet starts going up again in or around 2025, I’d lean bearish on the dollar depending on what else is happening at that time.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

The Conference Board leading economic index was in recession territory for much of the past two years, but has emerged from it over the past two months without an NBER-defined recession having occurred:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-lei-6mo.png)

If we look at their year-over-year index relative to real GDP, we see an interesting divergence as shown by the green box below. GDP remained positive even as all the leading indicators flashed recession:

![Leading Economic Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-lei-yoy.png)

Back in my [February 2024 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/february-2024-newsletter/), I charted the past 70 years of fiscal deficits vs the total net issuance of corporate bonds and bank loans. Historically, the cycle of bank lending and bond issuance was the biggest contributor to the ebbs and flows of the economic cycle, but in recent times the fiscal side has become more prominent. Yellow areas marked the periods where annual fiscal deficits were larger than the total annual net issuance of corporate bonds and banks loans. Gray vertical areas represent NBER-defined recessions. For this report, I’m showing the same charts again but I’ve also put a green square around the only two yellow areas that *were not* triggered by a recession:

![Fiscal vs Monetary Forces](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-fiscal-vs-monetary.png)

Staring around the year 2017, the U.S. entered an early stage of fiscal dominance. This was the first time since the Vietnam war that fiscal deficits as a percentage of GDP began *rising* despite the fact that the unemployment rate was still decreasing amid an economic expansion:

![Deficits vs Unemployment](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-deficits-unemployment.png)

It occurred because 1) the large baby boomer generation began to retire at scale and 2) tax cuts were passed without corresponding spending cuts in the middle of an economic expansion. I was watching this trend begin to occur back then, and it heavily influenced my macroeconomic research ever since.

During the 2018-2019 period, the Fed tried to hike interest rates and decrease its balance sheet, which contributed to decelerating economic growth and rising recession risk, along with an inverted yield curve. But by 2019, after a few years of this early-stage fiscal dominance, the Fed had to start increasing their balance sheet again due to an acute liquidity problem in the repo market. At that time, the Fed went back to loosening its balance sheet to accommodate fiscal deficits and maintain liquidity in the banking system, and it started to look like the U.S. economy might begin to re-accelerate.

We’ll never know what would have happened after that in a normal year, because then by early 2020 China began doing pandemic lockdowns, and by spring 2020 the western world began doing the same. Just prior to that economic shock, the economy was weak but showing initial signs of fiscal-driven and liquidity-driven recovery.

Throughout 2022, economic indicators were showing recession risk again as the Fed and other central banks aggressively tightened monetary policy, with almost every measure of economic growth decelerating, and the yield curve inverting. But ever since late 2022 and early 2023, as various issues occurred (UK gilt market blow-up in late 2022, U.S. banking crisis in early 2023), the Fed and Treasury took turns re-introducing liquidity into the system. Higher interest rates translated to much larger U.S. fiscal deficits, and various economic indicators have shown signs of bottoming.

At this stage, the majority of economic indicators have shown a period of stabilization or even re-acceleration. It remains to be seen how robust this re-acceleration will be, but we’re pretty firmly into fiscal dominance territory, which is stimulating for parts of the economy that are directly or indirectly on the receiving side of those large fiscal deficits.

Neither the Fed nor the Treasury are letting system-wide liquidity go to problematic levels that threaten banks or the functioning of the Treasury market, at a time when these fiscal deficits continue to pour into the economy, and so they are rather constrained on their ability to fight inflation.

## Digital Assets Note

**Shifting U.S. Politics and Potential Ethereum ETFs**

Since 2022, the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission has had a staff accounting bulletin (“[SAB 121](https://www.sec.gov/oca/staff-accounting-bulletin-121)“) that effectively prevents banks from serving as a custodian for crypto assets.

However, a little over a week ago, both the U.S. House and the U.S. Senate comfortably [passed legislation](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/legal/senate-passes-bill-to-overturn-sec-rule-on-bitcoin-and-crypto-custody-biden-threatens-veto) to overturn SAB 121. It was somewhat bipartisan in nature, with the majority of Republicans and a significant minority of Democrats voting for it, notably including Senate Majority Leader Chuck Schumer.

Before the vote, the Biden Administration issued a statement that they would veto it if passed. They have about a week to do this, and it’s unclear if the bigger-than-expected margin of passing it will change that decision to veto it or not.

Additionally, the House passed a bill called Fit21, which would reshape regulation around crypto assets more in favor of the CTFC than the SEC, and shift securities laws away from the Howey Test. Although it was Republican-led, this bill also had significant bipartisan support, including from minority leader Nancy Pelosi and 70 other congress people of the Democratic party. Gary Gensler, the chair of the SEC, issued a [response](https://www.sec.gov/news/statement/gensler-21st-century-act-05222024) against the passage of Fit21. It hasn’t gone through the Senate yet.

Regardless of the specific outcomes here in the intermediate term, the anti-crypto wing of the Democratic party, led by Elizabeth Warren, is now on the backfoot. There is a lot of political funding coming from the crypto asset industry, and it’s not really seen as a winning strategy anymore to be explicitly anti-crypto. In recent weeks, Donald Trump has been courted by well-capitalized individuals and organizations within the cryptocurrency industry and has explicitly endorsed bitcoin and crypto assets, which is a departure from his position during his term as President. In a close election, it’s becoming part of his political strategy.

The ETHBTC ratio hit new cycle lows in recent weeks, but then the SEC approved a rule change that will likely allow for (non-staking) Ethereum ETFs to be issued in the coming weeks or months. While the SEC still has to approve individual ETFs, there is now a likely path toward the creation of Ethereum ETFs. Just days prior to the announcement, most ETF experts including those at Bloomberg who closely monitor this field placed only a 25% chance on its approval, but in the aftermath of the House and Senate votes regarding SAB 121 and Fit21, along with various industry rumors, experts’ odds suddenly jumped to 75%, and then it was confirmed with the SEC’s announcement days later.

**The Government of Kenya Gets into Bitcoin Mining**

This past Friday, the largest publicly-traded bitcoin miner [announced a deal](https://ir.mara.com/news-events/press-releases/detail/1358/marathon-digital-holdings-enters-into-agreement-with-the) with the government of Kenya to partner with the country and build out energy infrastructure.

> Fort Lauderdale, FL, May 24, 2024 (GLOBE NEWSWIRE) — Marathon Digital Holdings, Inc. (NASDAQ:MARA) (“Marathon” or “Company”), a global leader in leveraging digital asset compute to support the energy transformation, has entered into an agreement with the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum of the Republic of Kenya (“MOEP”) to support the utilization of energy and to optimize renewable energy projects across the Republic of Kenya. The partnership underscores Marathon’s commitment to supporting the sustainable growth of the energy sector and is part of the Company’s broader strategy to diversify its operations globally.
>

A Joint Steering Committee will be established that will be staffed by both MOEP and Marathon senior officials to ensure the development and implementation of these energy related projects across the country. With projected foreign investments expected to exceed $80 million, this venture is poised to deliver economic benefits to the Kenyan economy and generate revenue for the local energy sector ecosystem.
>
> “This agreement with the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum is a pivotal moment for our business as it provides us with a clear framework to pursue opportunities across the Republic of Kenya,” said Fred Thiel, Marathon’s chairman and CEO. “It demonstrates the innovative approach that Kenya is taking to optimize their energy usage and to enhance their technological infrastructure. We look forward to working closely with the Ministry of Energy and Petroleum, President William Ruto, and other Kenyan government leaders to drive progress through innovative and sustainable energy solutions.”
>
> Manoj Narender Madnani, Marathon’s managing director of the EMEA region, commented, “While Marathon has already successfully established operations in the UAE and Paraguay, this agreement marks the first direct collaboration with a government to enhance their energy infrastructure. It is a testament to the value our solutions provide and is an integral step to Marathon scaling its international business over the coming years. We look forward to working with the Kenyan leadership and any other entities that are looking for innovative and effective ways to enhance their energy infrastructure.”

I’ve spoken at length with Marathon’s CEO Fred Thiel at a number of events over the past three years, and I consistently come away impressed with his approach to things. And indeed the company has navigated this past cycle more aptly than a lot of other miners have.

But lets back up for a moment, and see why the government of Kenya would be interested in this kind of partnership. Throughout the world at any given time, there are many terawatts of wasted energy.

Sometimes energy is wasted because a source of power generation was built, such as a geothermal facility, but it takes more years and capital to build the surrounding transmission and distribution infrastructure to actually make use of it. Sometimes it’s because a large hydroelectric dam or nuclear facility produces a lot more electricity than their surrounding region can consume for years or decades to come. Sometimes it’s because variable sources of power like solar panels and wind turbines generate extra electricity during random parts of the day that nobody is in a position to use in real-time, and grid-scale batteries to store that surplus and use it later are very expensive and materially-intensive. Sometimes it’s because small oil wells come with associated natural gas that is insufficient to warrant a pipeline to take it away, and so it is just vented or flared into the atmosphere. Sometimes it’s because landfills continuously emit methane into the atmosphere and there is insufficient demand around the landfill to build electrical generation and distribution infrastructure to get that energy into the power grid.

There are some things that a dense amount of stranded energy can be used for. For example, the country of Iceland generates a lot of excess geothermal energy right near blue-water harbors, and so they use it for the electricity-intensive process of aluminum smelting, and then they export the aluminum via those harbors. It’s a way to monetize and export their energy surplus that would otherwise be wasted, but it requires very specific circumstances.

Most ways to use excess energy, such as aluminum smelting, hydrolysis, or AI datacenters, have specific needs like this. For example, they are capital intensive and therefore require high uptime in order to make economic sense. For smelting or hydrolysis, they also generally need to be near a shippable body of water or other cost-effective way to transport their product. For AI datacenters, they generally need to be in a high-bandwidth area, and pretty close to the end user, since either high latency or low bandwidth would make it uncompetitive.

When stranded energy exists in a lot of little places, or it has high variance of uptime, or it’s in a remote low-bandwidth area, those other uses can’t really go make use of it, but bitcoin miners are indeed able to go make use of it. A container of inexpensive prior-generation bitcoin miners can be put next to a small oil well, a hydroelectric dam, a remote set of wind turbines, a geothermal site, or similar sources of surplus/stranded energy, and make use of the wasted energy that those resources provide. For a developing country, profitably soaking up that lost revenue can be a big deal financially, and helps make their energy infrastructure a lot more cost efficient.

Alex Gladstein, the Chief Strategy Officer of the Human Rights Foundation, has described it concisely: “If you are *not* mining bitcoin, you are wasting energy.”

**A Brief History of Bitcoin Price Modeling**

Over the years, there have been many projections for what bitcoin’s price could be by a certain date. They’re all fun in some ways, but investors should be careful in terms of actually relying on them for anything important.

The majority of them have a logarithmic vertical/price axis and a linear horizontal/time axis, and basically extrapolate the price action that has already occurred as determined by some simple or complex mathematical model.

One of the first ones was [posted back in 2014](https://bitcointalk.org/index.php?topic=831547.0) on the original Bitcoin Talk forums by a user named Trolololo:

![Trolololo Bitcoin Projection](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bitcoin-projection-in-2014.jpg)

It had a humorously high level accuracy for the next *six years* through 2020. The price was in the hundreds of dollars when this was posted in 2014, and bitcoin indeed soared to over $10,000 in 2017, then fell back down to a much higher-low, and then soared to several tens of thousands of dollars in 2020 as initially projected. Only in 2021 did the price start to fall short of the $100,000 projections by that point, as it peaked at $69,000 for the year. Since then, the price has continued to make higher-highs and higher-lows, but has been under the original red line.

In 2018, Timothy Peterson [wrote a paper](https://caia.org/sites/default/files/metcalfeslaw_websiteupload_7-5-18.pdf) about bitcoin’s price following Metcalfe’s law, which is a growth model that applies to network effects. Using this, one can select what they think is an appropriate forward growth assumption, like for example that bitcoin will have a similar user growth rate as the internet in the 1990s or smart phones in the 2010s or something more conservative, and then work out a future price trend based on the past price.

Peterson has posted numerous versions of this over the years, but here’s one of his from 2020:

![Bitcoin Metcalfe Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bitcoin-metcalfe-value.png)

Bitcoin kept up with it for a few years, but started to undershoot it in 2023, and since then has remained under the model. But overall, I think Metcalfe’s law was (and still is) a good way to think about it conceptually. The hard part is choosing the right assumption for how many users it will have by a certain date in the future.

Following this, the most popular bitcoin price model ever, by an order of magnitude, was PlanB’s stock-to-flow model. He proposed it in 2019, was very active on blogs, Twitter, and podcasts for the next few years, and during the bull market in 2021 he became extremely well-followed. That was the year where he gathered most of his 1.9+ million Twitter followers.

In April 2020 I [first recommended bitcoin](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-4-12/) as an allocation in my research service at $6,900/coin, and then in July 2020 I wrote a [public article](https://www.lynalden.com/invest-in-bitcoin/) making the case for it at a little over $9,000. In that public article, I referenced some of PlanB’s charts since they were useful, but I warned against relying on their accuracy and described a more conservative price projection for that cycle as my base case:

That’s more bullish than my base case but it’s nonetheless a useful model to see what happened in the past.

On the other hand, we can’t rule out the bullish moonshot case if demand grows sharply and/or if some global macro currency event adds another catalyst.

Mainly what I warned about was that his model was based entirely on supply, and didn’t account for changes in demand, which is the hard part to predict for all of these models. Bitcoin peaked at $69,000 for the cycle in late 2021 and then bottomed at around $16,000 in late 2022, which undershot PlanB’s model by a significant margin. He subsequently lost a lot of popularity within the industry, partly because he was so publicly sure it would hit the target, and so many people assumed that it would.

The other models mentioned here all had a logarithmic vertical/price axis and a linear horizontal/time axis, but the Power Law projection is generally displayed with a logarithmic vertical/price and a horizontal/time axis. The underlying logic of a power law adoption is similar to Metcalfe’s law about network effect adoption, in the sense that the prior output becomes the input for the next calculation.

While the overall power law projection for the remainder of the 2020s decade is more conservative than the others and is basically in line with my general expectation (and with an enormous built-in allowance for variance), I would once again caution against overly rigid price expectations.

**Rough Guidelines for Price Expectations**

All price expectations, including any base case I have in my mind for a given period, are only estimations. And here is basically how I arrive at them.

First, I start with the fundamentals. Having analyzed the Bitcoin protocol and the competitive field for a long time, my conclusion is that the Bitcoin protocol is and will likely remain the dominant protocol of digital value in a telecommunication-connected world. It’s the world’s most decentralized and immutable database, serves as a great ledger for a finite number of self-custodial and portable monetary units along with a means to transfer them globally, and it has achieved critical mass of security and liquidity. Similar to how Ethernet, TCP/IP, SMTP, USB, and other network protocols reached critical mass against early competition and then became entrenched self-reinforced foundations, Bitcoin is showing similar characteristics.

Second, I look for risks to that thesis. I analyze the competitive field for various other technical solutions for the same use case, and I also analyze risks that could fundamentally impair the functioning of the Bitcoin protocol. If any of these risks significantly materialize, the value proposition of the Bitcoin protocol would be impaired, and price is more likely to structurally undershoot any bullish projection.

Third, I estimate the total addressable market in the long arc of time. What percentage of the world’s assets would want to be in the leading liquid, portable, self-custodial, secure supply-capped monetary asset? For starters, there’s not a great reason to hold any of the world’s 160 fiat currencies and short-duration money-like instruments beyond what one needs for personal or business working capital and portfolio volatility-smoothing. Additionally, there’s not a great reason to hold meaningful amounts of long duration bonds of sovereigns with over 100% debt-to-GDP that have yields lower than the supply growth rate of their currencies, other than maybe briefly for a trade. And then from there, bitcoin can compete for some small percentage of the monetary premium that is built on top of equity value, real estate value, gold, art, etc. In other words, if I’m looking to buy more stocks or a rental property or gold or art, buying bitcoin is now a competitor for a slice in my set of choices as I allocate that pie. And as [Jesse Myers](https://x.com/croesus_btc) charts, that’s a very big pie:

![Total Asset Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-total-asset-value.jpg)

Fourth, I monitor market cycles. Large asset prices have a limit for how much they can go up in a short span of time, because as the price goes up, eventually people get manic about it and over-leverage it, and then some sort of negative catalyst or stagnating momentum wipes that manic sentiment and leverage out over the course of a couple years. That’s why bitcoin’s price routinely overshoots and undershoots all of its models.

Ever since it became sufficiently large and liquid, bitcoin’s price tends to go up during periods of rising global liquidity, and tends to struggle during periods of flat or falling global liquidity. It’s the most correlated asset that I’m aware of to the USD-denominated global broad money supply:

![Bitcoin vs Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bitcoin-vs-global-m2.png)

Bitcoin’s market value to realized value looks at the current market capitalization to its average on-chain cost basis. Caution is warranted whenever it reaches unusually high multiples, although right now it’s still moderate:

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

Bitcoin’s HODL wave looks at the percentage of supply that hasn’t moved on-chain in over a year. When bitcoin soars in a bull market, many long-term holders have life-changing wealth and become highly concentrated in bitcoin, and so some of them trim part of their exposure to rebalance into other assets or to spend on consumption/family expenses. This deluge of existing supply coming to market eventually exhausts the new manic period of demand. Right now there’s been some distribution, but not to the extent that is historically associated with a major cyclical top:

![Bitcoin HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bitcoin-hodl-wave.png)

Bitcoin’s Energy Gravity, formulated by Joe Burnett in 2022, is a model (among similar models) that compares the price of bitcoin to the cost to mine bitcoin. Whenever the disconnect becomes very big, there is plenty of incentive for large pools of capital to build out new mining capacity rather than to buy bitcoin on the market. Right now it’s back to being near the low end of the range:

![Bitcoin Energy Gravity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-bitcoin-energy-gravity.png)

Basically, there are a variety of cyclical patterns based on global liquidity, sentiment, leverage, and owner rotation, which collectively can drive bitcoin’s price to the high end or the low end of its typical growth range, and can set up bull market or bear market conditions for a couple years at a time.

Overall, I currently continue to view bitcoin as fundamentally strong for the long term, and view it as not being particularly overheated according to various cyclical measures in the intermediate term (although no longer at the rock bottom of its typical cyclicality either).

For those that want exposure, I generally recommend [dollar-cost averaging into it](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) over time, preferably on a platform where you have the option to withdraw and take custody of your own coins one day should you ever want to do so.

## Komatsu Deep Dive Analysis

In a number of reports now, I’ve been pointing to the potential bottom in Latin American equities, Chinese equities, and overall emerging market equities relative to U.S. equities on a total return basis that has formed since 2022 or 2023 depending on the market in question.

This trend needs monitoring, but overall with the Fed having raised rates significantly and now paused for ten months, the worst of the damage to the large emerging market economies might be behind us.

Jurrien Timmer, the Director of Global Macro at Fidelity, recently posted a set of charts that furthers the case for emerging markets and commodities being in the early phase of an up-cycle.

![Timmer Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-timmer-tweets.png)

The obvious way to play this, to the extent that an investor believes that the trend has legs for the next 18-24 months, is by owning the emerging market equities.

Another way to play this is by owning equities in developed markets that tend to strongly benefit from this trend. Komatsu stock in Japan is a quintessential example:

![Komatsu vs EEM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-eem.png)

In a number of premium reports during the period from November 2020 through February 2021, I covered the Soga Sosha (a collection of Japanese industrial/commodities trading conglomerates) and Hitachi (a Japanese industrial conglomerate). They had been trading in a sideways range for a *long* time, but for a variety of fundamental reasons I began to find them interesting.

Basically, they are industrial conglomerates with a diverse array of good businesses and hard assets, along with some low interest rate yen-denominated long-term debt attached to them, which is basically a yen short. And they were trading at cheap valuations.

They’ve greatly outperformed since then in dollar terms, relative to both the American stock market and the Japanese stock market:

![Sogo Shosha](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-sogo-shosha-hitachi-nov2020.png)

Komatsu is another Japanese company that has been rangebound. It hasn’t yet exploded upward like the others have, and is starting to get interesting in my view. I don’t necessarily expect fireworks anytime soon, but accumulating a small position while the dollar index and U.S. interest rates are this high, makes for an interesting long-term play.

**Komatsu Overview**

Founded in 1921, Komatsu limited  (TYO: 6301, OTC: KMTUY) is the world’s second largest producer of construction and mining equipment after Caterpillar. Their product lines include bulldozers, excavators, dump trucks, forklifts, harvesters, and similar vehicles. Many are powered by internal combustion engines, and others are powered by electric motors, which they’ve been leaning more into in recent years.

The liquid version of the stock trades on the Tokyo Stock Exchange with symbol 6301, which means one needs a global brokerage account to access it unless one is based in Japan. For those that want to buy the ADR version with a small position size, there is KMTUY which trades over-the-counter but has much less daily trading liquidity.

Japan continues to operate a world-leading industrial base in general, both in terms of manufacturing capacity and in terms of human capital, and Komatsu is part of that overall aspect. Their products are served worldwide, and in many cases are made worldwide as well.

Their year-on-year results have been great, with good top-line and bottom-line growth:

![Komatsu Results](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-results.png)

*Source: [Komatsu FY2023 Results (ended March 31st, 2024)](https://www.komatsu.jp/en/ir/library/financial)*

Less than 10% of their equipment sales are in Japan. The other 90%+ are to the rest of the world:

![Komatsu Regions](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-regions.png)

*Source: [Komatsu FY2023 Results (ended March 31st, 2024)](https://www.komatsu.jp/en/ir/library/financial)*

Emerging market companies, as well as Komatsu that tracks them rather closely, tend to go through both cyclical and structural growth periods over time. And they’re largely tied to the cycles in the dollar index, since the dollar is the primary unit of external debt for emerging markets throughout the world.

During the 1970s as the dollar fell rapidly, emerging markets experienced a boom in economic growth and asset prices. Global trade and commodities were generally priced in dollars so they went up in price, and various dollar-denominated debts were devalued against other currencies and especially against harder assets, which led to a surge in global liquidity, investment, and growth.

But in the 1980s as the Fed rapidly tightened financial conditions to rein inflation back in, the dollar index spiked and contributed to a period of long globally weak liquidity, and subsequently stagnation. Most commodities, gold, emerging markets in aggregate, and Komatsu sales and profits went sideways for the 1980s and 1990s.

For those that read my [May 2024 public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2024-newsletter/) from last week, the 1980s and 1990s were the golden age of the U.S. bond market, where the U.S. debt-to-GDP ratio was low, and nominal interest rates were higher than nominal GDP growth. The high real interest rates and the overall strength of the dollar squeezed everything globally:

![Bonds vs GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/may-2024-newsletter-bonds-vs-gdp.png)

After the dotcom-bust, the U.S. economy weakened and the Fed greatly loosened monetary policy in 2001, resulting in a much weaker dollar index. Commodity prices rose significantly, dollar-denominated debts across the world were devalued, and emerging markets had an enormous economic boom from 2003 to 2007.

After that, as commodity inflation ran hot, U.S. monetary policymakers began tightening again. The Global Financial Crisis happened in 2008, and the dollar index entered another strong cycle that persists to the present day. Emerging markets, commodities, and profits of Japanese industrial companies like the Sogo Shosha and Komatsu trended sideways in terms of fundamentals and price for a long time.

![Komatsu Long-Term](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-longterm.png)

*Source: Komatsu 2023 Annual Report*

In my view, the past decade and a half has been a long consolidation period for a number of correlated areas, including emerging markets, precious metals, energy producers, commodity producers, Japanese industrial companies, the overall global capex cycle, and so forth. I expect them to begin gradually breaking out of that long-run range toward the next major range.

The ultra-long term oil price chart helps to illustrate this. The 1940s, 1970s, and 2000s represented major dollar debasement cycles, with structurally higher oil prices and commodity prices for the next couple of decades:

![Oil Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-oil-price.png)

*Data Source: EIA*

Although market structure varied over that time period, a lot of these assets that explode higher in price and performance when the dollar is debased tend to correlate along those major cycles. When global dollar liquidity eases in a big way, there is a big cycle of construction and capital expenditure in developing areas, everything eventually gets overdone in terms of leverage, inflation, valuations, and sentiment, and then there is a long period of tighter liquidity that puts it all back into a plateau for a while.

My base case continues to be that the year 2020 marked the structural bottom for the cycle. I don’t claim to know when the next major leg up will be, since that is dependent on all sorts of factors. But generally it’s best to position in these types of assets after signs of capitulation of the prior bear market trading range. A lot of these types of stocks are cheap, have good balance sheets, and pay you to own them in the meantime.

If I had to speculate on a catalyst for the next cycle up, it would be the next Fed easing cycle, either in terms of lower interest rates or in terms of higher liquidity. Due to reasons relating to market structure, liquidity requirements, and the Treasury security supply/demand situation, the Fed will likely reach a bottom in their balance sheet by 2025 and go back to gradual balance sheet growth after that point. The New York Fed is projecting that as well:

![Projected SOMA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma-chart24.png)

Even before that point, global liquidity probably bottomed in September/October of 2022, and that bottom was reinforced with the March 2023 banking crisis. We are now somewhat rangebound, until the next easing cycle.

As we go through that period in the years ahead, my base case expectation is that it’ll be a decent period for emerging markets, global construction activity, precious metals, stocks like Komatsu, and basically anything that benefits from higher levels of global dollar liquidity. Komatsu represents an indirect way to play it, but one that is arguably more safe than owning emerging markets equities that come with geopolitical asset-seizure tail risk.

**Komatsu Valuations**

Komatsu is currently inexpensive relative to its fundamentals, with a price/earnings ratio in the low double-digits:

![Komatsu FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

The cyclically-adjusted price/earnings ratio is way cheaper than their American competitor Caterpillar, which has not always been the case:

![Komatsu vs Caterpillar](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-caterpillar-pe5.png)

Komatsu management has remarked on this valuation divide and is aware of it. Their price/book ratio is also pretty low:

![Komatsu Price/Book Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-price-book.png)

Komatsu has a strong balance sheet, rated “A” by S&P. That’s the same credit rating that Caterpillar has.

Specifically, Komatsu has cash and short-term debt that approximately net out, and 618 billion yen in long-term debt, which is equivalent to about $4 billion USD. Their debt/income ratio is below 2x, which is healthy and a bit lower than Caterpillar’s ratio, and they have plenty of liquidity. Their long-term debt is basically an appropriately-sized long-term short on the yen, which is good to have in an era of financial repression.

Komatsu paid out 167 yen worth of dividends for the 2023 fiscal year (ended March 2024), which is a 3.6% dividend yield relative to their current price. That’s higher than the F.A.S.T. Graph above shows, because sometimes F.A.S.T. Graphs doesn’t correctly calculate dividends for foreign companies. Komatsu’s management projects another 167 yen for the current fiscal year. They generally aim to pay out a 40% dividend payout ratio.

![Komatsu Dividends Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-komatsu-dividends-annotated.png)

In their April 26th report, which covers up to the end of March 31st, they announced a share repurchase plan that will be active from the start of May 2024 through the end of September 2024, with an authorization to buy up to 3.5% of shares outstanding.

While it remains to be seen how much they will actually repurchase during this period (it’ll depend on market conditions), I think it is a good use of spare capital given their low equity valuations.

The current CEO has been in his position since 2019, had to navigate through the pandemic and lockdown economic issues in the subsequent years, and is strongly focused on 1) reinvesting into the company for future growth and 2) good shareholder returns. I think he has displayed a strong commitment to both in his tenure so far.

**Komatsu Risks**

In my view, the biggest risk to Komatsu is that it might remain dead money for several more years. It’s inexpensive, profitable, and doing well financially, but its fundamentals are likely stuck in a gradually growing trading range until there is more of a boom in emerging markets again. I think we’re seeing early signs of that, but I can’t be sure, and there is a lot that can happen over the next couple of years.

My prior investment into Sogo Shosha companies (Itochu, Mitsui, Sumitomo, and Mitsubishi) paid off more quickly than I had expected, but there’s no guarantee that the Komatsu investment will do the same. In fact, my Hitachi investment was a bit of a laggard, as it spent three years chopping along sideways before exploding higher more recently. I wouldn’t be surprised if Komatsu does the same thing.

In addition, Komatsu faces competitive pressure from Caterpillar, as well as from Chinese producers like LiuGong. The weakening yen should be decent for Komatsu and improve their competitive position globally, but a similar argument can be said for LiuGong and other Chinese producers. During the next global capex/construction wave over the next decade or so, Chinese equipment will have a notable market share.

## Final Thoughts: The Fed’s Next Cut

A lot of financial media is focused on when will the Fed cut interest rates next. This is pretty important for asset prices in general.

However, what I’ve not seen a lot of people discuss, is how *underwhelming* the next interest rate cut is likely to be on U.S. home refinancing volumes and thus consumer spending.

For four decades from 1981 to 2021, interest rates in the United States experienced a series of lower-highs and lower-lows, including the important 30-year mortgage rate.

![Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-mortgage-rates.png)

The mortgage refinancing cycle is a big deal, because with each meaningfully lower-low, people can reduce their monthly housing costs and therefore free up more money for spending on other areas, which contributes to economic growth. Here’s a chart that shows the burst of mortgage refinancing that occurs with each lower-low on the 30-year mortgage rate:

![Mortgage Refinancing](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-mortgage-refinancing.png)

However, I doubt we’re going to see a lower-low in the next cycle. The 2.65% 30-year mortgage rate in early 2021 might be the rate floor for a long time, with a series of higher-lows in the future.

If that’s the case, then the next period of lower mortgage rates is likely going to result in a lower volume of refinancing than prior cycles experienced, and thus will have less of a beneficial impact for the marginal consumer and the overall economy. Unless the mortgage rate drops to new lower-lows, there’s hardly anyone with a high mortgage that will refinance and free up extra income. The only people who would do so are those who locked in a mortgage during the 2022-2024 timeframe, but due to the high rates, that has been a pretty low number:

![Mortgage Originations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-26-mortgage-originations.png)

Similarly to how the U.S. economy was resilient against the Fed’s raising of interest rates this cycle, I think there’s a good chance that reducing interest rates will have a weak stimulatory effect in the next cycle. The U.S. economy is less interest rate sensitive in both directions.

Instead, the primary beneficiaries of the Fed’s cutting cycle will more likely be emerging markets. They have a lot of dollar-denominated debt, and so the high-rate strong-dollar environment is putting a lot of downward pressure on them. When that situation finally eases due to economic softness and rate cuts in the United States, it’s those regions that likely have the most to benefit, rather than the U.S. consumer economy.
