## U.S. Business Cycle Update

The U.S. and global economy have been in an indecisive period lately, with some parts re-opening, and other parts re-closing.

Beijing recently entered a partial lockdown. For months now, China was holding up relatively well, and had managed to localize most of the COVID-19 impact to the Wuhan area where it came from. However, a second wave of outbreaks recently emerged in Beijing, and the government responded with partial lockdowns there.

In addition, Apple announced that they are re-closing 11 of their stores in the southern United States. Some of those states are seeing a rise in COVID-19 cases, and overall, the United States never quite hammered down the spread of COVID-19 the way that much of Asia and Europe did, despite attempting lockdowns that were roughly as strict as Europe and more strict than many countries in Asia (ex-China).

For example, here is the daily new case count in the United States and Germany:

![Daily New Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-new-cases.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

Places like Singapore, South Korea, Japan, Italy, Spain, and the UK, look more like Germany’s chart. China’s chart looks like that as well, although their data is more questionable.

Russia and some other nations look more like the United States’ chart.

And then some of the later-hit regions, particularly in emerging markets like Brazil and India, are still on a solid uptrend of cases. Sweden’s chart falls more into this uptrend category as well, since they made the decision not to lock down their economy to the extent that their neighboring countries did.

What to do about the virus has become a hot political issue. The death rate is less bad than many experts initially feared, especially for people without pre-existing conditions or without advanced age, but various jurisdictions still want to keep the infection rate manageable enough to avoid overwhelming the hospital systems, as it started to do earlier this year in outbreak hotspots like Wuhan, Lombardy, and New York.

In the United States, it has become a particularly partisan issue with elections coming up in November.

Some Asian countries with experience from past pandemics, like South Korea and Japan, managed to mostly contain the virus with just partial lockdowns, and way less unemployment. Much of Europe managed to contain the virus with more complete lockdowns and have since opened up. Some countries like Sweden have been willing to avoid lockdowns and stick with some social distancing, and let the virus run its course despite the fact that it causes higher death rates than neighboring countries.

Proponents of the less-strict lockdown measures often point to percentages, like the fact that the virus killed just 0.05% of Sweden’s population so far, whereas proponents of stricter lockdown measures often point to per-capita death rates relative to neighboring countries, or the absolute number of deaths.

Overall, lockdown procedures have mixed success so far. There is some correlation between lockdowns and minimizing deaths per capita, specially when it protects the hospital system from being overwhelmed all at once by slowing down the spread, but it’s more about how effective people are at social distancing (with or without economic lockdowns), which Asian countries have past experience with and have been better at doing.

In the early stages of the crisis, governments didn’t know how damaging the virus would be, but now there is more data available about its fairly low fatality rate.

The United States has kind of experienced the worst of both paths, with less virus containment than most peer countries, but still a lot of economic damage from the rather extensive lockdowns. We have about 29 million recently jobless people in the United States at the moment that are projected to take years to recover, and although the spread of the virus stopped being exponential, it didn’t decline to very low levels like it did in much of Asia and Europe. So we’re in this gray zone with Apple re-closing some stores, news reports about an uptick in cases in various states, but the fatality rate continuing to diminish, and so forth.

In the United States and the rest of the world, death rates from the virus as a percentage of the population are low (currently under 0.04% for the United States), but it naturally continues to affect consumer behavior, business behavior, and government rules, and thus it affects financial markets.

With China as a partial exception, most governments seem to be past the point of enforced lockdowns, and now it’s more about self-separation and reasonable precautions to slow its spread, particularly to vulnerable members of society, while resuming economic activity wherever possible.

The world is likely going to play this behavioral dance with COVID-19 for some time, with consumers, businesses, and governments coping with social distancing, and monitoring periods of virus containment or upticks in case counts.

**The U.S. Current Unemployment Situation**

As the United States and other markets gradually re-open (and in rarer cases, re-close), the unemployment situation remains unclear.

Initial jobless claims continue to come in at 1.5 million per week, but other folks are heading back to work, so continued jobless claims has been hovering over 20 million. Jobs are neither “snapping back” nor are they continuing to death spiral.

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-jobless-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Normally, jobless claims only cover people that lost payroll employment, and don’t cover self-employed people. Due to the scale of this pandemic shutdown, though, the fiscal stimulus that Congress and the President passed in the spring offers a Pandemic Unemployment Assistance “PUA” program that covers self-employed workers as well.

If we dig deeper into the jobless claims report, we can see that the full number of people relying on some type of unemployment assistance is actually 29 million, which includes 9 million people under the PUA. Continued claims come out with a one-week lag vs initial claims, and this more complete data set comes out with a 1-week lag to that, so it has a 2-week lag in total. Therefore, the latest jobless claims data is available as of mid-June, but this more complete 29 million figure is accurate as of the end of May, even though the data were just released this past Thursday.

This chart shows the “total” jobless claims in blue as of the end of May, with the PUA portion (self-employed unemployment benefits) in orange:

![Total Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-total-jobless-claims.png)

*Data Source: U.S. Department of Labor*

Early May was the worst point in absolute unemployment levels so far, when it hit over 30 million people, but we face a potentially long grind to get the majority of these remaining 29 million people back to gainful employment, even as 1.5 million people are still filing new jobless claims each week. There’s lots of job churn out there.

To put a number on it, the cost of providing 29 million people with $500/week to get by with the absolute basics, is $65 billion per month, or $750 billion per year. What number they assign to unemployed people may change over time (with federal assistance, it’s currently much higher until the end of July), but that’s a baseline round number as an example. In addition, this level of unemployment results in lower tax revenue.

**An Unemployment Program Experiment**

For the rest of the year, in addition to seeing an experiment in the various ways that countries handle COVID-19, we’ll also be witnessing a global experiment to see which type of unemployment system works the best in this sort of extreme environment.

Germany and some other countries have in-built programs to keep employees attached to their existing employers, which can reduce instability (massive job losses due to a cyclical event), but can lead to inflexibility (keeping people employed at some companies that probably should downsize structurally).

This is called [*Kurzarbeit*](https://www.imf.org/en/News/Articles/2020/06/11/na061120-kurzarbeit-germanys-short-time-work-benefit) in Germany. Employers can basically reduce employee hours without layoffs, and get compensated by the government to maintain those payrolls, and these affected workers can stay attached to the workforce and keep their skills active with their existing employers, but with fewer hours.

Some of my German readers will most likely email me with details that I missed; I’m very happy to hear any experiences they have about what’s going on there, since it’s not my core market. In fact, I’m interested in anecdotal experiences from any country. I’m in the U.S. and I have family in Egypt that I get reports from, so on-the-ground reports from any other countries are helpful.

The United States and some other countries have a separate unemployment system, where companies lay off workers, and the state pays those unemployed workers for a period of time until those workers can get re-employed somewhere else. This results in people being fully detached from the labor force in greater numbers.

However, due to the unprecedented nature of this pandemic shutdown (the highest unemployment rate in post-WW2 history), the U.S. and some other countries scrambled to get a system in place to also keep at least some affected workers attached to their employers. In the United States, this was the Paycheck Protection Program, which provides loans to small businesses that can turn into grants if they kept employees on for a few months, and many of those are “maturing” at the end of June, meaning the small businesses will probably lay some of their employees off.

As of May data, the United States has roughly double the unemployment rate of Germany, but we’ll see how long this divide continues. I suspect that the United States may face a deeper unemployment issue than much of northern Europe and Japan, who have more extensive industrial sectors and so far haven’t had the same level of massive unemployment as the United States. Here in America, we’re very reliant on the services sector (about 80% of the economy), whereas for many other major countries it’s more like 70-75%. But then it goes without saying that regions that rely significantly on tourism, such as southern Europe or Thailand, are in for a rougher time this year. Flight traffic is recovering, but remains way down and has a long hill to climb.

One reason I suspect that programs like *Kurzarbeit* will have decent success this year in places like Germany, is due to private case studies in similar methods. Readers know that I added the steel producer Nucor (NUE) to my portfolio in mid-March after it became very cheap. That was part of my portfolio pruning process, when I sold some stocks that were unlikely to recover for a long time, but bought others that were beaten down but that I considered to have a better chance of coming out of this strong.

Why did I specifically add Nucor, and not any other steel producer?

Nucor is very well-managed. During recessions, despite being a highly cyclical company, they don’t do mass layoffs. Instead, they trim hours for most employees, and cut compensation for executives. This avoids the problem of losing experienced steelmakers during each recession, because they keep good workers on the payrolls (and of course, they let go under-performing employees as needed throughout the full cycle). They use more of a shared-pain strategy rather than a high-turnover strategy for their employees. In other words, Nucor as an American steelmaker, internally uses a program more like *Kurzarbeit*.

The result is that Nucor has much higher employee satisfaction/motivation ratings than their peers, longer employee tenures, more experienced steelmakers, and so forth. Of course, they also maintain low financial leverage levels, which gives them flexibility in down markets. It’s easier for a company to do programs like that when the company is financially resilient.

**The U.S. Business Cycle Heatmap**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years (20 quarters), and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, I now use a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation is not necessarily “good” or “bad” like the rest of the heat map. Blue indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and red is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme now.

After already slowing in rate-of-change terms from late 2018 through 2019, the U.S. economy started to feel the pain from the pandemic in China in February. The economy was struck with a deathblow in March as countries around the world, and states throughout the United States, began shutting their economies down. Although Q2 will be worse than Q1 because it is entirely affected by the pandemic, there are strong signs of a bottom in place in April and May, when looking specifically at the economy in rate-of-change terms. However, it will be very messy for the rest of this year and likely for a number of years to come, due to the sheer magnitude of this economic impact.

**Dollar Update**

For readers that are particularly interested in macro, the [global dollar shortage](https://www.lynalden.com/global-dollar-short-squeeze/), relative dollar strength or weakness vs other currencies, and that sort of thing, I released a big [Twitter chart thread](https://twitter.com/LynAldenContact/status/1274007117020762113) on Friday that discussed the dollar, swap lines, and Treasury market.

So, check that out if you’re interested. I’ll probably turn it into a future article as well. It makes the case for eventual dollar weakness.

In the intermediate term, however, we should be concerned about potential dollar strength again.

Over the past year, and more broadly over the past five years, the dollar has had strong inverse correlation to U.S. (and global) equity performance. This chart shows the trade-weighted dollar vs the Wilshire, which is a measure of the total U.S. stock market capitalization:

![Wilshire vs Dollar](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-dollar-vs-wilshire.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The dollar spiked in March, and has since been on a downtrend as the Federal Reserve eased monetary conditions. In June, the dollar really fell off a cliff relative to other major currencies, and the stock market jumped up almost back to previous highs.

However, the dollar recently found some support, and has been inching back up, right when the U.S. stock market also became locally “toppy” and has been in a bit of a downtrend.

This dollar stabilization is largely because the Federal Reserve has been tapering its support in recent weeks. Over the past three months since mid-March, the Fed created about 3 trillion new dollars and used them to buy Treasuries, mortgage-backed securities, and corporate bonds, while also performing repo loans and foreign central bank liquidity swaps. They flooded the financial system with dollar liquidity, in other words.

And a lot of those dollars actually got out into the economy through fiscal spending, meaning that the Treasury issued debt securities, and rather than the Treasury extracting dollars from the existing system to finance those securities, the Fed created new dollars to buy/monetize those Treasury securities (through primary dealer banks as intermediaries), so it became basically a pure dollar injection.

During this past week, the Fed and their peer central banks no longer needed some of their repo operations and liquidity swaps at the moment, so the Fed reduced those, while continuing to buy Treasuries, corporate bonds, and MBS. The net result, though, was less dollar liquidity.

This equity rally has been very much tied to excess liquidity from the Fed, combined with fiscal spending to get it into the economy by Congress, and lately that has tapered, and the market has rolled over. The red line shows the Fed’s balance sheet, and the blue line is the Wilshire U.S. stock market capitalization:

![Fed Balance Sheet vs Wilshire](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-fed-balance-sheet-vs-wilshire.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

So, I remain quite cautious for the summer ahead. As I’ve been describing in recent reports, fiscal spending (and subsequent Fed debt monetization of that spending) is on track to taper this summer, and that constant injection of capital is basically what is holding up the markets. We’ll likely get more fiscal spending later this year, but between now and then, markets could get volatile.

As my base case, I don’t expect a sell-off as bad as what happened in March. However, until more fiscal stimulus occurs to keep the game of musical chairs going, the markets could be in for a rough time for a couple months, and the dollar could resume a relative strengthening trend, and inflation expectations might dip.

## ETF Spotlight: COPX

I have a bullish long-term outlook on copper producers, although like many stocks, I think they may struggle a bit this summer.

Southern Copper (SCCO) is already in my top twelve list, and in many of my portfolios. So, this report will focus on a second copper producer, Freeport-McMoran (FCX).

For investors that don’t like individual stock selection, the Global X Copper Miners ETF (COPX) is a decent way to get exposure to the sector. The expense ratio is 0.65%, which is reasonable for a niche small-position holding. It holds over 25 copper producers from around the world, including roughly 5% allocations each towards Southern Copper and Freeport.

I’ve said a number of times that mining is a crappy business. Miners don’t control the price of their product, they are subject to big booms and busts, and thus they tend to have low returns on capital. A handful of gifted and disciplined CEOs tend to do very well in the space, while the broad industry is very challenging.

However, every decade or two, there is an outstanding opportunity in commodities. I believe the 2020’s is one of those times, but it still may take some time.

This chart shows the ratio of a broad commodity index to the Dow Jones Industrial Average over a 120-year period:

![Commodities to Dow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-commodities-to-dow.png)

*Chart Source: [Incrementum IGWT 2020 Report](https://ingoldwetrust.report/igwt/?lang=en)*

This period of time is rivaled only by the 1960’s, and to a lesser extent by the late 1920’s and late 1990’s, in terms of how badly commodities have under-performed broad equities. Each of those times were amazing buying opportunities for commodities and commodity producers.

This is because, as well-known natural resource investor Rick Rule often says about the commodity space, “bear markets are the authors of bull markets, and bull markets are the authors of bear markets.”

In other words, when there is a ton of demand for commodities, the market overestimates the longevity of that demand, and tons of capital flows to the industry to open new supply, and that new supply ends up outpacing demand and killing commodity prices. Then, after a prolonged bear market, nobody wants to risk capital to produce new supply of commodities that barely earn their production cost, and mines have a very long term period of development before they start producing. So, supply fails to keep up with demand, until finally demand outpaces supply and starts a new bull market in commodity prices, and the cycle starts all over. These are 10-20 year periods.

However, it requires a lot of patience. As the chart above shows, commodities became historically cheap relative to equities in 2015/2016, but five years later, are now even cheaper. In the 1960’s, there was a decade-long consolidating bottom in the ratio. In the late 1920’s and late 1990’s, commodity investors were rewarded from those very low points more swiftly.

Since 2018, I’ve been bullish on gold and silver, with a small amount of base commodity positions as well, but was more cautious about that base commodity space due to my perception of being late in the business cycle. The gold and gold miner portion in particular, and to some extent the silver portion, have worked out extremely well, and I continue to be bullish on those metals. Now that we’ve gotten a brutal recession and further base commodity weakness, I’m becoming more bullish on those base commodities as well.

I still think it’s a bit early and the base commodity space will struggle a bit for a time, but I’m fine with being somewhat early, since nobody can time it perfectly. For me, it’s about diversification, re-balancing, and proper position-sizing, and capturing multi-year trends.

The global exploration budget for nonferrous metals peaked nearly a decade ago. The 2000’s decade saw the rise of emerging markets, and their strong commodity demand, but that ended in the early 2010s decade as many of those emerging markets slowed their growth rates over time. The full 2010’s decade was just the opposite, with a big disinflationary period, slow growth, outperformance of U.S. tech equities, and little interest in commodities, as the excess supply gradually worked itself off.

Here’s how the copper price looks over the past 5 years:

![Copper Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-copper-price.png)

*Chart Source: [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/commodity/copper)*

Copper prices did very well in 2017 as the dollar weakened by over 10% relative to other major currencies, and it was a big period of reflation and “globally synchronized growth” as the media called it. However, from 2018 and onward, U.S. growth and global growth slowed, the dollar strengthened, and copper prices languished in a sideways pattern.

The pandemic shutdown in China in early 2020, and then the broader shutdown worldwide, slammed the copper price down significantly. It began recovering from the March bottom, although like the stock market, I think it could easily experience a bout of weakness in the coming months.

Longer-term, we need to be aware of supply limits. Copper is about 25% of nonferrous exploration:

![Nonferrous Exploration](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-nonferrous-exploration.png)

*Chart Source: [S&P Global World Exploration Report 2019](https://www.spglobal.com/marketintelligence/en/news-insights/research/report-world-exploration-trends-2019)*

The big bottom in commodities was the 2016 period, and it began to recover a bit, until 2019 started to slow global growth, and COVID-19 slammed commodities down hard again in 2020. The full year 2020 is going to be a low one for exploration budgets, since they’re being slashed this year by producers to retain cash and due to lower near-term demand.

Interestingly, regardless of budgetary spending, it has been a difficult period for finding new gold and copper reserves in the world. A lot of the “easy deposits” have already been found:

![Gold and Copper Findings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-gold-copper-discoveries.png)

*Chart Source: S&P Global World Exploration Report 2019*

A lot of people are excited about a future of renewable energy and electric vehicles. As someone with an electrical engineering background, I’m certainly interested in those technologies as well.

However, that future, if it comes to pass at the scale that many people hope for, represents a gradual shift from fossil fuel demand towards more metals demand. Electric vehicles have a lot more silver, copper, and of course battery metals than gas-powered vehicles. Solar panels use a ton of silver, and wind farms use a ton of copper and steel. Nuclear reactors use uranium. Electrical grids (which use a ton of copper) need to be made more robust to handle increased electrification that comes with increased electric vehicle usage. A shift towards electric vehicles means less energy in relative terms is transmitted through gasoline transport, and more energy is transmitted through the electrical grid.

And yet, metals discoveries broadly peaked a while ago. Copper mines (and other metal mines) are environmentally damaging, and take many years of permitting and development to come online. It’s not an easy task to get copper and other metals, and low commodity prices have not encouraged much new exploration or development. Although we take copper for granted now, this is setting up potential scarcity 5-10 years down the line. All of the technology that we currently rely on and aspire to build in the future, relies heavily on copper, silver, nickel, zinc, cobalt, iron, and a host of other metals.

Before the 5+ year range when copper scarcity truly hits, many countries face a high probability of infrastructure stimulus and ongoing money-printing and currency devaluation, as the global economy recovers from high unemployment from the pandemic shutdown. This makes a good case for holding some hard, scarce assets. I like beaten-down copper producers, steel producers, other diversified metals producers, and non-shale energy producers.

Right when the world thinks it has achieved maximum physical abundance, that we don’t have to worry about raw materials, that inflation will never come, that the future is all about software, and when commodities are historically cheap, is exactly when I want to own some commodity producers in my portfolio. All that software needs hardware to run on, and that hardware needs a lot of copper, and that copper is not really being invested in or found in significant amounts.

## Freeport-McMoRan (FCX) Deep-Dive Analysis

Freeport-McMoRan is one of the largest copper producers in the world, but before I talk about it, I want to talk about another copper stock, Southern Copper (SCCO).

I included Southern Copper on my initial “Top Twelve Stocks” list on November 10, 2019, and it’s still on the list in the Google Drive today. Since then, Southern Copper has roughly matched the S&P 500, and has outpeformed Freeport and the broad copper ETF:

![Southern Copper Since November 2019](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-scco-nov10.png)

Southern Copper beat Freeport by over 10%, and beat COPX by 14%, since the time of selection onto the list. It began to significantly outperform the S&P 500 shortly after selection in late 2019, but underperformed during the COVID-19 sell-off, and has since rebounded to get a slight lead at the moment. Southern Copper is in most of my portfolios as a small holding.

The reason I specifically selected Southern Copper at that time, and continue to like it, is that it has historically been one of the most solid copper producers from a financial perspective. It has low debt, low production costs, and decades of copper reserves. What this means is that it tends to outperform other copper producers in copper bear markets, but can underperform them in copper bull markets. And what we went through since late 2019, was a bear market.

For the oil and copper holdings in my portfolio, I wanted some of the most conservative ones, due to my analysis at the time indicating that we were likely relatively late in the global business cycle.

If we look back over the past decade, Southern Copper has been a great performer relative to the rest of the industry, mainly because it lost less money in the bear market portion of that time period, particularly in 2015:

![SCCO 10 Years](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-scco-fcx-copx-10yr.png)

The commodity bear market reached a bottom at the start of 2016. Then, the commodity industry recovered for a bit, and then it got killed again in 2020.

During that 2-year bull cycle for copper producers that started in early 2016 and went through early 2018, though, Freeport and the broad copper ETF outperformed SCCO:

![FCX 2016 Outperformance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-scco-fcx-copx-2016-2018.png)

However, it required patience. If an investor bought Freeport on the first trading day of 2016, within a short period of time he or she would have had nearly a 40% loss on her position (that big orange dip at the start of the chart above). But then it turned around, and the stock produced a 180% return from the starting level by the first trading day of 2018.

Basically, Freeport is historically a “copper bull market” stock, while Southern Copper is historically an “I want to have copper exposure, but want some more defense” type of stock.

This is mostly because Freeport has a weaker balance sheet, which makes it more leveraged to the price of copper. When copper is in a bear market, Freeport can really struggle. When copper does great, Freeport tends to take off like a rocket ship.

Freeport is riskier than stocks that I normally like to feature in this premium service, but I’m doing so because I also presented COPX and Southern Copper as alternatives, and I like diversification in this sector. I personally would start with Southern Copper, and diversify from there.

One thing Freeport has going for it relative to Southern Copper, however, is that Freeport also has a decent chunk of gold mining exposure. In other words, Freeport has both cyclical metal exposure (copper) and monetary metal exposure (gold).

![FCX Sensitivities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-fcx-presentation.png)

*Chart Source: Freeport-McMoRan April 2020 Presentation*

Freeport makes more money if gold, copper, and/or molybenum rise in price, although copper is their main commodity so it dictates the profits the most.

The gold-to-copper ratio is cyclical, and tends to closely follow the growth rate of U.S. and global GDP over time. Copper is used for construction, infrastructure, vehicles, and other economically sensitive projects. Gold, on the other hand, is more of a defensive asset.

The blue line on this next chart shows the ratio of copper’s cost per ton divided by the price of gold per ounce, and the red line shows U.S. GDP in year-over-year percent rate of change terms.

When the blue line is high, it means copper is historically expensive compared to gold, and when it is low, it means copper is historically cheap compared to gold. Right now, it’s at 30-year record cheap levels relative to gold. Just three ounces of gold can buy a ton of copper:

![Copper to Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-copper-gold.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

In fact, I’ve looked at longer data sets that go back to the 1800’s, and there wasn’t a lower ratio of copper-to-gold in any data sets that I’ve seen. The only other time the ratio reached this low was in 1980, when gold briefly became extremely expensive, and even then, the ratio only matched this low; it didn’t get lower.

Most copper producers aren’t making money at these levels. It’s not that gold is extremely expensive at the moment; it’s that copper is extremely cheap. There’s no incentive to explore or bring new copper supply onto the market at these levels; Freeport estimates that copper would need to be over $3.30/lb to bring new mines onto the market. Remember, bear market are the authors of bull markets.

**Management and Performance**

Freeport has historically had messy management and performance. They over-leveraged and invested heavily into the top of the commodities cycle a decade ago, and that nearly drove them to bankruptcy during the subsequent commodity bear market in late 2015.

However, they got through it, and have managed to reduce debt levels. They still have more debt than I’d like to see, but this financial leverage gives the company a lot of upside potential in a commodity bull cycle.

Back in late 2015, Freeport had over $20 billion in debt and almost no cash, and thus were at risk of insolvency. The stock briefly traded below $4/share during that commodity bear market bottom. Since then, the company has strengthened its financial position. They’re down to $10 billion in debt, and up to $1.6 billion in cash, which gives them a net debt position of under $8.5 billion, or half of what it was in the near-insolvency period of 2015.

![FCX Debt Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-fcx-debt.png)

Over the past year, as the global economy has slowed towards recession, Freeport’s cash levels have inched down. However, during the pandemic crisis earlier this year, Freeport and many other copper producers announced dividend suspensions to ensure they have adequate liquidity.

Freeport also cut operating costs (lowered production), and cut capex and exploration costs. They also took advantage of the lower interest rate environment to refinance their debt; specifically they pushed some near-term debt maturities to later years without having to pay higher rates on that longer-duration debt. So, they don’t really owe much debt for years, and are good at re-financing as needed because they do have strong assets.

Freeport’s balance sheet isn’t great, but they’re solvent, and doing what they need to do in order to retain liquidity. As the saying goes, “buy when there is blood in the streets”. For copper producers, there certainly is blood at the moment.

The trick, though, is not to rush head-first into a pile of risk. I like SCCO’s balance sheet and production costs, I like EMX’s royalty model, and I like FCX’s somewhat riskier exposure (but generally moving in the right direction, and mitigated with gold exposure), so I currently invest in the first two and will soon build a position in the third.

**Valuation Analysis**

Mining companies don’t work well with a discounted cash flow model, so it’s more about the macro view and about how much risk one wants to take on with the types of companies they select. I like a diverse sampling of lower-to-medium risk, and tend to avoid the far tail of extreme risk.

So, I like royalty companies, I like miners with strong balance sheets and low costs of production, and then occasionally I like more troubled companies that are moving in the right direction, and that have more upside potential if the market turns in their favor.

Freeport’s valuations (in terms of price/book and price/sales, which are more useful metrics for commodity producers) hit very low levels in late 2015 and gain in early 2020. Their price-to-book ratio has been very volatile due to their changing net debt position over time:

![FCX Valuation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-fcx-ps-pb-ratio.png)

Freeport expects higher copper and gold sales over the next two years, compared to low numbers today, as their Indonesian mining investments come fully online:

![Freeport Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-fcx-growth.png)

*Chart Source: Freeport-McMoRan April 2020 Presentation*

So, how well Freeport does going forward is mostly a function of copper prices and volumes, and to some extent gold and molybdenum prices.

**Company Risks**

*Commodity Price Risk*

Freeport has considerable risk related to the price of copper, and to a lesser extent gold and molybdenum.

While their size and diversification gives them some advantages, Freeport is not on the very lowest end of the cost curve, and as previously described, their balance sheet leaves something to be desired. While I expect copper prices to stabilize and then eventually improve significantly in the years ahead as my base case, we could very well get further periods of weak demand and low copper prices over the next few years.

So, I consider Freeport to be somewhat higher risk than Southern Copper, although I do like to diversify outside of just one company, and keep each miner as a small position in an overall portfolio. Southern Copper is my first choice, and I round out that exposure with picks like EMX Royalty, and soon, Freeport.

*China Concentration, and Indian Potential*

About half of global copper demand comes from China. Any major economic setbacks in that country can sharply reduce the world’s need for copper, which is what happened in February 2020. China still has much lower per-capita copper use than the western world, so its demand for copper is by no means finished, but it is on a much slower trajectory now than it was 10-20 years ago.

India is an up-and-comer to replace some of that slower Chinese demand. India has an installed copper base that is much lower than China’s per capita, and India continues to grow at a very rapid pace over the long term, and doesn’t have the same aging demographics issue that China has.

*Jurisdiction and Technical Risk*

Freeport has mines in North America, South America, and Indonesia. This spreads out jurisdiction risk, but a legislative problem with any area would be a big setback for such a challenging business. For a number of years, Freeport and the Indonesian government had disputes, with the Indonesian government seeking a larger share in its country’s mineral rights, and Freeport seeking to ensure that their investments in the region are profitable. The situation is steady at the moment after terms were renegotiated, but things can change in a crisis.

Freeport is currently converting their Grasberg mine in Indonesia from an open-pit mine to an underground mine, with completion expected in 2022. This is a key reason why their metal output is expected to increase over the next two years. This is one of the largest copper mines in the world, and any technical setbacks or problems could cause considerable financial damage to the company.

**Freeport Cash-Secured Put Options**

Due to its volatility, Freeport makes for a decent option-selling stock. If an investor likes Freeport for the long run, but is concerned about a dip this summer, or concerned about the stock being dead money for a time, then selling cash-secured put options to generate income on the position or buy at a lower price, can make for a decent entry point.

For example, as of this writing, Freeport trades for $10.50/share. The September 18, 2020 put options with a strike price of $10 cost about 90 cents per share.

In other words, if Freeport stays mostly above $10 within the next three months, investors that sell these options will pocket better than 9% return on their capital from option premiums for three months, and then after that period will free up their capital to potentially sell another round of options. On the other hand, if Freeport goes below $10 and the shares are assigned to the option-seller, then the option-seller will buy the shares for a cost basis of about $9.10/share (the strike price minus the option premium), or about a 13% discount to today’s price.

![FCX Put Options](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-6-21-fcx-puts.png)

As Freeport stock bounces around each day, its option premiums will bounce around too. But the point stands; investors that are willing to get paid to potentially buy Freeport at a lower price, could use options to their advantage here. Freeport tends to be very volatile, so it could go much lower than $9/share during market sell-offs, but I’m happy to accumulate a bit down here. For volatile stocks that I have a bullish long-term conviction on, I am willing to absorb the volatility and sell options to buy at lower price points along the way.

I used this strategy for Southern Copper back in late February, where I sold some puts, and got assigned at a pretty attractive cost basis. I plan on potentially selling some cash-secured put options on Freeport next week.

I would not recommending putting too much of a portfolio into a stock like Freeport. For me, Freeport would be a portion of my commodity exposure, which itself is a portion of my full set of portfolios.

## Conclusion

As we move into the summer months, some fiscal programs in the U.S. expire, the Fed is tapering their asset purchases, valuations are stretched after a euphoric market rally in late May and early June, and market momentum is currently broken. Another round of fiscal spending is expected later this year, but between now and then, the market has little to work with in terms of bullish catalysts.

That doesn’t mean the market can’t move higher; it just means the risk/reward ratio is not really in favor of equity bullishness at the moment, in my view.

After a strong rally, the S&P 500 is not that much lower than where it was at the start of the year, and yet tens of millions of Americans are out of work compared to the start of the year.  The economy and the market are heavily supported by fiscal spending that is partly monetized by the Federal Reserve, including cash-handouts, corporate and small business bailouts, and so forth, which is not a healthy economic environment, especially since it is tapering at the moment.

I have a bullish long-term outlook on quality commodity producers, although like many stocks, I wouldn’t be surprised to see their stock prices decline this summer. For that reason, I like having some cash and/or T-bills and gold in the near-term, as a counter-cyclical position to play some defense.
