## Macro View

The macro environment has been at somewhat of a standstill recently, with conflicting data depending on which measure you look at.

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Rather than describing the economy in narratives or qualitative terms, or cherry pick data to fit a particularly bullish or bearish view, it quantifies a broad section of data.

The economic cycle peaked in year-over-year rate-of-change terms in Q2 2021, and since then has been decelerating but still generally positive.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Heat Map Indicators Referenced:**

* Real Gross Domestic Product
* Industrial Production
* Advance Retail Sales, Excluding Food Services
* Total Construction Spending
* Capital Goods Orders, Nondefense, Excluding Aircraft
* Exports of Goods and Services
* Imports of Goods and Services
* All Employees, Total Nonfarm
* 10 Year Minus 3 Month Yield Curve
* Consumer Prices, All Urban Consumers, All Items in City Average

Note that many of these are **nominal** figures. Retail sales, construction spending, capital goods orders, exports, imports, are all measured in nominal dollars, and thus have a significant inflation component. Retail sales growth at 7.46% year-over-year and capital goods orders at 6.64% both seem like fine numbers, until we factor in 7.76% CPI, which puts those two indicators slightly negative in inflation-adjusted terms.

In my view, the difference between nominal and real (inflation-adjusted) figures is confusing a lot of analysts since it makes things harder to analyze. For stock prices, things like nominal earnings and revenues are what matter. But for measuring the rate-of-change of an economy, the real figures are more useful. Most data, such as PMI data and the yield curve suggests that the sluggish real numbers are giving a better insight into how the economy is performing.

As shared by [Michael Arouet](https://twitter.com/MichaelAArouet) the other day, by some calculations, inflation-adjusted excess income from pandemic-related stimulus is now drained.

![Excess Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-excess-income.png)

What this means is that the excess income glut isn’t just behind us (that happened a while ago), but now the time below the trend has made up for the time above the trend, which basically makes it as though it never happened.

It is, however pretty hard to destroy broad money at scale. When that money is spent, it winds up somewhere else. Broad money for the most part is only reduced if loans are paid down. Overall, the amount of broad money in the system has been roughly flat throughout 2022 after increasingly at a historically high rate in 2020 and 2021.

The yield curve in particular has been flashing a red light for a likely 2023 recession:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-yield-curve.png)

Black Friday weekend e-commerce sales were at record highs, but rose compared to the prior two years at a slower pace than broad inflation. Both 2021 and 2022 had rather poor numbers by this metric.

![Black Friday Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-black-friday-sales.png)

*Chart Source: [NPR](https://www.npr.org/2022/11/26/1139274449/black-friday-sales-inflation-online-shopping#:~:text=Black%20Friday%20sales%20raked%20in,year%2C%20according%20to%20Adobe%20Analytics.)*

**China Lockdowns and Protests**

China has been dashing people’s re-opening hopes lately. Statistics out of China are always questionable, but as far as their data are concerned, they are currently having their third virus outbreak, and thus re-imposing significant lockdown measures in many cities.

![China Cases](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-china-cases.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/country/china/)*

The country continues to lack a clear path towards an endgame for lockdowns and extensive testing, meaning a return to normalcy. The virus has mutated to become more transmissible over time, but the country continues to pursue its zero-COVID approach of locking cities down whenever the virus begins to spread.

There have been [fresh protests in Xinjiang](https://www.wsj.com/articles/chinas-xinjiang-region-has-been-locked-down-for-months-casting-shadow-over-zero-covid-easing-11669371218), which has been under lockdown for months now. A fire occurred and killed a significant number of people, and the response time by firefighters was evidenced to have been impaired by lockdown-related barriers.

Meanwhile, the [protests at a Foxconn campus](https://www.wsj.com/articles/iphone-factory-workers-clash-with-police-at-covid-hit-plant-in-china-11669193001?mod=article_inline), where workers make iPhones, have been getting more media attention. These protests involve a mix of working conditions, pay, and frustration with lockdown policies.

Over the past day, [protests also spread to Shanghai and Beijing](https://www.cnn.com/2022/11/26/china/china-protests-xinjiang-fire-shanghai-intl-hnk/index.html), with some crowd chants for President Xi to step down. China frequently has small-scale protests, but it’s unusual for protests to break out across multiple cities at once, related to the same subject.

What I find challenging to grasp, is the actual scale of the protests so far. Social media is filled with videos of various medium-sized protests from multiple cities, but when we are talking about a country of 1.4 billion people, it is challenging to say how widespread support is among the population for these protests. A lot of reporting is done by Western media, which can make the true scale and depth of the protests hard to ascertain.

**Taiwan’s COVID Pivot, Analyzed**

For the first two years of the virus, as a small island, Taiwan meticulously maintained a zero-COVID approach. They mostly shut themselves off from the world, and would do extensive contact tracing and quarantining to ensure the virus wouldn’t spread through the population.

![Taiwan Tourists](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-taiwan-tourists.png)

They had among the lowest reported deaths from the virus in the world, and could keep a lot of things open internally by controlling the entrances. But in spring 2022, after the Omicron wave, their health officials changed the policy. Omicron was incredibly transmissible but less fatal, and trying to forever prevent the spread of the virus became increasingly untenable, like trying to prevent the seasonal flu.

So, the country changed course towards a strategy of living with the virus.

In terms of deaths, this was sadly like ripping a band-aid off. Many people were exposed to the virus for the first time, and there have been 14,000+ deaths.

![Taiwan Deaths](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-taiwan-deaths.png)

*Chart Source: Worldometer*

Each death is tragic, representing someone’s parent, sibling, or friend. A life lived, and a life now gone.

However, with Taiwan’s population of around 24 million people, the baseline number of deaths per year is nearly 200,000. These 14,000 deaths represented 0.06% of the population so far. Furthermore, the deaths were concentrated in the demographic of people who are both elderly and have pre-existing conditions, which means the number of total lost life-years is less than if, say, a similar number of people of all ages were killed in the bombing of a city or some similar horror of that scale.

This type of uptick in deaths is probably something that Chinese officials fear, if they do an approach towards re-opening. And unlike Taiwan, China made zero-COVID more of a matter of national pride, using it as evidence for their socioeconomic system supposedly being better than that of the West. This makes it harder to pivot in the face of changing facts.

But long-term lockdown policies do have a cost, aside from the obvious threat towards individual freedom in general.

For example, what percentage of the population suffers increased health problems from social isolation or insufficient exercise amid extensive and repeated lockdowns? How many people have heightened stress from failed businesses or struggling finances, that they might not have otherwise faced under non-lockdown conditions? Or to what extent is normal childhood learning and socialization disrupted by these types of policies?

Some of this is very hard to measure.

**EU Natural Gas Update**

The world has arguably been in an eye of a storm in terms of energy prices lately, with relative calm. Economic slowdowns, ongoing Chinese lockdowns, and ongoing drawdowns in the US Strategic Petroleum Reserve, have kept a lid on prices. And recently, the [US temporarily waived sanctions](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-11-26/us-eases-sanctions-on-venezuela-with-chevron-resuming-oil-output) on Chevron getting oil from Venezuela.

An unseasonable warm autumn for Europe thankfully helped them fill their natural gas storage capacity and (along with large shutdowns of energy-intensive industry, aka demand destruction) take the edge off prices. However, the price has been showing signs of a bottom lately, as Europe enters its winter months.

![EU Natural Gas](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-eu-natural-gas.jpg)

Europe always consumes more natural gas than is piped in during winter, and therefore supplements that by drawing down storage capacity, which they subsequently refill each summer when they use way less than is piped in. How deep into storage they get each year has historically depended on how cold or warm the winter is compared to the average. The problem this upcoming winter, as we all know, is that much of their pipeline capacity is now disrupted, and so their storage capacity is going to be pressured a lot more than normal.

That’s why when you hear “European gas storage is full”, it’s nowhere near being an “all-clear”. That storage capacity will be put to the test severely this year, supplemented by larger-than-normal LNG imports ([which crowds out the developing world](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-10-03/pakistan-fails-to-secure-lng-deal-in-latest-hit-to-fuel-supply)), and whether it will be enough or not will depend in part on whether Mother Nature is kind enough to grace the continent with a warm winter.

Meanwhile, US oil production has been going flat lately, after a partial recovery.

![US Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-us-oil-production.png)

US oil production is a mix of conventional oil and shale oil, and leaning more towards shale. Shale wells have a very fast decline rate. So when we look at this purple line, it looks like a pretty calm thing, when in reality it’s a constant churn of new wells and depleted wells. For example, the shale wells drilled in 2018 and 2019 are already majority-depleted, but their output has been replaced by new wells from 2020 and 2021 that are now declining rapidly too. It’s like running on a treadmill, and if producers start to go lighter on capital expenditures, it’s very easy for overall production to go flat or roll over.

I don’t have a strong view about oil prices in 2023, because the US could very well be in a recession, we don’t really know what will happen with Chinese lockdowns as they potentially enter their 4th year of them, and overall supply will still be pretty tight. But the longer we look out, such as into 2024 and 2025, the more bullish my expectations on price are, as a base case.

I don’t plan to trade around my energy positions- I plan to largely let them ride until I see a significant capex cycle to bring more supply to market.

My recent long-form [energy article](https://www.lynalden.com/energy-problems/) outlined my views for the decade ahead.

## Digital Assets Note

The digital assets section in my prior report was long due to the fallout from FTX/Alameda, so in this report I will keep it shorter.

Bitcoin has been in a holding pattern at around $16,000 since mid November, sometimes below and sometimes above, with neither signs of price recovery nor further breakdown.

One thing that’s notable, is that the number of small on-chain users went up significantly. As people learned a difficult lesson about counterparty risk, many people removed their coins from exchanges and put them into self-custody. Many hardware wallet makers reported a burst of sales, and many bitcoin-only companies that promote self-custody or collaborative custody reported a spike in sign-ups.

We can see this on-chain, with a 40,000 surge in individual addresses with over one full bitcoin. One address does not necessarily correspond to one person, but it’s clear evidence of a burst of self-custodied behavior:

![BTC Balance Over 1](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-btc-balance-1.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/charts/)*

Glassnode data further shows that the number of addresses with at least 0.1 bitcoin increased by over 200,000 during that same timeframe.

From now until Q1 2023, with the Fed continuing to tighten monetary policy into a decelerating economy, it’s hard to get overly bullish on bitcoin, and downside risks remain. However, it’s currently at very deep value levels, so my expectation is that it’ll likely provide strong 3-5 year returns from here.

I view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as part of an asset mix to be a useful strategy for investors who want some exposure.

**Grayscale on Watch**

Genesis is a huge trading desk and lender in the broad crypto industry, and has been in operation since 2013. It’s part of Digital Currency Group “DCG” which as a parent company also owns the Grayscale set of trusts, the Coindesk media publication, and has [portfolio exposure](https://dcg.co/portfolio/) to countless crypto and crypto-adjacent entities.

Genesis has faced massive losses this year, especially with the Luna/Celsius/3AC blow-up this past spring, and continuing into FTX exposure more recently. As a result, they had to pause withdrawals on their lending products, which impacted various other entities including Gemini, one of the largest crypto exchanges. The parent company DCG loaned money to Genesis, and so now DCG itself is in trouble, and is trying to raise emergency capital in a tough market.

Naturally, this brought up some questions about the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC). Grayscale is a self-contained entity, with its coins custodied at Coinbase’s institutional custody platform. Whenever you hold bitcoin through a third party, though, it always comes with some counterparty risks.

It’s possible that DCG will sell its Grayscale business to another asset manager. Grayscale provides large cash flows from relatively high fees, and can be sold for an upfront infusion of cash.

Alternatively, it’s possible that the Grayscale trusts could be liquidated. If the trusts are liquidated, it would result in selling a lot of bitcoin and other cryptos, which could hurt the price, but it would also close the gap to NAV as the shareholders are cashed out. In that event, it would probably do well to be long GBTC and short BTC futures, although it’s not a trade I intend to perform, since I view the potential liquidation as being outside of my base case.

It’s pretty unusual for a closed-end fund or trust to persistently trade more than 20% below net asset value, and yet GBTC’s discount is more than twice that.

![GBTC Discount](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-gbtc-discount.png)

Other than an eventual conversion to an ETF, or a liquidation of the trust, or a decision by Grayscale to do a buyback or allow redemptions, there aren’t any catalysts that would force it to trade at NAV.

A number of entities, ranging from Three Arrows Capital to BlockFi, have been forced sellers of GBTC, which seems to have put a lot of downward pressure on its price. I view GBTC as a deep value play for a portfolio, but I would continue to be cautious with position sizing.

Overall, GBTC has been pretty disappointing relative to holding bitcoin directly, but with the SEC not allowing a spot bitcoin ETF, there have been limited alternatives.

## Final Thoughts

Liquidity conditions have likely been a substantial factor for this year’s poor performance of risk assets.

These charts (5-year and 18-month respectively) show the S&P 500 compared to a particular liquidity calculation. The liquidity calculation, which I’ve seen used by a number of analysts and makes sense, is the Fed balance sheet minus the Treasury General Account and minus reverse repurchase agreements:

![S&P 500 vs Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-11-27-spx-vs-liquidity.png)

It’s arguably a chart crime to some extent, because correlation does not necessarily imply causation, but in my view the mechanism is pretty straightforward. As more bonds are issued and the Fed isn’t buying (or is even a net seller like they are now), then that means the private sector has to absorb more of the bond issuance, which crowds out potential equity investment. It’s also generally strong for the dollar, which slows down global growth.

The Fed controls the size of its balance sheet, and the Treasury Department controls the size of the Treasury General Account.

When the Fed expands its balance sheet by creating reserves and buying bonds, it is injecting liquidity into financial markets. When it reduces its balance sheet, by being a net seller of bonds and destroyer of reserves, it is removing liquidity from financial markets.

When the Treasury expands the TGA, by raising debt and bringing in taxes faster than it spends money, it is sucking liquidity out of the system into a void. When it reduces the TGA, it is spending more money into the economy than it is currently withdrawing, or in other words putting liquidity from that void back into the economy. So, the smaller the TGA, the smaller the void of dead capital. There are some months where the Treasury can be injecting liquidity while the Fed removes it, or vice versa.

Going forward, the Fed plans to continue draining its balance sheet month after month, as part of their goal of quelling inflation. The Treasury General Account is intended to be flat-but-choppy. Right now, the TGA is $480 billion. They expect to have $700 billion by the end of this quarter (which I doubt they’ll reach, I don’t think they’ll get over $600 billion), and then they expect to get to $500 billion by the end of Q1 2023 (which would be virtually unchanged from $480 billion).

So, overall, with the Fed continuing to drain liquidity and the Treasury probably being rather neutral, liquidity conditions continue to point downward unless or until something changes, which (at least as one important variable) suggests ongoing unremarkable performance for many risk assets. I don’t have a strong view towards whether the market will chop along in a big sideways band, or if it will reach lower lows. If we do get a recession as the yield curve expects, I would expect the latter, with stocks like Apple and Tesla near the top of the equity indices weighing things down.
