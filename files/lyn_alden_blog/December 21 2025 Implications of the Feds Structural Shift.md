## Macro View

**US Liquidity Conditions**

Base liquidity in the US financial system has likely bottomed. The Treasury General Account has come off its highs (i.e. no longer sucking liquidity out of the banking system), and the Fed has initiated balance sheet growth (which adds base liquidity to the system).

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-21-domestic-liquidity-annotated-2.png)

In the coming weeks and months, we should see usage of the Fed’s standing repo facility die down, and we should see the secured overnight financing rate settle down to being at-or-below the interest rate on bank reserves.

![Repo Activity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-21-repo-activity.png)

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-21-sofr-iorb.png)

**A Major Fed Shift**

Continuing on that prior point, the biggest news in macro lately is that at the Fed’s December 10th FOMC meeting, they announced that in addition to cutting interest rates by another 0.25%, they will begin expanding their balance sheet on a persistent basis.

Since spring 2024, I have been covering the timeline and new regime that the Fed would likely be operating under:

> I don’t have a firm view on liquidity over the next 3-6 months, but I have a positive view on liquidity over the next 18 months, and will adjust that view accordingly as more data come in.
>
> A couple weeks ago, the New York Fed [released](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/annual_reports) their 2023 annual report for open market operations. Notably, this includes their projections for what the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet will look like in the years ahead.
>
> They expect a tapering of the rate of balance sheet reduction in 2024, and then a bottoming and resumption in growth in 2025, at a rate that is in line with the growth of nominal GDP:
>
> ![Projected Soma](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma.png)
>
> And specifically, they expect a reduction in mortgage-backed securities as a share of holdings, and an increase in U.S. Treasury securities as a share of holdings:
>
> ![UST vs MBS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma-ust-vs-mbs.png)While the actual outcome will likely be bumpier and higher than forward expectations (both the Fed and the Congressional Budget Office always assume smooth growth with no recessions ever in their projections), I think these overall projections make sense as a middle range or lower bound, and are in line with my expectations.
>
> And specifically, because I view the United States as being in fiscal dominance mode (running large structural federal deficits that will be accommodated by monetary authorities whenever needed to ensure a functioning liquid Treasury market), I expect nominal GDP to run on the hot side, and for the Fed’s balance sheet to grow roughly in line with it.
>
> The government’s deficits and the Fed’s accommodation of those deficits has a stimulatory effect on nominal GDP, and their balance sheet growth is expected to be in line with nominal GDP. This means that their own balance sheet growth to accommodate ongoing federal deficits and bond issuance contributes to nominal GDP, in a self-reinforcing feedback loop.
>
> In short, nothing stops this train.
>
> -April 28, 2024 premium report

Specifically, the banking system ran into liquidity issues starting in September/October of this year, and that was basically the signal that the Fed, within a matter of months, would begin putting out that fire with active liquidity. I figured they’d do it by early 2026, but they cut to the chase and started in the last few weeks of 2025:

> Domestic liquidity currently remains rangebound. The overnight reverse repo facility is mostly drained (which puts liquidity back into the system), the Treasury General Account is up to $590 billion as it refills (which pulls liquidity out of the system), and the Fed continues to perform gradual quantitative tightening (which pulls liquidity out of the system.
>
> If the Treasury General Account reaches its $850 billion fully-filled target by the end of September, then it’ll likely drop liquidity to the lower end of its multi-year range.
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> Overall bank cash levels remain above prior acute liquidity events, but in Q4 2025 they will likely start to enter somewhat tight territory.
>
> ![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bank-cash-levels.png)
>
> My expectation continues to be that the Fed will end quantitative tightening in 2026, and possibly move toward gradual balance sheet increases (which they won’t call QE, similar to their balance sheet expansion from October 2019 to March 2020).
>
> So, domestic liquidity looks like it’ll be flat-to-down for a while, potentially running into lower thresholds, and when it does bottom it’ll be a pretty structural event that moves us up out of a multi-year range.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

Here’s the current chart:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-21-fed-balance-sheet.png)

The Fed plans to continue to let mortgage-backed securities gradually mature off their balance sheet. However, they will be buying $40 billion more in short-term treasuries per month, with an emphasis on T-bills but a willingness to buy durations up to 3-year T-notes as well.

They plan to continue that $40 billion monthly purchase rate through April 2026, to ensure ample system liquidity for tax season. After that, they expect the rate of purchases to fall a bit (and Powell said in the press conference that $25 billion monthly is likely closer to the baseline). However, in May 2026, Powell will likely be replaced as the Fed chair, so we don’t know for sure what the rate of balance sheet expansion will be at that time.

Here are some back-of-the-envelope numbers. Over the past two years, GDP grew by around $1.5 trillion per year. The Fed’s balance sheet is around 20% of GDP and they roughly want to keep it at that percentage going forward. That means around $300 billion per year in net balance sheet expansion (which is right at the $25 billion per month baseline figure Powell mentioned, once they get finished with this temporary $40 billion per month period to get some surplus liquidity in there).

Overall, I consider Powell’s estimate to be reasonable as a baseline structural growth of the balance sheet. If there is a recession, or some other event that leads them to actively do stimulus, then the numbers will likely be higher. We can consider this to be the approximate minimum, in other words.

The balance sheet is $6,556 billion currently. For the five months from December 2025 through April 2026, they’re set to add around $200 billion to that, bringing the figure up to $6,756 billion. Then for the eight months from May through December of next year, $25 billion per month would mean another $200 billion more, bringing the total up to $6,956 billion, which we can round to “around $7 trillion” by the end of 2026.

This is the baseline “gradual print” scenario that I referred to in my [most recent public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/december-2025-newsletter/).

Terra’s unsustainable Anchor protocol with 20% yields results in a lot of UST demand, and with this new reserve practice by Terra, UST demand now results in BTC demand. This can be thought of as a source of indirect, artificial, or unsustainable BTC demand, which should eventually dry up. Active bitcoin traders should keep an eye on Terra’s LUNA and BTC reserves relative to UST market capitalization because if it starts to break down and they are forced to defend the UST peg, we could see tens of thousands of coins worth of rapid BTC selling pressure. I’m not saying that will happen but it’s a new factor to monitor going forward.

## Potential Beneficiaries of QE

Some financial commentators have the view that the Fed is all that matters, as though their printing or lack thereof is what drives markets.

In reality, it’s more complicated than that. They simply control the base ledger, as well as the various levers that enable fiscal deficits and bank lending to continue. What really matters for investing are broad monetary aggregates, not the monetary base itself. However, the monetary base does influence those broader aggregates.

In other words, the value of *tens of trillions* of dollars worth of US equities does not get pushed around too much by the Fed printing a few hundred billion.

The key difference between a sound money system and a fiat money system is what happens in environments like this. In a sound money system, the monetary base cannot be easily expanded, so if there are too many IOUs relative to the monetary base, those IOUs start to default and go down. In a fiat money system, the monetary base can be easily expanded, keeping IOUs almost always nominally growing, and instead the value of each unit persistently diminishes.

This “gradual print” scenario of $300-$400 billion per year in balance sheet growth, combined with 6-7% deficits as a share of GDP, is not like the hyper-stimulus of 2020/2021. It can’t keep overpriced multi-trillion-dollar assets elevated on its own, if other forces pull them down hard enough. It is, however, a variable that provides a constant pull upward for assets that are reasonably priced and doing well enough fundamentally.

**Beneficiary: Banks**

I continue to be bullish on regional and super-regional banks.

They’re reasonably priced, they have pretty conservative lending standards, and the Fed and Treasury are constantly paying them money. A decent chunk of the interest that the US federal government is paying out on its debt goes to the banking system, either directly via them holding over $4 trillion in Treasury securities, or indirectly via the Fed holding Treasury securities and paying interest on $3 trillion worth of reserves to the banks.

The amount of bank reserves in the system will now be structurally increasing, and these reserves are interest-bearing from the Fed to banks.

**Beneficiary: Emerging Markets**

Emerging markets typically use the dollar as a primary funding currency, and have substantial dollar-denominated debt (which, for the most part, is ironically not owed to the United States but rather is owed to entities in other countries).

When the dollar strengthens relative to other currencies, it squeezes them. In that situation, their liabilities are strengthening relative to the value of their assets and cashflows. In addition, the flow of dollar funding tends to be tight in that scenario, which is why the dollars are strengthening in the first place.

When the dollar weakens, it alleviates that stress. In that situation, their liabilities are weakening relative to the value of their assets and cashflows. The flow of dollar funding tends to be loose and abundant.

The United States continues to run large trade deficits and large fiscal deficits. Additionally, the Fed will now be monetizing $300-$400 billion of those deficits per year, and has a dovish tilt for the future direction of interest rates. All of this should continue to let dollars flow into the world, cheapening them. Additionally, as the AI capex boom runs into frictions and limitations, the flow of foreign capital into US equities is likely to slow.

I continue to be bullish on Latin American equities in general, from the Covid low point in 2020. Whether it’s Brazil’s largest bank or Panama’s airline, I continue to see a lot of value down there.

My view on precious metals continues to be this: I am concerned about the froth and near-term overbought status of them. However, I think most of this move was a structural repricing from an undervalued status to a fairly-valued status, rather than to an overvalued status.

Thus, I no longer view precious metals as offering the great asymmetric upside-vs-downside potential that they used to, when I started accumulating them in 2018 and 2019 alongside my equities.

Now they have a more balanced mix of upside and downside potential. Silver could easily jump to $100 or fall back to $40, for example. Neither would surprise me. Rebalancing or trimming is warranted, but that’s different than saying I’m bearish. I don’t view these metals as being fundamentally overvalued, but perhaps just over-enthused at the moment.

Thus, I continue to hold the metals physically and in portfolios, but would recommend caution about new capital going into them, and it’s worth checking your portfolio to see if you’re comfortable with your existing percentage allocation after such a strong run.
