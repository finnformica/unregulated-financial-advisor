## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to trend sideways, which I generally expect it to do until the remaining $200 billion in the reverse repo facility is drained. Right now, liquidity by this metric is on the low end of its multi-year channel:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-domestic-liquidity.png)

If we look in the banking system, one thing I find interesting is that 2024 has been the opposite of 2023 and 2022 in terms of relative liquidity. During the rate-hiking cycle of 2022 and 2023, liquidity drained out of small banks faster than big banks, until the spring 2023 regional bank crisis occurred. The Treasury and Fed both took actions starting then, and so overall bank liquidity has been flat-to-up since then.

But in 2023, liquidity gravitated toward big banks while small bank liquidity trended sideways. In 2024, the relative liquidity of small banks has improved, which reverses that 2022/2023 trend:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-bank-cash-levels.png)

Overall, I think we’re likely to see “status quo” for liquidity over the next few months based on the existing state of the system, assuming no major shocks from war or other outlier events.

**Global Liquidity**

**The Department of Government Efficiency: Initial Analysis**

In the aftermath of every election, there is a gap between what is said will be done vs what is actually done during the post-election administration. The size of that gap varies of course, and the upcoming administration has more potential variance than most others in modern history. Proponents would consider that great potential upside, and detractors would consider that great potential downside, but few would disagree that the uncertainty gap is larger than average.

Since my prior report, there have been a number of department nominations made by the Trump-elect team, which gives us more information to analyze. Some of those things are unrelated to investing and thus excluded in my analysis, and others are related to certain sectors (e.g. Health and Human Services, Defense, etc) which I can save for later reports.

Tariffs and undocumented immigrant deportations are two potential wildcards that in terms of potential economic/investment impact almost entirely come down to size of implementation. Tariffs implemented by Trump’s prior administration had impacts around the margins, but were not game-changers, since the numbers were not massive. This time he has advocated for larger ones, and says he intends to use them as a negotiation tactic (e.g. country XYZ could be threatened with massive tariffs unless they make certain concessions). Proposed deportation policies vary, with the lower end pointing to emphasizing deporting undocumented immigrants caught doing crime, and with the higher end pointing to mass deportation of millions and millions of people (with all the associated logistical and legal challenges of doing so). The larger end of these policies do of course have major economic and investment implications. As a base case I start with the “under” on any given policy (and thus take the “over” on the gap between proposals and outcomes, which is historically the case), but each individual one needs to be monitored as they progress.

But for this report, I’m going to focus on what I consider the proposal that most directly affects one of my core investment theses: the Department of Government Efficiency, or “DOGE” for short.

Those that have followed my work know that I’ve been emphasizing fiscal dominance since around 2019, along with the broader idea that the U.S. is running unusually large fiscal deficits, is very likely to continue doing so despite various statements by officials to the contrary, and that this has investment implications.  So, my view on this began under the prior Trump term, continued through the Biden term, and as of now I expect it to continue under the next Trump term as well.

Mainly, I’ve mostly avoided the long end of the bond curve (my mid-[2019 article on bonds likely being a bubble](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/) is my starting point for that), and indeed bonds have had basically their worst bear market in modern financial history in these 5+ years. Instead I’ve emphasized stocks, gold, bitcoin, and cash-equivalents or other short-duration paper. This included avoiding long-duration bonds at times where I highlighted risks of economic slowdowns, which goes against most consensus thinking but has been a successful strategy this far.

I continue to expect large fiscal deficits in my forward analysis, which tends to “run things hot” compared to the baseline that we would expect when looking at credit cycles and other private sector metrics alone.

However, Trump plans to set up a presidential advisory committee called the [Department of Government Efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Government_Efficiency) to address that. It’s not going to be an actual department since that requires an act of Congress, but rather it’ll be an advisory group led by Elon Musk and Vivek Ramaswamy. The stated goals are to streamline the government, improve efficiency, reduce the headcount, and cut the fiscal deficits. And notably, they plan to achieve the bulk of the outcome by July 4, 2026, which is symbolic since it’s the 250th anniversary of the country.

The proposed leaders of this advisory committee have said that federal headcount could be reduced to up to 75% and fiscal deficits could be reduced by up to $2 trillion. Here’s the current Wikipedia snapshot, although I’m only using it as a summary source since I’ve seen many of the various social media posts by the advisory committee leaders directly:

> Musk has suggested that the commission could help to cut the U.S. federal budget by up to US$2 trillion through measures such as reducing waste, abolishing redundant agencies, and downsizing the federal workforce. Ramaswamy also stated that DOGE may eliminate entire federal agencies and reduce the number of federal employees by as much as 75%.[7][8] Musk has also proposed consolidating the number of federal agencies from more than 400 to fewer than 100.[9]
>
> –[Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Department_of_Government_Efficiency)

The federal government has somewhere in the ballpark of [4.3 million employees](https://www.whitehouse.gov/wp-content/uploads/2024/03/ap_14_strengthening_fy2025.pdf). About 1.35 million of them are uniformed military, and I don’t expect much if any cuts there. Another 550,000 or so (0.55 million) are postal workers. So that’s 1.9 million uniformed military and postal workers, leaving 2.4 million federal employees in other categories.

![Federal Employees](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-federal-employees.png)

The number of contract employees that serve federal contracts has grown over time, and most sources I’ve seen place that number somewhere around 4 million, or up to 5 million at the high end. The spending on them in 2023 was [$759 billion](https://www.gao.gov/blog/snapshot-government-wide-contracting-fy-2023-interactive-dashboard), of which 60% was spent by the Department of Defense and 40% was spent by every other department combined.

So 2.4 million cuttable federal employees (let’s round up to 2.5 million, assuming post office could be trimmed) and 4-5 million federal contractors results in 6.5 to 7.5 million jobs as a realistic starting point.

The average compensation (salary + benefits) varies but is generally over six figures, and I’ll round up to $150k-$200k. So as a rough number, every 1 million jobs cut could result in $150-$200 billion less in spending. However, those employees do pay taxes on the pay they get from the government, so we can really only take 70-75% of that figure, so we’re more in the $110 billion to $150 billion range for every million people cut.

They also propose cutting some facilities as well, although that’s likely going to be rather minimal. To include facility reductions, we can err toward the higher end of $150 billion per million people cut.

If these numbers seem small, it’s because the biggest spending areas of the government are light on employees and are heavy on transfer payments. Medicare, Social Security, Interest Expense, Veterans Benefits,  and those sorts of programs are where the bulk of spending occurs, and which are very unpopular to cut ([source](https://www.cbpp.org/research/federal-budget/where-do-our-federal-tax-dollars-go)):

![Federal Budget](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-federal-budget.png)

The Social Security Administration and the Department of Health and Human Services (which houses Medicare and Medicaid) have under 150,000 employees combined despite accounting for a huge portion of federal expenditure. And Interest Expense isn’t a headcount issue either.

Promise 14 at the top of the [2024 Republican Platform](https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/2024-republican-party-platform) is:

> FIGHT FOR AND PROTECT SOCIAL SECURITY AND MEDICARE WITH NO CUTS, INCLUDING NO CHANGES TO THE RETIREMENT AGE

So they can’t really cut Medicare too much, can’t really cut Social Security at all, can’t really cut interest expense, can’t really cut Veterans Benefits, and the rest of the non-Defense stuff is not all that big (i.e. cut a million employees and consolidate some facilities and save $150 billion/year, back of the envelop).

That leaves the Department of Defense as the big serious honeypot for cutting. The problem there is that untangling a web of failed audits that big, with that many entrenched interests, is a Gordian knot. The defense contractors are major political donors. Basically every Congressperson who has a military base or a factory that produces military goods in their jurisdiction (which is a lot of them) is going to push back on eliminating or moving those facilities. The same is true for the smaller civilian facilities. Cutting every $100 billion increment out of the Department of Defense will be like pulling teeth from an angry tiger.

My initial conclusion from this analysis is that I will take the significant “under” on the proposal that up to 75% of federal jobs could be cut, or that up to $2 trillion in deficits could be saved. I will monitor the actions and progress of this and other things that affect fiscal deficits, but I maintain my thesis that U.S. fiscal deficits will likely be large going forward for many years, and that I will continue to use my existing investing framework unless I see significant evidence to the contrary that changes that view.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Bitcoin broke to fresh new highs since the prior report, and has at times reached over $99k:

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

So far, this rally looks moderate; none of the major on-chain indicators that I track are showing signs of major cycle-top sentiment extremes yet. A lot of the indicators currently look similar to March 2024, which resulted in a 7-month consolidation. However, in prior cycles, indicators like this went on to break to much more extreme levels before finding a multi-year top. I have a positive view on bitcoin price over the next year, but no particular view on the path or timing it follows along that way.

And I would remind readers that bitcoin can have rather violent corrections during bull runs, and so I would caution against excessive leverage or position sizes that are above your volatility tolerance.

I initially recommended bitcoin in April 2020 when it was about $6,900/coin. So it’s up about 14x since then and I continue to be long-term bullish. It’s had a lot of volatility along the way, so I’ve often recommend dollar-cost averaging into it, buying a bit extra on major capitulations when it’s out of favor, and avoiding excessive leverage or in most cases any leverage at all. The only method I’ve been comfortable in terms of leveraging bitcoin is to own some MicroStrategy (MSTR) stock, since they use longer-term non-callable leverage.

**MicroStrategy’s Surge**

In a report from six weeks ago, I highlighted that MicroStrategy’s high premium to NAV will likely result in significant bitcoin accumulation:

> This is a recursive flywheel that I will continue to monitor. A lot of people monitor ETF flows, which I think are important, but this specific combination of high premium to NAV and thus the incentive to keep issuing capital to buy more bitcoin, creates a big vacuum that will keep sucking up bitcoin until it doesn’t anymore.
>
> At the current issuance rate, about 170,000 new bitcoins are created per year. That number will get cut in half in 2028 with the automatic block subsidy halving that occurs every 210,000 blocks, but let’s focus on the next few years. MicroStrategy alone could suck in 50,000 to 100,000+ per year if the premium remains so high and there remains such significant demand for its capital.
>
> -October 13th premium report

Well, since then their coin accumulation has kicked into overdrive. Since that report, the company jumped from 252,000 coins to over 331,000 coins, with more on the way. That’s the fastest that they’ve ever accumulated. As a result, they managed to hit my base accumulation expectation for the year within the first six weeks. This has been one of the drivers of bitcoin’s recent price action.

And since they continue to have a high premium to NAV, they still have more fuel to keep accumulating more. However, ever since they reached over a 2x premium to NAV in March of this year, I’ve warned about potentially overpaying for it. I continue to own it, but I trim some out whenever it has parabolic runs, so I’ve already extracted more than the cost basis and let the rest run. This description from my March 17th premium report continues to reflect my view:

> The company’s management has been excellently allocating capital based on their changing stock valuation over time, and has grown their company tremendously because of it. They can issue normal debt, convertible debt, or equity as market conditions indicate, and have been doing so. Whenever the stock trades well above its net asset value, it basically represents excess demand for bitcoin, so the company issues new capital to absorb that demand with the right type of security, and buys more bitcoin with it. This increases the per-share amount of bitcoin. When market conditions are not favorable (i.e. the stock is not trading at a premium to NAV), they hold back with new capital issuance until conditions become more favorable.
>
> But investors have to be careful at these levels. Buyers of the stock are paying a big premium relative to bitcoin. I do think the company is justifiably worth more than its pure net asset value, but 2x net asset value is quite rich.
>
> Closed-end funds have a less flexible capital structure and often trade at a 10-20% discount to net asset value. Some of the best-managed and unique ones can trade at a 10-20% premium to net asset value. MSTR is in the best-managed category and has a more flexible capital structure than closed-end funds, so a 20% premium would be justifiable, in my view. I’ve happily held it during periods of 20%+ premiums over the past three and a half years. But a 100% premium is a lot.
>
> Probably a factor that is helping MSTR to surge to such high levels is that nearly 20% of the shares are currently sold short by bearish traders or hedgers. Conditions for a short squeeze have likely been occurring, in other words. And during a short squeeze, major valuation dislocations can temporarily occur.
>
> So I am of two minds about MSTR. I’m not bearish on it, because I’m long-term bullish on their underlying asset (bitcoin) and management’s ability to allocate capital to garner a premium on top of that asset. But at current levels, I prefer bitcoin to MSTR, due to the latter’s unusually large premium to net asset value.
>
> I advise caution at these levels, given the atypically large premium to net asset value.

Meanwhile, Semler Scientific (SMLR) [accumulated](https://ir.semlerscientific.com/news-releases/news-release-details/semler-scientificr-announces-btc-and-atm-activity-raised-215) more bitcoin for their corporate treasury, and Metaplanet made a [similar announcement](https://www.coindesk.com/business/2024/11/18/metaplanet-follows-mstrs-lead-announces-113m-debt-sale-for-additional-bitcoin-purchases/).

A shareholder proposal was submitted to Microsoft to add bitcoin to their balance sheet, to which the board of directors recommended voting against the proposal. As a recent update to that, Michael Saylor (executive chairman of MicroStrategy) will give a short 3-minute presentation to Microsoft’s board on why they should approve the proposal.

While the base case is that the proposal won’t pass, it’s pretty powerful to have that interaction occur. MicroStrategy is one of the few stocks that has outperformed Nvidia. Since MicroStrategy adopted their bitcoin strategy in August 2020, their stock is up over 30x which is *insane* (in comparison, Nvidia is “only” up 12x during that time). I’ve been holding it since August 2020 in some of the model portfolios, although I occasionally trim a bit out when it becomes excessively overweight.

## Deep Dive: Oilfield Service Companies Update

Every once in a while, I like to zoom in on an industry that’s not performing to my expectations and assess whether it’s a value opportunity or a potential value trap or change of thesis. In any sufficiently diversified portfolio, there are always a few trouble spots that might need some tune-ups now and then.

For this report, it’s worth checking in on oilfield service companies, which have been underperformers this year.

**Schlumberger/SLB**

Schlumberger/SLB (SLB) is the largest in this category, and has a presence in over 100 countries. Over the next several years, I expect long-cycle offshore oil production to be a leading area of growth, and SLB is well-positioned for that.

They focus their corporate strategy on three drivers of growth.

The first growth driver is “Core” and includes reservoir performance, well construction, and production systems. The company helps oil and gas producers optimize their production in terms of cost and environmental impact, and their ever-expanding technology portfolio helps them incrementally improve over time.

The second growth driver is “Digital” and includes a variety of software products and services. The company has been focusing on their Delphi digital platform, which is a SaaS platform that brings together evaluation and production software applications, enterprise data management, and AI. They also have solutions for on-site software for customers that need data to stay locally.

![Delphi](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-delphi.png)

The third and smallest growth driver is “New Energy” where the company applies its expertise to other types of energy including hydrogen generation and geothermal energy. This includes carbon capture and sequestration as well.

The company is reasonably-valued at around 13x earnings. This is down from around 17x earnings back in March.

![SLB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-slb-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Basically, there are near-term pressures on industry-wide growth. In my view, they’re likely overblown. Concerns come partially from weak Chinese demand, partially from stagnant U.S. production and the high likelihood for O&G consolidation in the country, and partly from the general observation that the oil market is well-supplied at the current time.

While those are real concerns, I view a 13x multiple as very reasonable relative to the level of expected growth and shareholder return that this company can generate over the long-term.

The company has an A credit rating by S&P, and has a rather low debt/income ratio. That strong financial foundation is a solid plus for the company.

There is some uncertainty around how much cost-reduction synergy the relatively expensive acquisition of ChampionX will achieve by consolidating headcount, facilities, and supply chains into that of SLB. This isn’t a make-or-break acquisition but it’s a relatively large one, and so if this acquisition finalizes, it’ll add some upside or downside variability to 2025 and 2026 quarterly results.

The stock put in a double-bottom in the $39-$40 range in mid-September and early-November, and is up 10% off that level. I have no firm conviction whether that will be the true bottom of the correction, but I do find it notable and promising so far. I’d like to see a breakout of over $46 to start putting in some higher-highs from this series of lower-highs.

**Haliburton**

Haliburton (HAL) is a smaller oilfield services company. They operate in two main segments.

The smaller segment is Drilling and Evaluation, which represents about 41% of revenue. This is exactly as it sounds: the company provides various solutions and services to producers to model, measure, drill, and optimize their wells.

The larger segment is Completion and Production, which provides various solutions and services for operating a well once it’s drilled such as cementing, completion, pipelining, stimulating, and other optimizations.

Compared to SLB, a larger share of Haliburton’s revenue is from North America (a bit under half). They have a BBB+ credit rating and trade at a lower valuation (10x-11x earnings, down from 12-13x earnings earlier this year):

![HAL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-hal-fastgraph.png)

Like SLB, HAL seems to be trying to put in a bottom, and for this stock it’s in the $27-$28 range.

Overall, while this industry has been disappointing in 2024, my structural view hasn’t diverged. There have been some slowdowns, but most of the majority of the underperformance has been due to valuation reductions from reasonable to downright cheap.

## Final Thoughts: EPD Breakout

For a spot of good news in the energy sector, my favorite income investment has finally broken out above its $30 resistance.

Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) is a leading midstream partnership, with vast transportation infrastructure for oil, natural gas, natural gas liquids, and petrochemicals. Their A- credit rating by S&P gives them the highest credit rating in the midstream sector, and they have 25 years of consecutive annual distribution growth through the various booms and busts of the American oil and gas industry.

They’ve been chopping along and steadily increasing in share price in recent years while paying out a 7% yield, so I’ve been comparing them favorably to Treasuries and other income/value type plays. However, $30 served as resistance for a while. Over the past month, the stock finally pushed firmly through $30 and quickly found itself over $32.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-11-24-epd-fastgraph.png)

I continue to be bullish on EPD as an income play for American investors.

Starting in 2023, non-American investors were given new tax complications for master limited partnerships like EPD, and so for those investors that can access the Alerian MLP ETF (AMLP), that can serve as a way to access the industry without the tax complications.
