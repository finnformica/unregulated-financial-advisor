# British American Tobacco (BTI) Deep Dive Analysis

*September 13, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

This report focuses on big picture updates, discusses recent and upcoming portfolio changes, and then analyzes British American Tobacco, a beaten-down value stock with a high dividend yield, with fundamentals that are better than the stock price would suggest on the surface.

## Macro Update

The United States is unfortunately starting to turn towards the “backward square root sign” jobs recovery that I described back in April.

![Square Root Call](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-square-root-call.png)

In an [early June report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2020-6-7/), when we only had data through May, I described it as follows:

> My base case since April has been what I’ve called, for lack of a better description, a “backward square root sign” recovery. This combines a fast V-shaped partial recovery when the pandemic shutdown ends and businesses are allowed to open, followed by a longer grind to get back up to full pre-pandemic employment levels. And this scenario, if it plays out, will be uneven by industry. Some will recover far faster than others, but that’s my base case for nationwide jobs overall.
>
> -Lyn Alden, June 9th

That’s exactly what we’re starting to see play out. Here’s the private jobs employment count from two different sources, with monthly data through the end of August:

![Private Payrolls](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-private-payrolls.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The government data in blue shows a deceleration in the rebound of private jobs for the past two months, but it’s still an upward arc. The private ADP data is more concerning, with a sharper deceleration and almost flat period over those past two months.

Both data sources show that the total number of private jobs bottomed in April during the shutdown, bounced back sharply through May and June, and then stagnated in July and August. The precise degree of that stagnation will be shown in future reports and backward-looking corrections.

Initial jobless claims and continued jobless claims remain very elevated. This is a weekly data source, so we get more frequent updates than the monthly numbers above:

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-jobles-claims.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The number of continued jobless claims peaked at over 24 million and has since gone down to 13-14 million. There was a slight uptick in the recent week, but that week doesn’t make a trend yet. The overall trend remains down, which is good, but we need to watch the next few weeks to see if it resumes down or chops sideways or up.

For comparison though, the peak number of continued claims in 2009 was less than 7 million, so the current amount is still about twice the 2009 maximum level. A lot of jobs have to come back to even get back to a normal recession level of job losses, let alone to get back to actual employment growth.

The pandemic unemployment assistance “PUA” program continues to confound with its high numbers. All of these charts above just focus on employed people, and they don’t count the self-employed “gig economy” income loss, which is harder to measure. According to the Bureau of Labor Statistics, there was somewhere in the ballpark of 10 million self-employed people in the United States at the start of this year, and of course many of them faced reductions in income from the recession.

Normally, independent contractors and other self-employed people don’t get unemployment benefits. However, due to the scale of this economic shock, the CARES Act provided the pandemic unemployment assistance program for them, which lets them receive unemployment payments as well. Over 14 million people used the program simultaneously at its peak so far, which compared to the number of people that the BLS recognizes as self-employed, is an interesting statistic. A number of sources have said that [some degree of fraud](https://www.politico.com/states/california/story/2020/09/10/california-fraud-likely-driving-suspicious-spike-in-unemployment-claims-1316454) could be involved.

All-in-all, there are roughly 30 million people receiving some sort of unemployment assistance in any given week, and this number has been virtually static since the beginning of May, rather than decreasing. As fewer people filed for normal continued unemployment claims, more people filed for PUA claims.

![Total Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-total-jobless-claims.png)

*Data Source: [U.S. Employment and Training Administration](https://oui.doleta.gov/unemploy/claims_arch.asp)*

**Fiscal Cliff: Still Falling Off**

At the end of July, most of the CARES Act stimulus was already spent, including the one-time stimulus checks, the PPP loans that mostly get forgiven and therefore turn into grants, the corporate bailouts, and the extra federal unemployment bonuses on top of the normal state unemployment checks.

Both sides of Congress have been planning for months to pass another round of stimulus, but it has been held up in political gridlock. So, the Federal Reserve’s balance sheet stopped skyrocketing, and the broad money supply stopped soaring, back in June. There aren’t really any more massive government transfers occurring.

![Money Printing Stopped](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-money-printing-stopped.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

My long-term expectation is for very large deficits over the next several years, with significant portions of those deficits monetized by the Federal Reserve, and an eventual trend shift from secular disinflation to secular inflation. However, this will likely come in bursts and stops, and right now, we’re in a stop.

Therefore, disinflationary pressures are back in control right now, the pace of recovery is likely to slow or possibly reverse, and the stock market can’t point to “money printing” as its basis for rising rapidly like it could in prior months.

The TIPS market saw this as well. The market’s inflation expectations were rising sharply from the March lows through August, but peaked for now, right as August ended:

![Inflation Expectations](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-breakevens.png)

Now, to some extent this is relative. The U.S. federal government still ran a $200 billion deficit in August, which is $2.4 trillion annualized.

However, it pales in comparison to the numbers they were running earlier this year during the rebound in inflation expectations. For the next chart set, the top chart is the federal receipts/outlays for August, and the bottom chart is for cumulative fiscal year 2020 so far (which began in October 2019):

![Fiscal Budget](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-fiscal-deficit.png)

*Chart Source: [Monthly Treasury Report](https://www.fiscal.treasury.gov/reports-statements/mts/current.html)*

In other words, as far as broad inflation is concerned, I’m temporarily in the disinflation camp until the next round of fiscal stimulus happens.

![Rookie Numbers](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-rookie-numbers.png)

We’ll see what happens with fiscal policy through the rest of September. My base case for the past few months was that Congress would likely pass another $1+ trillion stimulus bill by this time in September, but it seems that the probability of them passing a large bill prior to the election is quickly shrinking at this point.

So, this is a very uncertain time, and I’m inclined towards defense until given a reason otherwise from the fiscal side. It appears increasingly likely that a market correction or increased civil unrest may be what dictates the timing of the next round of fiscal stimulus. Politicians might not pass further stimulus until they’re basically forced to by their constituents and donors, whereas before, they were signalling that they might front-run the need for it.

Through July, national personal income went \*up\* rather than down in this recession, due to government transfer payments that more than filled in the lost income from employment, on average.

![Personal Income](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-personal-income.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/PI)*

That’s what has made this recession unlike any other, and pushed back any sort of widespread insolvency and deleveraging event from happening so far. Government transfer payments for this recession are unlike any other, both in terms of magnitude and percent change:

![Transfer Payments](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-transfer-payments.png)

*Chart Source: [St. Louis Fed](https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/B087RC1Q027SBEA)*

But, those bigger transfer payments are now basically on hold.

Small businesses are getting less optimistic about survival. Their optimism ticked up during the stimulus period in the spring, leveled out in early summer, and began falling in the past two months when fiscal stimulus ended. This stagnation trend in July and August mirrors the previously-described backward square root sign in payroll employment numbers. With the monetized fiscal spending taps off, the economy is over-leveraged and weak.

![Small Business Survival](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-small-business-survival.png)

**Put Selling**

This is a great environment for selling cash-secured put options. My approach for this involves selling options on stocks or ETFs for which I’d actually be happy to buy, if they were to drop in price.

The reason it’s such a great environment for that is due to high volatility (meaning high option premiums) and relatively cheap valuations in certain value or commodity industries. If the stock or ETF doesn’t drop to the desired level, the option seller gets to keep the premium, and if it does drop to that level, they get to keep the premium and are assigned the obligation to buy the shares at the strike price, almost like a limit order that you were paid to do.

It’s important to only do it with stocks or ETFs you actually want to own. If you sell options on stocks or ETFs you don’t want to own, then you are basically capping your upside and exposing yourself to a lot of downside risk, which isn’t great.

It’s a useful strategy for contrarian folks who want to pre-plan to buy a dip at a specified price in their stocks or ETFs of choice, and get paid to wait for that potential dip, rather than chase current price levels. If you’re long-term bullish on particular stock or ETF, but think a period of correction or consolidation is probable, it can work well.

For this upcoming week, I’m looking to sell some cash-secured puts on GDX for expiration in January 2021, at a $37-$39 strike price, depending on price action early in the week. These options currently pay 6-8% in premiums for a 4-month period, which is pretty good for being notably below the current share price of $41+. In other words, I’m happy to set some capital aside that gets paid to wait to buy a potential dip in gold miners from current price levels (which already dipped about 10% to $41 from their $44-$45 peak high this year for GDX).

## British American Tobacco (BTI) Deep-Dive Analysis

In the previous stock report, I analyzed fast-growing and high-flying Tencent, the Chinese internet conglomerate. This report focuses on the complete other end of the spectrum; slow growth, deep value, defensive, and old-school.

British American Tobacco PLC ADR (NYSE: BTI) is the focus stock of this week’s report. It’s a London-based company but I’ll use the dollar-based ADR that trades on the NYSE as my base security. And in this report I’ll refer to the company by their common nickname, BAT.

![BTI Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-price.png)

In 2017, BAT acquired Reynolds American, which was the second biggest tobacco company in the United States, and this acquisition contributed significantly to them being the global tobacco conglomerate that they are today, since they were already one of the largest ex-USA tobacco companies prior to the acquisition. However, they likely overpaid for the acquisition, and now they are in the process of consolidating and reducing their debt levels from it.

The company has well-known brands like Camel, Lucky Strike, Newport, and Pall Mall. They’ve been diversifying into newer products with new brands like glo, which is a heat-not-burn electronic product that is popular in Japan and elsewhere.

For the full year 2019, the company earned £25.877 billion in revenue. Of that, £10.373 billion was from the United States, £4.261 billion was from the rest of the Americas and sub-Saharan Africa, £6.090 billion was from Europe and North Africa, and £5.153 billion was from Asia Pacific and the Middle East.

This broad geographic exposure reduces jurisdictional and cultural risk for the company, and makes them into a worldwide play on tobacco consumption. The tobacco industry consists of cash-cow consumer business that aren’t very economically cyclical, and BAT is no exception.

**Slow-Growing, But Cheap**

Here’s a quiz. Cigarette smoking and general tobacco usage has been in secular decline in many places, and particularly in the United States, for decades. Did tobacco stocks perform strongly or poorly in this environment?

Most people would assume they did poorly, and that it would have been better to invest in other industries instead. And yet, Altria, the dominant tobacco company in the United States, was among the [top 5 performing stocks](https://www.investopedia.com/articles/investing/022716/5-best-performing-stocks-last-20-years-gmcr-celg.asp) over the past 20 years in terms of total returns, including re-invested dividends and its various spin-offs. Going back further to the early/mid 1990’s, their returns were outright silly. It almost looks like a Bitcoin chart:

![MO Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-mo-performance.png)

BAT, with its international emphasis, wasn’t that degree of an outlier, but they also soundly beat the S&P 500 since the mid-1990’s, even after the recent major pullback in valuation:

![BTI Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-performance.png)

When something is cheap and out of favor, but pays high dividends and has strong returns on invested capital, it can do extremely well for patient investors. There has been a pullback recently, but that potentially presents another buying opportunity.

Furthermore, for tobacco companies with global exposure, there is less secular tobacco decline. In many places of the world, tobacco usage unfortunately continues to increase, both in percentage terms and in absolute numbers. Strong pricing/brand power, high profits margins, high dividends, and low equity valuations, can make for a lucrative long-term investment even in this sluggish old-school industry.

**Management Quality**

Many athletes, bodybuilders, and fitness models/actors use an approach of bulking up and cutting down in cycles. I did this for some years myself during my mixed martial arts days.

During a bulk-up phase, these athletes focus mainly on mass accumulation, including both muscle and fat. And then during a cutting phase, they reduce fat while preserving most of the muscle. The result after multiple cycles of this over the long run is that they build up muscle while cutting away fat periodically, to get ripped.

For the investing analogue, BAT is currently in a cutting phase. They bulked up with their 2017 acquisition of Reynolds American, and now they are streamlining, deleveraging, reducing management layers, consolidating reporting groups, and making sure their brand portfolio is focused rather than too spread out.

Tobacco stocks often have weird balance sheets. They earn a lot of income but are not very capital intensive, and are highly dependent on brand recognition. So, when you factor out intangible assets, they usually have negative equity, since they lever up based on income rather than assets. Their debt/income and debt/free-cash-flow ratios, which I think are the most useful balance sheet metrics for this industry, are pretty solid for the most part.

Depending on precisely how you measure it (meaning over which time period, and either net income or free cash flow), BTI has a 5-7x debt-to-income or debt-to-FCF ratio, which isn’t great, but also is not bad for such a noncyclical industry. Their credit rating is BBB+, which is better than competitor Altria (MO) but not as good as competitor Philip Morris (PM). Furthermore, a lot of the current debt for BAT was due to the 2017 Reynolds American acquisition, and since the end of 2017, they have been gradually de-leveraging, so the balance sheet is strengthening.

**Valuation Analysis**

Looking at the stock price alone, one would assume that the company has performed poorly in recent years. However, that’s not really the case; the fundamental situation in fine, and instead there has been a big drawdown from higher-than-usual valuations to near-record-low valuations in a brief period of time. This also happened to other tobacco stocks over the same time period, and was not unique to BAT.

More broadly than that, this is part of a broader trend. Value stocks recently have had their worst 10-year cumulative under-performance vs growth stocks in the past century. The 2000 dotcom bubble rivaled this period, and the Great Depression was in third place, in terms of how badly growth stocks outperformed value stocks over a ten-year period. Truly outstanding growth-vs-value differentials are happening here by historical standards, and a lot of it is due to valuation differences rather than just fundamentals.

BAT stock now has a very low P/E ratio, P/S ratio, dividend yield, and so forth compared to normal history. They currently have both a price/earnings ratio and a dividend yield that are in the high single digits. And this is at a time of record low interest rates, where the difference between BAT’s earnings yield or dividend yield compared to a 10-year sovereign bond yield is arguably bigger than ever.

Here’s the price/sales ratio, which shows how expensive they got in 2016 and 2017, vs how cheap they are now, compared to their fundamentals:

![BTI ps ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-ps-ratio.png)

And here’s dividend yield, which goes back longer in YCharts data, and is the second-lowest they’ve been in decades:

![BTI Dividend Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-dividend-yield.png)

The F.A.S.T. Graph for BAT is very bullish for contrarian investors. Analyst-consensus fundamentals are looking solid while the stock price sank over the past three years:

![BTI FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-fastgraph.png)

It’s basically the opposite of what tech/growth stocks like Apple are currently doing, where their fundamentals are solid but their stock prices outright doubled in short periods of time to far higher-than-normal valuations:

![AAPL FAST Graph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-AAPL-fastgraph.png)

In fact, BAT is over 50% down from its highs, which is the second biggest drawdown for the stock in modern history:

![BTI Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-drawdown.png)

Considering that the balance sheet strength, revenue levels, and profitability are fine, this has a decent probability of being a great long-term investment from current low levels.

I showed earlier in this report that BAT outperformed the S&P 500 over the past 25 years by a sizable margin if purchased in the mid-1990’s which is where my data go back to, but if one had specifically bought during the spring 2000 period where BAT had deeply underperformed (similarly to how it is underperforming now), its 20-year returns from that point compared to broad markets would have been even more staggering:

![BTI Returns from March 2000](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-march-2000-return.png)

Now, I’m not saying BAT stock will perform like that over the next 20 years. However, I think for contrarian investors, this does present a somewhat similar circumstance to that period in 2000, even if it’s just 1/4th as good.

If mean reversion takes a while, investors get a 7% dividend yield while they wait. The only main danger for investors, would be a scenario where fundamentals deteriorate notably compared to consensus expectations, which I outline in the risk section. So, the key is to focus on fundamentals.

Here is a rather conservative/bearish StockDelver analysis. If the company has slightly negative volume growth going forward, offset by pricing power that keeps pace with inflation, and the stock just chugs along with high dividends and no share buybacks, and ends the period with a P/E ratio of around 10x, then it is currently fairly-valued for 10% annualized returns going forward:

![BTI StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-bti-stockdelver.png)

More realistically, they should have stronger pricing power than that, should get a valuation bump over time as they reduce debt, and at some point after debt is reduced, they can go back to  doing share buybacks, which are beneficial for per-share EPS and dividend growth. Analysts currently expect an average of 6% earnings growth for BAT over the next few years; I used 1% annual growth for this valuation model.

BAT doubled their dividend from 2010 to 2017, which was their pre-acquisition period. So, that was a low double-digit annual dividend growth rate, whereas I have a pessimistic 1% forward annual dividend growth rate in the model. Most of their cash went towards dividends during that 2010-2017 period, and they also bought back shares regularly, which at low valuations for those purchases, was very beneficial for per-share metrics including per-share dividend growth. After BAT fully digests the 2017 acquisition and deleverages more, I think we could see a period like that again, although likely not to the same degree.

**Company Risks**

*Tobacco Decline Risk*

In most developed countries, especially in the United States, tobacco usage is on the decline. That’s good for public health, but not great for tobacco companies. However, smoking rates are pretty high in Europe, North Africa, and throughout Asia.

If tobacco usage worldwide declines at a faster structural rate than analyst expectations, BAT’s potential shareholder returns would be reduced.

Emerging markets are a source of growth for the tobacco industry, because as people get more discretionary income, they can afford to smoke international name brand products. Emerging markets are also a source of volatility, though, and for example in 2020 due to the global recession, those markets are weighting on BAT rather than benefiting them.

*Regulation Risk*

Tobacco companies face increasing regulatory pressure worldwide, in order to improve public health.

In particular, banning flavored tobacco is a big trend recently, and this includes menthol cigarettes in some jurisdictions. In the United States, menthol cigarettes account for about a third of cigarettes sales.

During the peak period of the pandemic earlier this year, South Africa temporarily banned cigarette sales entirely.

These regulations sometimes backfire in unintended ways. South Africa, for example, experienced a [boom in illegal cigarette smuggling](https://apnews.com/6a1f1be855bca641271034cca3b2d647), because smokers didn’t quit during the ban; they just went through the black market instead.

In the United States, menthol cigarettes are statistically consumed by African Americans far more than any other ethnicity. So, a ban on menthol cigarettes, followed by a likely uptick in illegal cigarette trade that would occur, could very well dis-proportionally add to arrests among the black community, which is already a heated social topic in the United States.

Furthermore, it was [announced earlier this year](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-britishamericantobacco-investigation/british-american-tobacco-under-criminal-probe-by-u-s-regulators-the-times-idUSKCN21W01E) that BAT is under a criminal probe by the U.S. Department of Justice for potentially violating sanctions. Which country or countries they were alleged to violate the sanctions for, is not yet public. This is an extra variable for uncertainty, since we don’t know if they did it, and what the financial consequences would be if they did.

*Innovation Risk*

The tobacco industry is not exactly the most innovative industry, but it’s currently undergoing one of its larger trend shifts in its history. Traditional cigarettes are partially being replaced by vapor and heat-not-burn products, as well as marijuana.

The vast majority of BAT revenue comes from traditional cigarettes. However, as that industry stagnates, vaping, tobacco heating products (“THP”, such as BAT’s glo brand), and new forms of oral tobacco, take some new market share. People still want tobacco, but the vehicle for delivery can change over time. We can see the growth in BAT’s newer products from a smaller base, offsetting some of the stagnation and decline in other areas:

![BAT Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-13-BAT-growth.png)

*Chart Source: BAT 2019 Annual Report*

So, for BAT to maintain market share against other big tobacco companies, they must deploy new products to retain market share in newer industries. They are doing that well currently, but there’s no guarantee that they will continue to be successful.

To reduce the risks of poor innovation or criminal probes, investors that want larger tobacco stock positions should consider diversifying into other tobacco companies as well, like MO and PM. Having two or three companies to fill out a larger tobacco industry allocation within a portfolio reduces single-company risk.

*Currency Risk*

BAT receives revenue in currencies from around the world, and reports results in pound sterling. American investors in the ADR then get those results translated into dollars. Due to the global nature of their revenue, pretty much any investor faces some degree of currency risk with the investment.

As someone with a long-term dollar bearish outlook from current levels (but near-term neutrality), I like the company’s broad currency exposure for a potential diversifier. However, dividend investors with expenses in a given currency must take currency risk into account in their investment portfolios. I’m also personally somewhat bearish on the pound sterling, so overall I’m rather neutral on BAT’s long-term currency exposure here.

## Summary

With highly-valued markets, a relative hiatus on fiscal stimulus and money-printing, and seven weeks until a U.S. presidential election that is likely to be contested, I’m more on the defensive side at the moment. That doesn’t mean we can’t have another big shoot higher, but given the odds, I think investors would do well to ensure that their portfolio is well-balanced for their own unique risk tolerance.

I prefer the value factor over the growth factor, prefer international equity exposure (more-so emerging markets and Japan, less-so Europe) along with my U.S. equity exposure, and am still rather bullish on alternatives like precious metals and Bitcoin, especially after their recent corrections and consolidations. While I’m not a big fan of cash and Treasuries in general for the long run, I like having them in this particular environment for some potential counter-cyclical buying opportunities that we might get.
