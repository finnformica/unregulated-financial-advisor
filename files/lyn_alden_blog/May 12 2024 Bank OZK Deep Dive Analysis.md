## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic banking liquidity continues to be rangebound. Around $5.8 trillion has been the floor since 2022, at which point the Treasury market (October 2022) or the banking system (March 2023) runs into problems and gets supported:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-domestic-liquidity-annotated.png)

The Treasury is targeting a $750 billion general account balance by the end of June, and a $850 billion balance by the end of September. They’re currently running on the high end at $851 billion, and were recently over $900 billion for a few weeks. This is their account that they have at the Federal Reserve, which they use for general spending. Any money that is in there is basically out of the financial system, and so a higher balance is negative for liquidity:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-tga.png)

The reverse repo facility has been flat over the past two months, which is neutral for liquidity. When it goes down, it puts liquidity back into the system:

![RRP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-rrp.png)

And of course, the Fed’s ongoing balance sheet reduction is steadily negative for liquidity. However, that will begin tapering off.

The Fed’s latest FOMC meeting occurred in early May, and they announced that they would hold rates steady and reduce the rate of quantitative tightening.

For the rates aspect, the Fed’s Chairman Powell has signaled that they intend to hold rates at the current level for a while longer, due to multiple months of data showing that inflation has been sticky and/or re-accelerating. However, he showed no significant probability of rate hikes any time in the foreseeable future either.

For the balance sheet aspect, the Fed announced that they will reduce the rate of monthly Treasury security tapering from $60 billion to $25 billion. For their current balance sheet reduction, the Fed is not selling securities, but rather when they mature they are not reinvesting them. And so each month the amount that matures varies a bit, and the Fed has had a policy of letting a maximum of $60 billion mature without reinvestment per month. By changing it to $25 billion, it means they will reinvest a larger portion of maturing bonds and thus slow their rate of balance sheet decline. They will continue to let mortgage-backed securities mature without reinvestment at the current maximum of $35 billion per month.

Overall, this was a slightly dovish outcome, and by 2025 we will probably see a bottom in the Fed’s balance sheet and a resumption in growth of the balance sheet in line with GDP growth, regardless of whether inflation is down to the target level or not. That’s what I expect from the bank reserve data, and is what the New York Fed is modeling:

![Projected Soma](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-projected-soma.png)

Global liquidity is also rangebound. Credit creation globally is middling, and the strong dollar weighs it down:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/permium-2024-5-12-global-m2.png)

I expect that by 2025 we’ll see a rise in global liquidity, coinciding with the bottoming of the Fed’s balance sheet. However, the timing of that may shift a bit, and will be a subject of ongoing observation. For now, the trend is rangebound.

**U.S. Business Cycle Update**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy was decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, entered stall speed for a while, but is starting to show signs of stabilization. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as most of them are now except for construction spending and to some extent retail spending), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms. Construction spending has been getting a big fiscal boost from the Inflation Reduction Act.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Foreign Headlines**

Whenever I see a given macroeconomic topic get nonstop chatter on social media, it usually means that it’s near the end of its current move. In recent weeks, the dollar/yen exchange rate got a lot of social media attention, since the yen was sharply weakening relative to the dollar to levels not seen in over thirty years.

I covered this in my prior report:

> Meanwhile, Japan’s yen-to-dollar exchange rate broke out to fresh multi-decade highs (meaning a weaker yen):
>
> ![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-4-28-usdjpy.png)
>
> Japan currently has 0.1% short term interest rates despite experiencing above-target inflation, at a time when most other developed countries have 4-5% interest rates or higher. This makes holding yen unattractive, and thus hurts the exchange rate. The Bank of Japan has thus far chosen not to meaningfully tighten monetary policy in the face of this, although they previously intervened when the exchange rate moved faster than they prefer.
>
> That’s the problem with being in fiscal dominance, which applies even more to Japan than the United States. Japan has such large government debts (over 250% public debt to GDP) that if they raise interest rates meaningfully in line with the rest of the developed world, it would blow out their public interest expense to huge levels, which in large part flows to the private sector and is nominally stimulatory and would require support from the central bank.
>
> If Japan wants to defend its exchange rate at some point again, they could sell some U.S. Treasuries and buy back some yen. They have a strong current account surplus and accumulated positive net international investment position, and are the biggest foreign holder of U.S. Treasuries in the world. Thus far, the yen has weakened because they have *allowed* it to weaken. They have plenty of ammo to deploy if the devaluation gets too disorderly for their preferences, even if interest rates are not necessarily the ideal tool for them to use.
>
> -April 28, 2024 premium report

Hours after my report came out late on April 28th (early April 29th their time), Japan indeed intervened by selling dollar assets and buying back the yen. The Financial Times estimated that it was around [$35 billion](https://www.ft.com/content/49fd2484-f9c9-4181-88c1-064e787e8a64) worth of intervention.

The effects from that were short-lived, so on May 1st (early May 2nd their time), Japan came in with a second invention of what [looks like around $23 billion](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-05-02/boj-accounts-signal-japan-s-second-yen-intervention-in-fed-week) that had more of a cumulative impact.

![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-usdjpy.png)

The success of a currency intervention can be judged with a couple different measurements.

One measure of success is to quantify how much the intervention improved the exchange rate relative to the money spent. Dropping the dollar/yen exchange rate from 160 to 155 might not seem like a lot, but that’s a 3% improvement on the value of Japan’s total money supply relative to the dollar, and Japan’s 1.6 quadrillion yen M3 money supply was worth about $10 trillion USD at the peak exchange rate of 160 USDJPY. Moving a $10 trillion money supply’s value by 3% is about $300 billion worth of total currency strengthening, for less than $60 billion spent on intervention. You can narrow that a bit by focusing on M2, or broadening it by including the whole bond market, but either way it’s a decent ratio. However, it remains to be seen how long that strengthening will hold.

A second measure of success is to see if it broke the trend and put two-way risk back into the market. If market participants believe that there is no risk to borrowing yen at low interest rates and using it to buy higher-yielding dollars or other non-yen assets, then they will keep doing so more and more aggressively. Borrowing yen in a fractional reserve system creates more yen, and so that’s a cumulative attack on the currency. The willingness to occasionally do sharp interventions makes it so that speculators have to think twice before leveraging themselves too much. In other words, the currency may keep devaluing over time, but the speed of the devaluation can be slowed down because the vicious cycle of more and more leveraged shorts gets disrupted from time to time and can’t feed on itself so quickly.

The root of the currency problem is that Japan has over 250% public debt to GDP, which puts them firmly into fiscal dominance. They can’t afford positive real interest rates on a sustained basis, which limits their ability to perform adequate monetary policy.

When government debts and deficits become structurally large and fiscal dominance takes hold, capital controls and financial repression tend to become commonplace. But the severity can differ significantly from country to country, and recent technologies (e.g. social media and open-source money) can make them less effective than they used to be.

Japan has one of the largest net international investment positions in the world, meaning that Japan owns more foreign assets than the foreign sector collectively owns Japanese assets, by a wide margin. In addition to the government selling its holdings to finance their interventions, Japan could increase regulations on large domestic pools of capital to force them to hold more domestic assets and fewer foreign assets. But it’s unclear to what extent that might occur or what the timing might be.

So, that whole set of topics regarding capital controls and regulation of pools of capital toward domestic assets is a nonlinear overlay on top of what is otherwise a pretty mechanical outcome of ongoing yen devaluation.

**MicroStrategy Price Reset**

Back in March, MicroStrategy (MSTR) became overvalued, in that it was trading at more than a 2x premium relative to its underlying net asset value. It’s fine for it to trade moderately above its net asset value due to the advantages that the company has, but more than a 100% premium is hard to justify.

Just within the period from February to March, MicroStrategy’s stock price relative to the price of bitcoin had more than doubled. A lot of bitcoin proponents on social media were talking about how this new valuation was actually justifiable, that it makes sense, etc. Sentiment was way too high.

So in my March 17th report, I discussed how this surge in valuation increased investors’ risk a lot. I announced that I would sell more than half of the MicroStrategy position from the No Limits portfolio, and that I would remove MicroStrategy from the Top 12 stocks list on March 18th. It’s not that I’m bearish on the company’s fundamentals or operators (quite the contrary); it’s that the valuation just temporarily got too high relative to bitcoin.

> As of Friday’s close, it is up $1,782/share. But now it’s time to re-assess the stock’s sentiment and valuation.
>
> MSTR holds 205,000 bitcoin, which at current bitcoin prices is worth about $14 billion in total. The underlying software business was valued at under $1 billion in early 2020, which we can round up to probably $1 billion or more now. The market capitalization after this price surge is about $30 billion, and the enterprise value is well over $32 billion. The company is now trading at a big premium to the bitcoin it holds and relative to the value of the software business.
>
> The company’s management has been excellently allocating capital based on their changing stock valuation over time, and has grown their company tremendously because of it. They can issue normal debt, convertible debt, or equity as market conditions indicate, and have been doing so. Whenever the stock trades well above its net asset value, it basically represents excess demand for bitcoin, so the company issues new capital to absorb that demand with the right type of security, and buys more bitcoin with it. This increases the per-share amount of bitcoin. When market conditions are not favorable (i.e. the stock is not trading at a premium to NAV), they hold back with new capital issuance until conditions become more favorable.
>
> But investors have to be careful at these levels. Buyers of the stock are paying a big premium relative to bitcoin. I do think the company is justifiably worth more than its pure net asset value, but 2x net asset value is quite rich.
>
> Closed-end funds have a less flexible capital structure and often trade at a 10-20% discount to net asset value. Some of the best-managed and unique ones can trade at a 10-20% premium to net asset value. MSTR is in the best-managed category and has a more flexible capital structure than closed-end funds, so a 20% premium would be justifiable, in my view. I’ve happily held it during periods of 20%+ premiums over the past three and a half years. But a 100% premium is a lot.
>
> Probably a factor that is helping MSTR to surge to such high levels is that nearly 20% of the shares are currently sold short by bearish traders or hedgers. Conditions for a short squeeze have likely been occurring, in other words. And during a short squeeze, major valuation dislocations can temporarily occur.
>
> So I am of two minds about MSTR. I’m not bearish on it, because I’m long-term bullish on their underlying asset (bitcoin) and management’s ability to allocate capital to garner a premium on top of that asset. But at current levels, I prefer bitcoin to MSTR, due to the latter’s unusually large premium to net asset value.

**MicroStrategy Orange Announcement**

Turning from stock price to company fundamentals, MicroStrategy held their annual event in early May.

At the conference, MicroStrategy announced a new product concept called MicroStrategy Orange. It’s a suite of services built on an open-source identity verification specification called the Bitcoin DID Inscription Method (did:btc). The draft spec is [available on their GitHub](https://microstrategy.github.io/did-btc-spec/). Here are some key snippets:

> The Bitcoin Inscription DID method (`did:btc`) uses the bitcoin blockchain exclusively to store and retrieve DID information. UTXOs on chain are used to control DIDs. Inscribing data in the witness of transactions allows for greater extensibility and verbosity when creating DID documents, while reducing fees and block space consumed.
>
> This specification conforms to the requirements specified in the DID specification [DID-CORE] currently published by the W3C Credentials Community Group.
>
> […]
>
> The goal of this method is to deliver trustless, tamper-proof, and long-lived decentralized identities using only the public bitcoin blockchain as a data source, while enabling full compatibility with the [DID-CORE] specification and forward compatibility for future extensions.
>
> `did:btc` leverages the inherent characteristics of the Bitcoin blockchain, including its decentralized architecture and immutable ledger, to ensure the tamper-resistance and distributed storage and retrieval of identity-related data.

There have been other DID methods that use the Bitcoin blockchain, but this version by MicroStrategy makes more use of on-chain space. Prior methods rely on URLs or IPFS which are not necessarily permanent, whereas this method is entirely on-chain. Thus, it is more fee-intensive but more immutable and secure than those other methods. In addition, this method makes a distinction between a controller of a DID and the subject of a DID, which is geared toward an enterprise context where the IT/security department can control the DID and allow for things like key rotation and key revocation, while the subject controls their own keys for signing.

As an example use-case, an enterprise can deploy this to give everyone at their company private keys, which can be used to hash and sign an email, to prove that the email came from them and the content was not altered from when they signed it. This can cut down on phishing attacks, which is relevant in a world where AI is making phishing easier and more effective to do over time. It connects to Outlook and similar business software.

![MicroStrategy Orange](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-microstrategy-orange.png)

*Source: MicroStrategy World 2024*

There are many other key methods with varying levels of use, but they generally don’t scale well and tend not to be very interoperable. This method is conceivably fast to deploy to a large number of employees, is open-source and thus interoperable, and is tailored toward an enterprise environment. And beyond email, it could be used for social media verification, online document signing, and other applications.

So, it remains to be seen to what extent this product will be launched and gather traction among MicroStrategy’s clients, but I think it’s an interesting specification, and is something I’ll be watching going forward. However, if it broadly catches on, some of its aspects might need to be transferred to a layer-two solution for increased scalability.

In my prior report, I re-iterated that Bitcoin at its core is the most decentralized and immutable database ever built. The killer use case of a decentralized, immutable, open-source, globally-accessible database is to use it as a money ledger, but secondary use cases can involve anything for which some sort of immutable timestamp or reference is useful, such as DIDs or stored pieces of important content.

And for people who have read [*Broken Money*](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR), I described this type of use case on pages 377-380 in the “Energy is the Arbiter of Truth” section at the end of chapter 24:

> In other words, Bitcoin is a network where humans let energy be the arbiter of truth for transaction processing, rather than a centralized entity like a bank or a technology company or a government. It’s automated accounting, and represents a ledger suitable for the 21st century. Other digital realms that we interact with can be connected to the Bitcoin network in various ways to inherit some of its scarcity, so that they can benefit from the borderless flow of value and connect with a reliable record of past events, while reducing spam and impersonation.
>
> For example, someone can create a public/private key pair, embed the public key into the Bitcoin blockchain with a real-world resource cost due to transaction fees, verify to many people in person that this key is indeed theirs, and use their private key that goes along with that public key to sign any digital content that they create or authorize. The same is true for any large journalist organization with known integrity standards. From that point on, this allows people to differentiate forgeries from genuine content (at least to the extent that it was created by that individual or organization, not necessarily that it’s objectively true), and it makes it so that a million impersonators cannot use similar key pairs that have been embedded, with real-world expense, into the Bitcoin blockchain.
>
> In a world of sufficiently advanced artificial intelligence and virtually costless impersonation or forgeries, we might learn to demand proof-of-continuity (cryptographic public/private key pairs) and proof-of-work (evidence that real-world resources were expended to add weight to a given public/private key pair), before taking certain types of digital content seriously.

**Taproot Assets over Lightning**

One of the notable speakers at the MicroStrategy conference was David Marcus, the former president of PayPal. After PayPal, Marcus went on to work at Meta to head their messenger division and then their Novi division, with the latter being the division that attempted to launch stablecoins but ran into regulatory challenges. Marcus is currently the co-founder and CEO of Lightspark, which is focused on building out Lightning solutions for enterprises.

Marcus, with his extensive background in payments, believes that the Bitcoin settlement layer and the Lightning payment layer, along with other layers built on top, will grow into the dominant global value and payment technologies respectively, not just for moving bitcoin but for moving other currencies as well. In a fireside chat with Michael Saylor, Marcus described Bitcoin as “the neutral language of value on the internet”, and talked about how it can be a protocol that different currency blocs can refer to without trusting each other. Coinbase recently integrated Lightning capability using Lightspark’s solution.

Related to this theme, Olaoluwa Osuntokun, the CTO of Lightning Labs, sent the first mainnet Taproot Asset transaction over Lightning in early May. Taproot Assets were previously available for on-chain transactions, but now are becoming available to move over Lightning channels.

The Taproot Asset protocol allows for tokens to be created on the Bitcoin network, and are efficient compared to other token protocols because most of the information is maintained off-chain even as ownership of the tokens is transferred using the chain. A key use case could be for stablecoins, meaning that it could allow Bitcoin/Lightning wallets to make use of bitcoin and stablecoins within one interface and on one blockchain.

Whether it’s using bitcoin for signatures or using bitcoin for tokens, a key question in any analysis between competing protocol standards should be to ask how much on-chain space it uses, and why. Neither more or less block space is inherently better, but it depends on the application. If you want something that is more immutable and scarce, keeping it on-chain is preferable, but it is more expensive. If you want something that is efficient and less costly, then storing more of the information off-chain is preferable.

**Hong Kong Bitcoin and Ethereum ETFs**

A number of spot Bitcoin and Ethereum ETFs launched in Hong Kong a couple weeks ago. A number of bitcoin proponents were playing this up as a big deal, but the size of the market just isn’t anywhere near the size of the U.S. market. And so while the inflows were decent given that context, they were tiny compared to U.S. inflows.

It’s possible that mainland Chinese investors will eventually be able to invest in these ETFs if regulators become comfortable with them, and some of the issuers are seeking that possibility. Cryptocurrency is currently banned in mainland China, although it nonetheless operates at a substantial scale on the gray market. What Chinese officials mainly don’t like about bitcoin is its ability to transfer value globally and be held non-custodially, which empowers individuals and threatens their top-down capital controls. A walled-garden bitcoin ETF can alleviate those concerns from top-down proponents.

This is something I’ll keep an eye on due to its potential impact on price, but either way, mainland Chinese investors that want the full power of bitcoin (non-custodial portable capital) have to look elsewhere than the ETFs.

**Ethereum Becomes Inflationary Again**

The Ethereum network completed a [hard fork known as Dencun](https://www.coindesk.com/tech/2024/05/09/eth-reverts-to-inflationary-asset-following-fee-reducing-dencun-upgrade/) back in March 2024 in order to increase the scalability of the network and reduce transaction fees. However, since the network uses a portion of the transaction fees to “burn” coins, this change reduced the burn rate of coins, and thus shifted the supply from being deflationary to inflationary, at least for now.

![Ethereum Supply](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-ethereum-supply.png)

There are plenty of assets that are deflationary in supply, mainly including companies that buy back their own stock. The supply of apple (AAPL) shares has been quite deflationary over the past decade, for example. The average annual deflation rate of Apple stock during that period has been way faster than Ethereum’s peak deflation rate:

![AAPL Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-aapl-shares.png)

The problem is that many under-performing assets like IBM (IBM) shares have also been deflationary, so deflation in and of itself isn’t a sign of a good asset necessarily.

![IBM Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-ibm-shares-updated.png)

A deflating supply is only meaningful if the ecosystem is growing and is the best-in-class at what it does. It doesn’t do much good if the ecosystem is stagnating. But more importantly, company decisions can change the supply of shares over time, so while an asset might be deflationary *for now* as long as their profits keep supporting buybacks and its board of directors agrees to do buybacks, that could suddenly change for any number of reasons. And as we see with IBM, supply stopped deflating in 2019. The same is true for Ethereum now.

Bitcoin’s money supply distribution policy hasn’t changed since it went live in 2009, and there’s nobody who really has the power to change it, especially to the upside. And it’s a very simple monetary policy. Every block, a certain pre-programmed number of new coins are produced, and every 210,000 blocks (approximately four years), that number of new coins per block gets cut in half until it reaches zero. And as a result, the supply of bitcoins asymptotically approaches 21 million. For someone holding it as portable savings, that reliability is a big deal. It’s not just that it *doesn’t* change, it’s that it’s *almost impossible* to change.

Ethereum’s money supply distribution policy has changed multiple times since it went live in 2015, and they use hard forks to perform those changes. Hard forks are changes to the network that are not backward compatible and that technically produce a new coin unless virtually everyone transfers over their nodes to that hard fork immediately. Only rather centralized networks can frequently perform hard forks like this. Most of the changes to Ethereum have historically resulted in lower money supply issuance in exchange for giving up proof-of-work and pushing transaction fees higher, but with increasing transaction fee competition from other blockchains to perform similar functions (NFTs, DeFi, etc) lately, this recent change has resulted in lower fees and higher supply issuance.

I can tell you what Bitcoin’s supply will be in 2034 or 2044, but I can’t tell you what Ethereum’s supply will be. Ethereum’s supply by then will depend on how frequently it is used and what future updates developers might make. That’s why I analyze Ethereum more from the perspective of being an equity than a money, which is not necessarily a bad thing per se (after all, I do invest in a lot of different equities), but is relevant in terms of assessing its use case and categorization.

I wrote about this unpredictable supply aspect back in 2021 after they initially implemented the feature that uses transaction fees to burn coins:

> However, my question is that given how many times Ethereum’s monetary policy has changed already, why would I assume EIP 1559 will be permanent? Ethereum developers change their monetary policy as often as the Federal Reserve does, and for similar reasons (to try to optimize aspects of the ecosystem’s economy). If EIP 1559 is in place for a number of years, proves itself to work as intended, and doesn’t change, and Ethereum 2.0 is operating smoothly, I’ll have reasonable confidence that it won’t change anymore and that the system is working as intended. Until then, all I can do is watch and see how things turn out, unless I feel like making a speculation based on my assessment of the developer team and the competitive environment.
>
> –[An Economic Analysis of Ethereum, January 2021](https://www.lynalden.com/ethereum-analysis/)

While I’m bullish on the ongoing growth in the supply of stablecoins and certain other tokenized assets, the problem is that the various blockchains that move, leverage, and trade those tokens compete with each other quite aggressively on speed and fees, which gives me low conviction on where those types of assets will ultimately end up five or ten years from now. The stablecoins and tokenized assets themselves are centralized, and most of the blockchains that move, leverage, and trade them have several points of centralization as well.

As a result, I continue to view bitcoin as being the primary long-term investable digital asset of interest, since there is no close competition for what it does (i.e. simple, robust, proof-of-work storage and settlement with high liquidity, security, decentralization, and scale).

## Bank OZK Deep Dive Analysis

I’m bearish on commercial real estate.

Although it has been a consensus view for a while that commercial real estate is in a bad shape currently, I think the sector is going to be an ongoing slow motion implosion for years to come.

This is for two main reasons. The first reason is that the uptick in remote work capabilities reduces the value of existing office space in certain regions and allows for population migration from certain regions to others. The second reason is that the sharp upward move in interest rates is still working its way through the system. Refinancing costs are going up as more debt matures, and cap rates for various types of properties are likely to continue to remain elevated due to higher costs of capital and opportunity costs, and that means lower property valuations.

Lenders are in less bad shape than equity holders, because in order for the lender to be impaired, the price of a property has to decrease enough that it wipes out the equity holder and starts going below the loan value, which is much lower than the assessed property value at the time of the loan. That being said, I’m concerned about a number of regional banks that have large amounts of commercial real estate lending exposure, since even modest loan losses could severely impair them.

And yet, I’m pretty bullish on Bank OZK, even though it’s primary lending activities are in commercial real estate. This is because I think the market has misjudged what they do. The bank is lumped into the commercial real estate “problem area” by investors and is priced accordingly for a bad scenario, even though what the bank does is quite different than the vast majority of what other commercial real estate lenders do. And this mismatch or market misjudgment, if indeed correct, offers a big upside opportunity with a 3-5 year view.

**Bank OZK Overview**

Bank OZK (OZK) is a regional bank based in Arkansas that has been around since 1903. However, the modern period for the bank began in 1979 when it was purchased by George Gleason. Gleason, now 70 years old, has run the company as its CEO/Chairman ever since, and the bank has been a massive outperformer under his tenure. It went public in 1997.

This is usually a good starting point for an investment. When the one who runs the company is a true owner and long-term thinker with a great track record, rather than just a steward who manages for the next fiscal year, that tends to be a strong variable for future outperformance.

And when Gleason one day steps down, the rest of Bank OZK’s executive team has long tenures as well and thus has a deep understanding of the company’s culture and operations. Most of the chief officers have 10+, 15+, or 20+ years at the company in various positions.

The bank takes in deposits from branches in Arkansas, Georgia, Texas, Florida, North Carolina, and elsewhere. I like the demographics of those states. The bank then makes loans nationwide, with an emphasis on commercial real estate. They’ve got other types of loans too, but commercial real estate or “RESG” in their case is the big component.

![Bank OZK Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/prmeium-2024-5-12-bank-ozk-loans-updated.png)

*Source: [Bank OZK May 2024 Presentation](https://ir.ozk.com/news/presentations)*

However, their commercial real estate loans are not the typical ones that you’ll find at most other banks. They call this the “Real Estate Specialties Group” or RESG. Here’s how they describe it:

> Started in 2003, RESG is a nationally recognized industry leader in construction, land & development (“CL&D”) lending and handles our largest and most complex loans. It has been our most significant growth engine and should continue to contribute meaningfully to our growth. RESG provides superior risk-adjusted returns through a disciplined and differentiated business model developed over its 20+ year history.
>
> RESG’s industry leading position reflects the fact that throughout our 20-year history we have been open for business every day, in every market, always pursuing the opportunities, and only the opportunities, that meet our rigorous credit quality standards. Our reputation for expertise, consistency, dependability and execution help us maintain our industry leading position.
>
> -Bank OZK May 2024 Presentation

In other words, Bank OZK is making relatively short duration loans to entities that are developing or constructing new commercial property, or otherwise doing complex things along those lines. And they are a national market leader at doing this; it’s their specialty.

Specialty insurers and bankers can develop partial economic moats, in the sense that they dominate a niche market and offer a lot of service differentiation vs other banks. They aren’t just holding a big loan book of long duration commercial real estate loans on existing properties.

Here’s a breakdown of their RESG exposure by property type:

![Bank OZK Loan Diversity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-bank-ozk-loan-diversity-updated.png)

*Source: Bank OZK May 2024 Presentation*

The main problem with commercial real estate in aggregate, and office properties in particular, is that a lot of existing properties are devalued or otherwise unsuitable. They were built and valued and lent against under a different economic environment (lower rates and less remote work). Now that the economic environment has structurally shifted (higher rates and more remote work), they’re in a world of pain.

However, *new development and construction* is a different matter. These loans are largely for commercial real estate that is being built now, for a real market need in a region that needs it. And it’s being valued and lent against in the current context of higher rates and the prevalence of remote work. So while they do have plenty of risks to manage, they don’t have the “legacy” problem of the wrong property type in the wrong region at the wrong price to worry about that most commercial real estate lenders do.

And because these are rather high-yielding short-term loans, Bank OZK has no problem paying high deposit rates to attract depositors. A lot of other banks are stuck holding long-duration low-yielding securities (e.g. 3%) that were created during the era of low interest rates, and so if they pay 5% to depositors to attract them or retain them, their profitability is going to be upside down. But Bank OZK doesn’t have that problem. They are holding a lot of high-yielding creditworthy short-term loans, and can pay up for deposits and still make a positive spread.

As a result, their deposits are holding strong, and they have a net interest margin of 4.71% as of the latest quarter, which is about 140 basis points (1.4%) higher than the average for banks.

**Risk Assessment**

Banks face two primary risks: credit risk and duration/liquidity risk.

Credit risk refers to loans being defaulted on, and is what impaired a lot of banks in 2008.

Duration/liquidity risk is when a bank is forced to sell otherwise good assets at a loss because deposits are fleeing. Or they are forced to raise deposit rates to keep deposits, and their assets don’t yield enough to support those higher deposit rates. This is what impaired a lot of banks in 2023 and is still an issue for some of them.

For Bank OZK, we can analyze each of those risks separately:

Bank OZK has moderate credit risk when examining it on the surface, since they operate in a difficult specialty industry. But their track record in this specific niche is strong, and they have both sector diversification and geographic diversification. The net charge-off ratio in 2023 was only 0.13%. And importantly, they have an above-average capital ratio, meaning they have a very solid buffer to protect them against loan losses.

Bank OZK used to have higher liquidity risk, but they’ve addressed it over the past year. Bank OZK’s asset side has a lot of loans and not a lot of securities, and loans are less liquid than securities. So if a lot of deposits pull out at any given time, they could face a severe liquidity problem. However, since Bank OZK’s assets produce a high yield and are held with rather short durations that can adjust as needed, they can pay a high yield to entice deposits to remain there, and have been growing deposits for that reason. They also maintain a high ratio of insured deposits, meaning that they aren’t over-exposed to big flighty uninsured business deposits like what Silicon Valley Bank had in 2023. And lastly, they switched their focus toward time deposits over the past year, which are a bit more expensive but are more resistant than demand deposits to being swiftly pulled out on a whim.

![Bank OZK Deposits](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-bank-ozk-deposits-updated.png)

*Source: Bank OZK May 2024 Presentation*

Overall, I think Bank OZK does a good job of addressing both risk categories. However, the final risk management approach is for an investor to manage their position size. Bank OZK is a $5 billion market capitalization bank; there’s inherently going to be risk with owning any bank of this modest size and level of concentration, and the position size in a portfolio should be determined accordingly. It’s a rather aggressive value play.

**Valuation Analysis**

Bank OZK currently trades at 8x earnings, and yet is showing no signs of distress. I think that’s because a lot of portfolio managers don’t want to touch something with overweight exposure to commercial real estate, and haven’t differentiated the type of lending it does in that space. They just put it in the “too hard pile” and move on, which is understandable.

![OZK FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-ozk-fastgraph.png)

Back in 2015, Bank OZK traded for over 25x earnings, which was overvalued. Since then, fundamentals kept growing and the stock priced chopped sideways, and so the valuation reduced significantly.

Their price-to-book ratio is also near its historical lows:

![Bank OZK Book Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-bank-ozk-book-value.png)

Their earnings growth slowed during the 2018/2019 period since the economy was decelerating, and then of course they ran into some issues in 2020 amid the pandemic lockdowns. They came out strong fundamentally, and yet are priced for zero or negative growth even though analysts expect them to have positive growth.

The bank has increased its dividend every year since their IPO, including through the 2001 recession, 2008 recession, 2020 lockdowns, and 2022/2023 pseudo-recession. They maintain a low payout ratio of about 25% of earnings.

When we look at nationwide senior loan officer surveys, credit tightness peaked during the Q1-Q3 2023 period, and is now easing. I don’t expect it to boom, but I expect some degree of stabilization:

![Senior Loan Officer Surveys](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-sloos.png)

And loan growth has been putting in an attempted bottom:

![Bank Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-bank-loans-zoomed.png)

Overall, both from a cyclical perspective and a structural perspective, Bank OZK looks undervalued, and shows little sign of being a value trap.

And if Bank OZK does determine that there will be a period of weak RESG loan opportunities, they could instead plow more earnings into stock buybacks. They previously authorized a large amount of repurchases, but didn’t use all of it because they had plenty of opportunity to plow that money into loan growth instead. At 8x earnings, they can get a lot of per-share value by buying back shares, if they determine that there aren’t enough loan opportunities for all of their retained earnings.

![Bank OZK Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-5-12-bank-ozk-shares.png)

I have no firm view of what Bank OZK stock price action will do over the next 6-12 months. However, from from the perspective of a fundamental investor with a 3-5 year investment horizon, I think there’s a strong case that the stock is undervalued and is likely to produce superior returns.

I caveat this bullish view with a cautious note toward position sizing, given the rather small size of the bank.
