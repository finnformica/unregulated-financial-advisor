## Macro View

The virus is currently showing very low numbers in the US and many other countries as the Omicron wave has subsided. However, Asia has been hit with it on a delay. And that’s relevant for the global economy because China is the world’s biggest commodity importer and is at the heart of the global supply chain.

China has had a zero-COVID policy for a while by using rather draconian controls, which may have delayed the Omicron wave for them but hasn’t been able to fully isolate their country from it. We can’t really rely on mainland numbers, but Hong Kong’s reported numbers are pretty striking:

![Hong Kong COVID-19](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-hong-kong-covid.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/country/china-hong-kong-sar/)*

China had strict lockdowns in certain regions during recent months, but with cases potentially peaking and rolling over in various regions, they are beginning to ease restrictions. They just allowed Shenzhen, China’s major tech hub, to [resume operation](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-03-18/shenzhen-lockdown-eases-as-xi-seeks-to-soften-covid-impact) of factories and public transport. And this came from the top according to that article and other major reports; President Xi recently stated that they need to shift policies to soften the impact on the economy and to balance the COVID-19 response with economic realities. Imbalances reached a tipping point, it seems.

This also coincided with Chinese regulators clarifying their views on regulation and pledging to support capital markets.

To review, Chinese stocks have been among the worst-performing assets over the past year, for a variety of reasons. The bear market in their stock market has been longer and deeper than I expected.

It began when Chinese authorities began cracking down on Alibaba (BABA) in late 2020. Jack Ma publicly criticized the Chinese banking sector and was preparing to IPO Ant Group, and that marked a massive turning point. Ma *disappeared* from public view for months, the Ant IPO was shelved, and regulators began cracking down on Alibaba and other major platform companies. This came amid moderate overvaluation and exuberant sentiment for Chinese assets throughout 2020 in general.

Under a common prosperity message/theme, Chinese regulators went after anti-competitive practices of Alibaba and Tencent and JD and other big platform companies, and also forced them to invest billions of dollars into a common prosperity fund. They cracked down on ride-sharing company DiDi for its data handling practices. They went after Tencent to limit the amount of gaming time that Chinese youths could have each day. They crushed the for-profit education sector. Their stock market got hit after hit after hit, and global investors began to view the market as uninvestable.

Meanwhile, China’s economy went through a declining credit cycle and popping of a real estate bubble. When we throw into the mix the Russian war and the associated response (rendering Russian assets literally uninvestable for an unknown period of time, along with sanctions), western investors seemingly and understandably had zero appetite to own any public securities in China by this point (despite having direct and indirect exposure to trillions of dollars worth of Chinese manufacturing/logistics infrastructure).

Chinese stocks recently had a massive capitulation to extremely low levels. Here’s JD for example, which has actually been one of the stronger ones compared to Alibaba and some others:

![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-jd-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Then, top Chinese regulators made public statements to clarify their intentions and ease market concerns.

Specifically, [Vice Premier Liu He held a financial stability meeting](https://www.cnbc.com/2022/03/16/china-says-it-will-support-chinese-ipos-abroad-calls-for-closure-on-tech-crackdown.html) that Chinese state media reported on. Topics included stimulating the economy, supporting the property sector while deflating excesses, recognizing rule of law and property rights, and encouraging foreign institutional investment into the stock market. Their stated goal is to wrap up their regulatory crackdown rather than keep it ongoing with scattershot new regulations, and get back to a growth cycle.

Chinese stocks roared higher in response to this news, with the biggest two-day advance in decades. Since then, they have been bouncing around, still at low levels overall.

Does that mean everything is good now? The CCP will respect property rights, and we all have a green light to invest? Roses and butterflies?

Of course not.

But it does suggest that the disorderly collapse in asset prices has reached a tipping point for the top officials in China, and that they do want to issue more clarification and consistency for their plans. I don’t think they want turbulent markets and a financial crisis going into their National Congress later this year, which is the closest thing that China has to elections.

There’s a good case to be made that this capitulation was “the bottom”, and that Chinese equities can correct down, bounce up, and grind higher over time now, although there were some prior periods that looked like bottoms before, so who knows.

Valuations are very low and sentiment was totally washed out. However, investors would do well to be careful about position sizes even if they are bullish (my basic view), since we are likely to see ongoing separation between the global West and the global East, with ongoing probabilities of tail risks for western investors in Chinese assets.

However, I do think there are many investable parts of US and global equity markets at current levels, for investors with a long-term view. You could say that I’m somewhat of a perma-bull on scarce and productive assets compared to fiat currencies, at least when the valuations are not excessive. The most attractive scarce and productive assets tend to rotate over time depending on valuations and the economic cycle, and I try to be somewhat contrarian with my allocation weightings when it seems appropriate to do so.

If I were to make the bearish case against what I just said, I would point to 2008. It is *usually* a good idea to fade AAII and other measures of sentiment like that; when they are bearish, go bullish. During any given severe market selloff, there is an 80%+ chance that when sentiment breaks down this badly, we’re already around a significant market bottom. But that other 20%? Well, that’s when it’s just a bull trap in a massive crash. Like 2008:

For me, it comes down to individual investments in this type of environment; if I know what I own, I’m more comfortable with the investment and willing to look through various uncertainty and potential choppy price action. And I don’t have enough data on hand to make a high-conviction bull or bear case here, so I’m playing both offense and defense.

**US Business Cycle Analysis**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the US economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Rather than describing the economy in narratives or qualitative terms, or cherry pick data to fit a particularly bullish or bearish view, it quantifies a broad section of data.

The economic growth rate in year-over-year terms peaked in Q2 2021, while Q3 and Q4 had decelerating (but still positive) growth, along with persistently hot inflation. Here in Q1 2022 we have an unclear situation, pointing towards ongoing deceleration but made more complex by the acute inflation problem.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Heat Map Indicators Referenced:**

* Real Gross Domestic Product
* Industrial Production
* Advance Retail Sales, Excluding Food Services
* Total Construction Spending
* Capital Goods Orders, Nondefense, Excluding Aircraft
* Exports of Goods and Services
* Imports of Goods and Services
* All Employees, Total Nonfarm
* 10 Year Minus 3 Month Yield Curve
* Consumer Prices, All Urban Consumers, All Items in City Average

Updated weekly based on incoming data, the Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow model currently has an estimated 1.3% annualized real growth for Q1 2022. This is low in absolute terms, but up from their estimate of below zero from three weeks ago.

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-gdpnow.png)

*Chart Source: [Atlanta Fed GDPNow](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow)*

The yield curve situation is rather mixed, but not looking great overall. The spread between the yields of the 10-year note and the 3-month bill remains wide, while the spread between the 10-year note and the 2-year note has collapsed to severely low levels.

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-yield-curve.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Basically, the 2-year note is pricing in a bunch of forward rate hikes that haven’t happened yet, so there is an unusually wide divergence here. Since we have the hottest inflation levels since the 1970s, I guess we shouldn’t be surprised by unusually wide yield curve divergences either.

The 10-2 yield curve is more famous, but the original studies about the predictive power of the yield curve focus on the 10-3 yield curve and that is the one with the slightly stronger track record. The current divergence between the two looks similar to how it did in early 1994, which is also when the Fed began hiking rates for a cycle. Back then, the 10-3 curve eventually caught down to the 10-2 curve by the end of the year.

Basically, there are a lot of mixed indicators at the moment. The economy is weakening by most metrics, but perhaps bouncing a bit from weeks ago, and with high inflation. That gives both bulls and bears something to base their views on, with potentially conflicting timeframes depending on whether they are traders or investors.

I think the ECRI Weekly Leading Index looks bearish for now. This suggests further economic weakness in general:

![ECRI LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-ecri-lei.png)

*Chart Source: [Advisor Perspectives](https://www.advisorperspectives.com/dshort/updates/2022/03/18/ecri-weekly-leading-index-update)*

The Fed raised short-term interest rates by 0.25% last week, which marks the first rate increase since late 2018. They signaled a plan to continue raising rates gradually, and to eventually look at balance sheet reduction.

The problem, however, is that the Fed has generally been a backwards-looking institution, and they tend to start tightening monetary policy after the fastest part of the growth cycle is behind us. After that point, they tend to tighten monetary policy into economic weakness.

## Update on Russia/Ukraine

Russia’s currency has stabilized somewhat from its lows (meaning USDRUB has come down from its highs):

![USDRUB](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-usdrub.png)

*Chart Source: [XE.com](https://www.xe.com/)*

The spikes in energy prices, food prices, and metals prices have also come off their highs. The dollar is coming off of its highs vs a basket of major currencies. Things reached a fever pitch and are now going through some degree of consolidation and price discovery. Where a lot of these things go in the next year depends in part on various policy choices.

Russia’s markets have been shut since their invasion into Ukraine, but they are reportedly going to start opening. For bond trading, the Russian central bank announced plans to support their bond market with purchases to try to keep things somewhat stable. The timeline for stock trading is still somewhat unclear.

Meanwhile, Russia’s invasion of Ukraine continues without a ceasefire or a clear direction towards a peace agreement. Thousands of people have died, and millions of people have been displaced. Russia has called on China for support, and the US subsequently threatened China if they do provide support.

So far, it seems that China intends to maintain a relatively neutral stance, [according to reports of the latest Biden/Xi meeting](https://www.usnews.com/news/world-report/articles/2022-03-18/china-indicates-to-biden-it-wont-send-weapons-to-russia-as-bloody-war-in-ukraine-grinds-on). China is maintaining economic and financial ties with Russia, and probably accelerating them, with reports that China has considered purchases of cheap Russian commodity assets. Additionally, a number of Chinese officials have criticized NATO recently. However, China doesn’t seem willing, at least at this time, to provide direct military support to Russia for their invasion of Ukraine. This remains a fluid situation and could change, and if it does, that could be a significant risk for Chinese asset prices.

Apart from the physical risk to Ukrainians and others involved in the kinetic war, the biggest ongoing risk for the world relates to global commodity scarcity, especially around energy, fertilizer, and food. Russia is one of the biggest oil and gas exporters in the world. They’re also one of the biggest exporters of wheat, nickel, fertilizer, platinum group metals, enriched uranium, coal, aluminum, and more. The world order is not really designed to function without their commodity exports.

As food prices have soared, many countries have turned to export restrictions. Food shortages and food inflation are among the biggest problems for developing countries when they occur, and are highly problematic for those with low incomes in developed markets as well. High food prices often spark protests, revolutions, and major election turning points in countries around the world.

The good news is that most of these commodities are off their highs at the moment, as the world awaits to see how this all shakes out. However, the range of potential outcomes and the probability of extreme events occurring is currently wider/higher than usual. There are some rather binary potential outcomes here.

We could wake up one day with headlines of a ceasefire where, for example, Ukraine cedes the Donbas region and signs something saying they won’t join NATO, in exchange for Russia backing out of the rest of Ukraine and having its sovereign reserves unfrozen. Or we could wake up to headlines of Russia doing something surprisingly aggressive again, with more potential for commodity supply chain disruption in its aftermath. My job as an investor is to try to be aware of the various right-tail and left-tail extreme outcomes in any given period of time.

For those reasons, I continue to view [dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) as the best way to get exposure to bitcoin for most people that want some as part of their asset mix.

Notably, bitcoin tends to be somewhat of a canary in the coalmine for other risk assets, including the broad S&P 500. Bitcoin trades globally 24/7, and is notoriously volatile, and so it often hits local bottoms and tops before broad equity markets do at various turning points.

Will this presage a local S&P 500 bottom here in March 2022? I wouldn’t be shocked if it does, although I’m not particularly of high conviction about any intermediate-term price action, since anything could change with a headline.

## BMY and DHI Analysis

In this report, I look at two value stocks from two very different industries that I like for the long run: healthcare and homebuilders. These are defense and offense, yin and yang. I wouldn’t mind a bit of both here.

**Bristol Myers Squibb (BMY) Update**

Investors that have been reading my reports for the past year know that I’ve been covering a bunch of healthcare stocks bullishly: BMY, CI, CVS, and more. CVS has been the winner of the group so far with a nice breakout, while BMY and CI have been more sideways and choppy, but they did hold up better than the broad market during the severe down moves.

And specifically, BMY just broke out to 5+ year highs. All that is left is to surpass the 2016 highs:

![BMY Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-bmy-price.png)

The second half of 2021 was rough for BMY because the US House and Senate were publicly working on drug price reform legislation that could have made it through with the Build Back Better package. If passed, this could have dented the prospects for drug makers, in an effort to reign in prices for consumers.

However, the Senate was too split to get it through, and that doesn’t seem to be changing any time soon. As I’ve said a number of times, the key risk for healthcare companies is about legislation, which makes them a good diversifier for a portfolio of stocks that mostly have a key risk around the economic cycle.

Since then, healthcare stocks have had a nice bounce, which was further supported by the economic slowdown and then concerns around the war and other macro bearish things. In that sort of environment, consumer stables, healthcare, utilities, and similar defensive stocks tend to catch a bid relative to the broad market.

BMY is in a unique case because it has a very severe patent expiration cliff, but also one of the strongest drug pipelines in the industry at the moment. Its three biggest drugs are about to lose patent protection, which will allow generic competition to come in and take market share and drive down prices. To offset that, the company has several drugs lined up for approval, which management and analysts expect to replace the lost revenue from their current crop of blockbuster drugs. So, BMY has to effectively undergo a rotation of its product offerings, which offers both risk and reward, and currently the market prices the stock very cheaply due to this lack of clarity.

![BMY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-bmy-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Perhaps the key thing here is that all of those risks are almost entirely unrelated to the economic cycle. BMY could do well as the S&P 500 falters. BMY could do poorly as the S&P 500 surges. BMY and the S&P 500 could go in the same direction. It’s very uncorrelated, which is nice to have in a diversified portfolio.

Early in 2022, BMY increased its quarterly dividend by 10%, and accelerated its share buyback plan. The stock yields over 3%, and the company is steadily reducing its share count with free cash flows in its post-acquisition form.

![BMY Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-bmy-shares-outstanding.png)

The company has substantial debt due to the Celgene acquisition (which is mainly what strengthened its pipeline so much), but has a high A- credit rating, and thus pays very low interest on its debt, and is slowly reducing its post-acquisition debt load.

**D.R. Horton (DHI) Analysis**

D.R. Horton is the largest homebuilder in the United States.

With mortgage rates on the rise, the recent increase in US home prices has stalled. Homebuilders face supply chain bottlenecks, and their stocks are priced very pessimistically. DHI trades for less than 6x forward estimated earnings, because the market is concerned about an economic slowdown and weakness in the homebuilding sector:

![DHI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-dhi-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Back during the subprime mortgage crisis, consumers with weak credit were given easy loans by banks, and homebuilder stock prices kept pace with their rapid earnings growth.

In contrast, during this current 2020s environment, bank lending is mostly restricted to well-qualified borrowers, and homebuilder stock prices are lagging their rapid earnings growth. Basically, it’s all more conservative this time around, in terms of both lending risk and valuations. It’s already priced for a slowdown.

Mortgage rates have been on the rise recently (red line below), but remain deeply negative on an inflation-adjusted basis (blue line below):

![Real Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-real-mortgage-rates.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

If we look at DHI’s stock price vs mortgage rates over the past decade, we can see a pretty strong inverse pattern. When mortgage rates had quick jumps up, DHI stock generally suffered:

![DHI Stock vs Mortgage Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-dhi-vs-mortgage-rates.png)

However, if we look at DHI revenue and earnings, we would have a hard time pointing out where most of those changes in mortgage rates occurred:

![DHI Revenue and Earnings](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-dhi-revenue-earnings.png)

Ultimately, when it comes to homebuilder stocks, my main focus is less about mortgage rates and more on 1) whether there is a credit bubble or not and 2) whether the rate of housing starts is excessive. While home-buying did get somewhat overdone, I don’t see signs of a residential real estate credit bubble in the US, nor do I see excessive new home construction compared to ongoing demand levels.

I do see a period of consolidation after a period of rapid changes (geographic dislocations, a huge surge of money supply growth, etc).

The annualized rate of housing starts is not excessive in absolute terms, and started this cycle from a very low base after the subprime crisis:

![Housing Starts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-housing-starts.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Meanwhile, housing supply remains middling:

![Housing Inventory](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-housing-inventory.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

DHI benefits from mild economies of scale as the largest homebuilder. It can negotiate better prices on supplies, and has relatively low overhead costs as a ratio of its operations.

If the homebuilding market remains relatively strong, DHI can simply continue to build homes and buy back very cheap shares. On the other hand, if the homebuilding market gets ugly, their smaller and weaker-capitalized competitors will fail first, and DHI can acquire them at bargain prices, either with cash or by issuing shares, and continue expanding its market share.

![DHI Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-dhi-shares.png)

Through bull and bear cycles, DHI kept increasing its market share for over three decades:

![DHI Market Share](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-dhi-market-share.png)

*Chart Source: [D.R. Horton Q1 2022 Presentation](https://investor.drhorton.com/news-and-events/presentations/presentations-2022)*

A lot of people look at capital ratios as the best risk indicator for the banking sector, but capital ratios actually don’t have much predictive power for financial crises. A better predictor historically is the percentage of bank assets that consist of loans and risky securities vs the percentage of bank assets that consist of nominally risk-free assets like cash and government bonds. In other words, it’s the overall riskiness of the asset mix that matters more; a risky asset mix can blow through a seemingly well-capitalized bank in no time. Most bank analysts are trained to focus on the capital ratio regardless.

US banks currently have historically high levels of cash and Treasuries as a percentage of total assets (40%), due to various regulations and due to a lackluster willingness to engage in aggressive lending. In contrast, in the run-up to the subprime mortgage crisis in the mid-2000s, banks had an extremely low ratio of cash and Treasuries to total assets (13%):

![Bank Risk Estimate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-3-20-bank-risk.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Basically, I’m playing a bit of offense and defense at the current time, at least for companies that are better-positioned than their peers and able to capitalize on weakness in their industry.

I like both healthcare stocks and homebuilder stocks as kind of a yin-and-yang set of equities. I wouldn’t make the homebuilder stocks too large of a position size, though. Among homebuilder stocks, DHI, PHM, LEN, and TOL all look fine to me.

## Final Thoughts

Investors are broadly bearish, but initial signs suggest that global markets may have moved past the point of maximum pessimism and have reached a local turning point. Commodities and the dollar are down off their spike highs, and equities are up off their spike lows.

That doesn’t mean I’m strongly bullish, or that there are not major risks remaining, and that we can’t grind lower later this year in terms of broad asset prices. I could see markets grinding around choppily for quite a while. Commodity and supply chain dislocations of this size don’t just magically work themselves out without issue. The Fed is tightening monetary policy into a period of decelerating growth in the US, which in a vacuum doesn’t bode well for assets in general. There is a long list of real things to be bearish about.

However, what it does mean is that I think there are plenty of investable risk assets in various places. I like some defensive assets such as gold and cash, but I also see increasing opportunities in certain US and global equity sectors, and bitcoin doesn’t exactly look bad here either with a carefully-sized position.

Basically, the summary of the bullish case is the following. Investors are broadly (and understandably) bearish amid the war, Fed tightening, and economic deceleration. More often than not, fading this level of bearish sentiment is the right move. Meanwhile, bitcoin as the canary in the coal mine may have presaged a local bottom in broad equities, at least for a period of time. Commodities are off of their spike highs, at least for the moment. Now, Chinese officials are signaling somewhat of a white flag for COVID-19 lockdowns and regulatory crackdowns and suggesting that maybe they had enough of their market crashing, and are now ready to be supportive of markets with an upturn in the credit cycle leading into their National Congress.

If the more bearish scenario comes to fruition and we do end up reaching lower lows on many of these things in the months ahead, then I have some further rebalancing fuel for it in the form of cash-equivalents and gold. If the more bullish scenario comes to fruition, then I already have plenty of risk exposure. I think there will probably be a lot of choppiness in general.

My focus here is on striking the right balance between opportunity and defense, although that balance differs for people depending on their unique financial situation.
