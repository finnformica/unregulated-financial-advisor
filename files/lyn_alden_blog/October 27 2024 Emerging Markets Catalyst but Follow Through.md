# Emerging Markets: Catalyst, but Follow Through?

*October 27, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report does a deep dive on liquidity, including some potential paths ahead that the election might affect. It also examines the current state of the U.S. economy.

The digital asset section looks a BTC price action, ETH price action, and a recent Microsoft shareholder proposal regarding bitcoin.

The investment analysis section re-examines emerging markets in light of recent Chinese stimulus announcements. The takeaway is there are some serious catalysts for a period of emerging market outperformance, but it remains to be seen if they will follow through or not.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to trend sideways, as the Treasury and Fed partially offset each other. Despite ongoing quantitative tightening by the Federal Reserve, overall liquidity remains above the “problem level” that it reached in March 2023 during the regional bank crisis as reverse repos continue to drain back into the banking system.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-domestic-liquidity.png)

Notably, the end of Q3 saw the first significant usage of the Fed’s repo facility in four years. Liquidity needs become the most complex at each quarter-end, but this was the first time where major institutions used the Fed’s repo facility since the pandemic, to the small but meaningful tune of over $2 billion:

![Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-repos.png)

There is still about $200 billion sloshing around in the Fed’s *reverse* repo facility, which is where entities trade cash for Treasuries from the Fed. That’s a sign of excess liquidity, in other words, where entities put liquidity that they don’t need right now.

But at the same time, there were entities that had plenty of Treasuries and needed cash, i.e. needed liquidity, and the private repo market became overwhelmed and expensive, resulting in minor spillover into the Fed’s repo facility. This isn’t too notable in and of itself, other than that it’s a small sign of growing liquidity issues. There are pockets of the financial system that have plenty of liquidity, and other areas that are getting a bit tight. This should be monitored at each quarter-end to see if it starts to become a recurring and growing trend, as that could signal that the end of quantitative tightening is getting closer.

**The Debt Ceiling Liquidity Impact**

My upcoming public newsletter is going to touch on this, but I’ll mention it here first. One of the biggest variables regarding liquidity in the first half of 2025 will be the debt ceiling. The debt ceiling is currently suspended, but will automatically go back into effect at the start of January 2025. That means the Treasury Department won’t be able to issue net new debt starting from that point. They’re running a large deficit, and so in order to keep paying their bills they’ll have to start draining their cash balance, a.k.a. their Treasury General Account, at the Fed.

When it happens, a debt ceiling dispute that prevents the Treasury from issuing new debt and forces it to draw down its cash balance is ironically *good* for liquidity for several months. This is because the Treasury has about $800 billion sitting in its account, which is kind of a liquidity void, similar to reverse repos, and as it starts drawing down that balance, it goes back into the banking system. Back into bank reserves.

Back in early 2023, when most people were still bearish, I was a lot less bearish than most with a six-month view and wrote about that a lot in my reports, due to the debt ceiling issue at the time. The Treasury was draining hundreds of billions of dollars into the system, so my view was that until that’s finished, it’s hard to be too bearish. I was concerned about what would happen when the Treasury *fills back up*, but was bullish during the draining process. Well, if this happens again in 2025 and Congress fights about it to to the bitter end, there could be about $800 billion drained back into the financial system again.

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-tga.png)

The likelihood of this repeating again in the first half of 2025 is mostly based on politics and will need to be monitored in real time. There are a few points worth making, though.

-The debt ceiling has been around for over a century but it has mainly been used as a political tool by the out-of-power party against in-power party starting about a decade and a half ago when the Tea Party faction of the GOP used it against President Obama. Since then, it has become a recurring political tool, but one that is primarily used by Republicans against Democratic presidents.

-However, the sample size for that is small. During that time, there have been three terms of Democratic presidents (two Obama, one Biden) and only one term for a Republican president (Trump). Democrats didn’t press Trump on the issue to the same extent that Republicans pressed Obama and Biden on the issue, but it doesn’t mean for sure that they wouldn’t do it in the future.

-My base case is that a Harris victory is more likely to result in a prolonged debt ceiling dispute between her administration and Republicans, and thus a higher likelihood of almost $800 billion getting drained back into the financial system from the TGA in the first half of 2025. If Trump wins, which betting markets and polls are leaning toward, then a prolonged debt ceiling dispute is less likely, since Democrats seem less likely to use it. However, it would depend on the make-up of the House, and whether Democrats start using the tool more aggressively or not. So regardless of the election outcome, it’s a variable to watch for. There are of course many other ramifications from a Harris or Trump victory, but this section is focusing on liquidity, of which the $800 billion TGA and the associated debt ceiling is a huge factor in the first half of 2025.

**Global Liquidity**

The dollar index has rallied from under 101 to over 104 in about one month, which is a sizable move and is pretty negative for global liquidity.

It means emerging markets with dollar-denominated debt around the world are a bit more squeezed this month than last month. And it means that global dollar-denominated broad money supply is pressured.

**U.S. Economy**

The U.S. economy continues to operate at two speeds.

Asset owners, largely referring to those in the middle and upper portion of the income spectrum and skewing older in age, continue to be in pretty good shape. Delinquencies in that category continue to be low, and spending continues to be robust.

Non-asset owners, largely referring to those in the lower portion of the income spectrum and skewing younger in age, continue to be in pretty rough shape. Delinquencies in that category have been showing signs of distress, and spending has weakened at all but the top-tier discount stores (e.g. Walmart, Costco).

Numbers for Q3 GDP are not out yet, but Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow calculator expects the numbers to be robust:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-gdpnow.png)

Manufacturing continues to be in contraction. It was showing signs of rallying in late 2023 but throughout 2024 has been stuck down on the mat:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-pmi.png)

Retail sales continue to inch up in nominal terms, but have been *down* in inflation-adjusted terms since 2021:

![Retail Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-retail-sales.png)

Continued jobless claims have risen to a new cycle high. In absolute terms, under 2 million is still pretty good, and not indicative of a recession. But after a year of sideways numbers, a breakout isn’t great to see:

![Continued Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-continued-claims.png)

Overall, I continue to classify the U.S. economy as operating under a fiscal dominance regime. The U.S. government is running 6-7% of GDP deficits during a non-recession period, which generally leads to a pretty hot nominal environment, but a more mixed real environment, and a lot of bifurcation between different economic groups depending on whether they’re directly or indirectly on the receiving side of those deficits or not.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

From March to September, bitcoin was consolidating with lower highs. Since then, it has built a small pattern of higher highs:

![BTC Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-btc-price.png)

The way that I interpret this is that the launch of the spot ETFs pulled forward a lot of excitement, and that has had to work itself off. The average cost basis of the network rose from about $510 billion in March at its price peak, and is now about $650 billion, even as the market cap is down from its March peak.

With the ratio of market cap and cost basis no longer falling, we might be starting to put this consolidation behind us:

![BTC MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-btc-mvrv.png)

There could be kneejerk election outcomes. Whether correct or not, bitcoin is kind of considered a “Trump trade” and so a Trump victory could see a spike in the price, and a Harris victory could see a drop in the price. But when looking out 12 months or so, I’m pretty bullish either way and I don’t trade in these short timeframes.

I could be wrong but I think the cycle low is in, meaning that ~$50k level where it fell to during the yen carry trade mini-blow-up is where I wouldn’t expect it to go under as a base case. I think it’s more likely to break to fresh highs rather than break down through that level.

**Ethereum Price Action**

The ETHBTC ratio hit fresh cycle lows recently:

![ETHBTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-ethbtc.png)

While that ratio will likely find a cycle low and bounce at some point, I don’t really know where that level will be. And it’s down significantly from where its spot ETFs came out and when I re-iterated that I have no interest in owning Ethereum for the model portfolios.

> I plan to continue holding Bitcoin ETFs in the model portfolios, but don’t intend to add a position in the Ethereum ETFs.
>
> -July 21st premium report

A few weeks ago, I participated on a crypto panel at Simplify Asset Management’s annual conference, which was interestingly located within the New York Stock Exchange. One of the big takeaways from the panel is that bitcoin is interesting, stablecoins are interesting, but there’s not exactly a whole lot else out there gaining any traction. It was recorded and is available [here](https://youtu.be/x7_n3xxb1Og).

The altcoin cryptocurrency industry went through a series of bull-run narratives: ICOs, DeFi, and NFTs. Right now, the main narrative is meme coins, which is small and unsustainable. And so I view focusing on quality as important, which in my view primarily means bitcoin.

**Microsoft’s Shareholder Proposal**

Microsoft received a shareholder proposal suggesting that the company should diversify its corporate treasury into bitcoin for their upcoming annual shareholder’s meeting on December 10th.  The board of directors recommended that shareholders vote no on the proposal.

The company has $75 billion in cash and generates tens of billions of free cash flow per year. Even just a 20% share of their corporate treasury, or $15 billion, would give them a stack of bitcoin almost as big as MicroStrategy’s (MSTR) stack at current prices.

The shareholder proposal will most likely be voted down. However, it’s worth noting that the second biggest shareholder of Microsoft is BlackRock (BLK), which is now supportive of bitcoin.

MicroStrategy’s outperforming returns were mentioned in the shareholder proposal, and so in response, MicroStrategy’s executive chairman Michael Saylor tagged Microsoft’s CEO Satya Nadella on Twitter/X with a positive call to action.

![Saylor Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-saylor-tweet.png)

It remains to be seen how this will play out, but either way I doubt this will be the last instance of this kind of thing occurring.

## Investment Updates: Emerging Markets

Since emerging markets were basically defined as such in the late 1980s, there have been two major emerging market cycles. When this line is rising, it means emerging markets are outperforming the S&P 500, and when it’s falling it means the S&P 500 is outperforming:

![SPX vs EM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-spx-em.png)

There were cycles before then, but they were less defined. And they were inversely correlated with dollar strength:

![SPX vs EM vx DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-spx-em-dxy.png)

There are two main camps of thought going forward.

One camp is that there aren’t really going to be meaningful cycles going forward; that it’s structurally different now. U.S. tech stocks will continue to dominate, global capital will continue to gravitate toward the United States, and emerging markets are structurally uninvestable in the broad sense.

The other camp is that we’re approaching a sentiment bottom. Emerging markets are considered uninvestable right at the bottom, and they’re super cheap. With China starting to stimulate, and the U.S. starting to cut rates, if there’s a time for a reversal, it’s right about now.

I’m more in the second camp, but am not betting the farm on it. The way I view it is that we can identify catalysts, but we then need to monitor for follow-through. What 2001 and 2024 have in common is that global capital is stuffed disproportionately into U.S. markets, and the Fed is starting a cutting cycle.

There was a Fed cutting cycle in 2007, but global capital was not stuffed into the U.S. at the time. Quite the contrary; that was a top of an emerging markets cycle and capital was stuffed into the BRICS (Brazil, Russia, India, China, South Africa). That was the cycle when the BRICS were named in the first place.

There was also a Fed cutting cycle in 2019. Global capital was stuffed into the U.S. at the time, but not quite as much as now, and then the whole 2020-2022 period was disrupted by a global pandemic and lockdowns and extreme fiscal and monetary actions.

So, 2024 might or might not be the start of a reversal, but there are conditions present for it. Perhaps another disruption will occur and prevent any sort of reversal, such as a war that sends oil to $200/barrel. Maybe major emerging market leaders will shoot themselves in the foot with war, anti-market political actions, or other types of own-goals.

And so I’m in the phase of watching for follow-through or the lack thereof, and am treating countries separately even though they are partially all linked to the dollar and global capital flows.

**Examples of Emerging Market Stocks and ETFs**

Chinese stocks had an almost 50% rally from their low in mid-September to their high, and then they gave up about half of those gains:

![MCHI 1 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-mchi-1-year.png)

This was due to a combination of re-positioning and short-covering. I plan to watch to see if that new base starts to hold, to see if we get a higher-low, in other words.

When we zoom out, even that huge 50% spike was a mere blip:

![MCHI Full](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-mchi-full.png)

An example of a Chinese stock that I like is JD.com (JD). It’s a major e-commerce stock, it’s trading at under 11x earnings, and it has more cash than debt. But it’s broadly impaired by the fact that China is viewed as uninvestable. Investors are worried about economic conditions, but then also worried about the geopolitical tail risk of war-related impairments. In other words, even if JD does okay, a foreign investor’s claim of ownership on JD could be taken from them, either because China takes it, or because the investor’s home country sanctions them from it.

![JD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-jd-fastgraph.png)

This is where an investor can use something akin to [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value) to build a probability-weighted outcome.

For a simple example, JD’s ADR is currently around $40. An investor might say that over the next three years, due to a combination of Chinese stimulus, a global rate-cutting cycle, and rock-bottom valuations, that the stock has an 80% chance to double in price to $80. However, she also acknowledges that there’s a 20% chance that, perhaps due to a conflict in the South China Sea, the value of her investment will go to zero.

The probability-weighted price in three years would therefore be .8\*80 + .2\*0 = $64. That’s a 60% total gain, or 17% annualized over three years. And of course that’s a very simple model. An investor would generally have a bigger range of outcomes than that. There’s a chance it triples, a chance it doubles, a chance it goes up 50%, a chance it stays flat, a chance it gets cut in half, a chance it goes to zero, and so forth. The big variable in that calculation are the odds that the ownership of the asset is zeroed-out for any reason. One investor might assign 5% odds over three years, and another investor might assign 50% odds.

Similar analysis can be done on Alibaba (BABA), Tencent (TCEHY), and so forth.

If we move over to Brazil, I continue to find their banking system interesting as a very small value slice. Their biggest bank had an explosive run of more than 8x price appreciation during the BRICS boom of 2002-2007, and then has chopped along for over a decade and a half since then:

![ITUB YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-itub-ychart.png)

If any point in the second half of the 2020s we get a proper dollar downcycle and an emerging market upcycle, the move that something like ITUB could make is quite large.

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-itub-fastgraph.png)

And then there are emerging market proxies in developed markets. I’ve highlighted Japan’s heavy equipment maker Komatsu (KMTUY) as an example of this before. With a stock like that, you generally get emerging market correlation with a developed market jurisdiction. The reason for the correlation is that emerging markets tend to be more physical economies than developed markets, and so marginal growth there tends to translate into more commodity usage, more development, etc. They’re starting from a lower base of installed physical capital.

![Komatsu vs EEM](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-komatsu-vs-eem.png)

Singaporean banks are also a way to get some southeast Asian emerging market exposure with a foundation based in Singapore, so it’s like one-step removed:

![DBSDY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-dbsdy-fastgraph.png)

## Final Thoughts: Updates on Gold and the Dollar

Over the past month and a half or so, I’ve been sharing concerns that while I’m bullish on gold, that I thought gold was a bit overbought and the dollar was a bit oversold in the intermediate term. For example, this was six weeks ago:

> The price of gold touched a new high at over $2,600 per ounce, amid dollar weakness and heightened geopolitical tensions as potential catalysts. However, I continue to have some intermediate-term concerns that the the dollar is a bit oversold and that gold is a bit overbought, and thus both could consolidate for a few months. This is not a concern that I’m actively trading around, but rather something I am making note of to set expectations even as a gold bull.
>
> -September 15th premium report

So far, that concern has been more valid for the dollar than for gold.

The dollar index bounced from about 100.7 on September 15th to 104.3 recently, which is a rather sharp move in six weeks and is a headwind against global liquidity.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-dxy.png)

Gold, however, has ignored that uptick in the dollar, and continues to power higher:

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-gold.png)

I still have concerns about gold’s overbought status, but I’m glad that I’m not trying to trade around that view since I remain structurally bullish on the asset. I plan to let gold run for a while and let corrections play out when they do.

I added gold to the newsletter portfolio in October 2018, about a month after the portfolio started, and it has been a great decision so far:

![Gold Added](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-gold-added.png)

Gold has nearly kept up with the S&P 500 during that time, and has absolutely crushed bonds, and I’m primarily using it in place of where I might otherwise have more bonds in the portfolio, as someone who has been structurally bearish on bonds during this time:

![SPX vs Gold vs Bonds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-10-27-spx-gold-bnd.png)
