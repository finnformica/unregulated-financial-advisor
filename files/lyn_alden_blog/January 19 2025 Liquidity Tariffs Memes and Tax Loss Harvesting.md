## Macro View

**Domestic Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic bank cash levels reached pretty close to their estimated liquidity floor during the window dressing at the end of the quarter, and then bounced back into the range they’ve been in:

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-bank-cash.png)

![Bank Cash 1 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-bank-cash-1-year.png)

The Treasury Department has a target of $700-$850 billion in their cash account at the Fed. The $700 billion figure applies to the end of calendar Q4 2024, and the $850 billion figure applies to the end of calendar Q1 2025. They recently dipped under that level and are sitting at $641 billion:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-tga.png)

I’m not sure the reason for the dip. It might merely be a short-term issuance timing thing with no real meaning. Or it could be intentional to facilitate the quarter-end window dressing liquidity shortages that were well-telegraphed in advance and that I discussed in prior reports.

When the Treasury reduces its cash level, it’s good for domestic liquidity, since that money finds its way back into the financial system. Excess amounts will spill back into the reverse repo facility.

Additionally, outgoing Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen [sent a letter](https://apnews.com/article/treasury-debt-limit-janet-yellen-7e598f2811d75ad5159f9338f7cdce16) to Congress this past week saying that the Treasury will begin “extraordinary measures” on January 21st. The debt ceiling was reinstated automatically at the start of the year, so the Treasury cannot issue net new debt. Until the debt ceiling is lifted, the Treasury will need to begin shuffling accounts around and ultimately drawing down its cash balance to keep paying the government’s existing bills.

Over the past decade and a half when the debt ceiling began being used by Congress against the President for negotiation purposes, it was generally an inter-party dispute. Congressional Republican party representatives would use it against a Democratic party president. Democratic party representatives didn’t really use it much against Trump in his first term, nor did Republican party representatives.

At the moment, there is a building conflict between Republican party representatives and Trump over the debt ceiling, or an intra-party dispute, which is a first. Trump publicly wanted the debt ceiling removed before he took office, and that didn’t happen. Now, in the first several months of his term, he has a debt ceiling to contend with.

I expect it to be a shorter-term debt ceiling dispute this time than the two that occurred during the Biden administration, since the issue is between Republican-controlled Congress and a Republican President. However, if it *does* last as long as the prior debt ceiling issues, and goes all the way to the end, then it could last up to six months and result in the entire Treasury cash balance being drained back into the banking system, like in 2021 and 2023. This would be good for domestic liquidity while it happens, but then would open up major liquidity issues when it’s time to refill it. Especially because this time, they won’t have the reverse repo facility to tap to refill it, which is how they avoided liquidity problems when refilling the Treasury cash account in 2023.

Right now, the situation looks pretty decent for liquidity until further notice when the debt ceiling is resolved. We’ll have the Fed continuing to pull liquidity out of the system via quantitative tightening, and the Treasury slowly draining its cash balance into the system to offset that.

The likely incoming Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has a number of issues to contend with, however. He has previously been critical of Yellen’s shortening of Treasury durations toward T-bill issuance and away from T-note and T-bond issuance, and so he might attempt to increase T-note and T-bond issuance. That would bring the Treasury back toward its normal operation, but would be negative for liquidity, of which he is well aware. Even if he doesn’t do that, there’s only $118 billion left in the Fed’s reverse repo facility that excess T-bill issuance can draw liquidity from:

![Reverse Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-rrp.png)

I continue to expect that we’ll see the Fed end quantitative tightening in 2025 to address liquidity issues. However, in the meantime, the draining of the Treasury’s cash balance is decent for liquidity, and we have to wait a bit to see what Bessent’s Treasury will do in terms of duration issuance.

And of course, the reason I cover liquidity so much is how correlated liquidity conditions are with asset prices. And we’re entering a rather pivotal few months compared to the relative autopilot the U.S. system was on throughout much of 2024.

**U.S. Structural Trade Deficits**

A topic I’ve been discussing for several years is the structural U.S. trade deficit and the role that the dollar plays in these trade deficits due to its role as the global reserve currency.

In particular, there is both a benefit and a cost of being the issuer of the global reserve currency.

The “cost” of maintaining the Bretton Woods system was that the United States’ gold reserves were constantly drained to foreign hands until the system was defaulted on in 1971. Likewise, the “cost” of maintaining the Petrodollar/Treasury system that has been in place ever since then is that the United States’ industrial base is gradually hollowed out and sent to foreign hands via a persistent trade deficit. I covered this in detail in [Broken Money](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR/), specifically in chapter 13 called “Heavy is the Head that Wears the Crown”.

![US Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-us-trade-balance.png)

The mechanics of this are as follows.

Since the 1970s, the United States has encouraged countries to hold dollars (primarily in the from of Treasuries) as their primary reserves. Even without this encouragement, the post-Bretton Woods network effects of the eurodollar system were already strong compared to other currencies. The majority of cross-border trade is priced in dollars, the majority of cross-border lending is denominated in dollars, and accumulated trade surpluses are stored primarily in dollars. The dollar is the most salable currency, and thus serves as the world’s primary ledger.

In other words, historically, if a Japanese company sells construction equipment to a Brazilian company, it’ll often be priced in dollars and then those dollars will be exchanged up to Japan’s sovereign level and used to buy Treasuries and held for the long run. In addition to the dollar being the most liquid and salable currency in global markets, the U.S. capital markets (stocks and bonds) represent the biggest and most liquid place to store longer-term value.

Similarly, if a Canadian firm lends money to a Brazilian firm, they’ll often do so in a U.S. dollar-denominated contract. The foreign sector has more than $13 trillion in dollar-denominated debt, and that’s primarily owed to other foreign entities rather than to American entities. All of those debts represent inflexible demand for dollars, since dollars are required to service those debts.

This means practically the whole world needs dollars, both for buying international goods and for servicing their dollar-denominated debt. So the next question is: how does the United States supply dollars to the whole world? The answer is that they primarily do so through a structural trade deficit. It’s not even fully intentional; it happens on its own due to the organic demand and self-reinforcing liquidity network effects for the dollar. Most currencies are not held long-term by foreign entities at scale, and so interest rate differentials and trade balances account for most of their relative value to each other. But the dollar has this additional component of demand that other currencies don’t have: almost all countries are using it for international trade and are using it to store value in over the long run. As such, the dollar is almost always “overvalued” on a trade balance basis.

This makes it very hard to manufacture low-margin things in the United States in any competitive sense. Manufacturing goes elsewhere, and what thrives in the United States are financial services, technology designers, pharmaceutical designers, and military arms makers. In essence, we gradually sell our share of the world’s industrial base away to China and others, and prop up our financial, technology, healthcare, and defense sectors.

So, the rest of the world collectively runs trade surpluses against the United States, and reinvests those surpluses into U.S. Treasuries, U.S. corporate stocks and bonds, and to a lesser extent U.S. real estate and U.S. private equity. Or, like China with their Belt and Road initiative, they now use their trade surplus dollars to buy up commodity and infrastructure assets around the world. The trade deficit may seem like Americans are trading pieces of paper for goods and services (which would likely be a good trade) but due to this reinvestment, what we are actually selling is the ownership stock in our companies and future interest payments from our government. The foreign sector owns a larger and larger share of U.S. companies and government outlays over time, meaning that we sell our appreciating financial assets in exchange for depreciating goods and services.

The cumulative result of this is that the world owns about $24 trillion more U.S. assets than entities in the United States own of foreign assets. Another way to phrase that is that the net international investment position of the United States is negative $24 trillion:

![US NIIP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-niip.png)

This cycle has persisted for decades, and has become imbalanced enough to reach center stage in politics via two main pathways. The first pathway is that it contributes to populism, since “rust belt” areas of the country correctly feel left behind by this arrangement and start to want their industry to return. The second pathway is that it destabilizes military strength, like for example the U.S. has [had trouble](https://www.cnn.com/2024/03/10/politics/russia-artillery-shell-production-us-europe-ukraine/index.html) keeping up with Russia’s manufacturing output of artillery in recent years despite being a far larger economy overall. During the pandemic years, it was also quickly recognized how things like ventilators and core medical components are typically made in China, not the United States. Our industrial base has stagnated for three decades while the world’s total industrial base grew, with most of that growth being in China.

![Industrial Base](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-industrial-base.png)

What makes this even more imbalanced and self-reinforcing is that after two generations of outsourcing the country’s marginal manufacturing capability, we lost out on training of highly-skilled manufacturing technicians at sufficient scale. China and other countries have that human capital in massive numbers now. We also have the highest healthcare costs in the world, and that’s a burden that often falls on employers, which leads to worse competitiveness for outsource-able types of labor. Additionally, manufacturing is typically a very electricity-intensive activity, and the build-up of that electricity infrastructure is likewise out in China and elsewhere now.

![China Electricity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-china-electricity.png)

![Global Steel Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-global-steel-production.jpg)

So, bringing this capacity back to the United States is no small matter, and would take a long time if tried in earnest. It relates to the structural nature of the dollar system itself, and then both human capital and electricity production that have developed around where manufacturing has been supported, and have left where it has been ignored.

The fact that we exported it in the first place is part of why inflation has been pretty low over the recent decades. The rise of Chinese labor as it was unlocked to the world, mixed with Western capital, was a massive disinflationary force which came with costs.

The likely incoming Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent says he wants the U.S. dollar to remain the global reserve currency, but also wants to fix the trade imbalance between the United States and China. But in the current system, those trade deficits are what supply the world with dollars so it can be the reserve currency, and being the reserve currency causes imbalances of capital flows one way or another. During the 1970s and 1980s the trade surplus bogeyman was Japan. In the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s it has shifted to China. Individual areas can be targeted but if the system remains structurally similar, it keeps happening somewhere.

**Tariff Analysis**

One way to try to curtail trade deficits is to tax imports. If it’s more competitive to produce goods elsewhere, a tariff can offset that competitiveness.

But of course, that comes with a number of costs. Although proponents of tariffs have said that the foreign sector will pay for them by cutting prices and suffering lower profit margins, many companies operate with 10-20% profit margins, so if large tariffs are put in place, companies have to raise prices or stop selling to that market. This can therefore be quite inflationary. In addition, other countries can retaliate with tariffs of their own.

Now, if you ask a proponent of tariffs what the goal is, it would likely be to bring those companies back home. Instead of paying the tariffs, manufacturers could stop making things in China and make them in the United States instead. But as discussed earlier, that’s not a short-term thing to do at scale. It takes time to relocate and/or reestablish manufacturing facilities, electricity infrastructure, and skilled technicians. And they’ll be doing so in an inherently higher-cost jurisdiction, thus likely being inflationary compared to the status quo.

That’s why 2025 is certainly a year to watch. We don’t know to what extent Trump’s proposed tariffs will actually be put into effect at scale, or primarily used for negotiation purposes.

If we look at the numbers, the United States is currently running about $80 billion per month in trade deficits, or a little under $1 trillion per year. Roughly speaking, we import about $4 trillion in stuff and export about $3 trillion in stuff per year, giving us about $1 trillion in annual red ink. Put another way, that’s $1 trillion per year in dollars flowing out to the foreign sector, which needs dollars if the dollar is going to be the world reserve currency. If we start limiting dollars that flow into the rest of the world, then they have to sell dollar-denominated assets to get dollars to service their dollar-denominated debts, which starts to unravel the intent of the system.

About $500 billion of our $4 trillion in annual imports are from China, although the real number is ultimately larger because China sells to other countries that we then buy from.

![US Imports from China](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-us-imports-from-china.png)

China is the opposite. Their total exports are about $4 trillion per year, and imports are under $3 trillion, giving them a trade surplus of over $1 trillion per year. Their exports are very spread out; they go throughout the world since they’re now the biggest trading partner with most countries:

![China Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-china-trade-balance.png)

![China Exports by Country](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-china-exports-by-country.png)

If the United States government puts 20% tariffs on *all* U.S. imports (let’s call it $4 trillion worth of annual imports in round numbers), that’s $800 billion in annual tariff revenue. But of course, that would be highly disruptive to both the domestic economy and global economy, quite inflationary, and thus is unlikely to happen from a president that very much cares about both the stock market and economic performance. The possibility can’t be ignored, however.

If they put 20% tariffs on Chinese imports to the U.S. (let’s call it $500 billion in round numbers), then that’s $100 billion in annual tariff revenue. If they then put 5% tariffs on the ~$3.5 trillion in U.S. imports from other countries, then that would be an additional $175 billion in tariff revenue, for a grand total of $275 billion. That’s a more approachable figure. It would barely make a dent in the U.S. fiscal deficit, and it likely wouldn’t structurally change where production occurs, but around the margins that’s a possibility that could occur with less disruption.

Another option is to ratchet up tariffs. Rather than all hitting at once, they can be announced to go up over time unless certain agreements are made in the country’s favor. For example, the U.S. could say that this year all Chinese imports will have 5% tariffs and other imports will have 1% tariffs. And every year that goes up by another 5% for China and another 1% for the rest of the world unless deals are made to avoid that. That gives companies more time to relocate some manufacturing facilities or, when they plan new manufacturing facilities, decide to potentially put them in the United States. This again is a more approachable strategy, although it’ll take far more than four years to relocate human capital, electricity production, and re-align global money flows, and thus is still likely to have noticeable upward impacts on price inflation.

And all of those solutions fail to address the core issue that trade deficits are primarily what provide dollars to a world that needs dollars if the dollar is going to be the world reserve currency. The key change that can address that issue at the root, is for a neutral reserve asset to replace the dollar as the primary reserve asset, or for the world to become more multipolar in terms of its reserve assets, but the U.S. government doesn’t want that.

Tariffs could also come with an intentional dollar devaluation. Trump’s Treasury Secretary (presumably Scott Bessent) could initiate an intentional 10-20% devaluation of the dollar vs other major currencies via a Mar-a-Lago Accord (see “[Plaza Accord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plaza_Accord)” for reference) to try to make U.S. manufacturing more competitive and offset potential market disruption from tariffs. I expect something like that to probably occur during the four-year administration, and potentially in the first year, but it remains to be seen for sure. And if it does occur, it risks being inflationary for Americans but probably good for asset prices and GDP.

In my view, the potential imposition of tariffs, along with any potential intentional dollar devaluation or lack thereof by the Treasury Department, are among the biggest macro moving parts to watch this year that are hard to predict in advance because they are political in nature.

**Meme Coin Season**

The altcoin market goes through various narratives in each bull cycle. The 2017 bull narrative was initial coin offerings or “ICOs”. The 2020-2021 bull narratives were decentralized finance “DeFi” and non-fungible tokens “NFTs”.

So far, the 2024-2025 bull run narrative has mainly just been “meme coins” and so the altcoin cycle hasn’t had much legs to it. There’s no pretense of value creation; it’s just digital casino trading. It’s kind of the nihilistic end result of these cycles. People just gamble on which meme community will have the most momentum behind it for a period of time. The best thing that can be said about it is that it’s ironically more transparent.

On January 17th, Trump launched the biggest meme coin ever, or at least the biggest one since Dogecoin which is in a different category as a layer-one blockchain. And notably, he did it hours after SEC chairman Gary Gensler’s last day on the job. It has touched a circulating market cap of over $14 billion, and a fully diluted market cap of over $70 billion.

The way a meme coin works is that someone launches the coin, typically on top of another blockchain (Solana is the most popular one right now, and where the Trump coin was launched), and holds a big percentage of it themselves and sells others to the market to trade. Over time, the insiders can then start selling the coins at higher prices for a profit. Insiders and early traders can make a lot of money, but those who buy in late get left holding the bag. This can hurt a lot of uninformed buyers.

Recently, one of the biggest meme coins was launched by Haliey Welch for her “Hawk Tua” meme (which I’m not going to explain here; it’s a sexual meme and many are already familiar with it), and right after it began trading she and/or her team allegedly rug-pulled it. In this context, to “rug-pull” refers to insiders selling a ton of coins early, thus shortening the life of the meme and quickly profiting at the expense of anyone who bought into it and still holds it at the time of the rug-pull. The coin’s price dropped to practically zero, and she has been heavily criticized and risked facing legal action over it (at least from the prior SEC).

For Trump’s coin, insiders owned 90% of it at launch, and the other 10% was sold to trade on the market. That 90% insider ownership is subject to gradual unlocks over the next three years, meaning that insiders can gradually sell it and take profit. He’s already received criticism for launching the meme coin, but how it’s traded by insiders afterward can further inflame or dull the reaction. If they quickly sell like Welch allegedly did, then it could result in major community backlash. If it’s held longer, then the thing could trade around in size for a while.

Initial chain analysis showed that some of the unlocked coins were already sold, although I’ve seen some debate regarding whether those coins truly left the hands of the owners or just shifted around in wallets:

![Trump Coin Sell Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-trump-coin-sell-tweet.png)

On paper, the majority of Trumps’ net worth is now in this meme coin; that’s how big the launch has been. But it remains to be seen how much can be extracted without crashing the price. It opens up a lot of questions around campaign finance laws as well. The coins will be unlocking throughout Trump’s administration, giving him and/or his team the opportunity to sell to the market and make money without a product or service.

Eventually, we’ll see a top in this narrative like we’ve seen with other narratives, as people will become accustomed to it and the set of potential bag holders will be exhausted by it. There’s probably no bigger meme coin that could occur than this Trump meme coin, with an Elon Musk meme coin as a possible exception. He’s already adopted dogecoin as his meme coin of choice, however, which limits the probability of that occurring. In addition, a meme coin can’t materially affect Musks’ 12-figure net worth to the same degree that it can affect Trump’s 10-figure net worth, which reduces the incentive of launching one.

One of the indirect losers from all of this is Ethereum. A lot of these meme coins are being launched on top of Solana rather than Ethereum. This bull cycle is basically just Bitcoin for store-of-value and meme coins for casino speculation, leaving little leftover for Ethereum.

As such, the ETHBTC ratio reached new cycle lows:

## Tax Loss Selling Aftermath

Toward the end of the year, investors generally have some positions that have risen in value and others that have lost value. When those winning investments get sold, it generates a capital gains tax liability unless those assets are held in retirement accounts. And so, investors often try to pair the selling of winning investments with losing investments, so that the realized losses of the losers offsets some of the realized gains of the winners.

The result of this is that if security A goes up for mostly fundamental reasons, and security B falls for mostly fundamental reason, then there is often additional selling pressure on security B at the end of the year in order to use those losses to offset gains elsewhere in a portfolio. There is “irrational” selling on top of the “rational” selling that already occurred, in other words. I use “irrational” in quotes because the year-end selling is actually pretty rational for the investor, but it results in price action that diverges from the fundamentals for a period of time.

By extension, when the calendar year ends, the tax loss selling ends, and those assets can be left at unusually cheap valuations and with an exhaustion of selling pressure.

**Brazil Example**

One might ask “If Brazilian equities have performed so poorly, shouldn’t we sell them?” What is happening in markets, however, is that Brazilian equities are finding what is at least a short-term floor, because a lot of extra selling was done at year end to book realized losses before the calendar year ends.

This is Brazil’s largest bank stock, for example, with a bounce occurring at the start of the year after a sharp sell-off:

![ITUB Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-itub-price.png)

And here’s their F.A.S.T. Graph. There is a sharp divergence between analyst expectations and investor sentiment and share price:

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-itub-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

Brazil’s economy is doing okay currently, but the main issue that is spooking investors is their growing fiscal deficit, which has sparked a currency sell-off. The prior administration ran a relatively austere set of fiscal policies, while the current administration has grown the deficit significantly. The goal of the administration is to support the working class with these deficits, but the risk is that it can stoke higher inflation and trigger a balance of payments crisis, which Brazil has seen multiple times in the past.

Ironically, Brazil’s deficit relative to GDP is similar to that of the United States, but the United States has entrenched global demand for its currency, which as mentioned in a prior section of this report damages the United States’ trade balance but helps keep the wheels on the fiscal situation. Countries like Brazil don’t have that anchor around their trade balance, but they have a very thin line around fiscal policy.

Officials at Brazil’s central bank have raised public concerns around the country’s fiscal deficit, and have raised interest rates from 10.5% to 12.25% over the past several months to support the currency.

It remains to be seen how effectively Brazil’s government will approach the fiscal deficit in 2025, but investor sentiment is near the bottom right now, and the combination of positive real rates and current account surpluses are supportive.

I tend to view the sell-off in Brazilian equities as overdone, and a potential bullish trade opportunity for 2025. However, I would maintain cautious position sizing.

**S&P 500 Example**

The biggest winners in the U.S. stock market for 2024 were the mega cap stocks. Many of the mid caps were flat or down for the year, and some of them were ripe for tax loss harvesting, driving further sell-offs.

However, when we zoom out and look at the equal-weight S&P 500 vs the market-weight S&P 500, January is also starting to be somewhat of a near-term floor in that ratio. In these ratio charts below, “up” means that the equal weight version is outperforming (i.e. mid caps within the S&P 500 are doing better than large and mega caps) and “down” means the market weight version is outperforming (i.e. large and mega caps are doing better than mid caps):

It wasn’t just the Mag-7 tech stocks that have gathered a lot of investors dollars relative to other investments during 2024. There are a bunch of other blue-chip mega-caps like Walmart (WMT) that have seen a big disconnect between stock price and fundamental growth as well:

![WMT FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-wmt-fastgraph.png)

Going into 2025, I am concerned about the future performance of some of those big stocks with major valuation disconnects, and optimistic (with careful position sizing) on some of the oversold equities of 2024 that still have decent fundamentals. This includes some U.S. mid-cap stocks and some emerging market stocks.

The equal-weight S&P 500 can be bought via the Invesco S&P 500 Equal Weight ETF (RSP), and is one way to express this view. It gives access to the same blue-chip stocks in the S&P 500 as the normal index, but with less concentration toward the mega-caps. This can hurt it during times of mega-cap outperformance, but also protects investors from valuation bubbles in those mega-cap stocks on a relative basis.

## Final Thoughts: Gold

There’s been a decent bounce in gold throughout January, bringing the price closer to its prior highs:

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-gold.png)

The price has a higher-low in place, and so it’s worth watching in the coming weeks to see whether it breaks out to new highs or hits resistance at the prior high and continues chopping. The outcome is likely going to be quite correlated with liquidity conditions and the dollar index discussed earlier in this report.

I’d be happy to see a bit more consolidation in gold to further reduce investor sentiment around it before the next run higher, but either way I remain structurally bullish on gold, as more countries continue to stack gold as a reserve asset in lieu of additional Treasury securities.
