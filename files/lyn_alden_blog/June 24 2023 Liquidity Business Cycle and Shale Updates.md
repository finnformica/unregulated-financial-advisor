## Macro View

**Liquidity Regime Recap**

Liquidity is one of the biggest intermediate-term drivers for both financial assets and even the economy itself. Valuations, structural growth, and macro factors serve as the foundation of my long-term views on various asset classes, but in recent years liquidity tends to be a driving force over any given multi-month period.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-domestic-liquidity.png)

-From March 2020 through the end of 2021, the market was under a very pro-liquidity regime, which was very bullish for most assets. The Federal Reserve in particular was adding massive amounts of liquidity, which when combined with fiscal stimulus was an enormously stimulative market environment.

-From the start of 2022 through September 2022, the market was under a very negative liquidity regime, which was bearish for most assets. The Federal Reserve was performing quantitative tightening, and the Treasury was also refilling its Treasury General Account, while reverse repos were grinding a bit higher. This ended with the UK sovereign bond market breaking, the US sovereign bond market become rather illiquid, and the dollar index peaking.

-From October 2022 through May 2023, the market was under a mostly flat liquidity regime. The Federal Reserve was still doing quantitative tightening (negative for liquidity), reverse repos were flat (neutral for liquidity), and the Treasury was rapidly drawing down its general account back into the financial sector (positive for liquidity). With the dollar index falling as well, this was pretty good for global liquidity even as it was relatively flat for domestic liquidity in the United States.

**The Current Liquidity Transition**

We’re now entering a new liquidity regime with the Treasury refilling its cash account, but it’s not 100% clear how it’ll play out. We can, however, map out the key forces that are likely to affect how it’ll play out, which allows us to discern certain probabilities.

The main question to figure out is how much forward liquidity is the Treasury going to suck out of bank reserves (which would be bad for liquidity) or reverse repos (which would be neutral or good for liquidity).

*Force 1) Deposit Flight Towards Money Market Funds*

Money market funds hold a combination of T-bills and commercial paper, but in addition to that they are currently the biggest users of the Fed’s reverse repo facility.

And we can think of the reverse repo facility as a spillover from excess demand for T-bills. The reason the Fed has the facility is that it helps them maintain the floor for their interest rate target. If they didn’t offer the facility, there might be too much demand for T-bills compared to supply, and this might push the interest rate on very short duration T-bills below the Fed’s interest rate floor. At this time, reverse repos pay 5.05% interest to users of the reverse repo facility, which helps maintain that floor.

Currently, money market funds offer higher interest rates than bank deposits, and are in many ways safer. So, there has been a gradual flight of capital from bank deposits to money markets. Money markets have to buy short-term safe debt or basically park it in the reverse repo facility.

Here’s the current level for money market funds through the end of May 2023:

![Money Markets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25.png)

*Data Source: [OFR](https://www.financialresearch.gov/money-market-funds/us-mmfs-investments-by-fund-category/)*

At the end of May, money markets had $5.867 trillion in assets. During March, $398 billion flowed into them due to the banking crisis. In April, this flow slowed to $67 billion. In May, the flow increased a bit to $107 billion. This can come from a lot of sources, but one of the main sources consists of people realizing that they can move excess savings from their bank savings account to a money market fund in order to earn a notably higher interest rate and at lower risk level.

So far in June, money market levels have been relatively flat.

If this re-accelerates, and bank deposits continue flowing into money markets and then winding up in reverse repos, then that’s bad for banking system liquidity. If this flow generally remains under $100 billion per month, then liquidity may hold up pretty well.

*Force 2) The Treasury’s Debt Maturity Composition*

The Treasury is going to continue running very large fiscal deficits, while also refilling its general account from nearly zero, which is a big reversal of the prior liquidity regime. They’ve already partially refilled it due to the June 15th quarterly tax payments combined with a lot of T-bill issuance.

During June, the Treasury shortened the duration of its Treasury security issuance compared to normal. By increasing their supply of T-bills (which can serve as a form of cash-equivalents, unlike long-duration Treasury bonds), they aim to get pools of cash-equivalent capital like money market funds to buy the government debt. If successful, this will rotate some capital out of reverse repos and into T-bills. Managers of these sorts of funds [are saying](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-06-13/invesco-s-brignac-says-money-funds-scrambling-for-treasury-bills) now that rate hikes are slowing down and more T-bill supply is coming, moving into more T-bills makes sense.

The Treasury’s average maturity for its debt typically ranges between four and six years. Lately it has been on the upper end of that range. Going forward, they are planning to lower their average duration, which will potentially allow them to suck liquidity out of reverse repos to finance the debt rather than suck it all out of bank deposits (which would likely seize up the banking system and cause liquidity problems in the Treasury market again). In other words, the Treasury’s average debt duration is likely to follow the purple line or maybe even lower:

![Average Treasury Maturity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-average-maturity.png)

*Chart Source: [Quarterly TBAC Presentation](https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/financing-the-government/quarterly-refunding/most-recent-quarterly-refunding-documents)*

With an inverted yield curve, long-duration Treasuries have much lower yields than T-bills. In terms of minimizing government interest expense, it would make sense for the Treasury to *extend* their average duration at this time, not shrink it. However, the liquidity problems in the banking system are overriding that at the moment. So, the Treasury plans to issue most of their debt on the shorter and more expensive part of the duration curve.

*Putting Two Forces Together*

From January 2023 through May 2023 I had a very clear picture of liquidity that I wrote about frequently: the Treasury would offset the Fed’s liquidity reduction and thus we’d have a flat liquidity situation (and thus probably a relatively benign asset price environment) until the debt ceiling is lifted. That played out as expected and it’s finished now.

Going forward, the liquidity outlook is a lot murkier.

We can picture bank deposits and money markets as two pools of water, with mostly one-directional pumps between them.

-The first pump is pulling water from deposits toward money markets, as people search for higher yields and better safety.

-The second pump is the Treasury issuing extra T-bills, and convincing money market funds to pull money out of reverse repos and use those funds to buy the T-bills. The Treasury then spends that money back out into bank deposits during the normal course of government expenditure.

If you expect fund flows from bank deposits to money markets to accelerate, then liquidity is likely to be poor. On the other hand, if you expect those fund flows to remain under $100 billion a month at a time when more than $100 billion per month in net T-bill issuance is likely to occur in their effort to suck money out of reverse repos, then liquidity is likely to be decent.

I’m starting to get more optimistic about liquidity, but I need to see a couple more weeks of data for confirmation to develop any sort of conviction. At the moment I remain neutral, with the Fed continuing to perform quantitative tightening while the Treasury sucks at least some percentage of its spending out of reverse repos and back into deposit circulation.

However, large-cap U.S. stocks are already pretty stretched to the upside compared to where liquidity is now. When we consider the current high valuations of the mega-cap stocks (especially when compared to a rather high discount rate thanks to high interest rates), we could easily have a situation where liquidity continues to trend sideways while the mega-caps correct a bit down.

**U.S. Business Cycle Update**

Overall, a number of indicators continue to point towards recession risk later this year.

The yield curve remains deeply inverted:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-yield-curve.png)

The Atlanta Fed’s GDP Now estimator suggests modest 1.9% annualized growth for Q2, which finishes at the end of this month:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-gdpnow.png)

The labor market, which has been the strongest part of the economy over the past year amid various other pseudo-recessionary indicators, is starting to crack:

![Jobless Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-jobless-claims.png)

The Conference Board Leading Economic Index decreased in May relative to April and continues to be at recessionary levels, but has briefly stabilized in year-over-year % change terms.

![LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-lei.png)

Housing starts, however, have picked up. Demand for new homes is tight due to higher mortgage rates, but also, nobody wants to sell their home with a 3% fixed-rate 30-year mortgage attached to it either, since they can’t replace that mortgage. So, homebuilders have been enjoying a period of stabilization in early 2023 and a spike in May, after being under pressure during the second half of 2022.

![Housing Starts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-housing-starts.png)

Meanwhile, recession-resistant businesses in aggregate like hospitals and restaurants continue to grind higher in terms of revenue.

**Base Case: A Mild Stagflationary Recession**

The past two recessions (2008 and 2020) have been absolute cataclysms, with huge deflationary contractions and massive spikes in unemployment. People naturally expect the next one to be the same. Maybe it will be, but in my view the data isn’t pointing that way for now.

If we look back at recession behavior, the 2001 recession stands out as mild. Real GDP in year-over-year % change terms didn’t even go negative, and the unemployment rate didn’t get too high. That didn’t stop it from being highly destructive to the bubbly stock market, but the economy itself hummed along.

![Business Cycles](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-business-cycles.png)

I wouldn’t be surprised to see the next recession look kind of like that, except more stagflationary.

The reasons are twofold. The first is that top-heavy demographics have created a rather tight labor market, especially in blue collar work. From welders to airplane mechanics, there just isn’t a lot of slack for the number of people able and willing to do physical work. It’s possible that the layoffs in unprofitable tech companies combined with pressures from AI will put enough tech/office people out of work and some of them will eventually rotate into more hands-on work (I’m suddenly reminded of the movie *Office Space*), but that’s not a smooth transition.

Secondly, the U.S. government is running a fiscal deficit equal to about 8% of GDP. This is driven largely by a combination of deteriorating demographics and high interest expense. As more Baby Boomers collect Social Security, it results in larger deficits. As interest rates go higher, it also results in larger deficits.

![Fiscal Deficit](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-fiscal-deficit.png)

Importantly… the deficit is 8% of GDP ***without*** being in a recession. This is very rare.

World War II had enormous deficits relative to GDP. The peak of the COVID-19 stimulus reached 15% deficits relative to GDP. The period from 2009-2011, during and after the biggest recession of the modern era, ran roughly 10% deficits. Deficits during the mid-1930s, amid the worst of the Great Depression, ran about 8% per year. You can see these figures via the gray bars on the chart below, although I haven’t updated for 2023 yet:

![Century Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-century-chart.png)

We’re currently running 8% deficits despite nearly record low unemployment levels. Most of that fiscal spending is locked in. If we enter a recession, tax receipts will stagnate, and so the deficit will naturally widen, unless interest rates fall a lot. Deficits in the high single digits as a percentage of GDP are expected to continue all decade going forward.

A deficit is basically a stimulus. The government is sending tons of money out to the healthcare sector, to seniors, to the military, and to anyone holding government debt. It’s hard to have a deep deflationary recession with $2+ trillion in deficits pouring into the economy prior to the recession even starting.

In addition, there’s not a ton of spare capacity in the oil or commodity markets. It’s not as though there was a big capex cycle that brought a lot of new supply on followed by a contraction. The whole commodity sector is still somewhat tight because we haven’t had a major capex cycle in a while.

As a result, this potential upcoming recession looks like a grindier, stagflationary type of recession. This outlook could of course change based on all sorts of things including centralized policy decisions, but in my view that’s the most probable scenario at this time.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has reached over $30,000 again, and for a brief period reached over $31,000 and touched a new year-to-date high. This allowed it to continue a trend of higher-highs and higher-lows this year so far.

During bear markets, more coins end up in illiquid hands, which refers to entities who rarely sell. Eventually the existing sellers run out of coins to sell, and it only takes a small spark of new demand against illiquid and hard-capped supply to trigger the next bull run.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-hodl-wave.png)

This spark of new demand historically occurs with risk-on environments (rising liquidity and rising PMIs), particularly in the past two or three cycles as bitcoin has become a larger asset. The halving every four years also tends to help, since the new supply that comes to market gets cut in half.

![BTC vs PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-btc-pmi.png)

And then during bull market manias, some of those illiquid holders do eventually sell a little bit, tons of new hyped-up altcoins come to market which creates endless new cryptocurrency supply to absorb market hype, and liquidity and overall risk-on behavior starts to roll over, and so eventually the bull market exhausts itself.

**BlackRock Trust Filing**

BlackRock (BLK), the world’s largest asset manager, filed with the SEC to launch an exchange traded spot bitcoin trust a week and a half ago. It’s generally similar to an ETF, but much like SPDR Gold Shares (GLD), a trust gives more discretion to the asset manager regarding redemptions.

![BlackRock Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-blackrock-tweet.png)

Other countries have spot bitcoin ETFs, but the SEC has turned down all applications so far for one in the United States. The SEC’s main objection has been around the possibility for market price manipulation, which hasn’t been an issue for other countries’ spot bitcoin ETFs. Additionally, the SEC just approved the first *leveraged* bitcoin futures ETF, before they have approved any sort of unleveraged spot bitcoin ETF, despite the fact that the former product obviously has more risk.

BlackRock’s filing is technically for a trust, and BlackRock has a 99.8% success rate at getting their filings approved with a sample size of over 500. In addition, BlackRock included a specific proposal to address the SEC’s concerns around market price manipulation; they will use a surveillance-sharing agreement about trading activity to provide assurances about transparent pricing.

So, we’ll see whether the unstoppable force moves the immovable object or not. If this launch is approved, it should be bullish for bitcoin’s price over the intermediate term, all else being equal. It creates an institutional access point wrapper for bitcoin as a commodity that is 1) a registered security 2) run by the world’s largest asset manager, and 3) is traded on the Nasdaq. The recent price spike in bitcoin is likely tied in part due this new information.

The Blackrock filing was followed by a flurry of other institutions filing or re-filing spot bitcoin ETFs as well. Meanwhile, an institutional crypto exchange backed by Citadel, Fidelity, and Schwab called EDX Markets also [went live a few weeks ago](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-06-20/crypto-exchange-backed-by-citadel-securities-fidelity-goes-live#xj4y7vzkg). EDX Markets is a noncustodial U.S. cryptocurrency exchange that specifically caters to institutions, rather than individual investors directly.

## U.S. Shale Update

Shale oil wells start producing oil faster than conventional wells, but also start depleting very quickly. It requires constant new drilling to replace prior drilling in order to maintain production, let alone grow production.

The number of active U.S. oil rigs peaked in late 2022, and during the first half of 2023 there has been a small but firm downtrend. This tends to be a leading indicator for overall U.S. oil production (e.g., the red dots tend to lead to the green dots):

![US Oil Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-us-oil-production.png)

The absolute number of oil rigs doesn’t tell us much. As we can see on the chart, the last few drilling cycles had lower-lows in terms of rig count, but oil production reached higher highs. This is because drilling locations and technology have changed during this time. However, the rate-of-change in the number of oil rigs within a given year *does* tell us quite a bit. Unless this starts turning around, production levels are likely to stagnate and possibly decline over the next year.

This is supported by the fact that the number of drilled-but-uncompleted wells is at eight year lows:

![Drilled But Uncompleted Wells](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-ducs.png)

As the name describes, drilled-but-uncompleted wells occur when a company drills a well but then doesn’t extract hydrocarbons from it. Instead, they leave it in a nearly-completed state. From that point on, this represents a certain type of inventory; the company can complete the well and start producing oil from it at a lower cost than drilling an entirely new well.

Companies have spent the past few years conserving their capital by tapping into their prior drilled-but-uncompleted wells that were built up during 2018 and 2019. In other words, it’s like using up some of their inventory. Going forward, they’ll likely have to put more capital into the ground in order to sustain current production levels, let alone grow them. And with oil around $70 with a potential recession on the horizon, they’re not exactly eager to do aggressive drilling. They would need to see a more sustained price uptrend.

Meanwhile, the U.S. Strategic Petroleum Reserve [plans to start refilling this summer](https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/us-hopes-buy-12-mln-barrels-oil-reserve-this-year-source-2023-06-13/). It drew down aggressively starting in late 2021 in order to combat high oil prices. They even kept reducing it a bit here in 2023 when oil prices were already mild. This drawdown in 2022 pushed spare capacity into the market and helped alleviate the supply/demand imbalance.

![Strategic Petroleum Reserve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-spr.png)

They only plan to add about 12 million barrels for the remainder of this year. But, this is one source of oil sales that is now likely off the market, which combined with some tightness out of OPEC, can help firm up the oil price market.

I remain constructive on oil producers with significant reserves, such as most of the oil majors including BP (BP). As U.S. shale producers start to stagnate in terms of production over the longer run, it benefits major non-shale oil companies that have more constant oil production and longer capex cycles. More specifically, many oil majors do have some shale assets, but it’s generally not the dominant part of their business. At current oil prices, they can support substantial dividends and rapid share buybacks, which helps boost forward per-share dividend growth prospects.

![BP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-bp-fastgraph.png)

Additionally, I remain constructive on diversified pipeline companies, particularly including ones that have a lot of non-oil business lines. Enterprise Products Partners (EPD) for example has a large NGL and petrochemical transport business. It pays a 7.6% yield which is higher than T-bills, and has low double-digit annual distribution growth with a 25-year consecutive track record of annual growth. I continue to like it as part of a diversified portfolio.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-epd-fastgraph.png)

Instability within Russia, depending on the magnitude, could affect Russia’s oil output (among other things, including their nuclear arsenal), but the situation remains too early to draw conclusions from. **A Note on Uranium**

I initially covered uranium bullishly back in my October 25, 2020 report. It was $30/lb, and could be bought at a 20% discount to NAV (so ~$24/lb) via the Uranium Participation Corp which is now called the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust (SRUUF).

I followed it up with a September 5, 2021 report at $38/lb where I confirmed my long-term bullishness.

During that time, it broke out to $50, and mostly chugged along sideways for a couple years, save for one brief price spike in spring 2022 amid Russia’s invasion of Ukraine.

![Uranium Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-uranium.png)

Well, since April 2023 it seems as though it may be breaking out again, and this time it might be more durable. It’s up to $56 as of this writing. I don’t have a firm view on the next few months, but I continue to view uranium trusts (Sprott Physical Uranium Trust or Yellow Cake plc) as attractive assets to hold with a multi-year view as a small slice within a diversified portfolio. Nuclear power plants will need to buy a lot of uranium contracts over the next few years, and ongoing mine production is below ongoing consumption levels.

## Final Thoughts: China Stimulus or Lack Thereof

In late 2022 amid widespread protests, China’s government pivoted on their long-lasting “zero-COVID” policy and began a process of opening back up.

However, it has been a generally sluggish recovery in 2023, and in particular, [Chinese youth unemployment](https://www.goldmansachs.com/intelligence/pages/why-has-youth-unemployment-risen-so-much-in-china.html) is at multi-year highs of over 20%. Their real estate sector is in a significant slump as well, which led the central bank to cut interest rates to 3.55% in an attempt to alleviate the pressure.

![China Property](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-china-property.png)

In general, there’s a tale of two Chinas at the moment. Most of the demand side is weak. However, their supply side continues to fire on all cylinders. Their auto exports in particular have been on an absolute tear:

![China Autos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-china-autos.png)

This divergence is in large part attributable to China’s current fiscal policy. While the U.S. floods its private sector with fiscal deficits, China has been more fiscally restrained at the moment. Throughout the pandemic/lockdown period they didn’t turn to aggressive fiscal spending as a main lever to pull, and even amid a tepid recovery they’ve still been slow to turn to it. Bloomberg [had a good article](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-06-19/goldman-sachs-cuts-china-gdp-forecast-on-limited-stimulus-space#xj4y7vzkg) and chart on it.

![China Fiscal Policy](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-6-25-china-fiscal.png)

China’s fiscal policy remains a key variable to monitor for the second half of 2023 and then into 2024. If they turn towards fiscal stimulus to counter a stagnant economy, it should be powerful for Chinese equities and for emerging market growth broadly (more Chinese construction, more Chinese consumption, etc). However, to the extent that they maintain their current status quo, then the current doldrums are likely to persist, albeit with a strong ongoing industrial export base.
