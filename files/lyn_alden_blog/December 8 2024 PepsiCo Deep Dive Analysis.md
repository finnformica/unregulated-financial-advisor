## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to trend sideways, as the Treasury and Fed effectively balance out their liquidity-related actions.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-domestic-liquidity.png)

This is likely to continue until banks hit their limits under the “ample reserves” framework that the Fed is currently operating under, which is likely to be in 2025.

Bank cash as a % of assets continues to suggest that banks are moderately above their liquidity thresholds, probably by about $100-$200 billion.

![Bank Cash](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-bank-cash.png)

There is $130 billion in excess liquidity left in the reverse repo facility as well:

![RRP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-rrp.png)

Global liquidity has been pressured by a strong dollar move ever since the U.S. election, but that seems as though it may have topped out for the moment:

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-dxy.png)

Overall, domestic and global liquidity continue to be rangebound, with catalysts pointing to an end in Fed quantitative tightening some time in 2025.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Despite many analysts calling for recession, the U.S. economy continues to chug along. The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator, for example, is back up above 3% annualized growth for the current Q4:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-gdpnow.png)

The Conference Board’s Leading Economic Index is weak, but not declining as it was during the past two years:

![LEI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-lei.png)

Manufacturing continues to be in mild contraction, and this is the longest that it has ever hovered at such weak levels without collapsing to outright recession levels:

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-pmi.png)

House sales are also near multi-decade lows:

![Home Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-home-sales.png)

Basically, there are several parts of the U.S. economy that are in mild recession or operating at stall speed. But deficits reaching up to 7% of GDP are a rather powerful stimulatory force. That spending is entering the economy and then circulating into other areas. This is why I continue to emphasize fiscal dominance as an important metric to monitor.

And for the first time since Q2 2022, nearly a year and a half ago, banks in aggregate are no longer tightening their credit standards. The past four times that more than 40% of banks were tightening credit standards, it occurred with a recession. This fifth time during 2022-2023 was the exception.

![SLOOS](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-sloos.png)

**Egypt Update**

Those who have followed my work for a while know that I live part of each year in Egypt. I’ve been back here since mid-November, and it’s always neat to comment on the state of the economy when I’m here, since the commentary often applies to many developing countries and gives an example of on-the-ground realities throughout much of the world.

Egypt’s broad money supply is growing at almost 30% per year now, and the official inflation rate is 26%. Official inflation peaked in 2023 at 38%, bottomed in July 2024, and since then has steadily been on the rise again:

![Egypt Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-egypt-inflation.jpg)

This is why tens of millions of people in Egypt, let alone billions of people worldwide, seek global assets like dollars, gold, bitcoin, or developed market equities and real estate. The stock market is not very reliable here, so the main local investment that people make is in real estate, which is illiquid. In many neighborhoods there are large swaths of unoccupied and unfinished real estate, as people store wealth in it rather than the local currency.

I discussed currency debasement at length in [Broken Money](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR/). This continual debasement means that every contract that exists, ranging from employee salaries to business supply contracts, constantly has its unit of account debased, and has to be frequently updated to keep up, which results in high administrative overhead and a burden on those who are on the receiving side of the contract to push aggressively for those updates. And most people on the receiving side end up not keeping up; anyone in Egypt who is not getting about a 25-30% raise each year is getting their income diluted relative to the total currency supply in the country.

In terms of everyday life, the economic issues and currency devaluation show up most apparently in the form of expensive imports. Cars in particular have become a lot less affordable recently, if one compares the average car price to the average annual income. Traveling outside of the country, buying electronics, buying cars, and buying other types of imports have become unaffordable for a lot of people. In many cases, car prices have tripled over the past several years, while someone’s salary roughly doubled in that timeframe.

One of the ways that I like to assess the economic health of a developing country is to look at its per capita energy usage. It’s a rather hard statistic to fake or game, and gets down to the root of it. The average Egyptian uses only about 1/8th of the energy as the average American, and sadly, their raw energy use per person has been steadily *declining* for the past decade:

![Egypt Energy Usage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-egypt-energy.png)

If it were a developed country with high per-capita energy usage, we might say that people are using as much energy as they need and economic development is coming from elsewhere (e.g. from more digital sources, having met most of their day-to-day material desires). However, in developing countries with low energy use, a lack of energy consumption growth is a sign of an ill economy; the majority of people would prefer to be living more comfortably, traveling more comfortably, and having access to other things that require a lot of energy including most recently AI and its associated boost to productivity.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Touches $100k**

Bitcoin reached over $100k for the first time during the past week. Since then it has gone above and below the $100k level.

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-btc.png)

I initially recommended bitcoin back in April 2020 at $6,900/coin, and here during 2024 I made the case that bitcoin was far more likely to break up out of its March-November consolidation range than to break down from it. It has been nice to see the price break out to such a significant degree, and to cross back and forth over the psychologically relevant $100k threshold.

In addition, the Bitcoin network as a whole touched $2 trillion in market capitalization for the first time. There are about 19.8 million bitcoin in circulation, and so the price breaking slightly over $100k was enough to touch $2 trillion in market cap.

Bitcoin has historically been one of my most controversial recommendations. There are plenty of people that say they like my macro research but aren’t a fan of my bitcoin enthusiasm, and yet this asset has increased over 14x in price since I recommended it, making it my most successful investment recommendation to date, along with MSTR as a levered bitcoin play, which is up about 30x since I added it to the Newsletter portfolio in August 2020.

Bullish sentiment is reaching rather significant levels, which suggests near-term caution (i.e. be careful with leverage, manage expectations, and expect periods of consolidation or correction), but I continue to be bullish on bitcoin over the next 12 months, and expect to continue holding bitcoin for the long-term unless my thesis structurally changes in some way, which has not occurred thus far in my 4.5 year holding period.

Historically, whenever bitcoin soars to new heights, it starts to trigger some selling from long-term holders. Depending on how long they’ve held, they might be up 5x, 10x, 20x, or more in gains, and they either want to rebalance or they want to increase consumption (e.g. buy a house or otherwise materially upgrade their lifestyle), and so they sell some portion of their coins. This is how the network widens its holder base over time, as early adopters keep holding but sell some of their coins to newer buyers.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-hodl-wave.png)

When we do see a lot of selling like that, it starts to warrant caution. Basically, higher prices allow more locked-up supply to come to market until current demand is saturated.

Back in early 2016 after a long bitcoin bear market, 61.5% of coins had not moved on-chain in over a year. By the end of the two-year raging bull market that followed, less than 41% of coins hadn’t moved in over a year. That massive run-up in price unlocked a lot of selling from long-term holders, to the tune of over 20% of coins.

And then in 2020, again after a long bear market, over 63.6% of coins had not moved in over a year. By the end of the bull market over the next year, this figure was below 54%. About 10% of coins had moved on-chain.

After another bear market, by late 2023, over 70.7% of coins hadn’t moved in over a year. That figure is down to about 63.2%, and so about 7.5% of coins have moved on-chain. I expect that this bull market still has legs to run, and expect that we’ll probably exceed the 10% figure of the prior bull market, which was a rather stunted one. Along with the ratio of market capitalization to on-chain cost basis, this unlocking of strongly-held coins is a primary metric I’ll continue to monitor.

## Deep Dive: PepsiCo

PepsiCo (PEP) is a global beverage and snack company that was founded in 1898. Their latest annual report gives a good breakdown of their segments:

> We are organized into seven reportable segments (also referred to as divisions), as follows:
>
> 1) Frito-Lay North America (FLNA), which includes our branded convenient food businesses in the United States and Canada;
>
> 2) Quaker Foods North America (QFNA), which includes our branded convenient food businesses, such as cereal, rice, pasta and other branded food, in the United States and Canada;
>
> 3) PepsiCo Beverages North America (PBNA), which includes our beverage businesses in the United States and Canada;
>
> 4) Latin America (LatAm), which includes all of our beverage and convenient food businesses in Latin America;
>
> 5) Europe, which includes all of our beverage and convenient food businesses in Europe;
>
> 6) Africa, Middle East and South Asia (AMESA), which includes all of our beverage and convenient food businesses in Africa, the Middle East and South Asia; and
>
> 7) Asia Pacific, Australia and New Zealand and China Region (APAC), which includes all of our beverage and convenient food businesses in Asia Pacific, Australia and New Zealand, and China region.
>
> -PepsiCo 2023 Annual Report

Their non-alcoholic beverage business is the second biggest in the world after Coca Cola (KO) and their snack business is also one of the biggest in the world after Nestle and up there with other packaged food titans.

The biggest challenge for the company currently is topline growth. It has historically gone through cycles of strong growth and topline stagnation, and right now they’re in a period of stagnation, which is offset by the company’s capital returns.

![PEP Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-pep-revenue.png)

One of the negative catalysts recently, at least for the share price and potentially for the topline, was the introduction of powerful new weight loss drugs like Ozempic that curb cravings. While this will need to be monitored, it’s important to note that PepsiCo’s product portfolio shifts over time to match consumer preferences. And so as the snack industry changes, PepsiCo’s line-up gradually changes as well.

The company’s profit margins are remarkably stable, due to the broad diversification of their product portfolio, global reach, and well-oiled operations with large economies of scale.

![PEP Profit Margins](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-pep-margins.png)

**Capital Returns**

PepsiCo has paid dividends that have increased per share every year for 52 consecutive years. Over the past 20 years, the average annual increase has been about 9.5%, and over the past 10 years it has been about 7.5%.

The current dividend yield is over 3.3%. In my view, a diversified collection of stocks that pay 3%+ in dividends that grow by 7%+ per year, is more attractive than holding a large amount of 10-year Treasuries that pay 4.2% with zero growth for a decade.

PepsiCo also buys back shares, meaning that each shareholder owns a larger and larger percentage of the company over time if they keep holding. So, investors own an increasing share of a nominally larger company over time, and receive growing payments that they can spend on consumption, spend on other investments, or reinvest into more shares of PepsiCo.

**Balance Sheet**

PepsiCo has a solid A+ credit rating. The company has $45 billion in net long term debt, which is pretty reasonable compared to their $9+ billion in annual net income that is stable and steadily growing.

One thing I’ve highlighted before, including in my “[Most Investments are Bad](https://www.lynalden.com/most-investments-are-bad/)” article, is that large corporations often issue debt purely for arbitrage purposes. They don’t *have* to have debt, but they do so in order to lower their cost of capital and, more bluntly, to short fiat currency.

At the end of 2023, PepsiCo had $41 billion in long-term debt. Most of this was locked in at well below 3.5% interest rates for years and decades, relative to the money supply which grows by an average of 7% per year in the United States. They use that capital to buy other companies, pay dividends, and buy back shares, all of which generally gives investors a higher rate of return than 3.5%, as long as they don’t go overboard with it.

![PEP Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-pep-debt.png)

Basically, one of the best investments that large corporations make, is to short fiat currency for long periods of time at low interest rates.

Likewise, my husband and I have a villa here in Egypt as our second home, and we bought it with the functional equivalent of a 7-year loan at about 3% interest rates (when one does the math of an upfront lump sum vs 7 years of installment payments that nominally add up to only about 20% more than that upfront lump sum would be). If we would have bought it up front we would have had to sell some minor portion of our stocks or bitcoin, but that would be foolish compared to financing it with locked-in installment payments that don’t sum to much more than the upfront purchase would be, in a country where the money supply is growing by 20-30% every year.

![Egypt M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-egypt-m2.png)

Effectively what we are doing here is shorting the Egyptian pound for a term of seven years to finance the other assets (mainly a portion of our U.S. stocks and bitcoin) that we would otherwise have had to sell in order to have bought the villa and meet the housing needs of ourselves and our family here. And those stocks and bitcoin are considerably up in price since we would have otherwise sold them, indicating that it was a good decision *not* to sell those high quality assets, and instead to finance the purchase.

And that’s the same type of logic that many corporations do when carrying voluntary debt. They could pay it off with cash flows, but why do that when the hurdle rate is under 3.5%? If interest rates remain elevated, then they might put more of their cash flows toward debt reduction as some of that debt matures, rather than refinance all of it at higher rates, but that would be a gradual process.

**Valuation**

PepsiCo has been of interest to me ever since it dipped in 2023 back down to its 20-year average price/earnings ratio:

![PEP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-pep-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

At 20x earnings, a 3%+ dividend yield, and 7%+ earnings growth, the dividend-adjusted PEG ratio of the company is about 2x, which is reasonable for a mature blue-chip company with a wide economic moat.

**Risk Analysis**

The primary risk of PepisCo, as previously discussed, is top line growth. They face a host of smaller risk and challenges, but ultimately at this valuation an investment in PepsiCo comes down to answering one key question: will their product mix be in demand five and ten years from now, or no?

If consumer preferences change too fast, then PepsiCo’s revenue can suffer. But if they gradually change over time, as they historically have, then PepsiCo can adjust its offering with acquisitions, sales, and new product launches.

For example, with the rise in demand for flavored sparkling water, PepsiCo launched the Bubbly line of flavored sparkling water to compete with La Croix. Between the two, I personally like Bubbly better. But that’s just one of countless examples for how the portfolio of products evolves over time.

My base case is that PepsiCo will successfully adapt its product portfolio to remain relevant with global consumers. If the company only had one big market, like North America, then I would be more concerned about obsolescence. However, because they have a global customer base, there is a lot of resistant against fast change, which gives the company ample time to adapt its product mix to each of its regions of operation as preferences change.

## Final Thoughts: Gold’s Healthy Consolidation

Gold and bitcoin have been going through inverse patterns lately.

Bitcoin reached new highs earlier this year in March 2024, and then consolidated sideways until November 2024, which healthily released a lot of the ETF-launch enthusiasm.

Gold on the other hand broke out in March 2024, and steadily powered upward until October 2024, and lately it has been consolidating. Much like the bitcoin consolidation, I think it’s healthy for gold to consolidate a bit here and let people stop talking about it for a moment.

![Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-12-8-gold.png)

And as gold has been consolidating, bitcoin broke out to the upside in November, jumping from about $70k to over $100k in a span of a month. It’s nice owning some of both assets, in my view.

I don’t have a firm view on what gold will do in the next 3-6 months, but I continue to have a long-term constructive view on it relative to bonds and cash.
