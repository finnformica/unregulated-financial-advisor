## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

The public [April 2023 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/april-2023-newsletter/) focused on liquidity, so I suggest reading that if you have not. I’ll continue to monitor it in my reports every two weeks while it remains a key driver of of markets.

Total domestic liquidity is down a bit over the past two weeks partly due to tax season. The Treasury General Account rose from $109 billion to $291 billion over a two-week period, which sucked liquidity out of the market:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Fed continues to sell securities (quantitative tightening) while providing loans to troubled banks (which is pro-liquidity). As a result, their balance sheet was down over the past week, but still above the March lows:

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-fed-balance-sheet.png)

During 2008 and 2020, the Fed’s initiation of loans (red line below) ended up marking a change in the Fed’s permanent balance sheet (blue line below). In other words, during emergencies the Fed made loans (a provision of temporary liquidity), but had to turn to outright buying securities and providing permanent liquidity as well. Those permanent purchases of securities is what allowed the Fed to eventually wind down their loans.

![Fed Loans and Securities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-fed-loans-and-securities.png)

My base case is that the Fed will be unable to permanently wind down their current loans unless they cease quantitative tightening and perhaps turn to a bit of quantitative easing as well. There are some paths that could prove me wrong on that, such as the Treasury issuing a ton of T-bills and sucking money out of reverse repos, and if those events start to happen I’ll be reporting on them and shifting my base case as appropriate. Outside of those specific pathways, they’re running into a pretty tight liquidity situation.

Michael Howell of [CrossBorder Capital](https://twitter.com/crossbordercap), who specializes in liquidity analysis, posted a great chart the other day of global liquidity. It has been turning up from its lows, but remains at a low level:

![Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-global-liquidity.png)

**The Fall of First Republic**

First Republic (FRC) has experienced a second collapse.

As a recap, First Republic was one of the heavily impaired banks during the initial mid-March bank crisis. Its base of generally wealthy clientele has a high percentage of uninsured deposits, and so clients were quick to pull deposits out at the first sign of bank contagion risks. However, First Republic was stabilized due to loans and deposits from other banks; basically the people at the top of the financial system deemed this bank salvageable and took big actions to keep it afloat.

But, First Republic’s recent quarterly report showed bigger-than-expected deposit outflows, which triggered another round of stock selling and deposit flight. With banks, any sign of weakness is likely to lead to more weakness, and that’s what happened here. The stock price was over $200 at one point, collapsed to the $12-$16 range during March and April where it temporarily stabilized, and as of the past week has quickly fallen to $3.51, which is a further 70-80% decline from that prior $12-$16 stabilization level. As of this weekend, large banks have been bidding on acquiring what remains of First Republic, since it’s no longer workable as a stand-alone entity.

With current interest rates where they are, I am not optimistic on small and medium banks in general. Exceptions will apply for certain individual banks, which requires extensive bank-by-bank analysis. The largest banks appear to be solid, but even they will likely curb share buybacks and dividend growth as they seek to retain capital to have the strongest possible position.

Much of this is self-imposed by the Federal Reserve’s extreme whipsaw in monetary policy. In 2020/2021 they caused the biggest growth rate in broad money supply since the 1940s, and dropped rates to zero, while saying that the sharp rise in monetary aggregates will not be important for price inflation, that any price inflation would be temporary, and that they are not even thinking about thinking about raising rates. In 2022/2023, they freaked out about price inflation as it materialized and proved persistent, raised rates at the sharpest pace in terms of basis points since the 1970s (and the sharpest pace in percentage terms ever) and reduced the broad money supply by the fastest pace since the 1930s.

Some banks’ business models and approaches to risk management helped them to navigate this better than others, but in aggregate it has been an extreme environment to operate a bank in, especially for banks outside of the top ten by size.

**Fed Rate Hike Expectations**

The interest rate market is currently pricing in an >80% chance that the Fed will hike interest rates by 0.25% next week, which will bring the effective federal funds rate to 5.00-5.25%. From there, the market is pricing in a pause, followed by a greater than 50% chance of mild cuts by late 2023.

The Treasury market is pointing in a similar direction. The 2-year Treasury rate rolled beneath the 3-month Treasury rate late last year, which tends to happen close to the top of a rate-hiking cycle.

![Treasury Rates YCharts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-rates-ychart.png)

My view at this time does not particularly differ from the consensus expectation. Disinflation has been occurring, the economy has been weakening, and banks have been failing around the margins, and so the focus will likely shift from pure inflation-fighting to a balanced approach between financial stability, economic growth, and inflation.

I shared this chart through Q4 2022 in my April newsletter, but the data received an update for Q1 2023 right after publication. It shows annualized interest expense in dollar terms with the blue line (now up to $929 billion) and interest expense as a percentage of GDP with the red line (now up to 3.5%):

![Interest Expense](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-interest-expense-chart.png)

This is fine for now but, as discussed in the April newsletter, when the debt ceiling is eventually resolved and the Treasury needs to refill its account and continue issuing new Treasuries, it’ll represent a liquidity problem for investors and policymakers to navigate through. There’s a ton of new Treasury supply that will be hitting the market over the next couple years.

Most likely, a recurring topic in the years ahead for developed market central banks including the Fed will be the idea of providing liquidity to the government while simultaneously trying to restrain liquidity for the private sector, as they need to keep sovereign bond markets functional but want to limit total loan growth to keep inflation in check. If so, this will be different than the 2010s decade approach of providing widespread liquidity, and would instead be a policy of more targeted and narrow liquidity.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy has been decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, and is beginning to reach stall speed or becoming mildly recessionary in many metrics. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as several of them are now), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

My base case expectation is for a stagflationary recession, with unemployment levels that won’t spike as high as the average recession. It’s hard to have a full-blown deep deflationary recession with $1.5 trillion annual federal deficits already built-in before the recession even starts, and which would further widen during the recession. However, I’ll update that view as needed as new data come in. Brief deflationary shocks are certainly possible.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has recently been consolidating in the $29,000+ range.

I remain uncertain about its price performance over the next six months amid the liquidity concerns that I outlined within my April public newsletter. Beyond that, however, I am bullish with a two or three year view specifically, within the context of a long-term bullish perspective. I expect new highs by the end of 2025, as a base case.

![Bitcoin Charts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-bitcoin-charts.png)

When we look at past cycles in terms of holder behavior and general liquidity conditions, it seems to be firmly past the bottom in this cycle as well. If that’s the case, the question is whether it’ll gradually grind higher like it did starting in early 2016, or if it’ll consolidate with a number of false starts like it did in 2019.

Between those outcomes, I don’t know. Future liquidity shocks could make it look like the 2019 cycle or the 2016 cycle depending on policymaker decisions that are nearly impossible to predict.

What I can focus on, however, is the development of the ecosystem.

At [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/portfolio), I continue to talk with startup founders and advise the fund as we deploy capital within the ecosystem. On Thursday, I spoke to a group of startup founders at [Wolf NYC](https://wolfnyc.com/), a Lightning accelerator located near the top of One Vanderbilt in Manhattan, and was happy to see in detail the types of projects they are working on. In this upcoming week, I’ll be speaking at the MicroStrategy (MSTR) [Bitcoin and Lightning for Corporations](https://www.microstrategy.com/en/world-2023/bitcoin-for-corporations) event in Orlando.

In my experience, most macro investors and economists who are dismissive of Bitcoin tend to have little or no familiarity with the developers in the ecosystem, are unable to steelman the arguments against their position, and in general tend to be shocked by its repeated ability to withstand 75%+ drawdowns and keep grinding higher. For many of them, it’s just a price line on a chart, and they don’t understand why it has value.

While it has risks, the reason Bitcoin has value is that it’s a decentralized network with its own bearer asset, meaning that it’s a scarce and transmittable money that is backed by energy and user-run nodes. It’s the world’s most decentralized and immutable database, and allows people to bring their money with them globally and send it around the world at the speed of light in a non-custodial way. And compared to copycat networks, Bitcoin is the most decentralized and most liquid cryptocurrency. When it comes to competing monies, liquidity is critical and tends to beget more liquidity.

Prior asset class bubbles in history were usually one-and-done, whereas Bitcoin keeps experiencing mini-bubbles, subsequent crashes, and bouncing back from them to higher highs and higher lows each time. It has survived four different drawdowns of more than 75% each over 14 years and counting, which is something that normal bubbles don’t do.

Raphael Zagury recently published a [white paper](https://www.nakamotoportfolio.com/static/docs/Schrodingers_Coin_Model.pdf), [DIY calculator](https://www.nakamotoportfolio.com/apps/shrodinger), and [open source details](https://github.com/pxsocs/Schrodinger_Model) on a topic called Schrodinger’s Coin Model, which explores different scenarios for Bitcoin’s path of monetization over time. It’s part of [Swan.com](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/)‘s new research arm.

## Energy Sector Update

The prospects for energy stocks remain unclear for the next six months as the United States works through its current liquidity problem and potential 2H 2023 recession.

In the meantime, oil prices have been finding a consolidation range above $70/barrel. We could see further sharp illiquid spikes below that level like we did in March, especially during a recession, but overall with the Strategic Petroleum Reserve already drawn down to multi-decade lows and OPEC+ willing to cut production to defend this price level, it’s a decent risk/reward to assume that prices will pop back up over $70 within a reasonable timeframe if they temporarily go under that level like they did in March.

![WTI Crude](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-wti-crude.png)

Overall, most energy stocks never became particularly expensive even when oil was $120 in 2022, and thus have held up well even as oil has corrected back to sub-$80. Even at these middling oil price levels, many large-cap oil producers make good money and trade at low valuations, which makes them able to pay high dividend yields, buy back a lot of shares, and strengthen their balance sheets.

There hasn’t been much of a capex spree or M&A spree in this oil cycle yet, which continues to suggest it’s still in its early innings. There isn’t a lot of new supply coming online on the observable capex horizon, even as the spread between supply and demand remains rather tight. Higher interest rates also increase the development cost for new production, and so while they can rein in demand to varying degrees, they also rein in new supply.

A good example for an oil producer whose stock has held up well is Canadian Natural Resources (CNQ). The stock price was rangebound for nearly two decades but has enjoyed a sizable breakout and consolidation, which is entirely supported by its fundamentals:

![CNQ FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-cnq-fastgraph.png)

CNQ now has a trailing twelve month income that is higher than its net debt for the first time in its modern operating history, both due to an income surge and due to using that income surge to reduce existing debt:

![CNQ Net Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-cnq-net-debt.png)

They have sharply increased their regular dividends and paid out a one-time special dividend amid their high cashflow:

![CNQ Shareholder Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-cnq-shareholder-returns.png)

*Chart Source: [CNQ Corporation Presentation](https://www.cnrl.com/investors/events-presentations/)*

Their new capital allocation strategy emphasizes a strong balance sheet and aggressive shareholder returns. Whenever net debt is $10-$15 billion, management plans to split incoming cash between paying dividends, buying back shares, and reducing net debt. Whenever net debt is under $10 billion, management plans to allocate most incoming cash towards dividends and buying back shares.

**Oil and Gas Supermajors: Cash Rich**

Many oil companies made harmful acquisitions at the top of the prior energy cycle. They bought when prices were high, and when those prices collapsed, they had to write down massive losses on those acquisitions. As a result, many of them are being picky with acquisitions and new drilling at this time, and instead are focusing on maintaining steady-state production and returning a lot of capital to shareholders.

Some of the supermajors like Exxon Mobile (XOM) have lower borrowing costs than CNQ, and have been using almost a bank-like strategy. The yield on Exxon’s $37 billion worth of long-term debt is around 3%, which is lower than what T-bills and similar cash-equivalents earn these days. Exxon has over $29 billion in cash-equivalents, and due to higher yields they might actually be earning more total income on that cash than they are paying in total interest on their debt, or roughly-so:

![XOM Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-xom-balance-sheet.png)

Javier Blas reported on Exxon’s balance sheet in an [article for Bloomberg](https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2023-04-28/exxon-xom-has-way-too-much-cash-laying-around) last week:

> Even if Exxon cannot sustain the pace, the obvious question is what to do with all the money it has already accumulated? Kathy Mikells, the company’s chief financial officer, is enjoying having such a problem. When I asked her if it wasn’t time to break the piggybank, she quipped the company was earning good money from its cash pile. Exxon pays about 3% on average on its debt, while short-term interest rates in America for savings are currently above 4%.

**Smaller Oil and Gas Producers: Acquisition Targets**

Compared to large producers, smaller energy companies provide more upside potential, but have a lot more insolvency and/or dilution risk during bear markets. I’m willing to hold large and diversified energy companies through bear markets for tax-efficient long-term compounding, but to the extent that I trade around smaller companies, I want to protect my downside a bit more and focus on up-cycles.

I’ve been watching Southwestern Energy Company (SWN) closely. After a drop from a 2022 high of $9.15 down to $4.76 and a current rebound to the $5-$6 range, I’m getting interested. I’m not buying yet, but it’s firmly on my watch-list for potential purchase in the weeks or months ahead:

![SWN FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-swn-fastgraph.png)

Companies like Southwestern are likely acquisition targets for cash-rich oil and gas supermajors in the years ahead. Many of these small and medium-sized producers have very low valuations relative to their proved reserves. A larger company with lower borrowing costs can come in, buy the company, refinance the debt, and benefit from economies of scale. In many cases, it’s less risky than developing new reserves themselves, and it’s immediately accreditive to earnings. In Southwestern’s case, their enterprise value is about $10 billion.

**Pipelines: Still Great**

I remain bullish on the highest-quality energy transport businesses, like Enterprise Products Partners (EPD):

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-epd-fastgraph.png)

Many of the c-corp pipelines currently offer mediocre valuations. The best pipelines currently are not c-corps, and instead have a bit of friction in them: they still have an MLP structure and issue a K-1 form during tax season. This keeps them out of a lot of ETFs and other funds, and gives them a lower average valuation.

For investors that buy into them with that lower valuation with a plan to hold for the long run, they get higher distribution yields, and any buybacks that occur are more impactful. The tax treatment, despite being more complex, is generally a bit better too (at least for American investors).

**Energy Sector Summary**

Overall, this sector seems to remain underappreciated across the board.

-Large oil and gas producers with low decline rates likewise are inexpensive cash-cows in this environment, even with $70 oil in many cases. Their balance sheets are generally great, and they are roughly maintaining production levels while sending a lot of capital back to shareholders. Some have expanded production more than others, but as an industry they’ve been using a conservative strategy overall.

-Small producers are generally very cheap relative to reserves at this time and represent attractive acquisition targets, but have more volatility and insolvency risk during periods of lower oil and gas prices.

-The pipeline companies that issue K-1 forms during tax season, like EPD, remain historically cheap, high-yielding, and with above-average distribution coverage ratios. Rather than constantly issuing equity like they used to, some of them are even buying back units.

Apart from the oil price, a key risk for these companies continues to be windfall taxes and other external interventions. Oil company stocks have generally been rangebound for a decade or two, meaning they massively underperformed other stocks. Now that they are experiencing their current windfall, many of them are under pressure by politicians. Rather than rush into acquisitions or rapid drilling like they did in the prior cycle, oil and gas producers are generally being a lot more conservative, which will likely continue to be the case until oil prices become elevated for longer periods of time.

## Final Thoughts: Managing Three Timeframes

If I were to summarize my investment timeframes, it’s that I try to be on the right side of decade-long structural trends in terms of shortages and valuations, but temper that outlook with typical 3-year liquidity/business cycles to manage the risk and intermediate-term expectations.

In the background, I also acknowledge the sovereign debt cycle, which changes things every few generations and makes things act in unprecedented ways amid that otherwise-normal cyclical approach.

**Timeframe One: The 3-Year Cycle**

The (roughly) 3-year cycle can be visualized with the purchasing manager’s index. It tends to spend a year or two going up (relative risk-on conditions) and spend a year or two going down (relative risk-off conditions):

![PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-pmi.png)

Similarly, the global liquidity cycle (orange line below) illustrates that cycle well, and tends to lead PMIs slightly:

![Global Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-global-liquidity.png)

**Timeframe Two: Decade-Long Trends**

Decade-long structural trends are mainly based around the dominance of inflation or disinflation, which is mainly based around the question of whether commodities are abundant and cheap or scarce and expensive. These cycles of course vary in duration and magnitude, but tend to be quite long overall due to the timelines of capital expenditures that fuel them.

Cheap periods of commodities result in under-investment in new commodity supply development, until eventually they became scarce and expensive, which results in a period of high prices and new investment. Eventually that new investment brings enough new commodity supply online that they make them cheap and oversupplied again.

These trends can be summarized in part by the value vs growth equity chart:

![Value vs Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-value-growth.png)

They can also basically be represented as the S&P 500 vs commodities index chart:

![S&P 500 to Commodities Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-spx-crb.png)

During inflationary decades, value stocks, commodities, and emerging markets tend to outperform until they get taken to excess. During disinflationary decades, commodities are abundant and interest rates decrease, which benefits equities in general and growth stocks in particular, until they get taken to excess. Of course, each cycle will have unique characteristics and various surprises.

**Timeframe Three: The Sovereign Debt Cycle**

The sovereign debt cycle is ultimately about the geopolitics around money itself, and is a source of major alpha for investors that can get it right. It can’t really serve as a basis for an entire investment approach due to how long-term and political it is, but it’s something that investors should be aware of as part of their background context. Being familiar with these issues gives protection against some extraordinary events.

The major theme is that private debt problems, cycle after cycle, end up getting pushed up to the sovereign level. From there, sovereigns basically have to default, devalue or restructure their debt. When that debt is denominated in their own currency, they usually choose the devaluation route rather than nominal default. In other words, when sovereign debt is very high, savers and bondholders usually don’t get paid back their full purchasing power, one way or another.

What makes it particularly hard is that several ways for investors to “get it right” during a sovereign debt problem end up being blocked by governments via capital controls. For example, from the mid-1930s through the mid-1970s, gold was banned for American investors (a benign yellow metal was made illegal to own because it represented a better ledger than what the U.S. federal government had to offer with the dollar system at the time), but real estate remained open and was a major source of protection against the currency debasement that occurred.

I have been representing the sovereign debt cycle with this chart, among others:

![Century Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-4-30-century-chart.png)

**Current Cycle Summary:**

The roughly 3-year cycle is down deeply, as central banks try to rein in price inflation. The liquidity portion has been turning up in recent months, but faces some turbulence ahead. The economic portion hasn’t really shown much sign of an upturn yet, but will likely find a bottom this year.

The decade trend remains constructive for value equities and commodities in general, ever since it began in the depths of 2020. Software companies have been heavily invested in over the past decade while commodities, infrastructure, and other hard assets have generally been underinvested in. Shortages and valuation differentials provide an interesting reversal potential which is probably nowhere near done yet, but it’ll have ebbs and flows along the way.

The sovereign debt cycle suggests that public debts are currently unsustainable and will continue to devalue relative to scarce assets. This occurred heavily during 2020-2022, is taking a break during 2023, and is likely to be a recurring issue in the years ahead.

I’ve been recommending a three-pillar portfolio consisting of 1) cash-equivalents, 2) profitable equities, and 3) commodity exposure and hard monies. The first pillar gives us protection against cyclical contractions and periods of disinflation, the second pillar gives us exposure to long-term compounders that create a lot of wealth, and the third pillar gives us protection from currency debasement, along with exposure to the upside that is likely to come from the recent period of commodity underinvestment.
