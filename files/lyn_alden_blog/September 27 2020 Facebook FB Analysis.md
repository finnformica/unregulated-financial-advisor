## Macro Update

Across most asset classes this year, there is basically one view that governs them all: inflation vs deflation.

And that view turned down at the the beginning of September. This chart, for example, shows that 10-year inflation breakevens in blue on the left axis, and the broad U.S. stock market in red on the right axis, are essentially the same:

![Breakevens vs Equities](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-breakevens-stocks.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Normally they are not that tightly correlated, but this is a very “macro heavy” year which ties them together more tightly than usual, and that big downward crunch in March also makes things look even more correlated by aligning the axes of the graph around that point.

On one hand, we have a generationally large amount of debt in the system, and the system was hit by the external shock of the pandemic. On the other hand, we have the counterforce of extreme fiscal stimulus pushing back against that.

As such, the relative flow of that tug-of-war impacts the inflation/deflation trade as well as most risk assets broadly. Whether a given company performs strongly or weakly in this year or next, is more dependent on fiscal policy and other macro forces than in a normal year, because those fiscal policy decisions affect the solvency of their customers in a broad sense.

Other assets like gold, silver, and Bitcoin have followed the same basic pattern: crashing in March, surging into August, and then beginning to roll over, roughly in line with the inflation trend shift or in some cases front-running it a bit. In other words, as the Treasury market shifted its expectations from a rebound in inflation to a cooling off of inflation, risk assets and inflation hedges across the board have pulled back.

Back in March, virtually all asset classes other than cash and T-bills were crashing (even briefly T-bonds were falling at the worst part of the liquidity event), and a deflationary view was forming in the market. Congress got serious about passing a multi-trillion-dollar fiscal emergency bill, inflation expectations bottomed on March 19, the stock market bottomed on March 23, and the CARES Act was passed on March 27.

This was followed by several months of sharp upward moves in the prices of most asset classes, against the backdrop of rapid broad money supply increases, deeper and deeper negative real yields, fast central bank balance sheet growth, and a weakening dollar. In other words, it was a direct fiscal stimulus into the economy, monetized in large part by the Federal Reserve.

Beginning in my late May report, I began covering the topic of U.S. fiscal stimulus running out at the end of July. My view back then was that a second round of fiscal stimulus would likely be passed by now, but that if not, disinflation/deflation would be back in control.

In some of the mid-June reports due to overbought conditions and unclear fiscal policy, I shifted a bit of equity exposure back to Treasury exposure in the newsletter portfolio, as a slight risk-off move. Then, with the potential next round of fiscal spending getting increasingly stuck in gridlock, the focus in the reports began shifting to the fiscal cliff.

In recent weeks we’ve been doing some portfolio pruning, such as taking profits on half of the Freeport-McMoRan position in the No Limits portfolio, selling some of the high-flyer growth stocks in the newsletter portfolio, and increasing the defense segment of the Fortress Income portfolio.

Instead of being repetitive, I’ll reference my August 30th deep dive report to describe what happens as that fiscal stimulus shuts off in an environment of high debt and high unemployment:

> **Fiscal Cliff: Still Falling**
>
> Extra fiscal spending pretty much ended at the end of July, and that’s a topic we’ve been covering in these reports for months.
>
> From April through July, people received stimulus checks and extra unemployment benefits, and small businesses received loans that turn into grants, nearly $3 trillion in free grant money overall, but most of that stopped by the end of July. Some of it happened with a lag; unemployment benefits attributed to previous weeks still went out to people into mid-August.
>
> Now, at the end of August, the economy has been operating for a couple weeks in “non-fiscal” mode. Unemployed people are not receiving additional federal unemployment benefits, and businesses are pretty much on their own.
>
> Small business revenue peaked in the beginning of July and has since been on a downtrend. July consumer confidence reached a new cycle low after enjoying a brief bounce.
>
> If this situation continues with high unemployment and low government stimulus, the natural results of a major recession will play out, with many people and businesses unable to pay rent, which trickles up to landlords and banks, and results in a broad solvency crisis and many defaults.
>
> So far, national personal income was up in 2020 (rather than down as in a normal recession), due to increased government transfer payments that more than offset the loss of normal income. And that led to retail sales also being up in 2020 (largely lead by e-commerce), because consumers have that government cash to spend, even as employment conditions, imports, exports, industrial production, and capital spending are all down as per what we’d expect from a severe recession. That divergence between incomes going up and economic activity going down is not inherently sustainable, unless government spending artificially continues to sustain it.
>
> Both political parties want another round of stimulus. The Republicans and President want about $1 trillion in stimulus, mainly targeting businesses and consumer stimulus checks again. The Democrats want $3 trillion in stimulus, targeting state budgets and larger consumer stimulus checks this time. Both sides have signaled compromise; Democrats are willing to go down to $2 trillion, and Trump is willing to go up to $1.3 trillion. Political events related to the upcoming election and unrelated to stimulus, largely involving the Post Office, have further slowed down these negotiations.
>
> The situation remains in gridlock, and so the economy has gone off the “fiscal cliff”.
>
> Analysts don’t expect S&P 500 earnings and revenue to surpass 2019 highs until 2022, even though the stock market has already reached new record highs in 2020, due to increases in valuation.
>
> As long as that fiscal gridlock remains, a disinflationary pressure and broad solvency risks grow. The Fed’s balance sheet stopped increasing nearly three months ago. Broad money supply stopped increasing about a month ago. The stock market and consumer finances are no longer held up by “money printing” at this particular time.
>
> Equities and other risk assets, which have kept going up, are vulnerable for correction in this environment.
>
> -August 30 deep dive report

In essence, whenever the backdrop involves massive fiscal spending, rising inflation expectations, negative real interest rates that are becoming more deeply negative, and rapid increases in broad money supply; the logical move is to be in scarce assets. This includes things like precious metals, commodities, good-quality stocks, and Bitcoin, depending on your preferences.

However, when the backdrop turns into gridlock and lack of fiscal stimulus, falling inflation expectations, rising real yields, and slower broad money supply growth, a more defensive position that includes cash and T-bills makes sense, potentially with hedges in place against drops in risk assets depending on what type of portfolio you run.

We’re in a defense/disinflation environment until signs of stimulus emerge. On one hand they are getting a bit closer, with the Democrat-controlled House of Representative currently drafting a $2.4 trillion spending bill that is closer in size and scope to what the Republicans have said they would support. On the other side, the Republicans have moved up their target to $1.5 trillion, so the numbers are starting to converge towards the middle of maybe $2 trillion.

The markets being in correction mode since their top on September 2nd certainly puts some added pressure on politicians, and particularly on Trump’s side, due to his frequent emphasis on stock market performance as a gauge of his success. There was less pressure for policymakers to do much as long as the stock market was going up and civil unrest was at bay, but now pressure is ratchetting up. However, the upcoming election and Supreme Court vacancy fight are potential detractors from getting a bipartisan stimulus deal done.

I’ll leave the probability assessment of stimulus happening prior to the election to political specialists, and instead just watch the debate play out. Over the long run, more rounds of fiscal stimulus are nearly inevitable (as policymakers become pressured by struggling markets or heightened civil unrest), but between now and February, there’s a significant lack of policy clarity.

**The U.S. Business Cycle Heatmap**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Notice how much retail sales still stand out from everything else at the moment:

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation is not “good” or “bad” in quite the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher consumer price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

Overall, Q2 was the economic bottom, but the speed with which the economy gets to new highs remains an open question that I’ll continue to track.

So far, it is going along with the “backward square root sign recovery” that I’ve written about since April, meaning a quick mini-V rebound after the shutdown ended, followed by a longer grind back up from there, at least in terms of jobs and GDP.

## Facebook (FB) Deep-Dive Analysis

Facebook, for many good reasons, is among the least-loved of the mega-cap internet stocks, and among large and medium-sized software stocks in general.

For software stocks where there is basically no controversy, their prices have gone parabolic relative to fundamentals. This is especially true for software companies that shifted from one-time sales to recurring subscription-based revenue over the past several years, as they made their software more cloud-oriented. The corporate tax cuts that occurred at the start of 2018 super-charged this as well.

Adobe (ADBE), for example, has done great fundamentally, but its stock price took off like a rocket ship above those fundamentals, which is why I sold it on September 21. It’s hard to justify a 50 P/E ratio for a stock that is expected to have a growth rate in the teens going forward. They already got a big growth spurt from tax cuts and conversion to recurring revenue, so now, it’s less enticing:

![Adobe FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-adbe-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

Similarly, Cadence Design Systems (CDNS), which supplies design software for engineers, went parabolic. Investors acted as though its growth spurt in 2018 would be its new permanent growth rate, but this was in large part driven by their shift to a recurring revenue subscription model. As a result, the stock has a 40+ adjusted P/E ratio at a time when analysts expect forward earnings growth to be in the low double digits or even the mid/high single digits:

![CDNS FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-cdns-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

With interest rates so low, and economic growth so sluggish, investors piled into those names at any price, because other than valuation bubbles, there weren’t a lot of tail risks to worry about.

The key growth stocks that are still reasonably priced relative to their growth rates tend to be ones with big tail risk possibilities. These include the Chinese internet names like Tencent (TCEHY), Alibaba (BABA), and JD (JD), as well as the American firm Facebook (FB).

For these stocks, their valuations relative to growth are much lower than some of the high-flying stocks, because although their growth and cashflow production capabilities are extraordinarily strong, investors are concerned about potential negative outcomes, like regulators cracking down on Facebook’s data collection practices, or the trade war disrupting the Chinese economy including their top internet stocks.

However, targeted diversification can reduce these risks and likely unlock most of the underlying rewards. In other words, I wouldn’t place a huge bet on any individual companies like this, but I do think a basket containing Facebook and some of these Chinese internet stocks makes sense as part of a growth stock position at the current time. The probability that Facebook gets regulated out of growth, and that Chinese internet stocks get disrupted too, is a lot smaller than just one of those events happening.

**Facebook Company Overview**

Facebook operates its namesake social network, but then also owns Instagram and WhatsApp, and a host of other smaller intellectual property besides those big three such as Oculus Virtual Reality and a stake in India’s Jio Platforms.

Although Facebook stock has done well in recent years, it hasn’t surged to the same degree as most of the other FAANG stocks. Here’s a 3-year price chart:

![Facebook Price Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-price-chart.png)

The market has been in a downtrend recently, including Facebook stock, so depending on their time horizon or how much they watch the market on a daily basis, it would be ideal for investors to wait until it has a positive MACD crossover (meaning the blue line goes over the orange line on the bottom panel above) before buying in size.

I added Facebook to the newsletter portfolio last week at about $245/share as part of the portfolio pruning process, but that portfolio is very diversified and uses dollar-cost averaging (adding new funds each month), so if Facebook stock dips down further before finding a bottom, that’s fine and new contributions will flow into it. So far it is up from that initial purchase, but depending on overall market action in Q4 of this year, that might not last.

Facebook has one of the strongest balance sheets in the world, with $58 billion in cash and no long-term debt. Meanwhile, they generate in the ballpark of $20 billion per year in free cash flow.

![Facebook Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-balance-sheet.png)

Facebook’s returns on invested capital are consistently high, which is one of the key metrics for successful compounding:

![Facebook ROIC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-roic.png)

Facebook’s acquisition strategy has been effective. Their Instagram acquisition in 2012 was basically a steal for $1 billion, and their WhatsApp acquisition for $19 billion looks smart as well.

The namesake Facebook platform changed over time from primarily college students (including myself back in 2006, shortly after Facebook’s founding) to now being heavily used across the age spectrum, with a median age of around 40. Meanwhile, Instagram’s age demographic skews younger, which is good for growth but less good for monetization.

WhatsApp is heavily used for text messaging and VOIP by all age demographics around the world. Some countries ban it, while for many countries, it is the primary messaging platform.

The following chart shows how global Facebook’s reach is. They have over 2.7 billion monthly active users, of which over 1.7 billion are daily active users. And notably, only 11% of those users are in the United States and Canada:

![Facebook DAU](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-dau.png)

*Chart Source: Facebook 2Q 2020 Results Presentation*

Of course, about half of their revenue comes from the United States and Canada, because particularly in the United States, it is a wealthy and consumer-oriented culture that makes for very lucrative advertising:

![Facebook Revenue Breakdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-revenue.png)

*Chart Source: Facebook 2Q 2020 Results Presentation*

If Facebook were primarily an American phenomenon, I wouldn’t want to buy it at this point. It’s saturated and faces increasing regulatory pressure.

It’s specifically that global reach that interests me. As per-capita wealth grows across the world, if the combination of Facebook and Instagram and WhatsApp can maintain a strong network effect with billions of users, the growth runway remains quite large even at this point.

Social networks have extraordinarily wide economic moats, because they are exponentially self-reinforcing. Google tried for years to break into the social media business with Google+ and failed to do so despite emphasizing it right on its homepage, which is the most popular website by traffic in the world.

The company also introduced “Facebook and Instagram Shops” this year, which allow small businesses on Facebook and Instagram to set up a customized shop (kind of like Etsy), and sell items from it, with Facebook taking a small cut. I’ve been watching this development because I’m bullish on eBay and Etsy, and Facebook is a new competitor in this arena. The growing e-commerce market is big enough for all of them but it’s a question of market share.

**Valuation Analysis**

Facebook’s revenue growth continues to be strong, and tends to hit cyclical peaks in Q4 around the holiday season. As the business has matured, its price-to-sales ratio has decreased:

![Facebook Revenue and PS Ratio](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-revenue-growth.png)

If we look back to when the company became public, their revenue growth was extraordinarily high, but that early period is no longer relevant for analysis, and we must focus on the past few years.

* Revenue growth 2016-2017: 47%
* Revenue growth 2017-2018: 37%
* Revenue growth 2018-2019: 27%
* Consensus estimated revenue growth 2019-2020: 13%
* Consensus estimated revenue growth 2020-2021: 24%

The pandemic is pressuring small businesses, so Facebook’s revenue growth (which comes primarily from small business advertising) is taking a hit this year against their backdrop of growth, so looking at 2021 revenue will be absolutely critical. And the health of that 2021 revenue will in part depend on fiscal policy (stimulus for consumers and small businesses, or the lack thereof), which ties back into the theme of this being a very macro-heavy period in the historical sense.

If Facebook’s revenue growth lingers in the low teens for years, the market should re-value the company a bit lower. On the other hand, if they go back up to growth in the high teens or low twenties, the stock is likely undervalued. Watching 2021 results each quarter will be critical for investors with a view on Facebook, and if any of those quarters surprise notably to the upside or downside, Facebook stock could easily have 5-10% price gap swings in response to those quarterly reports.

Facebook’s adjusted earnings chart looks decent. With a P/E ratio in the low 30’s and a solid growth rate, the PEG ratio, both backward-looking and based on analyst forward estimates, is firmly below 2x. This gives a margin of safety for Facebook’s 2021 growth:

![FB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

The big question, of course, is what happens with regulations and fiscal policy drivers going forward. If analysts are correct about Facebook forward growth, this stock should do great. If analysts are way off and the revenues and earnings come in light, Facebook should correct or consolidate for a while.

Facebook’s earnings dip in 2019 on the chart above was due to $6 billion in legal fees and fines, rather than an actual fundamental decline in their business. Their revenue was up by 27% but their profit took a cluster of one-time hits.

A StockDelver model has a lot of variables to consider for Facebook. As a base case, I have a two-stage model that starts with 15% annual EPS growth for the first five years, 10% annual EPS growth for the next five years, and a reduction in the price/earnings ratio from 33 to 25 over that decade. This is more bearish than analyst projections, but not the most bearish scenario.

![FB StockDelver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-9-27-fb-stockdelver.png)

By those bearish-leaning set of metrics, Facebook is fairly-valued around the current price level for potential 10% annualized returns over that period.

Of course, the precise path for Facebook’s fundamentals and valuation will depend on many factors. The summary is that by moderately fast-growing standards, Facebook is reasonably appealing at the current price level from a long-term perspective, unless or until its growth trajectory gets disrupted by cultural backlash or more severe regulation.

As Facebook matures and growth inevitably slows, it would be prudent to return some capital to shareholders, either in the form of a small dividend or share buybacks. Microsoft and Apple have long-since gone down this route, while Amazon and Facebook have not yet. In that sense, a slower growth rate becomes offset by those capital returns, which makes sense when you have tons of cash, are generating more cash each quarter, and are already a huge company.

From its IPO until 2015, Facebook aggressively issued shares. Since then, its share count has been relatively flat. I would not be surprised, in years ahead, to see Facebook begin reducing its share count a bit, as its cash hoard grows very large and the growth rate declines. Share repurchases would start capping the cash balance and adding some upward bias towards earnings per-share.

**Company Risks**

In many ways, I view Facebook and some other social media companies as modern day “sin stocks”, similar in some regards to tobacco companies, in that they face increasing scrutiny from regulators and a degree of pushback from society.

*Regulation Risk*

Social media companies, especially in regards to data privacy, are under pressure from a wide-range of angles, from regulators to documentary makers.

For the highest-profile case, Facebook was charged with a record-breaking $5 billion fine last year by the Federal Trade Commission after an investigation into the Cambridge Analytical data scandal. The company vowed to change their data practices and Zuckerberg publicly apologized.

The European Union continues to pressure social media networks, especially because they are American companies, so EU regulators are more detached from the success of the companies.

*Enhanced Data Privacy*

Facebook is so profitable in large part because it can target advertising to individuals, based on harvesting their data. This makes people more interested in the ads, resulting in more money per ad.

For example, if a social media platform figures out what kinds of clothes and shoes I like including brands, or that I am into healthy eating, or that I like wine and which types, or that I’m an investor, or that I travel internationally and stay at specific types of hotels, its ads can be a lot more effective than if it just targets me based on age and gender.

So, specific regulations that enforce data privacy and reduce the ability of Facebook to target ads to users, would reduce the effectiveness of Facebook ads, and therefore reduce their returns on invested capital and their overall profit margins and growth rate.

And the result is nonlinear due to operational leverage: fixed expenses vs variable income. By rough calculations, if their ads were 25% less profitable but the company’s expenses were the same, it would be in the ballpark of a 40-50% profit reduction. If their ads were 50% less profitable, the company would have approximately zero profit.

*Cultural Backlash*

After the Cambridge Analytica data scandal, #DeleteFacebook became a popular movement. Like many movements, it didn’t really go anywhere. Facebook’s reputation was tarnished a bit, some advertisers pulled out, but their users and revenue keep going up around the world. Notably, the movement didn’t really include Instagram or WhatsApp.

I deleted my Facebook account back before it was “cool” to do so, years ago. After having used it in college, I stopped using after I graduated and just checked it yearly, and then eventually deleted it. I haven’t used Instagram either. Twitter is the only broad social media platform I personally use, since it is popular among investors.

*Anti-Trust Risk*

The past couple decades have been an era of loose anti-trust activity by historical standards. We have an unusually large degree of consolidation of platforms into a handful of mega-corporations.

Regulators may at some point conclude that maybe having all social platforms in the hands of a few mega-cap corporations isn’t in the country’s best interest anymore. Maybe Facebook would be forced to divest Instagram, or Alphabet would be forced to divest Youtube, or Amazon would have to split its retail business from its cloud business.

*Risk Mitigation*

I view three key variables to mitigate these risks.

The key way from the investor side, is to keep position sizes reasonable and maintain measurable outcomes. A collection of growth companies can work well if you spread out your bets by a reasonable degree. In that sense, the returns come from having a sound process rather than a concentration into a few big stock bets. Facebook is a stock that I currently like as part of a portfolio, but would not make a huge core position out of, and will monitor its fundamentals especially in 2021.

From the company side, Facebook has $58 billion in cash and no long-term debt, which gives them remarkable flexibility to change and adapt as needed to keep their business model relevant, and in line with shifting regulator and cultural standards.

Lastly, Facebook as I previously pointed out, has about 90% of their users outside of the United States. They operate in jurisdictions around the world. Regulations in their wealthier jurisdictions can harm them in the intermediate term, but the ultimate success of Facebook in the long run will likely come down to whether or not they can maintain a strong international userbase as many countries increase their per-capita wealth.

## Summary

I continue to be in a relative risk-off mode, as without further stimulus, disinflation is temporarily back in control and slow-growing insolvency issues remain a broad problem. For me that doesn’t include large portfolio moves since I don’t trade frequently, but it did include a variety of stock-specific trimming and some Treasury bill accumulation.

Catalysts that would spur me to go more risk-on again, meaning to shift some Treasury exposure into a bit more equity weighting, would include another round of fiscal stimulus, or a clear upward technical reversal in the major equity indices (for which I am currently using daily MACD as my key intermediate-term indicator, since it has been pretty clean at this time).

The degree of my risk-on move would depend on whether just the technical variable turns positive, or if both catalysts turn positive, and at what price level those catalysts occur.
