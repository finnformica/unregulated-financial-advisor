# Deep Dive: BP and Shell with Investor Activism

*February 16, 2025*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report covers liquidity conditions, weak U.S. retail sales, and reciprocal tariffs.

The digital asset section covers bitcoin price action and the bitcoin ETF investment by Abu Dhabi’s sovereign wealth fund.

The investment analysis section takes a look at BP and Shell, given that there is an interesting new bullish catalyst potentially in play.

## Macro View

**Domestic Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity continues to be rangebound:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-domestic-liquidity.png)

Spare liquidity in the Fed’s reverse repo facility is nearly drained, currently sitting at under $60 billion. However, the Treasury General Account still has over $800 billion in liquidity, and could spill into the banking system if the debt ceiling debate pushes deeper into the subsequent months.

When we break apart large and small bank cash levels as a % of assets, it gives us another interesting picture:

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-bank-cash-levels.png)

In September 2019, it was large banks (which have tighter capital and liquidity regulations) that ran into liquidity problems, causing the Fed to end quantitative tightening. In March 2023, it was small/regional banks that ran into different types of liquidity issues. Since 2023, small bank liquidity has improved, while liquidity has drained somewhat out of large banks, albeit still at workable levels.

I view the Fed as likely to end quantitative tightening by the end of the year, and potentially go back to balance sheet expansion or offer some type of SLR adjustment to make it easier for large banks to buy more Treasury securities and operate with lower cash levels. However, there are no imminent signs of liquidity problems at this time.

**Global Liquidity Indicators**

The dollar’s exchange rate relative to other currencies plays a big role in global liquidity, because there is so much dollar-denominated debt in the world, and a stronger dollar hardens those dollar debts relative to local currency cash flows for various corporations and governments.

When the dollar spiked to its recent high in January, it results in net foreign trimming of Treasuries, which is typical of local dollar peaks:

![Dollar vs Custody](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-dollar-custody.png)

If we see the dollar index continue to push higher, then we would likely see more market turbulence. However, with a potential walking back of tariff threats and some degree of economic U.S. weakness (both discussed below), it seems likely that there may be a dollar reprieve for a bit.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

In January, inflation came in slightly hotter than consensus expectations, but retail sales came in much lower than consensus expectations. Consensus economists had expected a minor 0.2% decline from the prior month, but the result was minus 0.9%. December was a rather booming month for retail sales, and it seems as though January was a bit of a hangover month, even relative to the normal seasonal adjustments that they do to account for that.

As a result, the Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow indicator fell to its lowest level in quite a while:

![GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-gdpnow.png)

While the worst part of bank credit tightening may be behind us, there is still considerable weakness in lower-income consumers. This degree of relative weakness took the edge off of Treasury yields and the dollar index.

A bright spot in the economy is that manufacturing activity has been turning up lately. There was a fake-out in early 2024 where it seemed to be recovering and then rolled over again. However, this latest resurgence has been stronger, and it’s already been an atypically long manufacturing contraction:

![US ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-us-pmi.png)

**Tariff Updates**

President Trump has recently shifted toward a [public policy of reciprocal tariffs](https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2025/02/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-announces-fair-and-reciprocal-plan-on-trade/). That is to say, if other countries place tariffs on American goods, the United States would put the same level of tariffs against them in return. If those countries raise or lower their tariffs, the United States would do the same.

Fox business [reports](https://www.foxbusiness.com/politics/trump-signs-reciprocal-tariff-plan-countries-tax-us-goods):

> “On trade, I have decided for purposes of fairness, that I will charge a reciprocal tariff — meaning whatever countries charge the United States of America, we will charge them no more, no less. In other words, they charge us a tax or tariff and we charge them the exact same tax or tariff. Very simple,” he said at the White House.

Critics of this plan ([such as this WSJ opinion editorial](https://www.wsj.com/opinion/reciprocal-tariffs-make-no-sense-duties-trade-economy-policy-china-d682c356)) argue that it’s complex, prone to frequent change, and basically gives tariff decisions to foreign countries, then. In addition, there is some debate around what counts as a tariff, such as VAT taxes.

However, I view it as a milder and more predictable approach than the tariff policy that started to take shape back in late January. It gives somewhat more clarity on likely tariff amounts for businesses and investors, reduces some of the tail risks, and if anything might put some downward pressure on global tariffs.

This is obviously subject to potential change and needs to be monitored, but overall I view this as a potential de-escalation on tariffs.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Bitcoin continues to be rangebound as its average cost basis gradually rises, alleviating some of its temporary semi-euphoric valuation levels:

![MVRV Z-Score](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-mvrv-z-score.png)

The HODL wave continues to appear to be mid-cycle as well, meaning that the bull market so far has been insufficient to convince a lot of long-term holders to sell their coins to new buyers relative to prior bull cycle peaks.

![HODL Waves](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-realized-cap-hodl-waves.png)

As a base case, I continue to view this cycle as having higher to go in terms of price, although I have no view about the next few months or so.

**Abu Dhabi Sovereign Investment**

One of Abu Dhabi’s sovereign wealth funds, the Mubadala Investment Company, [bought](https://dailyhodl.com/2025/02/15/abu-dhabi-sovereign-wealth-fund-reports-owning-455672040-worth-of-blackrocks-bitcoin-etf-in-new-sec-filing/) a stake in BlackRock’s bitcoin ETF (IBIT) worth over $400 million. Abu Dhabi is the UAE’s largest emirate in terms of both physical and economic size.

There is some irony in a sovereign wealth fund using an ETF for this purpose when they could just buy and self-custody bitcoin itself, but it seems that for convenience they’ve gone with the ETF for price exposure for now.

More broadly, there were a wave of bank filings lately showing various nine-figure holdings in bitcoin ETFs. Much of this is likely attributable to their wealth management divisions, where clients and/or their advisors add a slice of bitcoin exposure to their portfolios.

Overall, this cycle has been driven mainly by institutional and sovereign entities, while retail enthusiasm has remained muted. Much of the retail activity has been in the altcoin space instead, but has been only a weak altcoin rally this cycle, and not a ton of new money entering the space.

I continue to recommend an approach of buying and holding bitcoin, rather than trying to trade altcoins, at least for most people.

**Javier Milei’s Crypto Flop**

Two days ago, Argentina’s president Javier Milei shared a big meme coin project on Twitter/X and Instagram. Some people initially assumed he had been hacked, but as hours went by, it became apparent that he was not.

The project surged to a value of $4.5 billion in the aftermath of the announcement, and then insiders cashed out or “rug-pulled” the buyers of the coin, wiping away almost all of the value within hours. Milei then deleted his post, and posted a follow-on to clarify that he was not aware of the details of the project and wasn’t directly involved with its creation.

This has been one of the biggest rug-pulls to ever occur in the cryptocurrency space.

The Kobeissi Letter provided a good thread on the subject [here](https://x.com/KobeissiLetter/status/1890611963203334190).

## Investment Analysis: BP and Shell

Activist investment firm Elliott Management has acquired an approximately 5% stake in BP (BP), worth over $4 billion.

FT [reports](https://www.morningstar.co.uk/uk/news/AN_1739529108574519000/activist-elliott-management-third-biggest-investor-in-bp-%E2%80%94-reports.aspx):

> Activist Elliott Management has become BP’s third-largest shareholder after building a near-5 per cent stake worth almost £3.8bn, as it seeks to force the troubled UK oil major to cut spending on renewable energy and make big divestments, according to two people close to the situation.
>
> […]
>
> News of Elliott’s involvement broke on Saturday, and Auchincloss promised a “fundamental reset” of BP’s strategy as he released another set of disappointing results on Tuesday. BP is due to unveil its plans at a capital markets day on February 26.

Broadly speaking, an activist investment firm buys a chunk of a company that is not performing well, and then uses its stake and its arguments to convince the board and/or shareholders to significantly change something about the company’s priorities or operations. While they sometimes get a bad reputation, they play an important role in rejuvenating struggling businesses, as catalysts. Elliott Management is one of the most well-known in the industry.

Especially given that BP already has a new CEO and has been slowly moving in a new direction, I expect Elliott Management to likely be successful in their investment outcome. Elliott Management is a new strong tailwind.

Among the supermajor oil companies, BP is one of the smaller and cheaper ones. It’s trading at a price/earnings ratio of only about 10.5x, which is a 30%+ earnings multiple discount relative to the big American equivalents:

![BP FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-bp-fastgraph.png)

Oil and gas companies in general have been under various forms of pressure over the past couple decades to diversify their energy exposure for climate purposes. Sometimes this has taken the form of government pressure, while other times it has been in the form of activist shareholder pressure, while other times it has been from activist public pressure.

In general, oil and gas companies that pursued that approach underperformed ones that did not, and Europe in general has become energy insecure, partially due Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, but also partially due to their domestic energy policies.

And sometimes even well-meaning intentions have backfired. For example, Europe burns a lot of wood pellets for energy and classifies it as green, and some of those [come from old-growth forests](https://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2022/09/07/world/europe/eu-logging-wood-pellets.html), both in Europe and in North America, which is not very green at all. I’ll let Google’s AI summarize from across the web:

![Wood Pellets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-wood-pellets-ai.png)

![Old Growth Forests](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-old-growth-forests-ai.png)

While I’m very supportive of efforts to keep the air, water, and soil clean, I don’t think going after the oil and gas companies for producing oil and gas is the right approach to environmentalism. I think they should of course be held accountable to produce oil and gas with as little disruption to their production locations as possible, and with plenty of safety measures in place to prevent spills or other issues. In other words, efforts should be taken to ensure they don’t socialize the costs of their direct oil and gas operations, but that’s a separate matter from whether an oil and gas producer should be building and/or operating a bunch of windmills and solar sites.

From a shareholder perspective, I think it would benefit companies like BP to focus on their core business.

And BP culturally leaned into this trend more than others. They changed their name from British Petroleum to Beyond Petroleum over two decades ago. With a history of safety issues and underperformance, I think BP is an ideal shareholder activist target, and I’m optimistic on the outcome of that activism. They have a lot of good assets, but seem likely to benefit from an internal shift of prioritization.

Looking at their full-year 2024 data, they still have a big pipeline of wind and solar projects that could probably find a better home elsewhere, which Elliott Management will likely want them to divest from:

![BP Renewables](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-bp-renewables.png)

Back in 2017, I wrote [an article](https://www.lynalden.com/blue-chip-dividend-stocks/) about why corporations that persistently pay dividends (or perform buybacks) tend to outperform those that do not. Ultimately it comes down to capital discipline rather than the dividends themselves. If a company has excess cash, what you don’t want them to do from the perspective of a shareholder is to invest it into anything and everything. You’d rather have them just invest in their best ideas, and then return the remaining capital to you so that you can either spend it elsewhere or reinvest it back into their best ideas. Here’s the key snippet from that 2017 article:

> In any given year, a company has a certain amount of money that it can invest into various projects. These could include internal growth opportunities like launching a new product or building a new store, or it could involve acquiring another company.
>
> Of course, not all of those projects will be equal. Some of them are excellent opportunities within the company’s core competency that are likely to generate excellent returns on capital, while other ones are more mediocre/borderline opportunities.
>
> And a company has a limited amount of money to invest at any given time. Ideally, they want to invest in the best projects to generate the best rate of return for shareholders. If they invest in bad ideas that lose money, it destroys capital.
>
> For an undisciplined company that is big enough that it should be paying dividends but isn’t, this graphic shows how they generally spend their money:
>
> ![Non Dividend Stocks Example](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/non-dividend-stocks-graphic.jpg)
>
> If they have enough money lying around, they have more liberty to just throw money at all the potential projects, even mediocre ones. A “project” in this sense could be an internal growth opportunity like a new product or new store, or it could be an acquisition.
>
> Tech stocks that don’t pay dividends are infamous for making overvalued acquisitions- they buy companies that don’t even make a profit yet for billions of dollars, like throwing darts at a dartboard to see what sticks. Ten years later you can look back and ask, “what value did they *ever* get from that?”
>
> However, suppose that a company has a disciplined capital allocation policy, and they pay dividends to shareholders every quarter, and grow those dividends every year. This leaves them with less capital to invest, and therefore they need to be more selective in terms of which projects they invest in.
>
> Their example would look more like this:
>
> ![Dividend Stocks Example](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/dividend-stocks-graphic.jpg)They don’t have tons of cash to liberally just invest in everything, so they invest in their best ideas relating to their core business, and give the remaining funds to shareholders as dividends or buybacks.
>
> Those investors can spend those dividends as income or they can **reinvest** those dividends into buying more shares of the company. That way, you keep compounding exponentially in the company’s best projects with 100% of the capital.
>
> ![Reinvest Dividends Graphic](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/reinvest-dividends-graphic.jpg)
>
> Individual dividends aren’t magic. When a company pays a dividend, its stock price goes down slightly in the short term to adjust for the fact that some cash has left the company.
>
> But what separates excellent companies from mediocre companies over the course of decades is that excellent companies produce better returns on invested capital, which I will cover in the next section. And as an investor, it’s better to have a company that is disciplined and selective with its projects and returns excess capital to you than a company that simply invests in all potential projects.

In this case, BP is *already* paying dividends and doing buybacks like other oil and gas supermajors, but for their various capex/growth initiatives, a decent chunk is spent on things unrelated to oil and gas, and were not likely decided upon based purely on investment return expectations. If BP divests those non-core lower-ROI areas, and either increases its capex on higher-ROI areas or accelerates its shareholder returns, I think that would be beneficial. And then along the way they might earn a higher earnings multiple by the market as well.

The number of BP shares outstanding is down 20% over the past three years, but this has been offset by the otherwise lackluster performance of the underlying business which could benefit from better capital allocation:

![Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-bp-shares-outstanding.png)

BP has an A- credit rating, which I view as well-deserved. They make prudent usage of long-term debt, have plenty of cash on hand, and their overall net debt position is reasonable relative to their typical cash flows.

They got directly clipped by Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and the subsequent sanctions, since they had a sizable position in Russian oil and gas assets. So, not all of BP’s misfortune is from their own allocations. However, one-time events don’t generally result in structurally lower valuations. Having some activist scrutiny on their capital allocation decisions and overall board structure, should be of value. This is a company that could benefit from a cleaning of house.

**Shell as Well**

BP’s larger UK-based supermajor peer Shell (SHEL) is similarly cheap and underperforming. They have a great position as one of the biggest liquified natural gas entities, but overall are dogged by a lot of the same issues as BP. They have an A+ credit rating, and yet trade at very low multiples.

![SHEL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-shel-fastgraph.png)

And like BP, they pay most of their profits as dividends and share buybacks. They reduced their share count by 21% over the past three years.

Activism tends to have spillover effects, even if self-imposed to avoid direct activism. I expect UK-based and continental European oil and gas companies to refocus in the years ahead a bit more toward their oil and gas core businesses.

From a sovereign perspective, Europe has had wartime pressure from Russia, followed up by economic pressure from the United States, while China started eating Germany’s lunch as the new global leader in car production and overall industrial exports. Politics are shifting in Europe, and while I don’t focus on the non-investment implications of politics and instead remain mainly apolitical on any given party perspective, from an investment perspective I expect the UK and Europe to get more practical about energy security gradually over time.

I will of course have to monitor this thesis over time, and things could change. Overall, my base case is that the Western world has shifted from an increasingly anti-hydrocarbon view toward a more neutral or pro-hydrocarbon view, with Europe not nearly as strong on that shift yet but starting to turn the corner on it.

## Final Thoughts: Gold

The most common way I’ve ended reports over the past year has been by touching on gold, and for good reason. The yellow metal is up a whopping 45% year-over-year.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-2-16-gold.png)

I continue to be bullish on gold, but I also note that the overbought aspects gives me some pause. While I do expect that we’ll probably see over $3,000 this year, it’s entering a region where even bulls such as myself shouldn’t be too surprised by lengthy consolidations.

On the other hand, this rally in gold has been relatively under-covered by financial media and not experiencing a lot of euphoria. Bitcoin’s year-over-year percentage gains have been higher, albeit it’s a much smaller market so that’s not too surprising. This bull market occurred despite high rates and a strong dollar, which gives more upside potential should we get a weaker dollar in the year or two ahead.

In short, I remain bullish on gold, but with increasing levels of caution.
