## Macro View

**U.S. Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic liquidity indicators continue to be in a flat trend for the United States. The Treasury has shifted the majority of its new debt issuance towards short-duration debt (T-bills), which has enabled them to suck capital out of reverse repos and thus avoid draining net liquidity.

![US Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-liquidity.png)

Stocks are outperforming what we would expect from the liquidity situation alone, although of course there are always other variables to consider.

Looking at the stock and bond correlation gives interesting results, since the correlation tends to change over time and on different timescales.

-Over the past four decades, declining Treasury rates have enabled higher average equity valuations, because the discount rate or hurdle rate is lower. In other words, stock prices and bond yields have been negatively correlated.

-During much of that time on a closer scale, however, stock prices and bond yields have been positively correlated. Usually if the economy and stock market are not doing great in the cyclical sense, bond yields are falling. So, despite the zoomed-out negative correlation, stock prices and bond yields in any given year tends to have some degree of positive correlation.

-Starting in early 2022 (marked by the purple dots in the chart below), stock prices and bond yields had significant near-term negative correlation, which is somewhat unusual. Meaning, when bond yields were going up, stock prices were generally falling. However, within the past couple months (highlighted in the green box below), stock prices have moved higher despite the fact that bond yields have moved higher.

![Stocks vs Bonds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-stocks-vs-bonds.png)

I continue to have some concerns around the sustainability of this equity rally. The valuations of many large-cap stocks compared to the discount rate of 4%-yielding T-notes or 5%-yielding T-bills isn’t very favorable, especially when the economy itself continues to show signs of deceleration.

**U.S. Business Cycle**

Here is an updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. This lets us see a broad spectrum of the economy rather than to over-emphasize any particularly strong or weak data point.

The economy has been decelerating from a high growth rate since the third quarter of 2021, and is beginning to reach stall speed or becoming recessionary in many metrics. Retail spending, construction spending, capital spending, exports, and imports are shown in nominal dollar numbers, and so when they are below the inflation rate (as several of them are now), it means their growth rate is negative in real terms. Imports and exports are additionally negative in nominal terms.

![Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-economic-heatmap.png)

The chart starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the chart, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**U.S. Shale Update**

The number of U.S. oil rigs continues to decrease, which in the prior two cycles was an 8-month leading indicator and a 15-month leading indicator respectively for a significant peak in overall oil production:

![Shale Oil](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-shale.png)

A number of variables have pressured the oil price over the past year. The U.S. drained its strategic petroleum reserve by unprecedented amounts. Iran’s oil exports hit 5-year highs, as China’s partnership has enabled Iran to sell oil around sanctions. Europe partially de-industrialized. However, I expect Saudi Arabia’s current output cuts and a double-top in U.S. shale production to firm up the supply side, which should help oil find a price floor.

Taking into account both the demand side (possible recession) and supply side (the factors above), I don’t have a firm view on the price over the next 6 months, although I’m not bearish. Looking further ahead, I expect another large energy bull market within the middle of this decade, driven by tight supply. We’ll likely need another big capex cycle to fill that gap.

I tested a similar deployment a few weeks ago. The BTC Prague event had a Fedi federation associated with it. Users could join the federation via the Fedi app on their phone, which served as a Bitcoin/Lightning wallet, an event-focused app (it had all the agenda items, speaker events, meet-up times, etc), and some other sub-apps all rolled into one. One of the sub-apps was that you could easily use ChatGPT within the app and pay it via Lightning on a per-question basis with one press. It was super easy to use.

Now, the right skeptical question to ask is, “why pay in Lightning for AI usage, rather than just pay in dollars?” The answer is that Bitcoin/Lightning is uniquely well-suited to working with AI for a few reasons:

**No Chargebacks-** Unlike many software applications, AI is very hardware intensive on a per-usage basis. When you use AI, you are using very electricity-intensive GPU hardware. The software itself is cheap once it’s developed, but each usage of that software has a substantial real-world electricity/hardware resource cost, unlike most types of software. If an API is left open to be paid via conventional means (e.g. credit cards), then the merchant opens itself up as a target for fraud. A user could spam the GPUs with thousands of uses, and then perform chargebacks on their payments. In that event, the merchant bore the cost of the electricity/hardware but then lost the payments. Lightning on the other hand is a method of bearer asset final settlement.

**Microtransactions-**Most payment methods have limits in terms of how small individual transactions can be. You can’t pay $0.03 with a Visa card, for example. PayPal has merchant-side minimum fixed fees of $0.49 per transaction. The closest you can do is pre-fund an account and then pay micro-payments per usage within their ledger. And this all gets more complicated globally; if someone in Malaysia wants to pay someone in California for some AI usage, what payment method should they use? Lightning can handle microtransactions globally. A user can pay a few cents per usage from anywhere to anywhere, without subscriptions and without pre-funding.

**Permissionless and Programmable-** AI agents can be spun up to do all sorts of things. An open-source programmable money is easy for them to hold and use, compared to a bank account or bank payment product. An AI agent might not be able to get a bank account, but they can run a Lightning node, or operate a Lightning wallet. And the programmable nature of Lightning, along with its real-time instant aspect, allows for dynamic pricing. In other words, the micro-price per API call can be programmed to vary from second to second depending on how many incoming API requests they are getting at the same time.

In a world with a lot of human-to-machine, machine-to-human, and machine-to-machine payments, digitally-native money is the most suitable.

## HDFC Bank Update

HDFC Bank (HDB) of India is a stock I’ve been bullish on for years, although there were times where I increased or reduced exposure to it based on valuation. The company just completed the merger-acquisition of its parent company at the start of this month, which creates one of the largest banks in the world by market capitalization.

**Company and Merger Background**

The Housing Development Finance Corporation (generally known as HDFC) was founded in 1977 and has been a primary financier of real estate in India.

From the country’s independence in 1947 until the early 1990s, India had a partially socialist economics model (state-directed and protectionist), and had rather slow structural growth. In the early 1990s, there were a series of reforms to liberalize and open up the economy to the world more broadly, and to incorporate more market-oriented aspects. Growth has been stronger ever since. As part of this shift, HDFC launched HDFC Bank as a subsidiary in 1994.

HDFC continued focusing on real estate finance, insurance, asset management, and a few other areas, while HDFC Bank remained partly owned by HDFC and grew into the largest bank in the country. HDFC retained some of the shares as a partial parent company.

In 2022, it was announced that HDFC Bank would acquire-merge its parent HDFC into itself, to create a more streamlined full-service banking giant. Early this month, that merger completed ahead of schedule.

In addition to streamlining the overhead costs and corporate structure, one of the biggest opportunities that comes from the merger is the ability to cross-sell HDFC’s products to HDFC Bank customers.

> HDFC Bank has around 83 million customers but only 2% have a housing loan with HDFC. An additional 5% of the bank’s customers have a housing loan from other lenders, he said explaining that it means 93% of HDFC Bank’s customers do not have a home loan.
>
> This presents a “significant opportunity to cross sell and a potential to tap into the customer base that have not taken a housing loan at all,” the director said, adding that HDFC Bank will now be able to offer mortgage services.
>
> Mortgage penetration in India is “extremely low” and only accounts for approximately 11% of its GDP.
>
> That’s much lower than 26% in China, and between 20% to 40% in South East Asia, HDFC said. Most developed markets have more than 50% mortgage penetration, the company added.
>
> –[CNBC, July 4th 2023](https://www.cnbc.com/2023/07/04/hdfc-bank-director-india-merger-wont-face-insurmountable-challenges.html)

India is one of the fastest-growing countries in the world in terms of real GDP, and the financial sector of the country is growing even faster than overall GDP, because India is starting from a baseline that is under-banked compared to its peers. The country has low mortgage penetration, and low household debt in general.

HDFC Bank now has a stronger opportunity and a stronger incentive to bundle services together, and increase the percentage of its customers that have a home loan with them.

**Primary Risks**

As the country’s largest bank, and one that does not trade cheaply, HDFC Bank is exposed to any macro risk that could slow down India’s growth or cause a financial crisis in the country.

Mainly, India’s ability to import enough energy and other commodities to fuel a 1.4 billion person country remains its key vulnerability (and by extension, one of the reasons I remain bullish on hydrocarbons over the next decade). To roughly quantify things, India consumes less than a third of the oil that China consumes per capita, and China consumes less than a fifth of the oil that the United States consumes per capita. By extension, India consumes less than 1/15th of the oil that the United States consumes per capita.

As another quantitative point, both Brazil and Mexico consume comfortably more oil per capita than China, and by extension consume 4x-5x as much as India. India could multiply its resource intensively significantly and still be at developing country consumption levels.

If India were to consume as much as China does per capita, that would require about 8 million more barrels per day, which basically requires finding another Saudi Arabia worth of oil supply. Some western nations may reduce their overall consumption, but then again countries like Indonesia and Nigeria and Pakistan are very large-population countries with very low per-capita consumption that could climb up the ranks as well.

India continues to trade significantly with Russia, and indeed has absorbed a large portion of the Russian energy exports that Europe has given up. India tends to use a pragmatic approach to foreign trade, being willing to trade with anyone that has commodities, including Russia and Iran. India and China are on tense political terms as two major regional powers sitting right next to each other, and yet India is part of the BRICS and the Shanghai Cooperation Organization anyway, and tends to align with China on various mutual economic interests.

## Final Thoughts: Stocks Nobody Talks About

Mega-cap tech stocks on the bull side and banks on the bear side have captured most of investor and media mindshare lately. However, there are plenty of stocks in the “boring middle” that continue to chug along.

Cigna (CI) continues to be one of my preferred healthcare stocks as part of a diversified portfolio. They focus on health insurance and pharmacy benefit management. Pharmacy benefit managers in general have been under legal pressure lately, which has helped push the stock back below its average valuation. But overall, this is a company with a price/earnings/growth “PEG” ratio below 1x, which is rare these days:

![CI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-ci-fastgraph.png)

Meanwhile, there are signs that payment companies like Visa (V) and Fiserv (FI) have put in a long consolidation and might be breaking out. The valuations of these companies got ahead of themselves prior to and during the pandemic years, but as things normalize and their fundamentals keep chugging higher, it should start to drag the price up over time. Between the two, Visa has a pristine balance sheet but relatively expensive equity, while Fiserv has much cheaper equity but a significant debt load to manage.

![V FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-v-fastgraph.png)

![FI FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-7-9-fi-fastgraph.png)

I continue to have an emphasis on a “three pillar” portfolio strategy consisting of 1) solid long-term profitable stocks 2) cash-equivalents like T-bills and money markets, and 3) commodity producers and hard monies.
