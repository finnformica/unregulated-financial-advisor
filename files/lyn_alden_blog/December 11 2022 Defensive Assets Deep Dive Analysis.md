## Macro View

The majority of US macroeconomic indicators continue to point towards economic deceleration, cyclical disinflation (amid a high potential for structural inflation), and a likely recession. A few large areas, like the services sector and consumption in general, continue to hold up reasonably well.

**Yield Curve**

The US yield curve continues to be inverted, which at least for the spread between the 10-year yield and the 3-month yield, has been an extremely reliable predictor of the past eight US recessions. The St. Louis Fed shows four decades of data, but the underlying data go back further than that.

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-yield-curve.png)

Data compiled by [@TXMCTrades](https://twitter.com/TXMCtrades) showed that during the prior eight yield curve inversions, the stock market bottomed within 315 to 963 days after the yield curve inversion:

![TXMC Yield Curve Market Bottoms](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-txmc-tweet.png)

In other words, the US stock market tends to bottom about one or two years after the initial yield curve inversion. In extremes, it can be a little more (963 days is just over 2.5 years) or a little less (315 days is around 10.5 months).

If we assume that the stock market bottom that occurred this year was \*the\* bottom for the cycle, then this cycle will have been an anomaly compared to the past eight cycles stretching back to the 1960s. It will have been a case where the stock market bottomed several days *before* the yield curve inversion, which would be about a year earlier than the next earliest bottom, historically speaking.

Here’s a visual that stretches back to the early 1980s, with green dots representing yield curve inversions and yellow dots representing recessionary stock market bottoms:

![Wilshire vs Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/permium-2022-12-11-wilshire-yield-curve.png)

Basically, the probabilities from this data suggest that it is more likely that the stock market has not bottomed yet, and instead will reach lower lows in the next year or two.

However, I continue to stress that we have to be careful about back-tests like this, because this macroeconomic environment is unlike anything since the 1940s. This is the century-long chart I keep referring to in terms of my high-level macro assessment:

![Long-Term Debt Cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-5-longterm-debt-cycle.png)

There’s an unclear path over the next year or so, but as we look out longer, the US and much of the western world is likely to enter a sustained period of runaway sovereign deficits and the monetization of those deficits. It’s very hard to get out from under this high of a sovereign debt load without a substantial degree of financial repression, which means holding sovereign bond yields well below the nominal GDP growth rate.

My high conviction expectation is that the stock market will continue to struggle in inflation-adjusted terms, with long sideways choppy nominal action. My lower conviction expectation is that we probably will see lower nominal lows in 2023 as part of that choppiness.

**2Yr vs Fed Funds**

The 2-year Treasury yield tends to be a good proxy for what the market thinks the Fed will do (which is partially but not completely based on what the Fed says they will do).

When the 2-year Treasury yield rolls over and goes below the Fed’s interest rate, that usually ends up marking the top. We’re not there yet, but by Q1 2023 we most likely will be.

![2 Year Treasury vs Fed Funds](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-2yr-fed-funds.png)

**Senior Loan Officer Survey**

The Federal Reserve performs a quarterly survey of up to 80 US banks and another 20+ US branches of foreign banks to determine whether they are tightening or loosening their lending standards.

They cover a variety of different metrics, but the most commonly cited one measures the percentage of banks that are tightening standards for commercial/industrial loans to large and mid-size firms. When this number is above zero, it means that banks in general are tightening their lending standards, meaning that they are being more critical of applications for loans:

![Senior Loan Officer Survey](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-senior-loan-officer-survey.png)

The recent rapid tightening of lending standards is in recession territory at this point. All four of the prior times that the percentage of banks that were tightening lending standards reached this level within the past three decades, ended up being a leading or coincident indicator for a US recession.

The closest exception was in late 1998 during the collapse of Long-Term Capital Management, where lending briefly tightened and then immediately loosened (and the Fed, led by Alan Greenspan, cut interest rates and organized a bailout of LTCM).

The consumer lending data goes back further, about four decades. In this chart, below zero means that banks in aggregate have less willingness to make consumer installment loans:

![Consumer Loans](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-consumer-loans.png)

When this level of willingness enters contraction, it usually signals a recession. The exception was 1996.

The final chart for this section is a bit noisy. The blue line on the left axis is the same as the prior chart, the percentage of banks with increased willingness to make consumer installment loans. The red line on the right axis is the Federal Reserve’s short-term interest rate, inverted for visual clarity to show the correlation:

![Fed Funds vs Bank Willingness ](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-fed-funds-vs-bank-willingness.png)

Generally speaking, the Fed’s higher interest rates lead to less willingness of banks to make consumer loans.

And that is basically the Fed’s intention- to reduce the availability of credit and to try to cause a slowdown in the economy to tame inflation. Historically, they rarely get the soft landing they desire. Instead, it usually contributes to recession, forcing them to pivot and put out a fire that they lit.

Or as I put it back in my [May 2022 public newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2022-newsletter/):

![Inflation vs Recession](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2022-5-inflation-vs-recession.jpg)

**Purchasing Managers Index**

The manufacturing purchasing manager’s index is one of the most important macroeconomic charts to keep track of, and it recently went below 50, which means it’s in a mild contraction.

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-ism-pmi.png)

*Chart Source: [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/business-confidence)*

During the past three recessions (2001, 2008, and 2020), the US PMI reached sub-45. During mid-cycle slowdowns that *don’t* result in a recession, the upper-40s tends to be where it reverses. Those mid-cycle slowdowns that reversed back up into not being a recession, however, haven’t historically had an inverted yield curve associated with them.

The freight index tends to correlate with the manufacturing PMI rather significantly. Right now the data only go through October, but it seems to be rolling over in year-over-year percent terms:

![Freight Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-freight-index.png)

The non-manufacturing (services) PMI is less cyclical overall, and while it’s not exactly booming at the moment, it’s by no means the weak point for the US economy:

![Services PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-ism-pmi-services.png)

**Jobless Claims**

The number of continued jobless claims bottomed in spring/summer 2022 at a very low rate, and has since been rolling upward.

![Continued Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-continued-claims.png)

This move isn’t large enough to confirm a recession, but combined with the other indicators, it’s something to keep watching.

I think the jobless claims and unemployment data are somewhat understating actual unemployment levels because unlike many recessions, the layoffs in this recession have mainly been in the technology sector, as well as certain financial sectors (e.g. mortgage origination) which are typically high-paying jobs with a multi-month severance package.

**Q4 GDP Estimate**

The Atlanta Fed’s GDP estimate for Q4 continues to hold up well, at a bit over 3% annualized. This is based on various economic data that rolls in over time.

![Atlanta Fed GDPNow](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-gdp-now.png)

Summing this together, the inverted Treasury yield curve, banks’ tightening of lending standards, the uptick in unemployment and jobless claims from a very low base, and the low reading for manufacturing PMI, all point towards a likely recession in 2023. The readings from the Atlanta Fed and services PMI, on the other hand, still point towards a reasonably strong consumer and services sector for the moment.

With the Fed still tightening monetary policy into that data set, my base case points towards a US recession within 2023, unless or until new data start to suggest otherwise.

**Princeton DeCenter Opening**

On November 30th, I attended and spoke at [Princeton University’s DeCenter Inaugural Summit](https://decenter.princeton.edu/inaugural-summit/).

This was a small invite-only event hosted by Princeton to formally initiate their new DeCenter. The [speaker list](https://decenter.princeton.edu/inaugural-summit/) included professors from a number of universities, various industry financiers and developers, the general counsel of the Securities and Exchange Commission, the chairman of the Commodity Futures Trading Commission, and the executive director of the American Civil Liberties Union.

The Princeton DeCenter, short for “Princeton Center for the Decentralization of Power Through Blockchain Technology” is a new cross-disciplinary organization within Princeton University that blends expertise from their engineering, economics, and politics departments to explore some of the technical and societal implications of blockchain technology.

Princeton University aims to become a larger force for research into this field, and I’m interested to see what kind of work they do over the next few years.

The opening remarks were from the president of Princeton (Prof. Christopher Eisgruber) and the co-directors of the new center (Prof. Goldsmith, the dean of Princeton’s engineering department, and Prof. Singh, a computer science professor with a specialty in parallel computing).

Following that, the first discussion of the event was by the chair of the CFTC, Rostin Behnam. He discussed the need for regulation in the aftermath of the various crypto collapses this year, but also stressed the limited authority that the CFTC has in this area, and pointed out that instead, much of this industry is up to the SEC to regulate.

One of the most newsworthy parts of the discussion was that he walked back his [prior comments](https://blockworks.co/news/cftc-chair-says-ether-is-a-commodity-hints-that-sec-disagrees) about ether being a commodity, and instead would only commit to bitcoin being a commodity.

As [reported by Fortune](https://fortune.com/crypto/2022/11/30/inaction-is-paralysis-cftc-chair-rostin-behnam-calls-for-regulation-in-the-wake-of-ftxs-collapse/):

> In the vacuum created by the lack of regulation, one of the central debates has been whether different cryptocurrencies should be classified as commodities or securities, with the latter under the remit of the U.S. Securities and Exchange Commission.
>
> Behnam said that the only cryptocurrency that should be viewed as a commodity is Bitcoin, walking back from previous remarks made in October when he suggested Ether may also be a commodity.

Previously, the CFTC chair and the SEC chair had somewhat opposing views on ether, but now the CFTC chair’s views seem to be in line with the SEC chair’s views.

It remains to be seen what type of legislation related to regulation might be passed in 2023, or what enforcement actions might be taken by the SEC in that year. Somewhat buried among the recent FTX/Alameda news over the past month, [the SEC recently won a lawsuit against LBRY, Inc.](https://www.reuters.com/legal/transactional/us-securities-regulators-win-case-against-crypto-company-lbry-2022-11-07/) regarding their coin being an unregistered security.

The rest of the event after the CFTC chair’s discussion is under Chatham House Rule, meaning that information from the event may be used, but that information cannot be attributed to specific attendees.

There were significant discussions on the potential applications of blockchain technology, discussions on proof-of-work vs proof-of-stake as differing consensus mechanisms, discussions around what the DeCenter should focus on, discussions on the lessons learned from the collapse of FTX and Alameda, discussions on the human rights applications of blockchain technology, discussions on how the crypto industry has evolved since the publication of Satoshi Nakamoto’s white paper in 2008, and discussions around the nuances of regulation.

A common theme among many speakers was that the use-case of bitcoin and stablecoins is pretty clear (enabling people to access permissionless payments and savings worldwide, including in many jurisdictions with problematic inflation or authoritarianism), and that the idea of tokenizing a broader set of assets also seems worthwhile but hasn’t yet necessarily borne much fruit. In theory, turning many types of securities into bearer asset tokens (upgrading the trading rails compared to the current system), can allow the assets to trade 24/7 and can allow more people around the world to access them.

*Hybrid Consensus Discussion*

The theme around proof-of-work vs proof-of-stake was interesting, with proponents of both in attendance. Professor David Tse participated in a panel, and I subsequently spoke with him privately to ask him some technical questions, and he said it was fine to report on his work (waiving the Chatham House Rule). Dr. Tse is the co-founder and CEO of [Babylon Chain](https://babylonchain.io/) which attempts to add security to proof-of-stake chains by using bitcoin, in kind of a hybrid system.

Babylon is a “timestamp aggregation service” that allows various different proof-of-stake blockchains to insert timestamps into the highly-secure proof-of-work bitcoin blockchain, in order to improve their resistance to certain types of attacks or problems. In some sense, this can turn any proof-of-stake chain into a kind of bitcoin sidechain, albeit with its own floating coin.

The primary (and insurmountable) downside of proof-of-stake blockchains is that they are not true timechains like bitcoin is; they have no unforgeable history of the state of the ledger.

If a proof-of-stake blockchain goes down for any reason (like what has happened to Solana on five separate occasions in 2022), then anyone can make an arbitrary number of alternative histories to the blockchain, and there is no way to determine what the real history was. Who, therefore, determines in practice what the “real” history was, meaning the official checkpoints that they can re-start the chain from? Does some centralized entity or group of large entities determine the checkpoints and become the arbiter of truth? If so, doesn’t that centralize the blockchain?

Babylon attempts to answer that dilemma by providing a way for proof-of-stake blockchains to regularly insert their checkpoints into the Bitcoin blockchain (which unlike them, uses a proof-of-work consensus protocol and therefore has a true unforgeable history to its ledger).

The Babylon team is not the only group who have proposed this; there was a [study published in August of this year](https://arxiv.org/abs/2208.05408) about regularly checkpointing proof-of-stake blockchains into the Bitcoin network. The unique selling proposition of Babylon is that they can aggregate multiple proof-of-stake checkpoints into one streamlined checkpoint at regular intervals so that they don’t clog the Bitcoin network, and therefore lower the cost of implementing these regular checkpoints.

The reasons I find Dr. Tse’s work interesting are twofold. One is that I’m interested in comparing different sidechain trade-offs in general, and two is that the work explicitly acknowledges some of the shortcomings of proof-of-stake systems, which in my view is not done enough by the proof-of-stake community.

*Regulatory Discussion*

There were a number of discussions regarding the theory of regulation, aside from what the chair of the CFTC discussed.

There is naturally a trade-off between trying to protect consumers, and stifling innovation.

-If regulations are too heavy-handed or unfit for the times, it can drive development overseas (and indeed, some consumers will also use overseas entities for trading, which may be less safe than if they could access those services in a domestic, regulated environment).

-On other hand, if regulation is applied inconsistently, then it allows scams to proliferate, and it creates an opportunity for regulatory arbitrage.

For an example of inconsistency, if an asset that would otherwise pass the [Howey Test](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/h/howey-test.asp) and thus be a security, has a loophole to avoid being labeled a security by issuing a token instead of a traditional stock or bond, then it creates an unnatural incentive for traditional assets to tokenize themselves.

The view of a significant number of pro-regulation people is the idea that any common enterprise that raises capital, reliant on the work of others, with the goal of generating a profit as part of a common enterprise (i.e. an entity that passes the Howey Test), is a security regardless of what sort of tech or distribution rails its liability claims run on.

The view of some anti-regulation people is that this stifles innovation and that digital assets should have some sort of exception or at least some limited sandbox to play in, and/or that 20th century securities regulation should be updated and streamlined for the 21st century so that this is less of an issue.

By the strict definition, virtually any digital asset that raises capital is probably a security. This excludes bitcoin, and perhaps excludes a few other hard forks or copycats that mostly took the Bitcoin network code and tweaked it a bit and thus didn’t need to raise capital to launch their system. But this includes almost everything else, and therefore the vast majority of digital assets remain in a state of ongoing regularity risk, and the exchanges that sell them to the public remain in a state of ongoing regulatory risk.

(Yahoo Finance had SEC Chair Gensler on their television program on December 7th, and me on the next day, to discuss the regulatory situation. You can see it [here](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=sWO7psfGQvM&ab_channel=YahooFinance).)

*Proof-of-Reserves*

There were discussions around the concept of proof-of-reserves, in the wake of the FTX collapse where it was found out that customer deposits were unbacked, or backed by the wrong type of collateral. For example, many FTX customers thought they had bitcoin exposure, but FTX ended up not having any bitcoin.

Proof-of-reserves involves a number of different cryptographic techniques for custodians, exchanges, or similar entities to prove what digital assets it has control over (the easy part), and what customer liabilities that they have (the more technically challenging part).

Proof-of-reserves has a fundamental limitation in the sense that you cannot prove a negative- you can’t prove the nonexistence of off-chain liabilities. However, there are ways to show that the organization’s assets and the organization’s customer deposits match up. So, these types of techniques are imperfect but can offer a series of tools for auditors, regulators, or the general users of a service, to evaluate the credibility of a given custodian, exchange, or similar entity.

Back when Obi Nwosu ran Coinfloor (the earliest UK bitcoin exchange), they were more committed to doing proof-of-reserves than other exchanges at the time. In the wake of the Mt. Gox scandal, many exchanges committed to doing regular proofs-of-reserves, but most of them didn’t follow through with it, except Coinfloor, which did so for years consistently.

The FTX/Alameda debacle put the topic of proof-of-reserves back in the spotlight recently. And over time, there have been and will be more advanced cryptographic methods to show reserve assets and customer liabilities, while preserving the privacy of individual account balances.

This still, however, does not displace the importance of self-custodying one’s own assets, especially bitcoin. Its ability to be used self-custodially and permissionlessly is the main value proposition of the asset.

![Yen Dollar vs US Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-yen-usd.png)

Gong back to my earlier comments about bear market rallies, we can also measure the prospect for bear market rallies in stocks if we denominate stocks in gold terms. This chart shows the S&P 500 divided by the price of gold, or in other words how many ounces of gold it takes to buy the S&P 500:

![S&P 500 vs Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2022-12-11-spx-gold.png)

My base case, unless or until I see evidence suggesting otherwise, is that the late-2021 high in that ratio (at little over 2.6x) was probably a structural high point for a while, and that the ratio will gradually roll over from here with lower lows and lower highs.

That of course is merely a probability assessment, but overall my view is that profit margins and earnings growth will be under some degree of pressure, and global central banks will continue their gradual shift towards gold accumulation as part of their FX reserves [that they’ve been doing since 2008](https://bmg-group.com/central-bank-gold-demand-trends/).

Silver and platinum are both also enjoying significant momentum shifts vs oil, and I’m bullish on them as well.

## Final Thoughts

The year 2022 was a story about valuation compression. As the Federal Reserve raised interest rates at the fastest pace in decades, it forced the market to re-rate various assets at lower multiples of their cash flows, and this is especially so for expensive long-duration assets. It also popped various sources of leverage and resulted in liquidations, especially in the crypto industry. Additionally, it put some downward pressure on gold in dollar terms.

Going forward, the year 2023 may instead be a story about corporate earnings stagnation. Valuations may not be what drives asset prices, but rather, a stagnant economy and weak earnings, especially among the biggest tech companies, may be what drives asset prices. This can change the game for what types of assets keep doing poorly vs what types of assets are able to make a reversal.

What I want to own in that environment, primarily, is relatively scarce assets that are 1) reasonably priced with negative sentiment and 2) that either don’t have earnings and thus aren’t valued for their earnings (e.g. gold or bitcoin) or that have earnings that are likely to hold up pretty well (e.g. Indian companies, Brazilian companies, healthcare companies, tobacco companies, and North American energy pipelines).
