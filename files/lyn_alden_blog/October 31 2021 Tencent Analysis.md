# Tencent Deep Dive Analysis

*October 31, 2021*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report covers the virus, economic activity, the dollar, gold, and financial system liquidity.

The report then dives into a broad update on bitcoin and ether, and finishes with a stock analysis of Tencent.

## Macro View

Official numbers for new daily cases and daily deaths for the virus seem to have bottomed, with the next upturn happening:

![Global COVID](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-covid.png)

*Chart Source: [Worldometer](https://www.worldometers.info/coronavirus/)*

Countries around the world are on different schedules for this wave, rather than being in unison. Some European countries such as the UK and Germany have pretty high new daily reported case counts, but low deaths. Italy has rather low cases and deaths in this cycle so far. Russia has high cases and high deaths.

Meanwhile the US and various southeast Asian countries are still in a downtrend from the prior virus wave.

**US Q3 GDP and Inflation**

Annualized real GDP growth was recently reported at 2.0% for the US in Q3, compared to consensus economist estimates of 2.7%. Due to relatively high levels of inflation over the past year, nominal GDP has roughly caught up to its prior trend whereas real GDP has struggled to regain its prior trend.

![GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-gdp.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The Atlanta Fed’s [GDPNow](https://www.atlantafed.org/cqer/research/gdpnow) model is currently pointing to 6.6% annualized real growth in Q4, which if becomes true represents a significant re-acceleration after a weak Q3. Their model previously started Q3 with similar numbers but gradually ground down to near zero by the end of the quarter as more information came in, so we’ll see if those high Q4 expectations can hold as we head deeper into the months ahead.

Invitation Homes (INVH), a company with over 80,000 single-family homes for rent, [reported for Q3](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-10-28/invitation-homes-boosts-rents-11-as-housing-shortage-persists) that they raised average rent prices 10.6% year over year, with a 7.8% rent increase on renewals and a 18.4% average rent increase for new leases.

Inflation continues to run pretty hot across multiple categories, but energy is starting to take a break after going rather parabolic in many parts of the world. China is directly trying to reduce coal prices, and indeed coal is heading lower for the moment. The rise in oil prices in the US and globally has stalled out for now, which makes sense after how consensus it became for a period of time.

Instead, rent prices and wage increases are driving inflation here in Q4. Mikael Sarwe of Nordea [posted a chart](https://twitter.com/MikaelSarwe/status/1454093385128677381) recently comparing US wages vs US median CPI. So far, median CPI hasn’t taken off yet like headline CPI has, but Sarwe’s chart suggests that it’s probably about to:

![Sarwe Tweet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-sarwe-tweet.png)

As an anecdote, yesterday my local supermarket had the biggest labor shortage I’ve seen from them yet. There were massive lines at the check-outs, including long lines at the automated check-outs, due to understaffing.

Overall, we’re likely going to remain in an inflationary environment for at least another quarter or two due to rent increases, and we’ll need to assess as more data come in to what extent it will remain in place beyond that point or take a break. Inflation has thus far rolled from category to category, with specific price spikes and shortages in certain areas at different times, rather than all together. There’s still a clear ongoing labor shortage.  Part of it is transitory but I continue to view [part of it as structural](https://www.lynalden.com/august-2021-newsletter/).

**Gold and the Dollar**

Gold has been in a sideways consolidation for several months now:

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-gold.png)

As a recap, I became bullish on gold in the second half of 2018 and remained so until August 2020, at which I point I was still structurally bullish but wrote that I thought it was extended, could take a break, and that bitcoin looked better into the end of 2021. However, I became relatively bullish on gold again in March 2021 and wrote about that, as it seemed to have bottomed with a bullish momentum shift. Ultimately, that subsequent rally was short-lived, and instead gold has chugged along sideways from that point.

There’s still not a lot to report at this time, as it is neither breaking down nor breaking out. Sticky inflation and deeply negative real yields are bullish for gold, but market concerns about central bank tightening and a relatively strong dollar have been fundamental drags on sentiment.

Catalysts for gold to break out would include things like the Fed coming out more dovish than market expectations, or the economy getting weak in 2022. Quantitatively, global fund flows into exchange-traded bitcoin products vs exchange-traded gold products have strongly preferred bitcoin products over the past year.

The dollar continues to be in a mild but persistent uptrend from 2020 lows compared to a basket of other large developed-market currencies:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-dxy.png)

What’s interesting about this is that it’s happening even though the Fed is remaining more dovish than most other central banks. A lot of international money is still piling into US large cap stocks, which supports the dollar.

Breaking it down currency by currency rather than looking at a basket of major currencies adds clarity at this time.

The two biggest currencies in the dollar index are the euro and yen. Both of those are significant energy importers (at a time of energy shortages), and both of them are also among the most dovish central banks with low rates and ongoing asset purchases. They both have structural current account surpluses which are currency-positive, but their dovishness and energy issues are taking precedent at this time.

Meanwhile, the dollar has weakened vs the Canadian dollar and the Australian dollar relatively sharply since September. Both of those are commodity exporter currencies, and their central banks have leaned a bit more hawkish lately, especially the Bank of Canada.

The dollar has been relatively flat against a basket of emerging market currencies in recent months. These are not part of the DXY dollar index. Specifically, the dollar has been flat-to-slightly-down vs the Chinese yuan since September, even though China is a big energy importer and has been struggling with both energy shortages and real estate leverage problems. China has, however, been rather tight on fiscal and monetary policy lately which is often a currency-positive scenario. Other emerging markets are a mixed bag. The Russian ruble has strengthened against the dollar recently due to rising energy prices, since Russia is a big energy exporter. On the other hand, the Brazilian real has been quite weak vs the dollar, which is largely attributed to their current fiscal issues.

So in summary, it’s not so much that the dollar is strong lately, it’s that the euro and yen have been quite weak, and they make up the majority of the dollar index comparison basket. Beyond that, the dollar has been strengthening and weakening vs various currencies individually, which makes this not quite the same type of dollar strength that generally wrecks emerging markets broadly.

**Global Yields**

Short duration government bond yields exploded higher for many countries last week.

![2 Year Yields](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-2yr-yields.png)

*Source: [@BChappatta](https://twitter.com/BChappatta/status/1453705779735842816)*

Australia’s was the most interesting move because their 2-year note yield roughly *tripled* from about 0.20% to 0.60% in a day, as their central bank decided not to defend their yield curve cap. Others soared as well, but none quite as sharply as that one.

In addition, the 10-year government bonds for Italy and Greece blew out 20 to 30 basis points within a day or two. These are some of the most indebted countries in the eurozone, they cannot print their own currencies, and so they rely on the European Central Bank to perform informal spread control to keep their yields from getting too high compared to German bonds. So that puts the ECB in a bit of a bind if these yields keep going higher.

Central banks are in a tight spot here. With high inflation, their mandate is to tighten monetary policy and raise interest rates to quell that inflation. However, with so much sovereign debt as a percentage of GDP, many of them can’t realistically do that beyond a mild amount. This goes back to the [concept of the long-term debt cycle](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2021-newsletter/), where central banks are eventually forced to devalue their currencies sharply by holding their yields well below the inflation rate in order to avoid a nominal fiscal default spiral.

This is a process that plays out over time and there are occasionally some big decision points to monitor, and this period is one of them.

The Fed’s next meeting is Nov 3, where it is widely expected by the market that they will announce a gradual reduction for their rate of asset purchases, which represents slight tightening in rate of change terms. If the Fed surprises and does not commit to that, then it would likely be dollar bearish, as it would be a dovish surprise, and they’re already behind many other central banks in terms of tightening. The most likely outcome is that they will indeed announce a tapering.

**Financial System Liquidity**

It was easy for me to understand and predict liquidity dynamics in 2019 and 2020, but 2021 has admittedly been harder for that topic. So, liquidity is a theme I’ve been ruminating on in the past week to get a better handle on it as we end the year and enter into 2022. This is the nerdiest part of the report so feel free to skip it if you don’t care about details of the US financial system.

The US Treasury Department previously had a plan to lengthen their average maturity of debt issuance. However, during the pandemic year 2020, the Treasury [sharply reduced its average maturity from 70 months to 65 months](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-economy-yellen-bonds/yellen-says-no-plans-to-lengthen-maturity-of-u-s-treasury-issuance-idUSKBN2BF2LP), as it strongly relied on short-term T-bill issuance to fund the large fiscal expenditures.

The Treasury noted this in their [November 4, 2020 quarterly refunding statement](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sm1174):

> In light of this, Kranbuhl highlighted that Treasury has continued to maintain financing flexibility as a matter of prudent risk management, including continued reliance on increased bill issuance through benchmark bills and a regular cadence of cash management bills since March.  In the second half of FY2020, Treasury’s borrowing needs increased by an unforeseen and unprecedented $3.2 trillion.  Two-thirds of that borrowing need, or about $2.4 trillion, was addressed through increases in bills, representing a doubling of bills outstanding.  This resulted in a reduced weighted average maturity of Treasury debt outstanding and an increased share of Treasury bills to an above-average level.  Treasury continues to manage this maturity profile change by shifting issuance toward coupon securities across the curve over time.

There are some investors and political analysts who stated that the US should have issued longer-duration bonds when interest rates were so low, to lock in decades of super low borrowing rates. They could have even introduced 50-year and 100-year bond durations as some countries have done.

However, there are other analysts (that in my view are more accurate in the grand scheme of things), who state that Treasury yields were only so low because the Fed and other central banks were buying so many Treasuries and other sovereign bonds, even though QE has somewhat of a mixed historical relationship with yields. By this view, if the Treasury were to have issued a bunch of long-duration bonds, it would have caused a supply/demand problem. After all, the Fed’s meeting minutes state that they buy long-duration bonds in order to suppress their yields.

Actions speak louder than words, and their actions support the second view. In a period of unprecedented low interest rates, unprecedented debt issuance, and unprecedented debt monetization during the pandemic and immediate aftermath, the Treasury *reduced* average debt maturity by sharply increasing its T-bills and T-notes outstanding, rather than extending its debt maturity.

The fact that they reduced rather than extended maturities means the Treasury was either 1) not smart or 2) couldn’t realistically do it, and I think it’s clearly the second option. Smart businesses I track, like Enterprise Products Partners (EPD), used the low yield environment to increase their average bond maturity rather than reduce it, because they’re not saturating the market with debt and there was plenty of real appetite for their bonds. The Treasury, like those smart businesses, *wanted* to increase its duration as well, but it was hard to do so when issuing trillions in new debt.

My friend and fellow analyst, [Luke Gromen of FFTT](https://twitter.com/LukeGromen), has been predicting this for years now on multiple mediums (with the added context being that 2014 was when the dollar strengthened and the overall foreign sector began sharply reducing their ongoing purchases of Treasury securities):

![Gromen Tweets](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-gromen-tweets-2018-2021.png)

Next, here’s a section from the Treasury’s [August 4, 2021 quarterly refunding statement](https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/jy0307):

> The amount of net new cash still being raised from coupon issuance will allow Treasury to continue gradual reductions in bills as a percent of Treasury debt outstanding, in a manner consistent with recommendations made by the Treasury Borrowing Advisory Committee (TBAC) at its November 2020 meeting.  Accordingly, Treasury plans to further modify its regular cadence of CMBs.  Treasury anticipates that weekly issuance of the 6-week CMBs will cease after settlement on August 19, whereas weekly issuance of the 17-week CMBs will continue at least through the end of October.

So according to that statement, the Treasury still seems to want to increase average duration by reducing T-bills as a percentage of their debt.

And yet back in March 2021, when the Treasury Secretary testified to Congress and was asked specifically about this, she said the Treasury [had no plans to increase duration](https://www.reuters.com/article/us-usa-economy-yellen-bonds/yellen-says-no-plans-to-lengthen-maturity-of-u-s-treasury-issuance-idUSKBN2BF2LP).

What’s going on? Why wouldn’t the Treasury increase the average duration of their debt while yields are so low? Every creditworthy corporation in this environment certainly wants to, and has generally done so. Especially back in 2020 when long-duration yields were super low. They’re still rather low in 2021.

From what I can tell, over the next year the Treasury is indeed likely to keep stable or further reduce average duration of government debt, rather than increase it. T-bills are where the demand is, which is the opposite problem that the Treasury had in 2019.

Over the past couple years, the Fed did so much QE that they absolutely stuffed the US banking system with excess reserves. Due to various regulations, the US banking system is like a cup of water; there is a limit to how full it can be in terms of excess cash (liquidity). If you pour water into a cup until it’s full, and then keep pouring more into it anyway, the excess water just spills out. That’s what happened with cash liquidity this year, and back in 2014 as well.

Excess liquidity spreads out in the system from banks to money markets funds, and spills into the Fed’s reverse repo agreements. With the reverse repo agreements, the Fed gives out Treasuries in exchange for cash on a temporary basis, and those agreements can keep being rolled to make that transfer pseudo-permanent. In other words, excess liquidity stays parked in the Fed’s reverse repo program, potentially for months and years.

And more excess liquidity entered the commercial banking system in 2021 than was intended. The Treasury’s General Account at the Fed (which fills up from issuing Treasuries faster than money is spent, and draws down from spending money faster than Treasuries are issued) was supposed to draw down from a record-high of $1.8 trillion to $800 billion. However, due to the debt ceiling issue extending for months, they had to draw it down to under $100 billion (and thus were at risk of technical default as they neared zero cash with no legal ability to issue debt). With the current debt ceiling extension, the TGA is back up to about $200 billion. So that was a $1.6 trillion flush of liquidity from the Treasury to the commercial banking system.

Currently there is $1.5 trillion in reverse repo activity, which is quite a lot, but is probably around the top it will reach for now. This represents strong demand for short-duration Treasuries as collateral, due to how the financial system is structured with all of this new excess liquidity. This surge in reverse repo demand was almost perfectly inversely correlated with the $1.6 trillion drain of the Treasury’s General Account at the Fed. Essentially, the Treasury drained cash from TGA out into the financial system and then into the Fed’s reverse repo agreements, since the US financial system was already at maximum liquidity when the Treasury began draining its account:

![TGA and RRP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-tga-rrp.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The market dynamics currently mean that due to excess liquidity, institutions currently have a strong appetite for T-bills as collateral (even though they are yielding well below the inflation rate).

Whenever the debt ceiling is resolved, the Treasury will need to issue hundreds of billions of dollars worth of Treasury securities, and eventually trillions of dollars. Exactly how many they issue and how quickly will depend on what sort of fiscal stimulus Congress passes (if any) and how quickly the Treasury wants to get back up to its target cash levels in its TGA account.

If the Treasury wants to extend its average debt duration, they would focus on issuing longer-dated securities. However, this reverse repo activity is basically screaming at the Treasury to issue T-bills, not T-bonds. The Treasury is indeed likely to issue a lot of its debt over the coming year at relatively short durations.

This means that the Treasury is quite sensitive to Fed rate hikes. Any Fed rate hikes to levels above the inflation rate would mean the Treasury needs to refinance trillions of dollars of outstanding debt at much higher interest rates, and increase the government’s annual interest expense. If the Treasury’s debt consisted more of 10-year, 20-year, 30-year, and even hypothetical 50-year or 100-year securities, then their interest rates wouldn’t be too sensitive to Fed rate hikes. But alas, their average duration is about 65 months, including trillions in T-bills and T-notes, and so they are quite sensitive to rate hikes.

This makes it pretty hard for the Fed to sharply and meaningfully raise short-term interest rates (even if they felt the need to quell inflation) without blowing out the government’s fiscal deficit.

![Yield Curve Control](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-ycc-tweet.png)

In summary, high levels of reverse repo activity are likely to persist for as long as debt ceiling issues exist. When/if the debt ceiling is resolved and the Treasury is allowed to issue a lot more debt (likely in December 2021, unless they just get another brief extension), a large portion of their net new debt will probably be short/medium duration, and they will suck some money out of the $1.5 trillion reverse repo facility. Reverse repo usage should top out at that point, and would go down if the Treasury aggressively fills up its TGA.

To me, the Fed tapering asset purchases isn’t that interesting at first. What’s interesting to me is what happens several months later if the Fed tapers for a while to the point of stopping their asset purchases. Overall, I continue to expect the 2020s decade to mostly consist of Treasury yields being below the prevailing inflation rate. The precise difference between yields and inflation will naturally change over time.

## Bitcoin Note

Bitcoin has consolidated after reaching new all-time highs. The long-term log chart remains very constructive:

![BTC Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-btc-price.png)

*Chart Source: [Blockchain.com](https://www.blockchain.com/charts/market-price)*

On-chain indicators still look reasonably favorable for the next several months as well, indicating a relatively tight amount of liquid supply, as many coins are locked up in cold storage by longer-term holders who aren’t selling at current levels, and indeed are still dollar-cost averaging in.

The wild card is that as futures become a larger part of the market, it contributes to speculation and rehypothecation and various short-term price spikes or dips. Ultimately it is spot-buying and spot-selling that drives the long-term price, while leveraged traders throw all sorts of spikes and dips into the price action along the way.

There were three significant items of fundamental interest for bitcoin over the past couple weeks, in my view.

**1) Bitcoin Mining Colocation News**

The Bitcoin Mining Council is the large trade association for bitcoin miners, and they issued their Q3 updates for the sustainability of the bitcoin mining ecosystem. They also [livestreamed a briefing](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JPai5uoN2tg) which included executives giving presentations and a section for analyst Q&A.

Ever since I published my [bitcoin energy article](https://www.lynalden.com/bitcoin-energy/), I’ve been on the lookout for signs of utility colocation, referring to instances where bitcoin miners are located on the site of utility-scale energy production. Many utilities have always-on sources of power generation (nuclear, hydro, geo) and variable-on sources that are outside of their control (wind, solar), and they have to match this supply with fluctuating demand or “load” from their electrical grid. Any amount that they produce that there is no demand for gets curtailed, wasted. That happens a lot around the margins, and it’s not ideal for the environment, it’s not ideal for utility profits, and it’s not ideal for consumer electricity prices when energy is produced and yet wasted.

Bitcoin miners are a unique electricity customer because they can go on-site of production, and they can deal with intermittent power as long as they have super-cheap electricity rates. If they go on-site with a power generator, the bitcoin miner can serve as a load balancer. Being on-site eliminates transmission losses and duplicate facility security personnel.

During his presentation, the CEO of Marathon Digital Holdings (MARA), Fred Thiel, said that he thinks power companies will eventually be some of the largest bitcoin mining host facilities. He used the example of a nuclear power facility- an always-on source of power that is hard to turn up or down to match fluctuating demand, and that can benefit from on-site bitcoin miners as load balancers to profitably soak up any excess supply.

In the analyst Q&A section, I asked if utilities are aware of this and looking into it, or if the topic is still mainly on the miner’s side (I know from private miners that there are some utility colocations, but was not sure of the scale since it’s just kind of starting to happen). Mr. Thiel responded that his company (the largest bitcoin miner by market capitalization) talked to four of the top six power groups in North America and those groups now have dedicated teams looking into bitcoin mining as a load balancer, are in discussions with miners and hosting providers, and have VC investments in the space. Jaime Leverton, CEO of Hut 8 Mining (HUT) nodded her head as Mr. Thiel described that as well. Sheldon Bennett, CEO of DMG Blockchain Solutions, said his company did some tests where they deployed mining containers to utilities as load balancers, and that the results were very positive. He agreed with Mr. Thiel that utility companies will increasingly get into this space.

In my view that’s very constructive and specific news. The most efficient bitcoin mining over time, in terms of environmental friendliness and cost effectiveness, would be on-site load balancing with power generators, which soaks up wasted/curtailed energy. This maximizes the profit of renewable-energy utilities (sources that are always-on or variable-on outside of the utility’s control) and minimizes the electricity costs of miners since they are bargaining for literally-wasted energy.

In related news, I was interested in [this article](https://bitcoinmagazine.com/business/bitcoin-mining-fits-texas-energy-grid) about using bitcoin miners as load balances for the electrical grid of Texas.

**2) Expansion of Bitcoin-Collateralized Loans**

Post Oak Motor Cars and NYDIG [announced a large partnership](https://nydig.com/news/post-oak-motor-cars-and-nydig-announce-partnership-integrating-bitcoin-services-throughout-the-luxury-auto-dealer):

> **HOUSTON, TX & NEW YORK, NY – October 28, 2021** – Post Oak Motor Cars (“POMC”), owned by businessman Tilman Fertitta, today announced a partnership with NYDIG, a leading bitcoin company, to integrate bitcoin into the Houston-based luxury auto dealer’s business operations. This partnership will allow POMC customers to purchase luxury automobiles financed through loans secured by bitcoin, enable POMC to use its appreciated bitcoin treasury holdings to finance a new expansion, and enable employees to allocate a portion of their paychecks to bitcoin – all powered by NYDIG’s secure, regulated, full-stack platform.

This is noteworthy for a few reasons. Let’s back up a minute and talk about a brief history of financing in the bitcoin ecosystem.

For a while, people would just buy bitcoin, maybe trade it or buy some things with it, and sell some if it went up a lot in price. Bitcoin mining businesses were generally small and without much access to capital, and would also have to regularly sell the bitcoin they generated in order to pay for their ongoing expenses.

Firms like NYDIG and some others over the past few years, however, have developed financing methods for individuals and companies, now that bitcoin has become an asset of institutional scale and recognition. For people unfamiliar with NYDIG, it’s an institutional-grade custodian and very large investor in many bitcoin-related companies that was founded in 2017. Basically, it’s one of the backbones of the institutional-grade bitcoin landscape today. Large pools of capital such as insurance companies, banks, corporations, endowments, pensions, and family offices that want bitcoin exposure for themselves or their clients often buy and custody it through NYDIG. (Indeed, the first pension fund in the US to invest in bitcoin and ether, [did so through NYDIG a couple weeks ago](https://nydig.com/news/houston-firefighters-relief-and-retirement-fund-announces-bitcoin-purchase)).

NYDIG has various lending services where a large holder of bitcoins can borrow dollars against their bitcoins as collateral, with a low loan-to-value ratio. Earlier this year, NYDIG also [invested in Unchained Capital](https://nydig.com/news/unchained-capital-announces-25m-series-a-led-by-nydig-and-stone-ridge-holdings-group), a firm that also does conservative bitcoin-collateralized loans, and NYDIG also serves as Unchained’s primary source of capital for those loans. NYDIG and some other companies also provide loans to bitcoin mining companies that are collateralized by their equipment (like ASIC miners) or the bitcoin they hold. This has a couple implications.

One implication is that due to NYDIG, similar lenders, and access to public markets, many bitcoin mining companies have access to affordable capital and are now HODLing bitcoins that they generate, rather than regularly selling them to the market. These miners denominate their liabilities in fiat currency (equity or debt or both) and retain their bitcoins into a larger and larger stack on their balance sheet.

A second implication is that large individual holders of bitcoin (individuals or companies) don’t have to sell their coins when they want some liquidity; they can take out a loan with a low loan-to-value ratio collateralized by some of their bitcoin stack. It’s kind of like taking a home equity loan out of a house, except it’s done with less leverage because bitcoin is a lot more volatile than a house. This helps those large holders avoid taxable events from selling coins, or from missing out on potential ongoing price appreciation, when they need some liquidity for a few months or years.

This announcement with Post Oak Motor Cars is another example of that, and an interesting one since it has multiple parts. This luxury auto dealership has bitcoins on its balance sheet as a corporate holding, and will borrow against some of them for a loan to expand their underlying business. In addition, their customers who have bitcoins can take out a loan against their coins to buy a car from the dealership. Lastly, their employees can automatically put a percentage of their paycheck into bitcoin. Basically, we’re seeing both retail-level lending and company-level lending, collateralized by bitcoins.

For many owners of bitcoin, this represents a way to hold their bitcoins for a very, very long time, while still getting liquidity when necessary for large purchases. Bitcoin-collateralized lending has been happening for several years but only in the past couple years has it really built momentum and scale, and companies that are otherwise unrelated to bitcoin (like a car dealership) are starting to do it.

The risk is that if this industry gets big enough, it could destabilize the bitcoin market. For example, if bitcoin’s price rapidly falls 75%, many borrowers may need to liquidate at once. That can cause a further cascade down in price to even lower levels. However, unlike DeFi protocols, liquidation decisions for these types of bitcoin-collateralized loans are generally not automated, so a human risk manager can make a decision about liquidating or not liquidating on a price crash, and give the borrower more time to put up additional collateral.

I don’t know the current size of this bitcoin-collateralized loan market but from putting together publicly-available numbers, it’s very small compared to bitcoin’s market capitalization, and the industry held up well during the sharp downward price spike of March 2020. I think it’s an industry with a lot of growth ahead of it.

Basically, the maturation of the lending market from NYDIG and other entities around bitcoin is an interesting development for large holders, and can reduce selling pressure. This bitcoin-collateralized lending market with maybe 10-40% LTV ratios is different from the tens of thousands of degenerate smaller short-term traders that load up on 2x or 5x or 10x leverage on crypto exchanges with callable margin debt and then get liquidated days later.

Bitcoin is currently in its longest stretch, about 19 months and counting, of there being fewer and fewer bitcoins on crypto exchanges, rather than more. This is partly due to miners HODLing, partly due to institutions buying long-term positions, and partly due to retail savers dollar-cost averaging into bitcoin. These coins find their way to cold storage where they are locked away, rather than held on exchanges. The orange line represents the number of bitcoins on exchanges, and we can see the structural trend change since March 2020:

![BTC Exchanges](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-btc-exchanges.png)

Since bitcoin is digitally auditable in real time, easily liquidated, and can even be held in multi-signature custody accounts, it’s oddly well-suited for collateralization, with the exception of its volatility.

Watching increasingly sophisticated lending markets develop around bitcoin as collateral is kind of like watching a developing country start to get banking access to its people, like what has been happening in India over the past decade. A little bit of leverage from nearly-zero to moderate levels in a developing country is often a big engine of economic growth; it allows people to buy homes, start businesses, etc. Access to credit is important. It’s only in the late stages that it starts to get concerning with high leverage ratios and financialization of the economy, as we see in developed markets today.

The same idea will likely hold true for bitcoin lending. At first, access to better credit products is good. It results in longer holding times, fewer taxable events, less selling pressure, and people and businesses can put their bitcoins to work. However, if theoretically more entrants come into the industry and these bitcoin-collateralized loans become a very large percentage of bitcoin’s market capitalization, then I can foresee a potential scenario at some point in the future where it leads to a massive liquidation event. The market is nowhere near that point yet, and I don’t think the sophisticated players like NYDIG would get caught up in that type of event down the line, because they would likely be monitoring the size and risk of the industry carefully. But some other players certainly might get caught up in it.

**3) India Rolls Back Proposed Crypto Ban**

Officials from the India’s Ministry of Finance [stated](https://www.businesstoday.in/crypto/story/law-to-regulate-cryptocurrencies-likely-by-budget-310439-2021-10-26) that they would probably put forth legislation in 2022 to regulate cryptocurrencies as an asset class. This is positive for Bitcoin, Ethereum, and other protocols, because India’s prior stance was to try to outright ban cryptocurrency.

India is a large and fast-growing market that does not currently have a cryptocurrency regulation framework in place. Back in 2018, India’s central bank banned financial companies from interacting with cryptocurrencies, but that was shot down by the country’s Supreme Court. Then in 2019, the Ministry of Finance proposed a draft bill that would ban cryptocurrencies if passed, and it made a lot of global headlines (especially among bitcoin bears and crypto bears), but that was never passed into law, and the country’s politicians just kept exploring the issue and kind of flip-flopping on it, as cryptocurrencies took off in the country to a significant degree. This news in late 2021 seems to further improve the country’s steps towards accepting cryptocurrencies, although still there is nothing tangible.

This shows the dilemma that countries face when trying to ban bitcoin or cryptos. If they do ban it, they can miss out on innovation and tax revenue of companies and trading gains. Politicians within a country often have differing views on the topic. Officials in India clarified that they were mainly concerned with protecting investors from exit scams, rug pulls, ICOs for scam coins, etc.

These concerns are valid and unfortunately scams are very common in the cryptocurrency/altcoin ecosystem. But that’s where regulation rather than broad bans come into play, so that companies can build bitcoin-based products, can work with stablecoins or smart contracts, and deal with other real innovations while still having consumer protection.

In related news, a commission in China is [reportedly seeking public opinion](https://cointelegraph.com/news/china-s-state-planning-agency-calls-for-public-opinion-on-bitcoin-mining-ban) on crypto mining. I don’t know if anything will come of it but it’s interesting as it could potentially reverse or limit their prior ban.

China has banned cryptocurrencies multiple times over the past decade, but never with much effect, and it remained the dominant jurisdiction for cryptocurrency mining for years. However in mid-2021, they took a harsher stance on it and more effectively banned crypto mining and crypto exchanges. Bitcoin still exists peer-to-peer and in small miners using VPNs there, but the big miners had to move themselves or sell their machines abroad. North America and Kazakhstan took most of the market share from the Chinese bitcoin mining exodus, and it has been very profitable so far.

Now that they’ve lost their dominant market share and have seen others profit from it, will China get FOMO and walk back their ban, or will they leave it in place to protect their upcoming central bank digital currency from competition? I don’t know but I’m watching to see if anything changes. Even if they do change it back the damage is probably done; if I was a bitcoin mining company, I’d rather stay in a more stable jurisdiction.

## Ethereum Note

Ether touched new highs a couple days ago, similarly to how bitcoin touched new highs a little over a week before that.

![Ethereum Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-ethusd.png)

I remain tactically bullish on ether in terms of price action despite some misgivings about the long-term risks and use-cases. My tactical bullishness is in part based on the Ethereum 2.0 lockup contract that continues to soak up a ton of ether (up to over 8 million now, which can’t come out), and as a consequence of that, ether continues to quickly get drawn down from exchanges even a bit faster than bitcoins are getting drawn down from exchanges. It’s a well-engineered supply squeeze.

Longer-term, whenever Ethereum 2.0 is launched and those 8+ million coins are released, it’s hard to say what will happen to the price. I wouldn’t be particularly bullish beyond that point. Plus there is still a lot of execution risk with Ethereum’s transition from proof-of-work to proof-of-stake, and proof-of-stake comes with centralization risks and a more complex security profile (as Solana’s day-long outage last month demonstrated). But until then, it remains a tactically bullish speculation in my view.

On one hand, the Ethereum ecosystem has hit a rather striking amount of adoption among influencers. Snoop Dog with 19 million Twitter followers, for example, has a crypto punk as his profile picture. A number of other rappers and celebrities have done that as well.

On the other hand, this week also had some messy news for the protocol. The Cream DeFi protocol, which runs on Ethereum, had $130 million drained from it in an exploit. In other words, a poorly-written smart contract was taken advantage of, which happens regularly. Whenever you put coins into a DeFi protocol to earn interest, you are risking a potential total loss from someone exploiting the code. This protocol previously had two other multi-million dollar exploits, but this one was by far the biggest.

There was also some scammy NFT trading. There was a headline that a crypto punk NFT was bought for $532 million, which would have technically made it the most-valuable art sale (digital or physical) in history, surpassing the 2017 sale price of da Vinci’s *Salvator Mundi* at $450 million. But [upon analysis](https://twitter.com/bertcmiller/status/1453876703273627656) from multiple sources, it turned out that someone likely “sold” the NFT to himself from one blockchain address to another using a massive flash loan (a loan that is near-instantly paid back). It was a fake and inflated transaction, in other words. NFT volumes on OpenSea are lower in recent weeks than their August 2021 peak.

While the NFT craze has caught on among certain groups of influencers, a big concern that many people have pointed out is that NFT sets are easy to manipulate the price for, especially in a broadly speculative environment of negative real interest rates, meme stocks, meme coins, and SPACs with no revenue. Since they are non-fungible and trade for large amounts of money, people can sell NFTs between their own accounts or between close contacts at ever-higher prices until they convince other people to buy in at high prices, and then sell some of their NFTs at those inflated prices to those new entrants, who thought they were buying into a legitimate market pricing environment. I don’t know to what extent that is happening in the industry but this big $532 million self-sale with a flash loan doesn’t have good optics.

**Meme Coin Speculation**

Back in spring 2021, there was rampant speculation among crypto tokens. Bitcoin had reached a local top and was consolidating, while altcoins soared across the board, with Dogecoin taking the prize as the biggest meme, pumped by Elon Musk on twitter. It soared thousands of percentage points up to a huge spike and then crashed, and still is trading at below half of its all-time high value despite the subsequent recovery in bitcoin and ether to fresh all-time highs.

Lately, a newer dog-themed coin called Shiba Inu soared in price and briefly surpassed Dogecoin in market capitalization (although it has not come close to Dogecoin’s all-time high market capitalization from back in the spring). Shiba Inu seems like it may have topped a few days ago in a parabolic spike, but we’ll see in the coming weeks if that was its final top or not. While Dogecoin is based on Litecoin (which is based on Bitcoin), Shiba Inu is an ERC-20 token that runs on Ethereum.

Days later, a yet-newer dog-themed coin called Floki Inu, named after Elon’s dog, had a parabolic price spike as well. It reached a much smaller market capitalization than Shiba Inu.

Also in recent days, a token called MANA went parabolic. MANA is an ERC-20 token for Decentraland, which was launched in 2017 as an initial-coin offering and went live to the public as an open game in 2020. Facebook (FB) recently announced it is going to [change its name to “Meta”](https://www.cnbc.com/2021/10/28/facebook-changes-company-name-to-meta.html) in order to focus on virtual reality and similar themes, which has rekindled speculation in similar virtual worlds like Decentraland. MANA seems like it hit a big top and is rolling over, but it’s unclear if that was just a local top or if that is all it will get.

Unlike April/May 2021, these token bubbles here in October 2021 have been pretty specific. There were layers of dog meme-of-meme-of-meme tokens soaring, each to smaller and less liquid market caps than the one prior, and now MANA has soared. The bubble-price-spike coins from that April/May 2021 period including memes like Dogecoin as well as older coins like Ethereum Classic, Bitcoin Cash, and Litecoin remain well off their highs with no new pump so far here in the autumn.

Investors should be very cautious about these sorts of tokens. The majority of the crypto tokens out there have a history of big illiquid spikes, enriching the creators if they sell into that spike, followed by a bust in which the token price often never recaptures its prior high for buyers, even years later.

I remain tactically and structurally bullish on bitcoin, and tactically bullish on ether with an outlook into Q1 2022, and have no position in any of these pump-and-dump crypto meme speculation spikes.

## Tencent Update

I’m doing one more update on Chinese tech stocks for now, to check if this big potential bottoming process is holding or not. So far it is.

Tencent’s (TCEHY) price chart is one of the better ones in my view, and the underlying business has some fundamental resilience against inflation, being a mostly online company:

![TCEHY Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-tcehy-chart.png)

The price is currently a bit over $60, and the low from over two months ago was $56. That $56 level is a reasonable invalidation point for risk management purposes if the stock starts moving in the wrong direction, firmly breaking below that level. Unless or until it does that, I’m treating it as a likely bottom.

If someone views Tencent as a bullish position trade with a stop loss at just under $56 (vs the current price of just under $61), then in most circumstances they risk about 8% of their capital, for a stock that could go up 50% or more in the next two years.

**Company Overview**

Tencent has the largest set of social networks in China, one of the top two mobile payment systems in China, a vast gaming conglomerate that serves customers in China and around the world, the second largest cloud computing provider in China, various content subscription packages, and a host of equity investments in tech/media companies both inside of China and around the world.

![TCEHY Segment Revenue](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-10-31-tcehy-segments.png)

*Chart Source: Tencent Q2 2021 Results Presentation*

While Facebook (FB) is renaming itself “Meta”, it seems to me that Tencent is better-positioned than Facebook/Meta for the now-popular “metaverse” theme, if the metaverse refers to shared virtual reality with tokens and digital property and such.

This is because while both Facebook and Tencent operate massive social networks, Tencent is also one of the top video game publishers in the world, with a vast army of development studios around the world. That’s actually the perfect blend for a corporation to have if it wants to create shared virtual worlds: social networks and video game publishing. Tencent already has NFTs as well, but due to China’s crackdown on speculation, they’ve shifted to referring to them as “digital collectibles” which is probably a better term anyway.

Throughout the past year, China’s government has cracked down on its big internet companies, ranging from antitrust issues (Tencent having a huge share of the music streaming industry) to youth behavior (strict controls on video game time among children), to payment systems, to how other companies can do an IPO, and this has dented most of the major internet companies in terms of fundamentals and valuation.

The largest companies, like Tencent and Alibaba (BABA) received tiny fines but have pledged very large amounts of money for social programs to support Xi Jinping’s common prosperity theme. Tencent plans to set aside the equivalent of $15 billion over the coming years for this, and clearly sums of money that large are to “get in line” with the government’s expectations rather than a voluntary decision.

**Risks**

As a Chinese company, Tencent has more major tail risks than an American or European firm, at least for American investors. We can divide these risks into a few categories.

*Chinese Regulatory Risk*

This past year was a huge example of the jurisdictional risk that companies in China face. Xi Jinping, President of China, has enough authoritarian power to decimate any Chinese company he wants, even going so far as to make their CEO, founder, or chairman kind of disappear for a while, to put it lightly. Apart from his Belt and Road Initiative, one of Xi’s big themes in recent years is the idea of shared prosperity, which has meant cracking down on powerful corporations to ensure that no tech titans have more power than the CCP and that the CCP’s policies are based mainly around the middle class.

Some of the reforms made sense (Alibaba and Tencent did use a variety of anti-competitive practices as part of what was/is basically a duopoly, which needed some cleaning up), but the manner in which the CCP carried those out is beyond the norm by western standards, and with few or no legal checks and balances.

Jack Ma, who was at the heart of this crackdown, recently traveled outside of China for the first time and visited eastern Europe, where Alibaba has a large presence. Overall, with energy shortages and real estate problems, the optics are starting to look like China is done with most of the tech-based crackdowns for this cycle and focusing its attention elsewhere, but there’s no guarantee that this is the case.

The main goal of the Chinese government seems to have been to reign in, rather than destroy the investability of, their largest companies. If that ends up not being the case, and we see renewed crackdowns on them, then the thesis could shift.

*Accounting/Legal Risk*

Chinese stocks held by foreigners are generally held through Cayman-based legal structures. Many American companies do this as well, but it’s more serious for China because it’s the main way for foreigners to have access to their shares.

In an extreme case, China could declare that those companies have no legal right to any Chinese assets anymore, effectively divesting foreigners.

In addition, investors must accept Hong Kong accountants for auditing these Chinese companies. I think the risk of a major accounting problem for a company the size of Tencent is relatively low, but it’s higher than for American or European firms.

*US Regulatory Risk*

On the other side of this, the United States government could delist certain Chinese stocks, and can outright ban Americans from owning certain Chinese stocks.

I’m not concerned about delisting, because Tencent is not listed on a major US-based exchange in the first place. Its dollar-based ADR trades over-the-counter as TCEHY. And despite being unlisted, it has historically traded at a higher earnings multiple than Alibaba, which is listed on the NYSE as BABA. Listing or not listing on US exchanges is not really a big deal for companies of this size, which don’t need western capital anyway.

A Chinese stock does not need to be listed on a major exchange in order for people to invest in it, or for large ETFs to hold it, although it does make it easier for some individual investors. I can’t hold Tencent in M1 Finance, for example, because they don’t have OTC securities available. But I can hold it through my other brokerages.

I do consider *banning* a risk, obviously. If Americans are *legally banned* from owning certain or all Chinese stocks, regardless of the exchange, then that’s clearly a problem. Investors would have to delist at an inopportune time, and valuations would likely take a hit.

*Keeping Risks in Perspective*

A lot of people are familiar with these risks when investing in Chinese companies, which is why some investors choose not to do so, and that makes sense. Sentiment is extremely low for Chinese stocks right now after a year of regulatory crackdown.

It’s important to keep in mind though, that many of these risks also extend globally due to how interlinked China, the United States, and other countries are. Many Chinese companies own US subsidiaries, and many US companies have large presences in China.

If China and the United States go into an outright cold war at some point, perhaps due to military action regarding Taiwan or some other event, then it’s not just the Chinese stocks that are at risk for foreign investors. Large US companies that make up big percentages of the S&P 500, such as Apple (AAPL) and Tesla (TSLA) and Nike (NKE) have supply chains and consumer sales points there. A disorderly decoupling between the countries would severely damage the S&P 500, including many mega-caps in it. The device you’re reading this report on is made with many Chinese parts and may have been assembled there as well.

We can already see this influence. Basketball players and pro wrestlers are often afraid to publicly say anything negative about China in regard to Uighurs or Taiwan or other topics related to human rights in general, and mighty Hollywood studios are fearful to make movies that negatively depict China, because the movies will be banned but more impactfully, the actors will be banned and the studios will be banned.

Overall, when I think about Chinese-related risks as a whole, it’s broader than just whether or not to own some Chinese stocks. It’s about how the relationship between China and the US could effect *everything*.

That is why I don’t mind owning relatively small positions in Chinese stocks when the setup looks attractive. My current view is to shorten my time horizon a bit for Chinese stocks. Normally I invest with a 3-5 year time horizon or longer, but for Chinese stocks I limit my view to about 18-24 months now, and thus consider them to be position trades. That doesn’t mean I can’t hold them longer but it means I’m a bit more focused on timing with these holdings.

While I am structurally bullish on energy and am not selling any oil and gas company shares, I would be cautious of getting carried away with price spikes like this. Ultimately, how cold the winter is and how much the wind blows will dictate whether natural gas prices keep rising in Europe or if this is a major top, but ultimately I’m looking out longer-term, since oil and gas stocks are not just priced on current-month futures, but on investors’ judgements about oil and gas prices over the next several years.

The market has priced in near-term energy shortages, but energy prices for futures contracts a few years out remain cheap. The consensus view is that these energy shortages are transitory. My base case is that the shortages are more structural and that this will likely be an ongoing issue, with peaks and troughs but that overall the 2020s will have higher average energy prices than the 2010s did.

Still, I’m not pounding the table on the energy trade at the moment until more data come in. I’m keeping my existing holdings, looking to add more on dips, and currently examining other equity areas that already fell a lot and have washed out sentiment, like Chinese stocks that nobody wants, or US healthcare stocks that nobody wants, and so forth.
