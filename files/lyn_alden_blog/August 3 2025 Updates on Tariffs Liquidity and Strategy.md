## Macro View

**Tariff Update**

In my prior report, I flagged tariffs as the key risk to monitor and took a more bearish stance than average.

> Overall, I’m a bit more risk-averse than average over the next couple of months, or until there is more clarity on August tariffs. While I’m not making taxable trades in portfolios around that concern, and I’m generally quite heavily invested in assets across the board, I am building up some cash around the margins and being slow to deploy to new ideas, to top off my dry powder for the moment.
>
> -July 20, 2025 premium report

I continue to hold that view based on data over the past two weeks. It currently looks like US tariffs are settling on average in the 15-20% range, which on $3.2 trillion in annual goods imports is $480 billion to $640 billion in new annual taxes unless volume reductions occur.

With import prices (which are measured pre-tariff) not going down, it means the majority of the new taxes are being paid by a combination of American consumers and American businesses. It can still negatively affect foreign exporters, though, in the form of reduced volumes as overall economic growth decreases.

Now, this is in the context of $7.3 trillion in annual federal expenditure and $5.3 trillion in annual federal receipts, or about a $2 trillion fiscal deficit on a $30 trillion nominal GDP economy. So, that $480-$640 billion annual new set of taxes is material but takes time to have its effects felt and is still just one variable among many to consider.

**US Business Cycle**

The Bureau for Labor Statistics released July nonfarm payroll numbers, and they were rather disastrous. The consensus expectation was for 106k net new jobs but the number came in light at 73k. That alone isn’t great, but they also revised the prior two months downward by 258k jobs, which nearly eliminated all estimated job gains over that period. Outside of the pandemic lockdowns, that was the biggest downward revision in decades. President Trump fired the head of the BLS after the report came out.

Ernie Tedeschi, the former chief economist at the White House Council of Economic Advisors, shared a multi-decade chart about it:

![Downward Revision](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-revision-chart.png)

Weekly initial claims remain quite contained, however. Thus it still looks more like a deceleration than an acute employment problem.

![Initial Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-initial-claims.png)

The ISM manufacturing purchasing manager’s index also came in weak for last month. Manufacturing prices are rising while overall activity and employment in the sector is falling.

![ISM PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-ism-pmi.png)

This is a case where reading the [actual report](https://www.ismworld.org/supply-management-news-and-reports/reports/ism-report-on-business/pmi/july/) is useful because the ISM PMI survey includes manufacturing business responses. Most of them list tariffs as their key concern.

We can also see that overall spending on the construction of manufacturing facilities is decelerating:

![Construction Spending](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-manufacturing-construction-spending.png)

The baseline used to be around $80 billion/year spent on manufacturing construction. Overall industrial production was flat in the United States, meaning that $80 billion/year is roughly the ongoing replacement cost of the existing manufacturing base. During 2022 and 2023 there was a $160 billion uptick in spending on manufacturing facilities, partly because the Inflation Reduction Act provided subsidies for the build-out of semiconductor manufacturing facilities and certain other industries that were identified as being of strategic importance. That is showing signs of rolling over.

In context, it would require trillions of dollars in new manufacturing investment to relocate trillions of dollars worth of goods imports.

**Liquidity**

The Fed’s FOMC meeting on Wednesday came in slightly more hawkish than market expectations. While the market didn’t expect an interest rate cut in July, they were leaning toward a cut happening in September. Chairman Powell’s hawkish remarks during the press conference dampened expectations for that September cut, and so the market quickly reduced the probability of a cut. The dollar index spiked on the headlines.

However, when Friday’s weak jobs report came out with all of those downward revisions, the market immediately re-priced in an expectation of a September rate cut and sold the dollar index off again, basically undoing all of Wednesday’s action.

![DXY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-dxy.png)

The Treasury announced plans to refill its cash account to $850 billion by the end of September from the current level of $370 billion.

There’s about $200 billion in excess liquidity in the reverse repo facility that this refilling can come from. Beyond that, it’s a pretty negative scenario for overall liquidity, and around the margins paints a not-so-great outlook for equities in the coming months. My base case is that they will undershoot their expected refilling speed and still be below $850 billion by end-of-September, but we’ll see.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin has been consolidating/correcting since it soared to new highs in mid-July. Once again, I think that’s healthy and helps take steam off the market.

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-btc.png)

There continues to be some selling pressure and profit taking by long-term holders as there is every bull cycle, which offsets some of the ongoing accumulation by corporations.

![HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-hodl-wave.png)

The market recently [made headlines](https://cointelegraph.com/news/early-bitcoin-investor-sells-80000-btc-galaxy-digital) because 80,000 very old coins (from 2011) rapidly sold to market (performed by Galax Digital), and it barely dropped the price. This showed that overall liquidity is very high and able to absorb major selling pressure.

Some analysts went and extrapolated that to what might happen in a bear market, and I think that’s a mistake. In a bear market, overall liquidity would probably be way worse, and sales of that scale would move the price a lot more.

Overall, I continue to have a favorable cyclical and structural view of bitcoin price action, albeit with plenty of allowance for volatility along the way. Most on-chain indicators continue to look healthy relative to prior multi-year tops, and the one yellow flag that gives me pause is the recent proliferation of altcoin corporate treasury strategies which points to some froth in the market.

## Strategy’s Shift from Convertibles to Preferreds

Strategy (MSTR, formerly MicroStrategy) reported Q2 earnings on Thursday, which they described as their most important earnings call in company history, since they also more fully laid out their longer-term plans. I was able to participate as one of the eight analysts to ask live questions during the Q&A section.

Back in August 2020, the software company became the first company on a major US exchange to start accumulating bitcoin on its balance sheet.

I added it to the Newsletter model portfolio that month in August 2020, and then later added it to the No Limits model portfolio in 2021, and have been holding it in those portfolios ever since with some occasional rebalancings. Since they adopted their bitcoin strategy, they have become basically the best-performing major US stock, even outpacing Nvidia (NVDA).

![MSTR Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-mstr-performance.png)

Notably, the market capitalization increased from under $1.5 billion to well over $100 billion during this time. So, in addition to performing well on a per-share basis, they also grew the absolute size of the company significantly, which has given them a strong ability to attract and retain executive talent and to operate with economies of scale.  They’ve joined the Nasdaq 100 and might eventually be added to the S&P 500. Ironically, they’re already larger than about three quarters of the S&P 500 by market capitalization at current prices.

Along the way, the company has gradually shifted its capital structure.

**First Stage: Cash to Bitcoin**

The company previously had a lot of cash and no debt, but its revenue and income were stagnant in the face of large competition. As major fiscal stimulus and money-printing began in March 2020 in response to the pandemic and economic lockdowns, the company was (rightfully) concerned about the purchasing power of its cash.

They explored their options and by August they had settled on a decision to shift much of it over to bitcoin. They announced the plan to their shareholders along with a modified Dutch auction tender offer to buy up to $250 million worth of shares from investors. This provided a pathway for the portion of existing shareholders that didn’t agree with the new direction to exit gracefully, as new investors such as myself bought in.

**Second Stage: Converts and Other Credit**

By December 2020, they began their move into levered bitcoin, where they announced plans to issue convertible bonds to buy more bitcoin. Convertible bonds come with a lower coupon in exchange for the ability to convert to equity at a higher price level, which gives the bond buyer downward protection combined with some upward price participation.

At times, their equity would trade at a significant premium to its net asset value, so the company began issuing more equity to buy bitcoin. Prior to that, I had some risk concerns about the scale of their leverage strategy, but when they began issuing equity to buy more bitcoin, I perceived it as de-risking because it was increasing the asset side of the balance sheet relative to the credit side.

Throughout this time they issued some more traditional higher-yielding bonds, and also dabbled in a bitcoin-securitized loan.

The 2022/2023 bitcoin bear market was rough, but because the vast majority of the company’s debt had durations of 5 years or so, they were able to weather it with little problem.

In 2024, the company was the largest issuer of convertible bonds in the US market. The bull market in bitcoin re-awoke a ton of demand for their securities, so they had plenty to issue in order to meet that demand. Their overall leverage ratio remained modest due to the appreciation of their bitcoin stack.

**Third Stage: Preferred Stock**

Throughout 2025 their capital structure began shifting toward preferred stock. So far, they’ve IPO’d four different preferred stock offerings with different risk profiles. They’ve discussed the possibility of eventually retiring their rather small amount of convertible bonds and relying entirely on preferreds as their source of leverage going forward.

These preferreds can be thought of as a hybrid between an equity and a bond. They exist perpetually like an equity rather than having a due date (i.e. for the most part the principal is never owed back to the lenders), but they pay a high yield with more capped upside, like a bond, and are senior to the equity if there is a liquidation event.

Strife (STRF) pays a 10% dividend fixed yield and is the most senior in the capital structure after the convertible bonds. If the company misses dividend payments on it, those dividends accumulate, so other than in a liquidation event, the holders should get their dividends retroactively even after a severe bear market. It currently trades at a premium to the $100 face value, pushing the yield down to a still respectable 8.5%.

Stretch (STRC) was just launched and pays a variable yield which is currently 9%. It’s the next-most-senior, and has various monthly mechanisms in place to keep its price fixed near $100. If the price drops, they intend to increase the dividend. If the price goes up, they have mechanisms including reducing the yield to cap it. It’s currently trading slightly under the target value and thus has a yield of around 9.5%. This so far is their most popular offering and I expect it to grow in demand, because this goes after money markets. Like STRF, its dividends are accumulative if temporarily missed.

Strike (STRK) pays a lower 8% dividend yield but is convertible to equity if certain conditions are met. Thus, it comes with the lowest current yield but allows the investor to participate in more upside. Due to trading at a premium, it currently pays an effective yield of about 7.5% along with that upside price potential of a convert one day. Like STRF and STRC, its dividends are accumulative if temporarily missed.

Stride (STRD) is the junior preferred. It pays a 10% dividend yield and is lower in the capital structure and thus is more exposed to liquidation risk. Its dividends are *non-accumulative*, meaning if the company is unable to pay them during a severe bear market, they don’t have to make up those payments later. This is basically the non-investment grade higher-yield offering and currently yields around 11.9% due to trading at a discount.

**Transparency Updates**

The company has set new rules that dictate when they’ll be willing to issue new equity. Previously, it was entirely at management discretion, and now they intend to follow more of an algorithm, although they can change that algorithm over time as their assessment of market conditions evolves.

The company often (but not always) trades at a premium over its bitcoin value, especially during a bull market. Currently for example the enterprise value of the company is 1.66x the size of their bitcoin holdings, meaning they have a market to net asset value ratio “mNAV” of 1.66x. Because of this, they can issue new equity to buy bitcoin, and thus increase bitcoin per share in the process. However, shareholders aren’t generally thrilled when the company does that, since they get diluted. In two prior reports, I trimmed my MSTR position when it touched about 3x NAV during periods of euphoria, which were good trading decisions.

![MSTR Trimming](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-3-mstr-trimming.png)

Here’s their newly announced equity issuance plan:

-Above 4x mNAV they will actively issue equity to buy bitcoin.

-Between 2.5x mNAV and 4x mNAV they will opportunistically issue equity to buy bitcoin (kind of a gray zone).

-Between 1x and 2.5x mNAV, they will only issue equity to pay bond coupons or preferred dividends, not to buy bitcoin. This is more conservative than their prior approach.

-Under 1x mNAV, they will consider issuing credit to repurchase equity.

**My Thoughts**

-I think the new mNAV transparency plan makes sense, although I’m not really sure about 2.5x mNAV being the right threshold for opportunistic equity issuance. Lowering it to 2x would give them more flexibility. They could change it in the future, and it’s hard to say where the optimal thresholds are. I think their goal is to boost the mNAV with these more transparent levels, and with more restraint than before on when they will issue equity. We’ll see after a couple quarters how it’s working.

-The shift toward preferred equity overall gives them a more conservative capital structure. It spreads out their liabilities and makes them less catastrophic for the company if they miss a payment in a severe bear market scenario. It shifts the company toward a “bend, not break” outcome during all but the most catastrophic of bitcoin bear markets.

-Their prior usage of convertible debt as their primary source of leverage minimized their quarterly payment obligations to near-zero, but opened them up to potential lump-sum obligations at key times if a prolonged bear market occurs (which they managed well via long durations, and thus never ran into a problem with them). The new usage of preferred stock as leverage increases their quarterly payment obligations (which is not great), but eliminates lump-sum obligations (which is great), and allows them to temporarily or in some cases permanently (in STRD’s case) miss dividend payments without major issues that would come from missing a convertible bond coupon or running into refinancing issues with a convertible bond coming due. Thus, they have more ability to bend before breaking in an extreme adverse scenario.

-I think the STRC offering is going to be huge in terms of demand. It’s already the largest IPO of 2025 (for any stock in the market), and I expect it to grow pretty quickly. A dividend yield of over 9% with a mechanism to suppress volatility, and indirectly collateralized multiple times over with bitcoin, is a compelling offering compared to sub-4.5% yielding Tbills, money markets, and commercial paper. I think the fair yield is probably closer to 7% than the current level, so we’ll see if the market pushes it in that direction once it’s out for a while.

-If STRC ends up being huge, and they eventually retire/convert their remaining convertible bonds, it’ll indirectly make their most senior preferred stock, STRF, notably better than it is now. This is because the debt that’s more senior than STRF (the convertible bonds) will be gone, and most of the debt will be slightly junior to them (concentrated in STRC), which would make STRF heavily collateralized by the company’s bitcoin.

-I’m not a big fan of the STRD preferred. The high yield is nice and there’s a market for it, but being low on the capital stack and being subject non-cumulative dividends isn’t an attractive trade-off in my view. I’d rather sell cash-secured puts on MSTR stock to generate yield than hold STRD.

-The shift toward preferred stock as their primary source of leverage widens Strategy’s economic moat. Sometimes critics wonder if all of the copycat treasury companies popping up will dilute them and take market share. However, Strategy benefits from liquidity network effects and economies of scale. The stock is incredibly liquid, which by extension makes the stock’s option markets very liquid (which is much harder to do, since liquidity is fractured across all the different strike prices and expiration dates). Preferred stock is harder to issue than convertible bonds and requires greater scale, and thus gives them an additional differentiator relative to other companies deploying a bitcoin treasury strategy.

**Risk Analysis**

Most corporate bonds or preferred stocks are paid out of a company’s operating cash flow.

Strategy’s bonds and preferred stocks are different; they’re paid primarily out of the company’s ongoing ability to raise capital, and they’re indirectly backed by a tremendous amount of bitcoin. This changes their risk profile, in some ways for the better and in some ways for the worse, relative to most other companies.

What does a failure scenario for MicroStrategy stock and its preferreds look like? Three things likely have to happen together:

-First, bitcoin has to have a major bear market of like 80% and stay down there for a long time. This would dramatically increase the company’s debt/asset ratio (by dropping the asset side) and hurt investor confidence and demand for their various security offerings.

-Second, the company’s common stock mNAV has to compress to 1x or below and remain there as well. This would prevent them from issuing new equity without reducing their bitcoin-per-share. If they can’t issue new equity, it puts the dividends of the preferreds at risk, at least temporarily and perhaps permanently.

-Third, their various preferred stocks would have to sell off, creating unsustainably high yields such that issuing more preferreds to pay the dividends on outstanding preferreds would also be overly dangerous.

In that kind of scenario, they’d likely be skipping dividend payments, which hurts STRD the most since their dividends are non-accumulative. STRF, STRC, and STRK could also temporarily miss dividend payments, but they’d at least be made whole if bitcoin and the company’s equity eventually recovers. That’s why it’s more of a “bend, not break” risk model than it used to be, whereas missing a convertible bond payment would be a “break”.

Although the industry is currently in a bull market, I used my question in this week’s Strategy earnings call ([here’s a link](https://www.youtube.com/live/Jez7rIVeClE) to the full presentation and Q&A) to ask about their stress testing assumptions for bear market scenarios. Michael Saylor estimates that an 80% bitcoin drawdown wouldn’t impair their ability to pay dividends on their preferreds, and that something more like a 90% drawdown would probably be in play to start impairing their dividends. For context, the 2022 bear market where FTX imploded was slightly less than an 80% drawdown.
