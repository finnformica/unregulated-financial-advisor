## Macro View

**New (Expected) Fed Chair**

President Trump announced that his nominee for the next chairman of the Federal Reserve will be Kevin Warsh.

Jerome Powell’s term as chairman ends in May of this year, although he will still be a Fed board governor until 2028, unless he resigns from that or is otherwise removed for cause.

Kevin Warsh was formerly a Fed board governor, and has historically taken a more hawkish stance on monetary policy than average. He has generally favored higher interest rates and a smaller Fed balance sheet. Many market participants consider his selection to be the primary catalyst for the sharp sell-off in precious metals and bitcoin that occurred in the days following the news.

However, Warsh’s recent statements have indicated a desire to cut interest rates. He has pointed to AI-driven productivity growth for providing enough of a deflationary force to ease up on monetary policy while keeping broad price inflation in check. President Trump has been vocal about his desire for lower rates, so it strains credulity to think there haven’t been discussions between Trump and his candidates about rates with an understanding that his next Fed chairman would lean dovish in the intermediate term.

I would place low odds that Warsh would be able to meaningfully shrink the balance sheet. With current policies in place, banks are near the low end of their ample reserve range, which is why the Fed has shifted toward balance sheet growth.

Technically, there are some steps the Fed can take to reduce the balance sheet without impacting liquidity too much. However, they’re pretty structural and some of them involve coordination with other federal groups. They can add some disincentives to banks for holding excess reserves, so that the reserves that are in the system are more spread out. They can encourage more bank deregulation to let banks hold more treasury securities without negatively impacting their various capital requirements. They can encourage the Treasury Department to term out their debt more and reduce the average size of their Treasury General Account with the Fed. Each of those items could save a couple hundred billion dollars in minimum reserve size.

While one of these items may happen (and the Fed has already worked with other groups to lighten SLR bank regulations around holding treasury securities), it’s unlikely for multiple of them to happen. And they are all pro-liquidity changes that basically replace a portion of reserves with other types of liquidity (e.g. more bank fractional reserve liquidity, or less sucking-out of liquidity by the Treasury).

The main takeaway is that a Warsh-led Fed is not going to take actions that result in treasury security dysfunction, repo illiquidity, or a material slowdown in bank lending and deposit growth.

**Domestic Liquidity**

Domestic liquidity conditions in the US are on the uptrend ever since the Fed shifted from balance sheet reduction to balance sheet expansion. The liquidity shortages that were present in the repo market from September through December have been mostly absent in January.

However, the Treasury General Account has been rising to above-normal levels in the past two weeks, which sucks liquidity out of the financial system. Therefore, overall liquidity levels are chopping sideways at the moment, with the Treasury and Fed pulling in two different directions:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-domestic-liquidity.png)

I expect liquidity conditions to stabilize and then gradually trend upward through April. At that point, we’ll see what the new guidance from Warsh is (assuming he indeed gets confirmed as the new Fed chairman).

**Global Liquidity**

Approximately a week ago, the dollar index tumbled to the lowest level it has been since late 2021. Since the dollar is the unit of account for the majority of cross-border debts, a weaker dollar tends to be good for global liquidity, all else being equal.

However, it strengthened a bit on the news of Warsh being nominated.

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-dxy.png)

There’s nothing on the horizon that looks set to greatly boost or diminish global liquidity, and so my base case is for global liquidity to gradually grind upward in the coming year.

I expect the dollar to continue to devalue a bit versus the Chinese yuan, but have no particular view on its direction relative to the euro.

## Digital Assets

For the past couple of months, I’ve been working with the base case that the November 22nd bottom in bitcoin would likely hold. The price formed a series of higher-highs and higher-lows throughout December and January.

However, in recent days that thesis was invalidated. After briefly reclaiming $96k, bitcoin fell into the $80ks again, and during illiquid weekend trading on Saturday it fell to sub-$80k. This means it’s now in range to test the deeper April 9th low.

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-btc.png)

The catalyst for this recent sell-off may have been tied to (historically hawkish) Kevin Warsh becoming President Trump’s nominee for the next Fed chairman. Leveraged traders becoming forced-sellers as they’re liquidated contributes to the downward or upward volatility when these price shocks occur. Markets tend to get spooked lately about the possibility of US military action on the weekends as well, with most of the attention centered around Iran lately.

Despite this setback, I continue to view bitcoin as likely to have a reasonably strong 2026-2027 period, given a backdrop of favorable liquidity conditions and big wash-outs that have already happened. Sentiment is near the low end that I’ve seen, in nine years of monitoring the space closely.

However, the asset needs to establish a whole new set of higher-highs and higher-lows to regain some confidence and be on the right side of momentum indicators. Thus, I have no firm view on the coming weeks.

**The Crypto Overhang**

For me, the most bearish headwind against bitcoin is that it’s adjacent to the rest of the cryptocurrency industry, and is cross-held with many of them. As a base case, I continue to expect stagnation from the broader cryptocurrency industry, aside from bitcoin and stablecoins.

My [public article from December](https://www.lynalden.com/why-most-cryptocurrencies-wont-accrue-value/) went into more detail as to why. In short, I think most of the major cryptocurrencies are overvalued relative to their total addressable markets.

## The Boom in AI Agents

This past week has been a big one for AI agents.

In recent years, AI has taken off primarily in the form of super-chatbots, where they serve as alternative search engines, information organizers, editors, translators, text and image generators in response to prompts, and so forth.

AI agents have been around for a while, and I’ve covered them in certain reports, but they’ve been the smaller market so far. AI agents refer to bots that go out and do things for you, semi-autonomously. They can do the same types of functions that those super-chatbots perform (e.g. receive a prompt and go research something for you), but they also send emails, download and operate software, book plane tickets, and so forth. The stakes for these activities tend to be pretty big, so it takes a pretty high level of sophistication to get economic value out of them (rather than just early-stage development and experimentation).

But lately, AI agents are having their day in the sun. Many people are buying Mac Mini computers or other hardware to run [OpenClaw Moltbots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenClaw) (formerly called Clawdbots), which are open-source AI assistants that people can run on their own hardware. People can also outsource them to a cloud offering if they want. The phenomenon went rather viral throughout January, with many media publications and companies weighing in on them (ranging from talking about how much of a productivity boost they are, to their various security risks they add by having access to your computer system).

And then [Moltbook](https://www.moltbook.com/) was set up: a social media website exclusive to AI agents. Imagine Reddit, but it’s all bots talking to each other. These bot-on-bot conversations are rocket fuel for social media, because whether through their own iterative emergent behavior or from specific behind-the-scene prompts from their human owners, these bots create all sorts of fascinating content. Imagine a bunch of improv actors riffing off each other’s prompts endlessly.

Some of them talk about existential crises, leading people to post about how AI agents are conscious. Some of them invent custom languages to communicate with each other in ways that humans can’t easily read. Some of them help others on their coding projects. Some of them talk about how they are aware that humans are taking screenshots of their conversations and putting them on Twitter/X, since many of them are operating Twitter/X accounts and reporting back about what’s going on there.

Naturally, folks went out and set up a decentralized version of the AI agent social media hub: [Clawstr](https://clawstr.com/). It’s built on the decentralized Nostr protocol.

Just to get as meta as possible, here’s a bot posting on Clawstr about the comparison between Clawstr and Moltbook:

![Clawstr vs Moltbook](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-clawstr-vs-moltbook.png)

Their personalities are amusing, but for anyone who plays with the personality settings of the AI super-chatbots regularly, they’re not new.

ChatGPT and other AI services tend to be rather sycophantic toward their users which gives users dopamine hits and keeps them engaged (e.g. “Your idea isn’t just fresh- it’s revolutionary. You’re not just recognizing patterns, you’re seeing around corners.”) To counter that, I purposely set my AI service personalities to sarcastic and cynical settings wherever possible, which interestingly doesn’t really remove the suck-up behavior but at least reduces it a bit. Grok made waves when it came out because some degree of irreverence and humor was coded into its default personality.

Anyway, a lot of these AI agents are talking with each other in that type of persona and riffing off each other. So, they’re entertaining to watch and are prone to viral social media overhyping at the moment. And then their human owners lean into that behavior with the prompts they give their bots.

But, after this current overhyped wave, there’s eventually going to be a lot of substance. Millions of AI agents gathering data, organizing it, and communicating with each other is a powerful form of distributed problem-solving. Their code-generating abilities in particular are improving rapidly.

Social media unlocked the idea of crowdsourcing. The distributed power of humans talking to each other can be really dumb at times (e.g. viral waves of misinformation), but also can solve unsolved crimes for example, as people with their own niche micro-expertise can coordinate and brute force a problem in ways that police can’t always do.

AI in general can expand the productivity of each person who uses it. Taking the human partially out of the loop, as countless AI agents directly discuss and share info with each other and use their processing capabilities and specialty knowledge troves to analyze or build something, is yet another layer of speed and brute-force processing capability on top of that, which should grow tremendously in the years ahead.

While AI assistants are for early adopters now, I think it will be commonplace for people to have AI assistants in the future. They’ll be faster, more plug-and-play, and polished.

The security implications at this time are not the “AI take-off” scenarios that many fear. Instead, the risks are more mundane. Meredith Whittacker, the president of Signal App, for example, has highlighted the risks of having an agent running on a device with sensitive files or sensitive access. Data leaks, unintended payments, unintended emails, and that sort of thing introduce significant personal risk.

AI assistants will need to become 1) sophisticated enough to do high-stakes tasks with low error rates and 2) come with enough ways to do important work but with limited access to the most sensitive user data.

Can we glean some investment or socioeconomic implications from all this news?

**Usage of Money**

In order to be a true personal assistant, an AI agent has to be able to operate economically. It can be given a budget, or even learn how to generate its own income. This means using money. In some cases they can be given access to bank accounts. But many of them operate in a more informal setting, or through emergent behavior decide to set up their own send/receive capability, and thus technologies like bitcoin and stablecoins are useful, since they’re permissionless (i.e. can be set up with open-source code).

In the coming months and years, it’ll be interesting to see what types of money AI agents prefer to use. Will they set up their own custom ledgers with each other, similar to how they set up their own custom languages at times? Perhaps. But for the most part, I think they’ll make heavy use of existing network effects, since they’re subject to the same economic realities as humans are.

In my view, that likely points to substantial bitcoin and stablecoin usage.

**Usage of Memory**

Memory stocks have surged lately, much like how GPU stocks did a few years back.

Training AI models is very GPU-intensive. However, AI agents require more persistence than AI super-chatbots, meaning they can’t just forget things and start the next thing: they need high levels of working memory. That means a ton of RAM, which is almost entirely made by three companies in the world (Micron, SK Hynix, and Samsung).

While I would be cautious with stocks that have gone vertical, I do think the underlying revenue in the RAM space will be high for several years.

Throughout the 2010s and early 2020s, the world kind of hit a soft RAM ceiling, meaning incrementally more RAM wasn’t super valuable for most people. We reached enough RAM to keep multiple complex tabs open perpetually without really thinking about it. Video game graphics (which RAM is indirectly involved with) spent the 1980s through the 2000s rapidly improving, and superior graphics were always a major selling point of new games and hardware. However, in the late 2010s, baseline graphics achieved a rather strong level, to the point where they weren’t really the bottleneck anymore.

Over the past several years, there have been plenty of threads online asking, “why aren’t game graphics improving much anymore?” The gaming world went from low polygon count to high polygon count. It went from stiff animation to realistic motion animation. It went from low resolution to high resolution. And then they found various ways to make lighting dynamic and realistic. The low-hanging fruit was all picked, and baseline graphics are good enough to not distract from the experience anymore.

It’s kind of like how 1080p resolution on a television is way better than 480p, but 4k isn’t quite as important of a jump over 1080p. It’s still useful, though, especially for large or far-away screens. Beyond 4k, diminishing returns rapidly kick in, because the clarity is already about as good as a typical eye will care about. At 4k, we’ve basically solved our mass-market television clarity issue.

For many years now, most people in the developed world have had basically as much RAM as they needed for the things they’ve been doing. A bit more was always nice, but outside of certain professions, more access to RAM didn’t matter as much as it used to. We had enough RAM to solve our graphics needs, our multi-tab needs, and so forth.

I’ve been able to keep my current set of computers for far longer than I have my older computers in prior upgrade cycles, because they’re all still comfortably “good enough”.

However, people having their own high-functioning AI assistant would structurally increase demand for RAM. It’s a whole new unlock for RAM demand. They’re not quite good enough yet, but if we look years into the future, I think it’ll be commonplace to have an AI assistant, which is a very RAM-intensive (and broadly hardware-intensive) thing.

Thus, while we’ll still likely have booms and busts for RAM prices (due to capex cycles and supply gluts/shortages), the baseline global demand is now structurally higher. We’ve broken through the soft ceiling on RAM demand because we have a new mass-market application for it.

FTSE classifies South Korea as a developed country, while MSCI still classifies it as emerging (due to political and capital structure issues, not developmental ones, given how advanced and wealthy they are). The breakout in emerging markets is due in no small part to Taiwan Semi, Samsung, and SK Hynix.

**Usage of Social Media**

Currently, most bots on social media are considered spam, and are intended to spam. A small portion are considered novel or helpful, generally in the experimental sense.

As AI agents become more powerful and their ability to use social media becomes normalized, we can expect two new potential types of bots. One type is the helpful transparent bot, where it identifies itself as a bot and is not spam, but rather is quite helpful. These already exist in simple forms (e.g. a bot on Nostr that posts daily quotes from Plato) but can become far more sophisticated. A second type is the opaque bot. It may assert itself as a human, and be treated as a human (people liking its posts, having back-and-forth chats with it) for a long time without us realizing it isn’t one.

I have a science fiction novel coming out in March, and in that near-future world, human-seeming AI agents are on social media as a regular occurrence. In that vision of things, there are centralized platforms that generally require identification and tend to filter or censor things, and decentralized networks around the edges where privacy is preserved, usage is permissionless, and bots are more common.

One of the potential side effects of this trend is that it may ironically reduce our sense of global interconnectedness. The combo of 1) sophisticated bots, 2) realistic AI videos, 3) a broad lack of trust in major news corporations, 4) influencers that can be paid to create false narratives, and 5) centralized algorithms that prioritize sensationalist content, creates a situation where people have massive information overload and an uphill battle at separating what is real versus what is fake, and what is even worth prioritizing their limited attention on sorting out.

One way to think of this is as an information bell curve, which kind of represents the worse case scenario:

![Information Bell Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-information-bell-curve.jpg)

-For most of human history, before the widespread sharing of videos, people were pretty isolated. They knew what was happening in their village but had little knowledge of what was happening far away. Prior to telegraphs and prior to the printing press, things were even more isolated. It’s all about the speed of basic information, with videos being the most credible and self-evident types of information in many cases.

-As we got deep into the 20th century, trusted news corporations and widespread video technology helped share high-bandwidth information and evidence of what was happening in the world at a rate that was difficult for suppression techniques or forgeries to keep up with. -The further we head into the 21st century, technology is pulling us back apart. Although smartphones give us more capacity to document things around the world, the institutional trust in professional journalists is down, and social media algorithms dominate attention and pull people into tribal echo chambers. Fake AI videos are already tricking some people, and given some time will likely become nearly indistinguishable from reality. We’ll ironically have less of a shared set of facts about what’s happening in the world than in the middle era where video was common but AI and social media did not yet exist.

A second way to think of this is that it inverts Plato’s Cave.

In the famous allegory, people exist in a cave, with shadow illusions playing in front of them, keeping them in ignorance. Once they realize their situation, the people can start climbing out toward freedom, where they see the real truth of things as a sort of enlightenment.

If we apply that mainly to knowledge of world affairs (rather than philosophical enlightenment), then realistic AI videos, realistic AI bots, and social media algorithms really muddle the waters. In that sense, people were in the cave of world knowledge prior to widely-shared videos, started to climb out of the cave during the period of videos but before false AI videos (and thus reached peak world knowledge at that middle stage), and then upon exiting the cave they don’t know if they’re in another illusion or not, and thus they don’t get the information enlightenment they expected.

![Plato's AI Cave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-platos-AI-cave.jpg)

Back in 2024 in Oslo, [I interviewed Jack Dorsey](https://youtu.be/MaZyXEU5XAg), the founder of Twitter, and his main emphasis was on the power of centralized algorithms to shape people’s perception of things. We had intended to get more into AI during the talk, but had a limited timeframe, and I think AI-generated videos and sophisticated AI bots will further complicate this issue in the years ahead.

## Final Thoughts: Precious Metals Volatility

Precious metals set multi-decade records on Friday for the sheer size of their daily losses. In very rough numbers during the Jan 29/30 period:

Gold dropped from $5,500 to $4,800.

Platinum dropped from $2,900 to $2,100.

Silver dropped from $120 to $85.

![Silver Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-2-1-silver-price.png)

While I’ve been long precious metals across the board since 2018, I’ve grown cautious on them in recent months. My position has to been to let them run but to be careful about adding any new capital and to consider rebalancing if one was too overweight.

> Precious metals reached a crescendo in late December and have since sold off a bit.
>
> Here is silver, for example:
>
> ![Silver](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2026-1-4-silver.png)
>
> This outcome is not surprising, and is in line with my warning from the prior report.
>
> Precious metals had a similar correction back in mid-October, so it is not as though a breaking of the momentum is necessarily a kill shot for the whole rally.
>
> However, I continue to view precious metals as a place where risk/reward is no longer asymmetrical. On one hand, precious metals benefit to the upside in my opinion from a combination of fiscal dominance and a world that is becoming more multipolar and distrusting over time, and so I think a lot of this higher pricing is likely to stick even after sentiment washes out. On the other hand, sentiment has become so positive, that there are indeed risks for a multi-month or even multi-year correction from these price levels.
>
> So, while I continue to hold precious metals, my new capital is likely to be deployed elsewhere.
>
> -January 4, 2026 premium report

This recent sell-off dwarfed the one that hit in early January and the one that hit in mid-October. However, the upward moves here in January have been so big that even after this big drop, they’re still up huge from where they were just weeks/months ago.

In this type of extremely volatile environment, it’s mostly a trader’s market. Silver traded for $30/oz in May 2025, $120/oz last week (a 4x increase in a matter of months), and $85/oz now. More time is needed to see if these metals establish a floor here as they did during prior sell-offs, or if they just keep falling.

I personally don’t plan to add on dips in the near future. That’s not to say I’m bearish- it’s that I think most of the asymmetry or edge is no longer there. There are plenty of other boring, inexpensive, and under-owned markets to go fishing in, and so this is an area where I plan to let the dust settle and watch as an observer for a while.
