## Macro View

The virus continues to show signs of having bottomed in the global sense, with another upswing starting to build:

![Global COVID-19](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-global-covid.png)

However, these waves tend to be getting less fatal as people build natural immunity, medical treatments improve, and vulnerable parts of the population are vaccinated. I’m looking forward to the time where I won’t have to include virus updates in my reports anymore. **Inflation Update**

This past week’s inflation report for October in the US came in above economist expectations at 6.2% year-over-year. This isn’t due to year-over-year base effects anymore; just high raw inflation that is broadly across the inflation basket.

![Inflation vs Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-inflation-vs-rates.png)

Rent and owner’s equivalent rent moving back up from their low base were key drivers of this inflation print, while other things remained elevated as well. This rent and owner’s equivalent rent CPI is just getting started based on most metrics that are available, so overall high official CPI should be sticky for the remainder of Q4 2021 and Q1 2022. Earlier this year these low numbers were an anchor on CPI, but now they are helping to keep it elevated:

![Rent CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-rent-cpi.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

Cash and bond yields remain well below the inflation rate in this environment of financial repression, which I expect to continue for a while. The Fed is not capping the long end of the yield curve, but by pinning the overnight rate near zero, and buying a large percentage of longer-dated Treasuries to remove excess supply from the market, and along with increased regulations over time to make institutions hold more T-bills, they’ve effectively submerged the yield curve well below the inflation rate and thus are devaluing Treasuries in an ongoing fashion.

Meanwhile, the overall monetary inflation rate is 13% over the past year. My forward analysis from last year pointed to 10-15% and we have continued to be in the middle of that range at 13% for the year. This is due to a blend of fiscal spending and central bank deficit monetization, since bank lending remains subdued. The broad money supply growth rate was 5% annualized pre-pandemic, followed by a huge burst to deal with the liquidity crisis, and then 13% annualized since then:

![M2 Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-m2-growth.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

The Fed’s November 3rd FOMC statement indicated, as expected and well-telegraphed, that they will begin to taper their rate of asset purchases at a rate of $15 billion per month. For a while they have been buying $120 billion in Treasuries and mortgage-backed securities per month. Here in November it will drop to $105 billion, in December it will drop to $90 billion, and so forth.

If they stick to that rate, then by July 2022 they will cease buying assets and hold their balance sheet flat. However, they have an important caveat in that they use the phrase “at least” to describe their expected rate of monthly asset purchases. The tapering reduces their floor of monthly asset purchases but does not cap them. If there are disfunctions in the Treasury or MBS market, they can perform an extra purchase at any time to smooth out market functioning. Meanwhile, they have a standing repo facility setup which can also absorb a certain mount of liquidity issues that may happen from their cessation of Treasury purchases.

This remains relatively bullish for risk assets for the moment, but as we move into mid-2022 things could get rougher for asset prices without that support of constant increases in the monetary base.

**Gold and the Dollar**

The dollar index continues to move upward, and has breached 95:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-dxy.png)

Throughout 2020 I was bearish on the dollar, but became somewhat tactically neutral in early 2021, and looking for technical signs of either breaking up or breaking down vs its consolidation trend. So far, it has broken up, and has been in a bullish trend for longer than my 2021 base case was. I have a fundamental longer-term bearish view on the dollar but I do not fight the price action on currency movements when it breaks out of pre-defined resistance levels.

It’s important to note, however, that this move is not so much that the dollar is strengthening; it’s that the euro and yen are weakening. That seems like a meaningless distinction, but it’s not. If the dollar strengthens vs virtually all currencies, that is likely to trigger a liquidity problem and slow down economic growth. However, if the dollar is relatively stable vs most currencies, but the euro and yen are weakening vs most currencies and they happen to make up a large part of the dollar index, then that’s a different story and there is less likely to be a liquidity problem.

Indeed, the Treasury/eurodollar spread is showing no signs of liquidity problems, but if the dollar trend does persist, those liquidity problems could start to show up. It’s an area I’ll be watching.

Year to date, the dollar is mildly down vs the Chinese yuan and Russian ruble, flat vs the Canadian dollar, mildly up vs the Australian dollar, etc. So overall, it’s not so much a dollar move, but rather that the yen and euro have weakened.

In the past month as inflation has come in hotter than economists expected and the “transitory inflation” narrative is weakening while interest rates remain low, gold has caught a small bid. It now has some higher highs and higher lows in place, showing some initial evidence of a bottoming process.

![Gold Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-gold-technicals.png)

Gold priced in euros has showed even stronger gains, due to the euro’s recent weakness. In euros, gold is at highs not seen since November 2020, and has surpassed its local May 2021 high (which it has not achieved yet as priced in dollars).

My base case, unless or until it is invalidated, is that the bottom is in and that gold has a rather constructive outlook in the next couple years.

Gold bears and gold bulls often cherry pick timeframes when discussing its performance.

Gold bears like to point out that over the past decade since 2011 (at the top of a tremendous gold bull run after it went up 500% in a decade), gold has underperformed most things, including equities and including the monetary inflation rate.

Gold bulls like to point out that since the beginning of the year 2000 (at the top of the equity bubble and the bottoming of the commodity trough), gold has outperformed the S&P 500 total return index, bonds, the monetary inflation rate, etc. This includes the terrible return over the past decade, because the prior decade was so tremendous.

![Gold Longterm Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-gold-longterm.png)

An overall issue for gold in the current environment here in 2021 is the scale, quality, and volatility of competing assets.

![Gold Meme](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-gold-meme.jpg)

Anyone who saw *Interstellar* would get the joke.

I think gold is likely to catch a bid from parts of the bond market over the next couple years in this period of the yield curve being persistently submerged below the inflation rate, but its price at this point is impacted by fund flows into bitcoin and other perceived inflation hedges so that sort of expectation is very much subject to market sentiment and the improving desirability of bitcoin as an asset.

## Uranium Note

Uranium has managed to hold onto a lot of its parabolic gains lately. It has been in a gradual bull trend since 2016, and I went long in October 2020 during the dip when I could buy it for a 20% discount to NAV, but the Sprott acquisition of the Uranium Participation Corp has been a major catalyst for a breakout:

![Uranium Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-uranium.png)

After the parabolic spike, Sprott went into a brief period where it no longer had a premium over NAV, and there was a significant correction in the price of uranium.

Over the past few weeks, however, Sprott has once again frequently seen a mild premium over NAV, meaning that the value of the shares slightly exceeds the value of the uranium they hold. When this happens, Sprott can issue more shares to raise capital and buy uranium, sucking all of these pounds of material off the market. As of this writing, Sprott has a healthy 4.5% premium over NAV and keeps issuing shares.

One of the catalysts for this renewed uranium interest in recent weeks may have been the energy crises in Europe and China. It sparked a renewed debate between EU countries about nuclear energy, and re-emphasized China’s [plans to build 150 new reactors](https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2021-11-02/china-climate-goals-hinge-on-440-billion-nuclear-power-plan-to-rival-u-s) over the next 15 years.

This partial stabilization in the uranium price and Sprott’s ongoing premium to NAV continue to make uranium an attractive portfolio diversifier in my view. Simply holding the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust (SRUUF) is my preferred way to play it.

![BTCUSD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-btcusd.png)

![Bitcoin Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-bitcoin-longterm.png)

Yesterday, the Bitcoin network activated the consensus Taproot soft fork, which is its biggest upgrade since over four years ago in 2017. This improves the cryptography for its digital signatures, and thus overtime allows for greater privacy and better scalability. It’s a good boon to the Lightning network as well and allows for some additional functionality on that layer including making it more private.

Basically, unless there are technical bugs (and it has been thoroughly tested at this point), it shouldn’t directly affect Bitcoin’s price in the near-term. However, it continues to strengthen the foundation of the protocol for ongoing innovation in this ecosystem.

## Bristol Myers Squibb Update

Bristol-Myers Squibb (BMY) has a potential bottoming formation in place:

![BMY Technicals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-bmy-technicals.png)

I’ve been relatively bullish on BMY for several months. For a while it was working and paying dividends and chopping along into a potential breakout, but then most of the healthcare sector dropped substantially in August and September. This was due to concerns about potential regulation of drug pricing in the US.

The summary is that House Democrats have been attempting to reign in drug prices as part of their upcoming fiscal spending plan, and in particular to reform Medicare Part D which limits price negotiation by the federal government. Their plan has been able to pass the House, but faces challenges in the more divided Senate.

As a point of history, Medicare Part D was passed into law in 2003 under the Bush Administration. [Congressman Billy Tauzin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billy_Tauzin) is considered to have played a large role in architecting the plan, and then he resigned from Congress and worked for years making millions of dollars as the president of the largest pharmaceutical corporate lobbying group.

The common argument for preventing the federal government from negotiating drug prices on behalf of its Medicare plan is that it would limit innovation and funding in the industry. The common argument against this (and thus in favor of letting the federal government negotiate drug prices) is that the current structure is basically a big giveaway to pharmaceutical companies, is an example of the problem of having so much donor money in Congress, and an example of the ongoing revolving door problem between Congress, regulatory bodies, and corporations.

Overall, Americans do often pay higher prices for the same drugs than people in other countries. In some ways, Americans are the ones that end up absorbing the research costs and profit margins for these drugs, while the rest of the world generally gets them at more competitive rates.

> Researchers analyzed 2018 data and found that prescription drug prices in the United States average 2.5 times more than in 32 other Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) nations.
>
> –[US News and World Report January 29, 2021](https://www.usnews.com/news/health-news/articles/2021-01-29/compared-to-other-countries-americans-pay-much-more-for-prescription-drugs)

So back to the present, Democrats are attempting to reform this in their current suite of legislative proposals. Some of the conservative Democrats, as well as most of the Republican party, favor keeping the current system, or much more limited reform. In order to get through the Senate (if it gets through at all), it would have to go through that filter. That’s why the House version of the bill has trouble getting through the Senate.

As Politico described two weeks ago:

> But if Democrats are able to reinsert a drug plan, lawmakers and aides say it will hew closer to the far more narrow and industry-friendly version put forward by House and Senate centrists and endorsed by the White House than the aggressive plan that already passed the House twice. Though the drug industry’s goal is preventing any government price negotiation whatsoever, limiting the bargaining to a narrow subset of drugs and leaning more heavily on measures like out-of-pocket caps that don’t impact the companies’ bottom line would be a victory in itself.
>
> Several progressives and frontliners said they would rather leave drug pricing out of the package altogether than pass what they see as a weak version that will sap motivation for future action. Rep. Susan Wild (D-Pa.) said: “My understanding is that if it goes back in, it’ll be the Peters version or even worse. I’m pretty upset about it. It’s just horrible.”
>
> –[Politico October 31, 2021](https://www.politico.com/news/2021/10/31/democrats-race-prescription-drug-deal-517974)

And as Health Affairs described:

> One key change between the H.R. 3 version of these inflationary rebates and the negotiated agreement’s version is that H.R. 3 would have benchmarked its inflationary rebate system to 2016 prices, creating retroactive financial responsibilities for many companies. The negotiated agreement instead sets the fourth quarter of 2021 as its benchmark, more closely resembling the Senate Finance Committee’s package and putting companies on notice of these requirements going forward.
>
> –[Health Affairs November 4, 2021](https://www.healthaffairs.org/do/10.1377/hblog20211104.184553/full/)

Basically, the probability of the major reforms going through is shrinking, and as more clarity comes in about the potential legislative packages, many healthcare stocks have been bottoming lately. The market doesn’t like uncertainty, and some of that uncertainty is dissipating. It remains to be seen what if anything will actually pass. Some type of price increase limit in line with inflation seems reasonably likely.

I continue to view BMY as a long-term dividend stock play with characteristics of undervaluation. I don’t know how 2022 elections are going to turn out but polls are pointing towards a potential divided Congress in 2022 and this will prevent major healthcare reform. Even the current state of the Senate likely prevents major reform, and thus keeps drug prices high in the US.

![BMY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-bmy-fastgraph.png)

## MicroStrategy Analysis

MicroStrategy (MSTR) is an enterprise software business that specializes in business intelligence solutions. Since August 2020, their other goal has been to accumulate bitcoins, and thus they became the first company on a major US stock exchange to put bitcoins on their balance sheet as a corporate treasury holding.

Within about a week of them announcing that decision back in August 2020, I added MicroStrategy to the M1 newsletter portfolio. The primary reason was that M1 Finance does not have a way to invest in bitcoin, and does not hold over-the-counter securities like the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC), and so that portfolio had no other avenues for bitcoin price exposure.

By that point I already had recommended and went long bitcoin and GBTC for four months in other areas including the No Limits portfolio and cold storage, but couldn’t put it in the newsletter portfolio. MicroStrategy was therefore a useful bitcoin proxy, and did quite well:

![MicroStrategy Returns](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-mstr-btc-spy.png)

Many large pools of capital are in a similar position as I was with that micro-example of the M1 Finance accounts. If any equity manager or bond manager is bullish on bitcoin but has a mandate to only own certain types of equity securities or bond securities, then buying MicroStrategy shares or MicroStrategy bonds is their indirect way to get some bitcoin price exposure.

Since MicroStrategy added bitcoins to their balance sheet, a number of publicly-traded bitcoin miners have arisen to become decent bitcoin proxies as well, but they have a much different risk profile than MicroStrategy has when it comes to their bitcoin exposure. Back in July 2021 during the big bitcoin price correction, I added bitcoin miners such as Marathon Digital (MARA) and Riot Blockchain (RIOT) to the M1 Finance portfolios, to accompany MicroStrategy. Marathon in particular has done very well from that point, but at current valuation levels, I once again prefer MicroStrategy over Marathon.

**Balance Sheet Transformation**

Back in early 2020, MicroStrategy was in a situation that was both good and bad. The bad news was that they were not growing their top line for a while. The good news was that they were profitable and had a huge cash pile with no debt. Their balance sheet was a major source of strength.

With rapid increases in the national money supply occurring and near-zero interest rates on that cash pile (which represented many years of retained earnings, basically the company’s entire history of profits), the company’s longtime chairman and CEO Michael Saylor researched other places to put that capital so that it would retain its purchasing power. He and his board eventually chose to invest it into bitcoins.

The way they went about managing their capital structure since August 2020 has been very intelligent in my view. Back in 2000 during the dotcom bubble, the company settled a lawsuit related to the alleged overstating of revenue and earnings, which was during the popping of the dotcom bubble when many shareholders for tech companies were disgruntled. So if I were to guess, the company was extra cautious this time in ensuring that their purchase and communication approach with regard to their bitcoin acquisition strategy was beyond reproach.

First, they announced their decision to use part of their cash to buy bitcoin. They also announced a share repurchase plan so that any investors who did not like this plan could sell their shares and the company would use some of its cash to buy them. Investors who wanted that bitcoin exposure (like me) could hop in, while investors who did not agree with that approach and did not want that bitcoin exposure, could gracefully exit with liquidity and support. It allowed for a smooth rotation of the shareholder base, especially because the company had little way ahead of time to predict what percentage of the shareholders might rotate out.

Second, they issued three rounds of debt over three fiscal quarters totaling $2.15 billion, and were clear that the purpose of the debt issuance was to buy more bitcoins. These bond issuance rounds were oversubscribed. These are longer-term bonds of various types, including convertible notes, rather than any sort of short-term callable debt. I’ve seen a lot of bears pay close attention to MicroStrategy’s cost basis as though if bitcoin’s price goes below that cost basis the company is in trouble, but that’s not how it works. Bitcoin would need to go down and remain down for many years for the company’s bitcoin strategy to not pay off. An analyst at Citigroup downgraded Microstrategy to a “sell” in 2020 on this news because he was uncomfortable with the amount of bitcoin purchases, and since then the stock has surged hundreds of percent in price.

Third, the company began issuing equity to buy more bitcoins. Equity can be thought of as an infinite-duration (but comparatively expensive) liability. So, by issuing equity to add more assets to their balance sheet, this started to rotate the company’s capital structure back away from debt, by reducing their amount of debt compared to bitcoin holdings for the moment.

**Valuation Assessment**

Most valuation metrics at face value don’t make sense for analyzing MicroStrategy because of the weird way that accounting standards treat bitcoins on company balance sheets.

Suppose a company buys a bitcoin for $25,000 and then it falls to $15,000 and then it rises to $60,000. The company would have to acknowledge a $10,000 impairment on the lost value of their bitcoin and then treat it as a $15,000 asset forever; even when it goes up to $60,000. They have to mark any lower prices, but cannot do the same for higher prices. That’s how the current accounting standards treat bitcoin, and some companies are trying to change that.

So, throw away any “book value” and “earnings” you find on stock websites for this particular company. You have to dig into the financials to get the more accurate picture.

MicroStrategy trades at a mild premium to their bitcoin holdings, which is why it makes sense for them to issue equity at these levels to buy bitcoins.

We can do a basic sum-of-parts analysis. Prior to 2020, the company had a rather stable market capitalization of approximately $1.5 billion, which could be thought of as a $1 billion software company and $500 million in cash, with no debt. When they started to buy bitcoins, their market cap went up faster than the net addition of bitcoin assets to their balance sheet. In other words, the market was assigning them a premium above their bitcoin purchases.

With 114,042 bitcoins as of the most recent quarter, they now have $7.3 billion worth of bitcoins at current price levels. Then we add on a $1 billion software company, assuming it is worth roughly what it was worth pre-pandemic. So that’s $8.3 billion in combined value. The short-term net assets and liabilities are marginal, and they don’t have the $500 million cash pile anymore because that is in their bitcoins. Then we subtract $2.15 billion in debt to arrive at a fair value of $6.15 billion for the company’s equity.

From there, we can re-rate the value of the software business higher because it is benefiting from increased brand recognition, is improving its margins, and is growing its subscription revenue. Non-GAAP operating income nearly doubled over the past year, largely as a result of cost savings as the company became more virtual in its marketing efforts, and more cloud-based in general. This should add 50-100% of value to the software company, so we can say the software business is now worth $1.5 billion to $2 billion rather than only $1 billion. That means we can add another $0.5 billion to $1 billion to our $6.15 billion valuation above, and get to a baseline approximate value of **$6.65 to $7.15 billion** for the company as of Q3 2021.

The current market capitalization is **$8.4 billion**. So, it has what can be considered a $1.25 to $1.75 billion premium over what the company is likely worth on a sum-of-parts basis. If that is roughly accurate, then it translates into a 17% to 26% valuation premium. The only real variable part is determining what the software side of the business is worth, so any estimate for this will be a range rather than a specific figure.

As previously mentioned, there are many pools of capital that want exposure to bitcoin’s price, but due to investing mandates they can’t yet buy bitcoin directly and instead need it as a security. MicroStrategy stock happens to be the security that most closely follows the price of bitcoin on a major US exchange, with high liquidity. It’s better than the new futures-based bitcoin ETFs. So, it makes sense that there is an ongoing appetite for MicroStrategy’s shares above NAV. Unlike the Grayscale Bitcoin Trust (GBTC), MicroStrategy trades on the Nasdaq rather than over-the-counter.

MicroStrategy has been using this valuation premium intelligently over the past quarter. Issuing equity makes sense when your company trades at a likely premium, specifically when your company is primarily valued based on its property.

This is essentially the same thing that the Sprott Physical Uranium Trust is doing; whenever their share price trades at premium over their NAV of the uranium that they hold, they issue more shares to buy more uranium, which is value accretive. MicroStrategy is now basically doing the same thing with bitcoins; as long as the valuation makes sense to do so; they can keep issuing equity to buy more bitcoins.

**The CEO Michael Saylor’s History of Bold Moves**

-As an MIT aerospace engineer, Michael Saylor founded the company in 1989, which as far as I can tell makes him the longest-running tech CEO out of all current tech CEOs of publicly-traded companies on major US exchanges, despite being under 60. There are longer-running public CEOs like Warren Buffett but not in the tech industry, and there might be some current private tech CEOs with longer tenures as well, but those would be hard to verify.

-He purchased a lot of single-word domain names in the 1990s, based on the idea that both the English language and the .com high-level domain were important network effects that would persist for the foreseeable future, making these domain names valuable digital property. The return on investment of those has been extremely high, as these types of domain names are generally worth millions or tens of millions of dollars now.

-He wrote *The Mobile Wave* in 2012, correctly predicting a lot of the technical trends that occurred over the following decade, and he invested in several large tech stocks with network effects to take advantage of those trends, which went on to outperform the S&P 500.

-He led MicroStrategy to be the first public company on a major exchange to put bitcoins on its balance sheet. And not just 5% or so; he went all-in and put the company’s entire savings into bitcoins. He had previously dismissed the Bitcoin network in 2013 but when it reached over $100 billion in market capitalization with a large network effect, along with the unusual macro backdrop of 2020, he performed more research and pivoted his view before bitcoin’s big late-2020 price breakout, and has since become a large proponent of bitcoin.

He often describes bitcoin as being an ideal form of digital property, which makes sense considering how similar his bitcoin strategy is in many ways to his early move into the field of domain name investment.

**Why Only Bitcoin?**

Saylor is often asked on news programs if he would consider diversifying into other digital assets, with his answer being various shades of no.

In addition to the Bitcoin network’s actual decentralization and security properties that other digital assets lack in exchange for their other attributes, Saylor often cites the regulatory risks associated with those other protocols.

![Saylor on Regulation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2021-11-14-saylor-on-regulation.png)

Here’s an example of one of his recent answers on a business news segment:

> Q: Are you at some point going to diversify MicroStrategy’s balance sheet with different crypto?
>
> A: You know, Bitcoin is universally recognized as property, so it’s the institutional investment-grade asset in the space if you want a long-term store of value. The other cryptos are either technical VC investments or they are speculative investments, and so for a speculator or for a venture capitalist they might be interesting. But if you are an institutional investor or a publicly-traded company, I think you need to stay with the institutional investment-grade asset.
>
> –[Saylor on Fox Business, November 2021](https://twitter.com/saylor/status/1459538689990836226)

Bitcoin (and likely some of its smaller hard forks) is basically the only digital asset that is known to specifically not be a security, since it does not pass the Howey Test. Instead, bitcoins are considered commodities, or a type of property. An asset, but not a security.

On the other hand, blockchain protocols with pre-mined coins, where the developers then work on the project to try to increase its value, generally pass the Howey Test and appear to be securities, despite often not being sold to the public in ways that comply with securities regulations. The majority of altcoins likely fall into that category, to varying degrees of obviousness. The challenging aspect for the crypto industry is that the SEC has thus far regulated through enforcement on individual cases rather than by issuing detailed industry-specific clarification guidelines, so for now there is a big gray area of regulatory arbitrage. I’ve spoken with VCs in non-bitcoin crypto companies that say they are considering relocating to Singapore or elsewhere due to regulatory issues around this topic. Some companies in the space have issued tokens that are in-line with securities regulations, and thus are officially and transparently securities.

Basically, in addition to the problems of centralization, unintended chain splits, difficulty bombs, chains going down for hours, and other problems like that, MicroStrategy’s approach clearly prefers to buy assets that are known to be property rather than having a decent likelihood of being considered unregistered securities, especially considering the sheer magnitude of their investment as a public entity.

Saylor’s approach with MicroStrategy is sometimes described as reckless by critics, while ironically in the digital asset space, he is a rather conservative investor from a technical and regulatory standpoint, ensuring that he buys the main asset in the space that has the fewest security issues and is known for sure not to be an unregistered security, but rather is a commodity/property.

**Obvious Risks**

The biggest individual risk facing MicroStrategy with this all-in approach, is the long-term bitcoin price. With $7.3 billion worth of bitcoin and $2.15 billion worth of debt, the company can be thought of as a moderately leveraged bitcoin play wrapped in a security (publicly-traded shares), with a $1.5 to $2 billion unleveraged software company attached that generates positive cash flow.

Due to being thoughtfully constructed, the company’s capital structure is not really subject to bitcoin’s volatility, meaning they are not vulnerable to liquidations if for example bitcoin’s price gets cut in half tomorrow.

However, if bitcoin goes down and remains down into the mid-to-late 2020s when their debt starts coming due, the bitcoin-buying strategy will have not paid off. The company may have to dilute its equity base at unfavorable valuations to cover those debts in that scenario, rather than being able to easily refinance it.

Therefore, only investors who have a long-term bullish view on bitcoin as an asset should consider an investment in MicroStrategy. The company’s share price is very much correlated with bitcoin, both fundamentally and in reality.

Because the software business is a smaller percentage of the value of the company now, its growth or lack thereof is in some ways less critical than bitcoin’s long-term price action for the overall company’s expected share performance. However, a drastic improvement in the software business from the current baseline, or targeted acquisition, or venture into bitcoin-related business lines, could potentially increase the value of that side of the business. Alternatively, competitive forces from larger entities or other problems could result in a reduction in cash flows and thus reduction in overall value for that business.

Overall, I have a structurally bullish view on MicroStrategy in a similar way that I do for bitcoin as an asset.
