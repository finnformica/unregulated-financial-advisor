## Macro View

Stock, bond, and currency markets all experienced tremendous volatility last week, exhibiting a type of market environment that is very rare in the United States (but not that uncommon for emerging markets).

The catalyst was a rapid escalation in tariff announcements and implementations, in what quickly became an all-out trade war. By the end of the week, it was partially de-escalated with a 90-day pause for most countries for more negotiations, offset by a further intensification of the US-China portion of the trade war, which is the main event.

**Trade and Capital Flows Update**

Before I get into market dynamics, I want to remind about how the stage was set. In my mid-January report, I discussed at length how trade deficits go hand-in-hand with the global reserve currency in the current structure of the global financial system since the 1970s:

> A topic I’ve been discussing for several years is the structural U.S. trade deficit and the role that the dollar plays in these trade deficits due to its role as the global reserve currency.
>
> In particular, there is both a benefit and a cost of being the issuer of the global reserve currency.
>
> The “cost” of maintaining the Bretton Woods system was that the United States’ gold reserves were constantly drained to foreign hands until the system was defaulted on in 1971. Likewise, the “cost” of maintaining the Petrodollar/Treasury system that has been in place ever since then is that the United States’ industrial base is gradually hollowed out and sent to foreign hands via a persistent trade deficit. I covered this in detail in [Broken Money](https://www.amazon.com/Broken-Money-Financial-System-Failing/dp/B0CG8985FR/), specifically in chapter 13 called “Heavy is the Head that Wears the Crown”.
>
> ![US Trade Balance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-us-trade-balance.png)
>
> The mechanics of this are as follows.
>
> Since the 1970s, the United States has encouraged countries to hold dollars (primarily in the from of Treasuries) as their primary reserves. Even without this encouragement, the post-Bretton Woods network effects of the eurodollar system were already strong compared to other currencies. The majority of cross-border trade is priced in dollars, the majority of cross-border lending is denominated in dollars, and accumulated trade surpluses are stored primarily in dollars. The dollar is the most salable currency, and thus serves as the world’s primary ledger.
>
> In other words, historically, if a Japanese company sells construction equipment to a Brazilian company, it’ll often be priced in dollars and then those dollars will be exchanged up to Japan’s sovereign level and used to buy Treasuries and held for the long run. In addition to the dollar being the most liquid and salable currency in global markets, the U.S. capital markets (stocks and bonds) represent the biggest and most liquid place to store longer-term value.
>
> Similarly, if a Canadian firm lends money to a Brazilian firm, they’ll often do so in a U.S. dollar-denominated contract. The foreign sector has more than $13 trillion in dollar-denominated debt, and that’s primarily owed to other foreign entities rather than to American entities. All of those debts represent inflexible demand for dollars, since dollars are required to service those debts.
>
> This means practically the whole world needs dollars, both for buying international goods and for servicing their dollar-denominated debt. So the next question is: how does the United States supply dollars to the whole world? The answer is that they primarily do so through a structural trade deficit. It’s not even fully intentional; it happens on its own due to the organic demand and self-reinforcing liquidity network effects for the dollar. Most currencies are not held long-term by foreign entities at scale, and so interest rate differentials and trade balances account for most of their relative value to each other. But the dollar has this additional component of demand that other currencies don’t have: almost all countries are using it for international trade and are using it to store value in over the long run. As such, the dollar is almost always “overvalued” on a trade balance basis.
>
> This makes it very hard to manufacture low-margin things in the United States in any competitive sense. Manufacturing goes elsewhere, and what thrives in the United States are financial services, technology designers, pharmaceutical designers, and military arms makers. In essence, we gradually sell our share of the world’s industrial base away to China and others, and prop up our financial, technology, healthcare, and defense sectors.
>
> So, the rest of the world collectively runs trade surpluses against the United States, and reinvests those surpluses into U.S. Treasuries, U.S. corporate stocks and bonds, and to a lesser extent U.S. real estate and U.S. private equity. Or, like China with their Belt and Road initiative, they now use their trade surplus dollars to buy up commodity and infrastructure assets around the world. The trade deficit may seem like Americans are trading pieces of paper for goods and services (which would likely be a good trade) but due to this reinvestment, what we are actually selling is the ownership stock in our companies and future interest payments from our government. The foreign sector owns a larger and larger share of U.S. companies and government outlays over time, meaning that we sell our appreciating financial assets in exchange for depreciating goods and services.
>
> The cumulative result of this is that the world owns about $24 trillion more U.S. assets than entities in the United States own of foreign assets. Another way to phrase that is that the net international investment position of the United States is negative $24 trillion:
>
> ![US NIIP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-niip.png)
>
> This cycle has persisted for decades, and has become imbalanced enough to reach center stage in politics via two main pathways. The first pathway is that it contributes to populism, since “rust belt” areas of the country correctly feel left behind by this arrangement and start to want their industry to return. The second pathway is that it destabilizes military strength, like for example the U.S. has [had trouble](https://www.cnn.com/2024/03/10/politics/russia-artillery-shell-production-us-europe-ukraine/index.html) keeping up with Russia’s manufacturing output of artillery in recent years despite being a far larger economy overall. During the pandemic years, it was also quickly recognized how things like ventilators and core medical components are typically made in China, not the United States. Our industrial base has stagnated for three decades while the world’s total industrial base grew, with most of that growth being in China.
>
> ![Industrial Base](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-industrial-base.png)
>
> What makes this even more imbalanced and self-reinforcing is that after two generations of outsourcing the country’s marginal manufacturing capability, we lost out on training of highly-skilled manufacturing technicians at sufficient scale. China and other countries have that human capital in massive numbers now. We also have the highest healthcare costs in the world, and that’s a burden that often falls on employers, which leads to worse competitiveness for outsource-able types of labor. Additionally, manufacturing is typically a very electricity-intensive activity, and the build-up of that electricity infrastructure is likewise out in China and elsewhere now.
>
> ![China Electricity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-china-electricity.png)
>
> ![Global Steel Production](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-global-steel-production.jpg)
>
> So, bringing this capacity back to the United States is no small matter, and would take a long time if tried in earnest. It relates to the structural nature of the dollar system itself, and then both human capital and electricity production that have developed around where manufacturing has been supported, and have left where it has been ignored.
>
> The fact that we exported it in the first place is part of why inflation has been pretty low over the recent decades. The rise of Chinese labor as it was unlocked to the world, mixed with Western capital, was a massive disinflationary force which came with costs.
>
> The likely incoming Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent says he wants the U.S. dollar to remain the global reserve currency, but also wants to fix the trade imbalance between the United States and China. But in the current system, those trade deficits are what supply the world with dollars so it can be the reserve currency, and being the reserve currency causes imbalances of capital flows one way or another. During the 1970s and 1980s the trade surplus bogeyman was Japan. In the 2000s, 2010s, and 2020s it has shifted to China. Individual areas can be targeted but if the system remains structurally similar, it keeps happening somewhere.
>
> -January 19, 2025 premium report

The concise summary of that description is as follows: Because the world uses the dollar for 1) the primary reserve asset 2) the primary international funding currency, 3) the primary FX trading pair and 4) the primary unit of account for international contracts, it has tremendous demand for dollars. The way it gets those dollars is by overvaluing the dollar, permanently forcing open the US trade deficit, and so dollars continually pour out into the world year after year for them to use as the global reserve currency.

Now, after five decades of that, the accumulated imbalances are tremendous, with implications for both politics (e.g. the “blue wall” in the hollowed-out rust belt flipping red) and national security (e.g. inability to build ships or missiles as fast as adversaries, or to make its own electronics at sufficient scale). So, it does make sense that after being unaddressed for a long time, it has become a top domestic priority by the current administration.

The key question in my view comes down to execution. I’ve argued for years that the trade deficits are indeed becoming a problem, and so now it’s a matter of assessing whether the current approach is likely to address the issue, and on what timeframe.

In early February, I provided an initial set of reactions to the early tariff proposals:

> -Capital from all around the world is stuffed into U.S. large cap stocks, which have high valuations and high exposure to tariffs. U.S. stocks now account for 66% of MSCI’s all-world equity index. This sets up a potential stumbling block in the year ahead.
>
> -Many countries will likely take a look at their U.S. Treasury holdings or other U.S. securities holdings. If a nation like Colombia can be threatened with sanctions, and Canada can be challenged with 25% tariffs, it lowers the bar for reserve confiscation and similar activities. Stephen Miran, President Trump’s nominee for chairman of the Council of Economic Advisors, has [proposed](https://www.hudsonbaycapital.com/documents/FG/hudsonbay/research/638199_A_Users_Guide_to_Restructuring_the_Global_Trading_System.pdf) placing taxes on reserve assets to discourage reserve accumulation and to weaken the dollar to make U.S. manufacturing more competitive.
>
> -For many years, Canada has forgone many of its construction options to export its oil and gas products overseas, and instead let itself be reliant on the United States. The country might end up rethinking that approach, and indeed the leader of their Conservative party has already tweeted about that, given the unilateral impact the United States can have on them if it becomes unfriendly. We could see similar resilience-planning by other countries as well.
>
> -I would think defensively for the time being. Gold, cash-equivalents, short-term Treasuries, and inexpensive equities can potentially provide some safe havens. Assume higher volatility ahead in terms of cash flow planning and balance sheet strength. I’m skeptical that the classic safe havens of this past cycle (mega cap U.S. stocks, as major beneficiaries of globalization) will be the best place to be.
>
> -February 2, 2025 premium report

Stocks performed poorly, and long-duration Treasuries went through a ton of volatility (for bonds, at least) just to end up where they started. **Bond and Currency Markets Update**

The huge volatility in the stock market is what captured most retail investors’ attention, but professionals were primarily looking at the extremely rare way the bond market was trading.

At its depth, the S&P 500 was down about 20%, depending on whether we measure it from daily close or intraday moves. That’s pretty routine; it happens every couple years although this one was pretty sharp:

![SPY FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-spy-fastgraph.png)

There still might be more to fall (it partially will depend on what happens with tariffs, which will be discussed in the next section), in which case it could become a non-routine sell-off. But so far, that’s not the main event. Notably, it’s still trading at a somewhat rich valuation, and aggregate S&P 500 earnings are likely to be challenged unless the trade war is de-escalated.

The fact that the bond market was sharply selling off when stocks were selling off, *and* the dollar index was dropping, was an extremely rare mix. It suggests capital outflows, meaning the foreign sector pulling out of US markets, rather than investors rotating from stocks to bonds or going to cash. Neither the dollar nor long-duration Treasuries were treated as safe havens during the tariff-driven kerfuffle.

The 10-year Treasury yield surged shortly after big tariffs were announced in early April, meaning bond prices fell:

![10 Year Treasury Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-10yr-treasury-rate.png)

And then, despite the fact that yields were rising, the dollar index sharply fell in recent days:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-dxy.png)

The MOVE index (the volatility index for bonds) hit near record-high levels:

![MOVE Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-move-index.png)

On that MOVE index chart, I put numbers on the five notable spikes.

Spike 1 was the March 2020 lockdown crash, during which the Treasury market literally broke (off-the-run securities became acutely illiquid), causing the Fed to step in with the biggest emergency liquidity bazooka it’s ever done by far.

Spike 2 was when the UK Gilt market broke (i.e. “the Liz Truss moment”) in September 2022. While that was happening, the US Treasury market didn’t break, but it was on thin ice. The Bank of England came in with emergency liquidity to bail out their sovereign bonds.

Spike 3 was when the US regional banking crisis happened in March 2023. The Treasury Department and Fed teamed up to provide targeted liquidity, which put a floor under the crisis. And then throughout 2023, the Treasury Department began issuing a higher than normal ratio of T-bills compared to longer duration Treasuries, which is a type of liquidity stimulus.

Spike 4 was simply the US presidential election. Nothing particularly notable happened, other than the market got new information.

Spike 5 was what just happened last week. And what was notable about it was that out of the five, it was the only instance where the dollar sold off sharply as the MOVE index rose.

Some of this weak dollar move began earlier. The actual peak was in mid-January, which was also when the foreign sector began sharply trimming Treasuries on net, which is historically a cyclical top signal. I highlighted this at the time:

> When the dollar gets very strong, it pressures the various entities throughout the world that have dollar-denominated debt. And it generally leads sovereign entities to sell some of their Treasury holdings to get dollars to keep their liabilities covered, which comes back and hits U.S. markets. The fastest-moving foreign Treasuries are generally the ones custodied at the Fed, and we’ve already seen a sell-off there that is of a magnitude we normally see during cyclical dollar tops:
>
> ![Dollar vs Treasuries](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-1-19-dollar-vs-custody-treasuries.png)
>
> -January 19, 2025 premium report

Here’s an updated version of that chart:

![Custody vs USD](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-custody-vs-usd.png)

What happens next? I don’t think anyone is sure, since it will partially depend on tariff decisions, but here are some points.

First, whenever the MOVE index gets this high, usually some intervention happens. Neither the Fed nor the Treasury particularly care about a 20% stock market drop, but they do care about bond market instability, since that market is at the core of the system. So far, the intervention was that the US partially backed down on tariffs, with the exception of tariffs placed on China. Additionally, Susan Collins (the Boston Fed president, not the Maine senator) said publicly that the Fed was “absolutely” ready to help stabilize markets if needed, which placated some investors’ concerns around the tail risk of a truly disorderly bond move.

Second, when things move this sharply, there’s usually a counter-rally at some point. I do think there’s a pretty high likelihood of 3-5 years of capital rotation out of the United States (similar to the 2000s decade), but I would be surprised if there’s not at least a small dollar index bounce here, after how sharply it was sold. I say that more-so to set expectations for myself than something I plan to trade around.

Third, this weak bond action is happening *despite* the fact that the Treasury has been draining its cash balance, due to the debt ceiling. When the Treasury drains its cash, it’s good for liquidity, all else being equal. It’s been about a $400 billion liquidity injection into the financial system since February. Once the debt ceiling is resolved, the Treasury will need to refill its cash balance and issue *more bonds*. In addition, Treasury Secretary Bessent was critical of his predecessor for relying so heavily on T-bills and vowed to issue more longer-duration Treasuries to get back to the normal ratio, which would add fuel to the fire.

![Treasury General Account](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-tga.png)

Fourth, the bond market issues are solvable by the Fed. If they perform QE, they can assist the bond market, but the optics would be bad with still above-target inflation. It would also represent a reduction in central bank independence, since they don’t particularly want to do QE. I think this lever will be pulled eventually; that’s always been basically the endgame for where this train is headed. The other option, or a complimentary one, is to provide permanent changes to the supplementary leverage ratio for banks. That would let them hold more Treasuries, but it would be seen as a gift to the big banks.

**Tariff Update**

Currently the United States has 145% tariffs on Chinese imports.

Over the past ten years, the annual US import volume from China has varied between $448 billion and $576 billion. On average it’s about $500 billion as a round number.

145% tariffs on $500 billion in imports (for exemptions) would effectively be a $700+ billion tax increase. That’s a *massive* negative shock to the economy and financial markets if it remains in place, although the goal is for that to be a negotiation point to de-escalate from. China also has triple-digit retaliatory tariffs in place. China can also respond by selling Treasuries to further disrupt the US bond market, if they should choose to do so. In addition, they have a large portion of the rare earth materials market and have already signaled a willingness to use that as a retaliation tool.

In reality, it won’t likely be a tax increase that large, because trade volumes will likely shrink and enter recession if tariffs of that magnitude are left in place. Those supply chains can’t move overnight, so it’s not as though they can just teleport over to Vietnam or to the United States. It takes years to build up an industrial base (and energy and logics infrastructure to support it) of the scale that China has.

In addition, over the weekend while I’ve been in the process of writing this piece, exemptions already started to trickle in. The United States is exempting phones, computers, and certain other things (particularly in the electronics area) from the extra tariffs.

Here’s a partial list from [Trading Economics](https://tradingeconomics.com/united-states/imports/china) of the categories that the United States imports from:

![China Imports](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-4-13-china-imports.png)

If the remaining tariffs do stay in place, then a key mitigation could include routing Chinese goods through other countries. That would get around tariffs, but would add to logistics costs. The US administration would have to choose whether to identify and push back on that behavior or not. If they want some of that trade to flow and yet still have an optical win, then they might look the other way as that re-routing occurs. On the other hand, they could prioritize stopping that type of re-routing if they wanted to.

Stephen Miran, the president’s chair of the Council of Economic Advisors, gave a talk a week ago where he basically said the United States is asking for tribute from other countries now. The White House posted the transcript on [their website](https://www.whitehouse.gov/briefings-statements/2025/04/cea-chairman-steve-miran-hudson-institute-event-remarks/):

> Moreover, it is critical not just for fairness, but for capacity.  We are under siege by hostile adversaries trying to erode our manufacturing and defense industrial base and disrupt our financial system; we will be able to provide neither defense nor reserve assets if our manufacturing capacity is hollowed out.  The President has been clear that the United States is committed to remaining the reserve provider, but that the system must be made fairer.  We need to rebuild our industries to project the strength needed to protect reserve status, and we need to be able to pay our bills to do so.
>
> What forms can that burden sharing take?  There are many options, here are a few ideas:
>
> * First, other countries can accept tariffs on their exports to the United States without retaliation, providing revenue to the U.S. Treasury to finance public goods provision.  Critically, retaliation will exacerbate rather than improve the distribution of burdens and make it even more difficult for us to finance global public goods.
> * Second, they can stop unfair and harmful trading practices by opening their markets and buying more from America;
> * Third, they can boost defense spending and procurement from the U.S., buying more U.S.-made goods, and taking strain off our servicemembers and creating jobs here;
> * Fourth, they can invest in and install factories in America.  They won’t face tariffs if they make their stuff in this country;
> * Fifth, they could simply write checks to Treasury that help us finance global public goods.
>
> -Stephen Miran, April 7, 2025

I don’t have a PhD from Harvard in economics like Miran does, but as a market participant who studies this for a living, I see two flaws here.

The first is that it doesn’t fully address the elephant in the room: trade deficits are what provide dollars to the rest of the world to use the dollar as the reserve currency. Most of the narrative is focused on unfair trade practices (which is not wrong, but rather just incomplete). However, it’s not because Miran doesn’t know about it. He had a [whole paper on it back in November 2024](https://www.hudsonbaycapital.com/documents/FG/hudsonbay/research/638199_A_Users_Guide_to_Restructuring_the_Global_Trading_System.pdf), which was very detailed and which I’ve shared before. In that paper, he specifically focused on the potential for 1) intentional dollar devaluation and 2) putting a tax on reserve assets to actively *discourage* foreign countries from accumulating US assets as reserves above a certain point. In other words, he called for a partial reduction in the dollar’s role as a reserve currency, especially for countries viewed as adversaries. J.D. Vance has also outright questioned whether the dollar as reserve currency still benefits the United States. At the same time, however, the official 2024 GOP Platform (Promise #13) was to keep the dollar as the global reserve currency, and other members of the administration have proposed finding ways to get the rest of the world to own *more* US assets rather than less.

The second is that the rest of the world is unlikely to take that well, and they do have a lot of leverage. For example, China has about $3.6 trillion in annual exports, and only about $500 billion go to the United States. That’s why the latest move by the administration is to try to de-escalate with the rest of the world and try to get everyone to target China together. That’s not an easy gambit to win, so it’ll be the key thing to watch in the weeks ahead.

There’s a lot to watch in the coming weeks. As it stands, this $700+ billion tax increase (more like $500+ billion after exemptions) is like pulling the emergency brake while speeding down the highway, so if left in place it’ll start having a lot of repercussions. Proposals for tax cuts are unlikely to reach anywhere near $500+ billion, and thus can’t really offset tariffs of this magnitude. So, we’ll probably see some attempts at negotiation between the United States and China, but it’s not happening yet and could take another round of market turmoil to make it happen.

As far as I can tell, the most effective way to address the trade balance issue would be to gradually discourage excessive purchasing of US assets by the foreign sector. In Miran’s paper, he suggested a fee on foreign capital in the United States in order to do that. Along with that would be an approach to encourage a more multi-polar currency world, with for example the Chinese yuan being used as the principle funding currency in developing Asia, rather than the whole world using the dollar as the primary funding currency. But that’s not the process that’s being done, and would basically represent an intentional reduction in the dollar as a share of reserve assets.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin**

Given all this recent volatility, and how many analysts talk about bitcoin’s correlation with the Nasdaq 100, if one were to ask whether bitcoin or the Nasdaq 100 is down more lately, they’d likely be surprised by the answer.

Year to date, bitcoin is roughly even with the Nasdaq 100. Over 6-month, 1-year, 3-year, 5-year, and 10-year periods, bitcoin is outperforming the Nasdaq 100.

The advantage to assets like gold and bitcoin currently is that they’re monies rather than companies, so they don’t have profit margins to worry about in a trade war. Bitcoin still faces challenges due to its perception as a risk-on asset, but it’s holding up well all things considered.

## Final Thoughts: Gold $3.2k

> I still think there’s a decent probability that looking back 5+ years from now, gold will have outperformed the S&P 500 during that time. If anything, the gold/SPX ratio looks like a gradual bottom forming.
>
> Another way to explore that possibility is to flip the chart around, and look at the SPX/gold ratio, which is basically the S&P 500 priced in ounces of gold rather than in dollars:
>
> ![SPX to Gold](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-3-16-spx-gold.png)
>
> In that sense, we’d be asking whether the S&P 500 is at risk of its third major bear market against gold within the past 50 years. The first one was in the 1970s. Then there was a second one in the 2000s. And lately it’s been gradually rolling over for a third time. Could a combination of tariffs, global capital flow shifts, and broad trade re-alignments result in another 5+ year bear market in that chart?
>
> It’s certainly possible. I’d err toward it happening.
>
> -March 16th 2025 premium report

The volatility over this past month has further supported the notion that the S&P 500 is rolling over relative to gold. It has now spent a full decade trending in a choppy sideways direction vs the yellow metal. The ratio will probably bounce at some point, but I think the trend is increasingly clear:
