# Elevated Market Risk Alert

*Published January 26, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

I’m sending out this brief note between regular reports to alert readers of higher-than-normal risk levels in the market at this time.

## Overbought Conditions with a Catalyst

After this multi-month stock market run-up since October, the S&P 500 now has some classic “overbought” conditions on the weekly technical chart according to basic momentum indicators such as the relative strength index (RSI) and moving average convergence divergence (MACD).

I indicated points of interest on this chart below with purple arrows and circles. At the top you can see the RSI, on the bottom you can see MACD, and in the middle you can see the S&P 500 price level itself:

![Technicals January 2020](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-1-26-technicals.png)

*Chart Source: StockCharts.com*

When markets go up sharply for a sustained amount of time without taking a break, they eventually “exhaust” the buyers, meaning that everyone who is buying is already doing so, and there aren’t enough remaining market participants to convert into fresh buyers for new highs.

As of this last week, the S&P 500 is at the most overbought level on the weekly chart since January 2018.

On Friday, worsening news of the coronavirus outbreak from the city of Wuhan in China triggered the S&P 500 to sell off, which made Friday the biggest down day for quite some time (even though it was only down -0.90%).

This doesn’t mean the market has to correct next week. In fact, it was about as overbought last week as it was back in November 2017 on that chart above. The forward result back in November 2017 was the market was able to rally for another three months into extremely overbought January 2018 conditions before experiencing a sharp correction. The market can often push things further than investors expect before reverting towards the mean.

However, I want to make sure members, especially ones that want to be more conservatively-positioned, are aware of this riskier-than-normal setup that exists at the moment.

## Wuhan Coronavirus Outbreak

Over the past week, news about a significant coronavirus outbreak, emanating from the city of Wuhan in China, began making front page international headlines. It is being compared to the SARS outbreak of 2003, but China is a much larger component of the world economy now, and with more international travel.

Earlier in the week, headlines suggested that the virus was being contained. By Friday, however, a couple confirmed cases were reported in the United States, as well as in Thailand, Japan, Australia, and other regions. News out of China became more dire, with well over a thousand confirmed cases, triple the amount from a few days prior.

China responded by quarantining the whole city of Wuhan, which has a population roughly equivalent to New York City, and is linked by high speed rail to other major cities throughout China. Since then, China has quarantined a number of other cities as well, putting the total number of quarantined people in the tens of millions. Because the virus has a long period of dormancy before showing symptoms, we won’t find out for weeks to what extent this virus has actually spread.

As of today (Sunday), the number of confirmed cases continued to jump compared to Friday, and it is widely believed to be under-reported by China’s state-controlled media.

Major virus outbreaks tend to negatively impact markets for a number of weeks, and rarely have sustained effects. This virus is likely to negatively impact China’s GDP, which then could ripple to the various economies that it is strongly tied to, including Europe, the United States, Australia, and multiple Asian countries, but if it is anything like previous headline-grabbing outbreaks over the past few decades, its impact should be temporary.

However, there is always the tail risk possibility that this one is particularly bad. With virus outbreaks, it’s impossible to say ahead of time. From a market perspective, the fact that these virus headlines coincided with overbought conditions in the S&P 500 and triggered a significant down day in the index is a combination that elevates risk at this moment.

When the market is highly-valued fundamentally and overbought technically, all it takes is some random piece of unexpected bad news to serve as a catalyst for a significant correction, to let some of the steam out of the market. There is a good chance we’ll see a gap down on Monday, and whether dip-buyers will swoop in to sustain the current bull market remains to be seen.

I have no plans to make any significant portfolio changes, because I’m already positioned comfortably for my situation. However, members that are perhaps very aggressively positioned may want to do a risk-assessment of their portfolio and make sure everything is in order for their unique situations.

As developments occur throughout the week, I might or might not make minor changes as appropriate, so be sure to check the model portfolios (linked to from the members area) if you are interested, and of course we’ll have an update next weekend during the regular report.
