## Macro View

I did some interviews on Yahoo Finance and Fox Business last week, and the primary macro themes I discussed were the following:

1. Economic indicators continue to decelerate towards a recession scenario as the Fed puts disinflationary tightening pressure on the private sector. However, this is somewhat offset or softened so far by ongoing large public fiscal deficits, which are still stimulatory and inflationary as a structural backdrop.
2. The Federal Reserve continues to drain liquidity from the financial system, while the Treasury is offsetting it by adding some liquidity back in. Overall liquidity conditions are likely to trend sideways for a quarter or two.
3. Longer-term, constraints in energy and commodity supply, and ongoing structural fiscal deficits, are likely to generate waves of inflation. As long as these factors persist, whenever we have a period of economic re-acceleration, it’s likely to be accompanied by a resurgence in above-target inflation.

This macro section will dig into some of these items in more detail.

**Liquidity Update**

After a period of falling liquidity during much of 2022, my base case continues to be for a relatively flat and choppy liquidity situation through Q2 2023, which began at the start of Q4 2022. This is because the Fed continues to pull liquidity out week after week with quantitative tightening, while the Treasury is putting liquidity in by drawing down its general cash account and will likely continue to do so until the debt ceiling impasse is resolved.

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-domestic-liquidity.png)

My understanding on the matter is that in a situation where they are constrained by the debt ceiling, the U.S. Treasury cuts T-bill issuance but tries to keep note and bond auctions on pace for the sake of predictability. The limited T-bill issuance during this period is likely to prevent reverse repos from going down much if at all, so that component of liquidity is likely to continue to trend sideways for a while.

The recent re-acceleration in inflation and economic growth indicators, meanwhile, has triggered more hawkish language out of Fed officials, along with a more hawkish expectation from the market regarding the direction of the Fed’s rate policy this year. This has contributed to a bounce in the dollar index starting in mid-January, which has dampened global liquidity a bit:

![Dollar Index](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-dollar-index.png)

There has also been a resurgence in Treasury yields, a slight worsening of Treasury auction performance, and generally a resurgence in risks of instability in the long-duration Treasury market similar to the dynamic that was occurring in late Q3 2022, but so far less severe.

![10-Year Treasury Yield](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-treasury-yield.png)

**Inflation: Two Opposing Forces**

The majority of new broad money created over the past three years was from fiscal stimulus rather than bank lending.

![Sources of Money Creation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/february-2023-newsletter-sources-of-money-creation-rolling-average-chart.png)

A burst of fiscal stimulus was given to the economy in 2020 and 2021, but even in 2022 and 2023 the structural fiscal deficits continue to be large and will continue to grow larger. This is basically an ongoing inflationary stimulus, and higher interest rates increase those fiscal deficits and contribute to even more inflationary pressure from those ongoing deficits being spent into the economy. The following chart is from the [CBO in February 2023](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/58848), and assumes lower interest rates and no recessions for the foreseeable future.

![Public Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-public-debt.png)

On other hand, the Fed’s tightening of monetary policy has various disinflationary effects, which is the primary purpose for the tightening. Interest rate sensitive industries (e.g. real estate and unprofitable technology firms) have seen sharp slowdowns, job declines, and deflationary asset prices. And this tightening of policy has a lagged effect; quarter after quarter as more business and consumer debts mature, they’ll need to be refinanced at these higher rates.

![Deflationary Housing](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-deflationary-housing.png)

When we also consider constraints in the labor supply (including a likely peak in the rate of globalization) and tight commodity supply dynamics, this is a very tough environment for central bankers to deal with. So far, central bankers are dealing with inflation as though it is primarily a problem of too much bank lending and demand (i.e. like the 1970s). But, by treating it like that with increases in the interest rate as the primary tool, they exacerbate the fiscal-driven inflation which is actually the major source of money creation these days (much like the 1940s) now that debts, deficits, and interest rates are all quite high.

My base case since [mid-2022](https://www.lynalden.com/may-2022-newsletter/) has been that the Fed will tighten into a disinflationary U.S. recession, and then the next growth cycle will see a resurgence in inflation (“the second wave”) because the underlying problems (structural and widening fiscal deficits along with tight commodity supplies) will remain unresolved. And so far, that’s the path we are on. Since mid-2022, the yield curve has inverted, the purchasing manager’s index has fallen from 55 to 47, and most economic indicators in general have decelerated towards stall-speed.

It’s possible, however, that the larger fiscal deficits due to higher interest expense from all of the Fed’s tightening on the public sector, along with China’s economic re-opening, will be inflationary enough to override the disinflation that the Fed’s tightening is causing in the private sector even in the intermediate term. If that’s the case, then disinflation will be shorter-lived and less deep, and the second wave of inflation will be pulled forward. That’s not my base case yet, but it’s a possibility that I’m watching for. The re-acceleration in inflation indicators and economic indicators over the past couple months offers a glimmer of this scenario, but now the Fed will be more hawkish to try to keep that scenario from occurring.

The 2-year Treasury yield had previously fallen below the Fed’s interest rate, but during this recent re-acceleration of inflation it reached new cycle highs and (along with the eurodollar curve) implies further Fed rate hikes ahead:

![Short-Term Treasuries](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-short-term-treasuries.png)

**U.S. Business Cycle Update**

Here is my updated heatmap of various economic indicators for the U.S. economy over the past five years, measured in year-over-year percent change terms. Rather than describing the economy in narratives or qualitative terms, or cherry pick data to fit a particularly bullish or bearish view, it quantifies a broad section of data.

The economic cycle peaked in year-over-year rate-of-change terms in Q2 2021, and since then has been decelerating but still generally positive.

![US Economic Heatmap](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-us-economic-heatmap.png)

The table starts from the bottom and goes up over five years, and each column represents one of the economic indicators.

For most of the table, I use a green/yellow/red color spectrum. Green indicates solid growth, yellow indicates medium, and orange/red indicate slow growth or contraction, relative to the rest of the 5-year period for that indicator. In other words, green is good and red is bad.

For inflation CPI, there is a separate blue/white/red color scale, because high or low inflation in a vacuum is not necessarily “good” or “bad” in the same way as the rest of the heat map. Red indicates higher price inflation, white indicates medium, and blue is lower price inflation, relative to the 5-year period. Economic slowdowns tend to be dis-inflationary while economic booms tend to be pro-inflationary, but there are also some business cycles that slow down with high inflation, and thus become “stagflationary”. So, inflation gets its own color scheme.

**Heat Map Indicators Referenced:**

* Real Gross Domestic Product
* Industrial Production
* Advance Retail Sales, Excluding Food Services
* Total Construction Spending
* Capital Goods Orders, Nondefense, Excluding Aircraft
* Exports of Goods and Services
* Imports of Goods and Services
* All Employees, Total Nonfarm
* 10 Year Minus 3 Month Yield Curve
* Consumer Prices, All Urban Consumers, All Items in City Average

Note that some of these are **nominal** figures. Retail sales, construction spending, capital goods orders, exports, and imports, are all measured in nominal dollars, and thus have a significant inflation component. Retail sales growth at 3.90% year-over-year, construction spending at 5.74%, and capital goods orders at 4.26% all seem like fine numbers, until we factor in 6.35% year-over-year CPI, which puts those three indicators negative in inflation-adjusted terms. The economy, in many aspects, is operating at stall speed or less. The labor market remains the primary source of strength, although it is generally a lagging indicator.

In my view, the difference between nominal and real (inflation-adjusted) figures is confusing a lot of analysts since it makes things harder to analyze. For stock prices, things like nominal earnings and revenues are what matter. But for measuring the rate-of-change of an economy, the real figures are more useful. Most data, such as PMI data and the yield curve suggests that the sluggish real numbers are giving a better insight into how the economy is performing.

The U.S. purchasing manager’s index had a small bounce in January that is worth watching (in line with the overall inflation re-acceleration theme) but it remains sub-50, which means contractionary:

![U.S. PMI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-us-pmi.png)

The yield curve, meanwhile, remains deeply inverted:

![Yield Curve](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-yield-curve.png)

The Atlanta Fed’s GDPNow estimate suggests 2.3% annualized real GDP growth for Q1 2023. Their calculation seems high to me based on most indicators I track, other than the labor market.

![GDP Now](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-gdp-now.png)

Overall, most economic indicators point towards continued economic deceleration, albeit with rather high/sticky nominal numbers due to the prevalence of inflation. This is an environment where topline revenue numbers are likely to be decent but earnings and margins are likely to be under pressure.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin is back above its average on-chain cost basis (i.e. “realized price”), and has a constructive HODL-wave setup, meaning that a significant percentage of the supply is held by accumulators that tend not to sell (as most of the leveraged tourists have left the space by this point).

During the next period of rising demand, it’ll likely take pretty high prices to pry this supply out of their hands, as was the case in prior cycles. The available liquid supply for new buyers will be pretty tight, in other words.

![Bitcoin Charts](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-bitcoin-charts.png)

*Chart Source: [Look Into Bitcoin](https://www.lookintobitcoin.com/)*

In terms of Bitcoin ecosystem development, a lot of work is being done. Lightning infrastructure improvements, multi-signature management improvements, Chaumian ecash layer implementations, and the adjacent Nostr protocol, are some of the major areas of work. I joined an episode of *Bitcoin Review* last week where they go over various tech updates, and the list of recent updates ended up being too long to cover everything within two hours.

In my advisory work at [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/portfolio), I get to talk to a lot of founders and see some of the early technical trends that are happening at seed-level companies.

I view the current period as a good long-term accumulation zone for bitcoin as a part of a portfolio, [especially for dollar-cost averaging](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/), with the caveat that buyers should be prepared for considerable volatility ahead and maintain appropriate position sizes. Due to its perception as a risk-on asset, I doubt we’ll get the next major bull run in bitcoin until we get a period of broadly rising liquidity and economic activity (i.e. a major rising PMI period), but in the meantime it can consolidate or chop around in a rather volatile range.

Silvergate Bank (SI) is a bank that does a lot of business with cryptocurrency companies, such as exchanges, including formerly with FTX. After a long price decline, Silvergate’s stock crashed last week from over $13 to under $6 due to an announcement that they are delaying their annual filing. Custodia is a state-charted bank run by Caitlin Long, and she provided correct and repeated warnings about crypto industry debt contagion to regulators and the public for the past few years, but it didn’t stop her bank from being denied access to the Fed.

For the most part, I continue to view the broad cryptocurrency space outside of bitcoin and stablecoins as being generally unappealing from an investment perspective, fraught with risk and ongoing regulatory concerns. There is interesting work being done with regards to the prospect for asset tokenization by a number of cryptocurrency developers and platforms, but broadly speaking, frauds and malinvestment are the norm rather than the exception within the industry.

**Nostr Update**

Nostr is an open-source protocol that enables people to build decentralized social media client applications for it.

It stands for “Notes and Other Stuff Transmitted by Relays”, and rather than a central company running servers for the ecosystem, many different people run servers (called “relays”) that speak to each other with this common protocol (as in, they “relay information” to each other). People can choose which relays to connect with, and can connect with many of them. Some of them are free, and others require a small fee.

Right now, the most popular client app for Nostr is called Damus for the Apple App Store, but there are also browser clients and Android clients. There is no “crypto token” associated with any aspect of the protocol, or any of the clients so far. Adoption of the protocol by users is organic rather than speculation-driven, since there’s nothing to speculate on.

Clients and relays instead integrate with the Bitcoin Lightning network in rather interesting ways. Premium relays accept payment over the Lightning network, and users can easily send each other payments using the Lightning network. In other words, the protocol is not built on any sort of blockchain, does not issue unregistered securities in the form of tokens, but does make use of the leading decentralized internet money to enhance its capabilities. Many Nostr client apps recently integrated “zaps” in the second half of February, which are transparent Lightning payments that can be counted and displayed on a post in a similar way that “likes” can.

In other words, zaps are social microtips using native bitcoin that can easily flow between multiple different external custodial and non-custodial Lightning wallet implementations across Nostr (unlike Twitter Coins which are just closed internal ledger tokens), and their usage has been taking off:

![Nostr Zaps](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-nostr-zaps.png)

*Chart Source: [Nostr.Band](https://stats.nostr.band/)*

The Lightning network in general continues to experience rapid growth, and this activity on Nostr over the past few months has been additive to it. It’ll be interesting to see what March data looks like in particular, because that will be the first month where “zaps” were implemented on Nostr clients at the start of the month.

## Midstream Opportunities Deep Dive Analysis

Blue-chip energy pipelines have basically been my benchmark investment comparison for the past year or more. Enterprise Products Partners LP (EPD) pays a safe 7%+ distribution yield, has a quarter century of consecutive annual distribution growth, is assigned industry-leading BBB+ credit ratings, is trading at below-average valuations, and has significant insider ownership.

In order for a stock to interest me for a five-year holding period from current levels, it has to compare somewhat favorably to EPD on a risk/reward basis, and in my view not many stocks do these days. My base case is that it’ll grind sideways-to-up in the years ahead while paying a hefty quarterly distribution along the way.

![EPD FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-epd-fastgraph.png)

*Chart Source: F.A.S.T. Graphs*

An important aspect of EPD is that its debt profile is rather low interest and long duration. The average duration is 20.3 years, and the average interest rate is 4.6%. This debt financed their asset side, which consists of hard infrastructure assets (e.g. pipelines) that generally increase their fees each year and have a high cashflow yield. This chart of their debt profile is from their February 2023 investor deck:

![EPD Debt Maturity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-epd-debt-maturity.png)

**The Midstream Realignment**

Throughout the 2000s and into the 2010s decades, there was a massive energy infrastructure build-out in the United States. This was financed by midstream pipeline companies continually issuing both debt and equity, and paying distributions to existing unitholders out of that ongoing massive debt and equity issuance. Many pipeline companies got over their skis with this approach, and when the 2015 oil crash hit, available capital dried up for the industry, and many of them ran into problems with refinancing this Ponzi-like financing strategy.

When that happened, even though many midstream companies didn’t take a very big revenue hit, they did take a huge hit to the total market demand for their debt and equity. In particular, with much lower equity valuations, their strategy of constantly issuing new equity didn’t make financial sense anymore. Instead, many pipelines had to abruptly cut their distributions, slow down or eliminate their equity issuance, and become more self-financing in general.

Some of the better-run companies like EPD and MMP had the foresight to do this gradually in advance, and thus sidestepped the problem. They never got over their skis, they bought out their incentive distribution rights before they became a burden for limited partners, and they purposely slowed down their distribution growth to achieve a lower distribution payout ratio, which allowed them to limit or eliminate the need for ongoing equity issuance by becoming more self-financing from these existing operating cashflows. Due to this, they’ve maintained industry-leading credit ratings and never had to perform distribution cuts. At the cost of slower distribution growth during this process, investors now get 1.9x distribution coverage and no more net unit issuance.

![EPD Distribution Coverage](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-epd-distribution-coverage.png)

![EPD Shares Outstanding](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-epd-shares.png)

Companies that didn’t have the foresight to do this gradually, instead did it the hard way, by cutting their distribution and sharply entering a self-financing type of strategy out of acute necessity. Some of them blew up their distributions in 2015, and other ones held on longer and blew up in 2020. But now, after those blow-ups, the industry is rather washed out, with more attractive valuations and more attractive self-financing capital structures.

The U.S. midstream industry is mature and slow-growing now, more like utilities (but at better valuations and with higher yields). Whenever there are few growth prospects, midstream companies can pay out special distributions or perform some unit buybacks. Whenever there are growth prospects around the margins, they can allocate capital to them for a slow rate of growth.

One of the key areas of remaining growth is export infrastructure for liquified natural gas, because U.S. natural gas trades at a sharp discount to global prices, and so there is a big incentive to arbitrage it and meet that supply/demand difference. Here’s a good chart on U.S. LNG export capacity from EPD’s February 2023 investor deck:

![LNG Export Capacity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-lng-export-capacity.png)

Europe and many emerging markets very much need this expansion in U.S. LNG exports. After the loss of Russian gas, European countries managed to avert a consumer-level heating crisis this past winter partially due to enjoying unusually warm temperatures, but also because energy-intensive industries in Europe sharply drew down their production into deep recessionary levels:

![German Industrial Output](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-german-output.png)

European countries scrambled for energy imports all through 2022, including bidding for a large share of global LNG capacity, and this squeezed a number of developing markets into rolling blackouts due to an inability to compete with Europeans on price to keep the lights on.

Meanwhile, some of Europe’s chemical production and energy-intensive manufacturing is permanently moving elsewhere, and how much will move will depend in large part on the continent’s ability to regain cheap energy inputs. As the [FT reported a week ago](https://www.ft.com/content/b0b2b2c2-ee63-4989-afab-6882feab4b73):

> BASF is to wind down several of its plants in Germany as the world’s largest chemical group by revenue struggles with high energy costs in Europe and plans increased production in China.

An uptick in U.S. LNG exports is one of the main variables over the next several years to smooth some of this out and prevent severe energy crises from re-occurring each year.

**Midstream Risk Analysis and Mitigation**

Overall, I view select midstream businesses such as Enterprise Products Partners and Energy Transfer to be attractive 5-year risk/reward opportunities. There are, however, a few key risks to monitor.

*Geopolitical risk:* Any protectionist legislation that would limit or block LNG or other energy exports from the United States to Europe and other regions can impair the growth prospects of pipeline companies in the United States. If the legislation is draconian enough, it could disrupt existing cashflow as well.

*Production decline risk:* Shale oil and gas production in the United States come with increasing decline rates, meaning it takes more drilling for the same amount of oil and gas. If shale oil and gas production in the United States tops out faster than expected, it limits the overall growth prospects for the pipeline industry.

*Disaster risk:* Any natural or man-made disaster that damages a significant part of a midstream company’s asset base can severely impact their cash flows and investment quality.

*Interest rate risk:* A rapid rise in interest rates and a sharp increase in credit spreads could make refinancing hard. This is less of an issue for EPD due to their super-long average duration, and ET has it under control as well, but if the refinancing rates get high enough it can eat into cash flow to a certain degree.

*Tax complexity:* While this isn’t a risk per se, investors should note that owning EPD or ET or similar master limited partnerships generally results in dealing with an extra tax form during tax season, and may present some issues for foreign investors. These are not c-corps. This keeps a lot of traders out, and currently results in lower average valuations and higher distribution yields for new capital that enters them.

Some of these risks can be mitigated through diversification. As bullish as I am on EPD and ET, I limit my overall exposure to a reasonable percentage of a total portfolio, by owning other equities, other asset types entirely, and cash-equivalents. Other risks can be mitigated by owning complimentary assets. For example, oil and gas producers with low decline rates (e.g. Canadian Natural Resources and Suncor of Canada) are useful assets to own to compliment exposure to the U.S. oil and gas ecosystem, since they would generally benefit from any stagnation or decline in U.S. oil and gas production.

## Final Thoughts: A Three-Pillar Portfolio

If an investor expects a return to structural disinflationary growth this decade, then something akin to a 60/40 stock/bond portfolio makes sense. A portfolio filled with large growth-oriented equities and long-duration bonds can smooth out volatility and grind higher in that type of disinflationary environment.

However, if an investor expects higher resource costs and higher labor costs as a sticky trend this decade, along with the backdrop of structurally widening fiscal deficits that are likely to be a source of ongoing broad money creation without the various disinflationary offsets of the past few decades, then a portfolio geared more towards inflation makes sense. This is in line with my outlook.

Historically in inflationary periods, a combination of 1) value stocks, 2) commodity exposure, and 3) cash-equivalents has held up better than a portfolio of growth-oriented equities and long-duration Treasuries. In other words, it’s useful to replace a 60/40 portfolio with a “three pillar” portfolio in that type of environment.

**Pillar 1) Value/Dividend Stocks**

Growth-oriented equities with high valuations generally do well in environments of low interest rates and disinflation. That backdrop is what allows them to have high valuations and low costs of capital.

In a backdrop of more inflationary pressures and higher costs of capital, cheap and profitable companies (“value stocks”) generally hold up better, and even more-so if they pay a safe high dividend yield to shareholders. I shared this following chart from Real Investment Advice back in my [October 2021 newsletter](https://www.lynalden.com/october-2021-newsletter/), and as we can see, the 1940s inflationary decade, the 1970s inflationary decade, and the 2000s pseudo-inflationary decade were all accompanied by value stock outperformance. The major decades of growth outperformance were the disinflationary 1930s decade and the disinflationary 2010s decade:

![Real Investment Advice](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/newsletter-2021-10-real-investment-advice.png)

*Chart Source: [Michael Lebowitz, Real Investment Advice](https://realinvestmentadvice.com/the-future-promise-of-value-versus-the-allure-of-growth/)*

We should notice that the 1930s and 2010s decades when growth stocks outperformed, were the decades right after the popping of a major private debt bubble, where interest rates went to zero for a while:

![Century Chart Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-2-19-century-chart-annotated.png)

If we expect the 2020s decade to continue to look more like the 1940s decade with significant fiscal-driven inflation, varying degrees of geopolitical de-globalization, and resource constraints, then we should likely expect continued value stock outperformance on a total return basis. Energy and commodity producers, energy pipelines, banks, various manufacturing businesses, select emerging markets, and dividend-paying healthcare companies at price/earning ratios below 15x are all reasonably well-positioned for that type of environment.

That doesn’t mean growth stocks need to be avoided entirely. I think Adobe (ADBE) for example is likely to perform decently over the next 5-10 years. It just means that growth stocks need to be selected more carefully because the overall trend will not be in their favor.

**Pillar 2) Commodities and Hard Monies**

The big winner in inflationary decades tends to be commodities and their producers. This chart I put together a while ago shows the rolling 5-year performance of the oil price relative to the U.S. consumer price index through the end of 2022:

![Oil and CPI](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-oil-cpi.png)

During disinflationary periods, when stocks are doing poorly, long-duration Treasuries are usually doing pretty well, and vice versa. However, during inflationary periods, stocks and bonds can both struggle at the same time, as higher input costs squeeze corporate profit margins and lead to higher interest rates, which pushes down both equity and bond valuations. If stocks and bonds are going down together (not a very common occurrence), it’s often because commodities are going up.

This commodity pillar of a portfolio can also include alternative monies with low supply growth rates, such as gold and/or bitcoin. These non-cashflow monetary assets can be self-custodied and offer protection against ongoing money supply growth and the associated currency debasement that it leads to, but can face sharp corrections during periods of time where central banks fight back and try to tighten up their currencies.

**Pillar 3) Cash-Equivalents**

Recessions still occur in inflationary decades, and they might or might not be stagflationary, depending on the causes of the recession. Cash equivalents are likely to lose purchasing power over the course of a decade with negative inflation-adjusted yields, but an allocation to cash equivalents can reduce portfolio volatility and offer the opportunity for rebalancing during broad market sell-offs or disinflationary periods.

T-bills and other short-term types of cash-equivalents don’t face the same pricing risk as long-duration bonds, and continually price themselves higher when central banks are trying to tighten up their currencies. For example, the SDPR 1-3 Month T-Bill ETF (BIL) was a lousy holding when interest rates were zero, but it does quite well in periods of rising rates or high-plateau rates. This type of asset can serve as a ballast for a portfolio when market conditions are choppy and rates are being maintained at high levels. If the Fed continues to fight 1940s-style inflation with 1970s-style monetary policy, then T-bills are probably a decent place to park some capital.

![BIL ETF](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2023-3-5-bil-etf.png)

Other relatively short duration Treasury assets such as the iShares 0-5 Year TIPS Bond ETF (STIP) and the iShares 1-3 Year Treasury ETF (SHY) have a bit more volatility, but can serve similar overall roles in a portfolio.

Overall, I expect this type of three-pillar portfolio to perform well if indeed this decade trends towards higher average inflation than the 2010s decade. Value and dividend-paying stocks for consistent income and mild growth, scarce commodities and monies as protection against currency debasement, and short-term Treasuries or money market cash equivalents as a volatility-reducing ballast during counter-trends, can work together to minimize risk and gradually grind higher in terms of purchasing power.
