## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Domestic U.S. liquidity has been rangebound since bottoming between 2022Q4 and 2023Q1. I expect this trend to continue for a while longer in 2024, and then expect to see liquidity break to the upside in 2025 as the Fed ends its quantitative tightening by that point. However, I will continue to monitor to see if that timeline remains in place or needs to be updated.

![Bank Cash Annotated](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-bank-cash-annotated.png)

The Congressional Budget Office [recently revised](https://www.cbo.gov/publication/60039) their federal deficit projections from $1.5 trillion to $1.9 trillion in 2024. This will likely mean that the Treasury’s borrowing estimates will increase in the coming quarters, which means a lot of bond supply coming to market.

However, the CBO expects a mildly shrinking deficit during the 2025-2027 timeframe specifically, in that they think there will be a three-year period of shrinking deficits until ultimately deficits go back to increasing indefinitely thereafter. The reason for this is that they expect pretty strong tax revenues during that three-year period, and they expect interest rates to go down which means less interest expense for the government.

While I do think interest rates will likely go down a bit in that period, I’m less optimistic than the CBO on the idea of a shrinking deficit as a whole for 2025-2027. This is because tax revenues are so closely tied to asset price performance in the United States, and I think that in the 2025-2027 period there is a moderately high chance of there being a malaise in stock prices among many of the mega-cap stocks. Furthermore, the CBO never includes the possibility of a recession in their analysis; they always look out ten years and assume no recessions even though recessions usually occur with more frequency than that.

However, if we 1) avoid a recession and 2) stock prices continue to jump higher while 3) the Fed is able to trim interest rates, then that’s a scenario where the CBO would likely be correct. The outcome of all three of those things occurring together is certainly possible, but is outside of my base case. The other big factor to watch is that by late 2025, a number of tax cuts that were enacted during the Trump Administration expire, and so unless those are extended, there is a possibility for the government to earn a bit more revenue there.

Meanwhile, measures of global liquidity continue to trend sideways, as gradual credit creation is offset by central banks’ quantitative tightening and a strong dollar index. Similar to my view on domestic liquidity, I expect this rangebound state of global liquidity to continue for a while longer, and then to start to break out to the upside later in 2024 or in 2025:

![Global M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-global-m2.png)

In the meantime, the dollar/yen pair is inching back up to levels for which Japan’s government sharply intervened last time, which refers to selling tens of billions of dollars worth of U.S. cash or bonds to buy back the yen. As long as the current rate differential exists as wide as it does now, and with the Fed doing quantitative tightening, these sorts of actions remain on the table.

**U.S. Business Cycle Update**

Since the Fed began their monetary tightening plan in early 2022, the economy of the United States has had very conflicting outcomes for its various sectors.

As I’ve highlighted, a lot of this is because the combination of loose fiscal policy (i.e. large federal deficits) and tight monetary policy (i.e. positive real rates and the fastest tightening cycle in modern history) makes it so that different sectors can have very different experiences based on whether they are primarily affected by the deficits or the interest rates. And because the government itself is so indebted now (unlike many prior business cycles), when the Fed raises interest rates it further increases the fiscal deficit with tons of interest expense, which partially counteracts the disinflationary effects of monetary tightening.

Commercial real estate operators, for example, are not really on the receiving side of deficits, but are suffering the brunt of higher interest rates. So they’re in a world of hurt. Businesses in the travel industry, on the other hand, are indirectly on the receiving side of the deficits, since many of them cater to older and/or wealthy travelers who are directly on the receiving side of those deficits and who own a lot of financial assets. They’re doing pretty well.

And so we’ve seen a recession in the manufacturing sector (about a year and a half of PMIs mostly below 50), a recession (and arguably outright depression) in the commercial real estate sector, and a right-sizing in the unprofitable areas of the technology sector outside of the AI industry due to the drying up of zero-rate money. But offsetting all of that, we’ve seen strong labor markets and strong service sectors.

It’s quite possible that this economic softening is starting to meaningfully reach the labor market and certain parts of the service industry. That’s what a preponderance of metrics is starting to show. And to the extent that this is occurring, it seems to be starting mostly from the bottom up, which makes sense given this mix of policies. In other words, those without much assets are showing increasing signs of financial distress, while those with plenty of assets and either low debt or long duration fixed-rate debt are holding up better for now.

The official unemployment rate has gradually increased from 3.4% to 4.0%. That’s a 0.6% increase, and historically a 0.5% increase in the unemployment rate has continued to feed on itself most of the time. As fewer people work, consumption decreases, and so employers hire less, and so consumption decreases further, etc:

![Unemployment Rate](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-unemployment-rate.png)

The absolute number of adults employed full-time decreased in Q1 2024, although different methods of data collection lead to somewhat different results. I suspect that this gap between different measures of employment is due to a rise of self-employment and gig-work in the post-pandemic economy, and of course some of these metrics are subject to revision in subsequent months and quarters.

![Employed Full Time](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-employed-full-time.png)

The number of unemployment claims was falling during 2020 and 2021 after the initial lockdown-related unemployment spike in early 2020. The unemployment claims then bottomed in 2022, and gradually began rising into mid-2023. Since mid-2023, unemployment claims began trending sideways. There’s been a bit of a spike lately, so this is a metric I’ll be watching over the coming month to see if it breaks out or continues to trend sideways:

![Continued Claims](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-continued-claims.png)

Overall, I continue to view the ongoing fiscal deficits as being stimulatory for parts of the economy that are receiving them, but the tightness of the monetary policy seems to be starting to accumulate enough to hit parts of the labor market and service sector. In my view, this raises the probability that the top in interest rates is in, and that the Fed will indeed be trimming interest rates by the end of the year, but this requires more monitoring. If this ends up being the case, it would correlate with the aforementioned expectation of a breakout in global liquidity in late 2024 or in 2025.

**FOMC Actions**

The Federal Reserve’s FOMC had their most recent meeting from June 11-12, and this constitutes their fourth of eight meetings that they will have this year. Their decision was to keep interest rates at the level they have been, which is between 5.25% and 5.50%.

They also updated their [forward projections](https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/fomcprojtabl20240612.htm). Compared to March (the last time they did projections), they increased their expectations for inflation in 2024 and 2025, and increased their longer run unemployment expectations as well. For interest rates, they increased their expectations both in the intermediate term and in the longer run.

![Fed Projections](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-fed-projections.png)

Overall, this meeting leaned hawkish. However, it also shows how uncertain Fed officials currently are as a group. In their dot plot for 2025 and 2026, for example, the difference between the highest and lowest projection for interest rates was 250 basis points (2.5%), which is a huge margin of difference.

Additionally, when forecasting or responding to inflation, the Fed will likely ignore idiosyncratic or geopolitical events. The rate of U.S. bank lending is already low, bank liquidity conditions are treading water on the bottom of the workable range, and much of the current rate of credit creation is from the federal government which is not sensitive to interest rates. The Fed will likely take this as a “job well” done from their perspective, and not over-tighten based on things outside of their control. That seems to be the prevailing view ever since the Q1 2023 regional bank liquidity crisis.

For example, ongoing geopolitical conflicts are resulting in more trade frictions, and container shipping rates are spiking again due to instability in the Red Sea, which is similar to what happened during the pandemic lockdowns. These are largely outside of the Fed’s purview:

![Containership Rates](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-containership-rates.png)

Members of the ECB have outright said things along these lines as it relates to energy pricing as well:

**International Snapshot: Egypt’s Inflation**

Speaking of the Red Sea, I’m in Egypt for the month, having come directly here from Norway. Those that have followed my work for a while know that I come to Egypt each year to visit family and friends, and I have a second home here.

My trips here generally serve as a catalyst to write about something Egypt-related, and more broadly related to emerging or frontier markets in general, since it gives a snapshot of how money works for billions of people worldwide, with macro implications.

Egypt, a country of about 110 million people, has about $4k nominal GDP per capita or $13k when adjusting for purchasing power parity. The country is currently struggling with a 28% official inflation rate, which is down from its recent peak of 38%. This is worse than normal for the country, but looking back over six decades, their inflation rate averages in the low double-digits which is quite high overall:

![Egypt Inflation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-egypt-inflation.png)

This level of inflation is common among developing countries, and if anything the fact that they had no hyperinflations in this long period is somewhat better than average. Dozens of countries have gone though outright hyperinflation or other nonlinear currency resets at least once over this timeframe, but Egypt has not.

Like any country, Egypt’s persistent inflation is caused, in large part, by the country’s rapid money supply growth rate relative to the production of goods and services. Over the past decade, the monetary base (controlled by the central bank) has grown by 16.7% per year on average, while the broad money supply (controlled from a combination of the central bank and commercial banks) has grown by 21.3% per year:

![Egypt M0](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-egypt-m0.png)

![Egypt M2](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-egypt-m2.png)

Additionally, the country has $168 billion in external debt, and much of that is denominated in dollars and other external currency. It has quadrupled under the current administration, as the country’s president el-Sisi (now in his third term) seeks to build roads, bridges, entire new cities, nuclear power facilities, and other mega projects to modernize the country. Many of those projects are indeed helpful for the country, but critics argue that his public works projects focus too much on big visual things (e.g. entire new cities) compared to productivity (e.g. improved manufacturing, education, and healthcare, which are less visible), which reduces the probability that the projects will recoup their external debt costs.

![Egypt External Debt](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-egypt-external-debt.png)

When Egypt’s policymakers run into issues with this strategy, they normally work with the IMF to get assistance, and this generally includes resetting the currency peg to a lower level, ending subsidies to consumers, and so forth. Therefore, these projects are paid out of the pockets of the people, although it’s in large part from opaque currency debasement rather than transparent taxes. They also make deals with the UAE to inject money for real estate development projects at strategically important times for stability.

The Egyptian military (and notably the current president was the former commander-in-chief of the armed forces and was a lifelong military man) owns businesses throughout the economy, ranging from hotels to egg producers to bottled water companies to plastic manufacturers to mining operations to fast food restaurants to gas stations. Military-owned companies are exempt from certain taxes that private sector companies have to pay. Many outside investors and lenders, including UAE investors and the IMF, have pushed Egypt’s leadership to reduce this practice, but with limited results so far.

In my view, reducing or eliminating this intermingling of the military with the economy is the area that would be most ripe for improvement, along with a series of reforms that would attract capital for manufacturing facilities, to help with Egypt’s persistent trade deficit. However, the current military-aligned leadership structure in Egypt has limited incentives to go in that direction.

For an Egyptian citizen, although the country continues to develop over time, monetary issues are an uphill battle to deal with. They don’t really have control over any of this, and yet this is the currency monopoly and economy that they find themselves in. They basically have to hope that the centrally-driven public works plan is reasonably effective. Along the way, it’s rational for them to try to protect 1) their wages and 2) their savings as much as they can.

Firstly, from the perspective of an Egyptian or any one of the billions of people who live in a similar currency environment, keeping wages growing in line with price inflation or money supply growth is like constantly running on a treadmill. If they’re not getting 15%+ wage increases year after year (and most of them are not), then their hourly income is getting diluted away. Many people earn fewer dollar-equivalents or gold-equivalents per hour of their labor than they did a decade ago. The ability to import things or to travel abroad are the hardest things to keep up with, and many people find themselves having to [replace gold jewelry gifts with silver ones](https://www.reuters.com/markets/commodities/silver-is-new-gold-egyptians-try-protect-savings-2024-02-08/) or having more trouble affording a car, which is not a great sign for true per-capita development. They look around and say, “yes, there are more bridges and a new city, and yet I can buy less with my labor than I could a decade ago.” Consequentially, many young people look for jobs abroad, such as in the UAE or Saudi Arabia, but it’s not so easy for most people to get those jobs.

Secondly, saving and investing is a challenge. A significant portion of the country is unbanked, meaning they deal mainly in cash markets, and thus don’t earn interest on what little savings they have. For those that are banked, interest rates on savings accounts and bonds are high but often don’t keep up with inflation.

![Egypt Inflation vs Interest](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-egypt-inflation-vs-interest-annotated.png)

The Egyptian stock market is very volatile and hasn’t quite kept up with money supply growth, which makes it undesirable to a lot of potential investors. The dollar-denominated Egypt ETF was delisted in March of this year by VanEck, after dismal performance over the prior decade.

![EGPT Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-egpt-price.png)

Real estate in Egypt is more understandable and attractive for many people to invest in, with the downside being that it’s illiquid, and when held unlevered it often doesn’t keep up with money supply growth, especially after maintenance and taxes are factored in. Additionally, if one is renting it out rather than living in it, then much like trying to get wage raises that keep up with money supply growth, it’s very difficult to consistently raise rents on tenants at a pace that keeps up with money supply growth, meaning that it’s easy even for landlords to get diluted away over time.

For those that have access to good financing, however, real estate can be pretty attractive. My property here, which was purchased to house our extended family year-round and to house my husband and I when we are here, was financed with a 7-year domestic installment loan at an effective interest rate of about 3% per year compared to if we had bought the property with cash up front, which is pretty nice considering that the money supply is growing by over 21% per year. The rate of return on the home equity can therefore potentially keep up with the money supply growth rate, even as the top-line value of the property mildly undershoots it. Persistent debasement like this often benefits those who have access to the best forms of credit or deferment (which are basically long-term shorts on the value of fiat currency), and harms those who are unbanked or that otherwise have no arbitrages or currency debasement hedges in place.

Egyptians often turn to physical cash dollars or physical gold for liquid savings, and at times of currency stress when the government is holding an untenable currency peg, a large network of informal (“black market”) cash brokers and gold brokers develops to provide genuine price discovery and liquidity for citizens seeking to get a hold of some monetary bearer assets at the real exchange rate. When that happens, the government often clamps down on this network, and turns to other types of capital controls to try to prevent leakage and maintain their currency peg and currency monopoly.

Younger and more tech savvy investors sometimes turn to bitcoin, which has been a major outperformer. Although it hasn’t really caught on at scale here yet, it’s hard for authorities to do much about since it goes around ports of entry and the banking system with relative ease compared to other more physical monies. The Central Bank of Egypt has repeatedly warned people to dissuade them from dealing with cryptocurrencies, but millions of people still do engage with them.

Egypt’s acute currency problems have stabilized recently, thanks to significant investments and credit provisions from the UAE and IMF (given recent issues around Israel nearby, maintaining stability in Egypt is something that a lot of stakeholders in the region consider to be quite important). However, the structural issues remain in place, so in the years ahead I would expect to see additional currency devaluations and external debt refinancing issues, unless the dollar (and by extension their dollar-denominated debt) weakens *considerably*. Furthermore, the country now faces natural gas shortages, and thus power shortages. Those shortages can be addressed over time, but often contribute to the trade deficit.

For Europeans or others that are close by, there are many beautiful beaches in Egypt, considering that it’s bordered by both the Mediterranean Sea and the Red Sea. The Egyptian Mediterranean coast is ideal for swimming and relaxing in summer, and the water of Almaza Bay is of particular beauty. The Egyptian Red Sea coast has particularly warm water, which can make it suitable for an autumn trip, and due to the reefs is ideal for snorkeling or diving and a variety of water sports. The relatively weak currency provides affordable trips to many people in surrounding countries, either to see the ancient ruins or to relax on relatively uncrowded beaches with warm waters.

## Digital Assets Note

**Working off the Heat**

So far, this cryptocurrency bull cycle has narratively revolved around 1) spot bitcoin ETF approvals and inflows, 2) memes and NFTs on top of the Bitcoin network and 3) meme coins more broadly.

Ethereum and similar blockchains have been relatively lackluster, as there has been no “big new narrative” like ICOs or DeFi or NFTs yet this cycle as there was in the 2017 and 2021 cycles.

If anything, the major narrative for them has been that those things have gravitated over to the Bitcoin network around the margins in this cycle, since the introduction of Ordinals, BRC-20 tokens, Runes, and BitVM-based scaling ideas, have increased the design surface for developers to build those types of things on the largest and most secure blockchain. Even on Bitcoin, many of these things have deflated relative to their initial hype period.

Meme coins have had their brief day in the sun in this cycle. Meme coins kind of cut to the chase and skip the whole development of a narrative to focus purely on speculation. Almost every cryptocurrency that gets launched by founders and VCs has a big ramp up and then a drawdown and then never recovers to new highs in bitcoin-denominated terms, and by the next cycle the industry moves onto the next big narrative. The VCs and the founders exit near the top without the project creating any sustainable value, and retail investors get left holding the bag. Meme coins instead skip the narrative and skip the inbuilt founder/VC advantage and get right to the speculation, and basically serve as an online casino. They haven’t had much broad retail investor flows in this cycle so far; it’s mainly been crypto veterans and “degens” and playing around.

The initial hype around the spot Bitcoin ETFs has died down, although I think over the long term the ETFs will continue to add to incremental demand for bitcoin. As a base case, I generally expect the bitcoin price to correlate reasonably well with measures of global liquidity, and ETFs are one way for institutional investors to express that trade.

![Global M2 vs Bitcoin](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-global-m2-vs-bitcoin.png)

Whenever the price rips higher, some percentage of existing long-term holders trim their holdings, either to rebalance their assets or for consumption purposes (e.g. to buy a new house and start a family), and this general rotation cycle after cycle is how bitcoin distributes across the world from the earliest miners and buyers to later adopters.

![Bitcoin HODL Wave](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-bitcoin-hodl-wave.png)

Crypto dollars or “stablecoins” continue to do pretty well. The top two crypto dollar issuers now have a combined market capitalization of over $144 billion, and have high velocity. They meet an existing demand for dollars globally for people in persistently inflationary countries like Argentina and Turkey and Nigeria, and they basically serve as a transformer for T-bills into eurodollars. An easy way to think of stablecoin issuers is that they are offshore bank accounts for the middle class and working class globally, rather than just the wealthy class globally, because they dramatically reduce the overhead costs of giving someone that service compared to what was possible in prior decades.

![USDT Market Capitalization](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-usdt-market-cap.png)

Bitcoin and stablecoins are, in my view, the most utility-driven use case for blockchain technology so far, and for the foreseeable future. Blockchains are basically just decentralized and/or permissionless spreadsheets, and the biggest use case for them is for borderless money.

**SEC Drops Suit Against Consensys**

Consensys is a major company in the Ethereum ecosystem, as a provider for the biggest wallet and node infrastructure service, and its CEO was one of the Ethereum co-founders. Along with the Ethereum Foundation, Consensys serves as one of the major development and leadership hubs for Ethereum as a whole.

The Securities and Exchange Commission had been investigating Consensys for a number of securities-related legal issues. Ethereum by most accounts passed the Howey Test at launch and therefore has many of the hallmarks of an investment security (unlike Bitcoin which does not), but kind of got a pass by regulators for a while and subsequently was grandfathered in relative to newer coins that similarly pass the Howey Test. The Commodities Futures Trading Commission has had Ethereum contracts listed for a while, meaning that the CFTC and SEC have had somewhat of a regulatory jurisdiction conflict that is awkward to resolve. Ethereum 2.0 (Ethereum’s switch from proof-of-work to proof-of-stake) somewhat revived that conflict, and renewed SEC scrutiny toward Ethereum as a whole.

From a technical perspective, older types of securities inherently remain securities indefinitely, whereas technically with blockchains there can be a situation where something starts as a security and then loses many of its security properties over time. In other words, a share of stock can never go from being a security to a commodity, but a software protocol potentially can in theory. The creator can eventually lose control over his or her creation such that it operates without their involvement, even if the method of creation met the definition of a sale of securities at inception.

In recent days, Consensys announced that the SEC *dropped* their investigation into whether Consensys’ sales of ether count as securities transactions. This is in line with recent announcements that Ethereum ETFs are likely to be approved. The CFTC has likely won over the SEC on this front.

However, there is still ongoing investigation into whether staking services on top of Ethereum are securities offerings. The upcoming batch of likely-to-be-approved Ethereum ETFs have stripped staking from their offerings. By most accounts, that’s where it seems the battleground will be for a while longer: staking services potentially being securities.

By most traditional assessments of securities laws, staking services likely are securities. But advocates within the cryptocurrency industry assert that decades-old securities laws are no longer the best regulatory lenses with which to view the landscape, similar to how decades-old broadcasting laws don’t really apply to podcasts and so forth. As the cost of creating a security trends toward zero due to the existence of blockchains, and anyone can do it from any jurisdiction and reach a global audience, it arguably necessitates reshaping how securities regulations works.

**Bitcoin/Crypto and U.S. Politics: Continuing to Amplify**

Over the past couple of months, I’ve been monitoring the bitcoin and cryptocurrency industry’s significant pivot toward supporting former President Donald Trump monetarily and publicly.

Large donors among cryptocurrency companies and bitcoin companies have historically been bipartisan, and still are in the sense that they support representatives and senators across party lines if they are pro-crypto or pro-bitcoin (which in this context often just means not being directly anti-crypto or anti-bitcoin). But in recent months, for the presidential race itself, many large industry players including the head of Bitcoin Magazine (which hosts the world’s largest Bitcoin-related conferences) have endorsed Trump and have raised millions of dollars for his campaign.

I touched on this in my prior report:

> Meanwhile, in recent months cryptocurrency industry executives have raised large amounts of donor capital for former President Trump’s campaign and have spoken with him. Two weeks ago, when Trump attended the Libertarian National Convention, he said he would pardon Ross Ulbricht (the early bitcoin proponent and creator of Silk Road) on the first day of his presidency if he wins, and would protect the right for Americans to self-custody cryptocurrencies and bitcoin. The specific nature and phrasing of these statements indicate direct involvement from the cryptocurrency industry donors.
>
> It remains unclear how the election will go, but we are at the point where the cryptocurrency industry as a whole is at a scale big enough to impact national elections to some degree, including candidate positions and potential voters.
>
> -June 9th, 2024 premium report

For this report, there is another update to that situation. In mid-June, a number of representatives from the largest bitcoin miners met with former President Trump. Trump subsequently posted on his social media platform, Truth Social, that he is supportive of the bitcoin mining industry, and he has since repeated similar messaging at his rallies.

I have a readership that comes from countries around the world and I don’t publicly endorse any political candidates in the United States or other countries. However, when political actions have an impact on investments (either via fiscal decisions, or pivots toward or against a given industry, for some examples), covering these political actions becomes investment-related news and thus something to be written about as part of non-partisan investment research.

In 2021, El Salvador made bitcoin legal tender and began holding bitcoin as a reserve asset. The Kingdom of Bhutan has been mining bitcoin for years with its surplus hydroelectric resources. More recently, the government of Abu Dhabi signed a joint venture with Phoenix Group (one of the world’s largest bitcoin miners), and one of the companies that Abu Dhabi’s sovereign wealth fund invests in has a joint venture with Marathon (another of the world’s largest bitcoin miners). In 2024, Kenya’s Ministry of Energy and Petroleum struck a deal with Marathon to monetize underutilized energy resources across the country.

As the Bitcoin network grows in size, and as its [unusually flexible](https://www.lynalden.com/bitcoin-energy/#wasted) mining operations are increasingly understood to be useful for soaking up stranded (and thus wasted) energy, it’ll likely continue to be endorsed by a number of governments around the world along these lines. Unlike virtually all other forms of energy consumption, bitcoin miners 1) can tolerate high variability and frequent periods of downtime from power sources and 2) can operate with low bandwidth and thus go to small, remote sites where stranded energy often is.

Meanwhile, some governments will of course continue to oppose Bitcoin and similar protocols, as they empower everyday people to avoid currency debasement and move money around and out of restrictive currency monopolies.

Generally speaking, policymakers for jurisdictions such as the UAE where capital actually wants to be will be more likely to welcome bitcoin and bitcoin-related industry, and cryptocurrency industry more broadly. In contrast, policymakers for jurisdictions where capital constantly wants to escape from such as Nigeria will be more likely stuck in a constant game of whack-a-mole to keep their capital controls in place, as ever-improving monetary technologies and ever-improving adoption and understanding of those technologies among their subjects makes it harder and harder for them to do so.

## Three Reasonably Valued Growth Stocks

There has been much written about how the top handful of stocks in the S&P 500 have been responsible for nearly all of the gains of the S&P 500 this year, and more broadly for many years as well.

The S&P 500 equal weight index is still down from its highs of 2023. The Russell 2000 index (U.S. small caps) is still down from its highs of 2021. The MSCI EAFE international developed country index, and the MSCI emerging markets index, are both a bit below their 2007 highs.

In just the past few days, there is a sign of a potential local bottom in value vs growth stocks, but it’s too early to tell yet:

![Value to Growth](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-value-growth.png)

I think this trend could reverse more durably later this year or in 2025, when the U.S. economy slows and the Fed goes back to easing in terms of both interest rates and balance sheet size. As I’ve written about before, the U.S. economy will likely be de-sensitized to interest rate cuts similar to how it was de-sensitized to interest rate hikes. However, the rest of the world, and emerging markets in particular, will still be quite sensitized to interest rate cuts or liquidity injections, and so that’s a potential trend change that I am monitoring.

But in the meantime, it’s U.S. growth stocks that continue to power the indices higher. Some of them have become quite expensive, but other ones are still reasonably priced. So, this report looks at three growth-at-a-reasonable-price “GARP” stocks in U.S. markets today.

**Elevance**

Elevance (ELV) with over 47 million members is the largest for-profit health insurance company within the broader Blue Cross Blue Shield health care association, which has a combined total of over 115 million members. Until about two years ago, Elevance was called Anthem.

![ELV FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-elv-fastgraph.png)

The company trades at a price/earnings ratio of a bit over 15x, and grows its earnings per share in the low double-digits each year. The dividend-adjusted “PEG” ratio is barely over 1x, which is quite cheap. And the company has a solid “A” credit rating by S&P.

So what’s the catch? Mainly, the state of U.S. healthcare is a mess. We have the highest per-capita healthcare costs in the world and are an aging population which will continue to balloon the total costs of healthcare on society, but we don’t have as good longevity or other health metrics as the rest of the developed world. We pay more for the same drugs, and we have higher rates of long-term disease such as diabetes.

The way we eat and the way our healthcare system works combines to make a fiscally challenging situation, but one that is profitable for the stack of drug makers, drug distributors, health insurers, and overall medical complex. So, from the perspective of an investor, it’s a very profitable (albeit messy) situation, unless or until policymakers do a major overhaul on the combined public/private system that is now in place. That risk of an overhaul is ever-present, but the highly polarized nature of U.S. Congress makes it unlikely in any given investable time horizon that a large change will occur.

Overall, I think Elevance is worth holding as part of a diversified healthcare portfolio, but I would keep a limit on how much total healthcare exposure is in a portfolio. Due to where its funding largely comes from, the healthcare industry is very buffered from recession risks, but is more sensitive than most industries to regulation risk. This makes the sector into a useful diversifier.

**Booking Holdings**

Booking Holdings (BKNG) operates Booking.com, Priceline.com, Opentable.com, Agoda.com, Kayak.com, Cheapflights.com, Rentalcars.com, and other online properties.

![BKNG FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-bkng-fastgraph.png)

I consider BKNG to be on the right side of the “fiscal dominance” issue, where higher interest rates pour out to asset holders that on average are older and have both the time and money to spend on travel and eating out. BKNG is an asset-light digital middle-man for that industry, with sizable network effects and a strong balance sheet.

Even as the U.S. economy potentially softens, I doubt it will impact the travel industry much, given all of the structural fiscal offsets.

I’ve been holding and recommending the stock for a while and it keeps making new highs, but those highs continue to be justified by the company’s rising fundamentals. After the post-pandemic rebound, consensus analyst expectations are for 16%+ earnings per share growth going forward, and the stock trades at 24x earnings which gives it a dividend-adjusted “PEG” ratio of under 1.5x.

The balance sheet is strong, with about the same amount of cash-equivalents as it has long-term debt:

![BKNG Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-bkng-balance-sheet.png)

The company recently launched a dividend. The company has been performing consistent buybacks since 2014, so I think it makes quite a bit of sense to have a small dividend as part of the capital return mix.

**Meta and Alphabet**

Meta (META) and Alphabet (GOOGL) are some of the mega-cap U.S. stocks that I don’t think are as expensive as some of their peers are.

If we look at Apple (AAPL) for a moment, they are trading at well over 30x earnings despite anemic growth.

![AAPL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-aapl-fastgraph.png)

Part of the power of Apple stock is their consistent return to shareholders. In 2013 they started using cash flows to buy back shares. They even issued over $100 billion worth of debt to make use of ultra-low interest rates and accelerate their share buybacks. This has been monstrously positive for shareholders, despite lackluster top-line growth:

![AAPL Shares](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-aapl-shares.png)

Meta and Alphabet both trade for under 30x earnings, despite having higher forward earnings per share growth expectations among Wall Street analysts than Apple does.

![META FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-meta-fastgraph.png)

![GOOGL FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-googl-fastgraph.png)

And while Apple has more long-term debt than it has cash equivalents, both Meta and Alphabet have less long-term debt than they have cash equivalents, meaning they are more flush with excess cash.

![AAPL Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-aapl-balance-sheet.png)

![GOOGL Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-googl-balance-sheet.png)

![META Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-meta-balance-sheet.png)

And relatively recently, both of them began transitioning toward a capital return model, with that existing excess cash along with their constant generation of free cash flows.

Alphabet began consistently buying shares back in 2019, and launched a new dividend here in 2024. Meta began consistently buying shares back in 2021, and also launched a new dividend here in 2024. They have firmly transitioned from pure growth stocks to capital returners, which means they have some value characteristics.

The downside for Alphabet and Meta is that they are reliant on advertising revenues, which are considered less robust than other models of profitability. So, one way to mitigate that risk is to spread it out and own smaller positions in both of them. If one of them makes a mis-step (like I would argue Meta was doing a couple of years ago by going too hard into virtual reality and the “metaverse” concept), then the other one can pick up the slack for a while.

And relative to Apple, both Alphabet and Meta have less risk associated with China, since much of what they do has been already banned from China for a long time. Apple’s supply chain is still quite reliant on China despite their ongoing diversification efforts, and Apple’s product sales into China are losing market share to China’s own products. China’s semiconductor capabilities and overall phone model quality have increased quite a bit in recent years.

## Final Thoughts: China’s Mixed Rebound

As I’ve covered in a number of reports, I view China’s stock market bottom likely being in as a base case:

![MCHI Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-mchi-price.png)

The strongest part of China’s economy, which continues to fire on all cylinders, is their industrial production. They continue to grow their industrial output at a rapid rate, and the time and capital that it would take for other countries to displace them as the world’s manufacturer are both significant. This is because it’s not just the manufacturing facilities that would need to be duplicated elsewhere; it’s also the logistics hubs (e.g. shipping ports) that come into play post-manufacturing, the sheer power consumption that the manufacturing facilities require, and the human capital that China has built up with a generation of highly-productive technicians with a lot of manufacturing know-how.

The weakest part of China’s economy, which continues to sag to lower and lower depths, is their housing market. During the prior decade they overbuilt and overinvested in housing with leverage, and so now the rate of new construction is all the way back down to 2005 levels, and unsold housing inventory remains at all-time highs, which weighs on housing prices and consumer balance sheets. Total housing value in China as a % of GDP still remains elevated, but is below the highest countries by that metric such as Australia. Pictet Asset Management’s chief economist Patrick Zweifel continues to have some of the best charts on the topic:

![China Property Value](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-zweifel-china-property-value.png)

![China Floor Space](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-zweifel-china-floor-space.png)

The middle-of-the-road metrics for China are the various non-housing consumption categories, such as retail sales, which are neither as strong as industrial output nor as weak as housing. The trend of consumer sales is declining from a high level over the past decade, and was disrupted by major lockdown shocks, but is showing signs of stabilizing. The May 2024 figures came out recently well above consensus, and at 3.7% their year-over-year rate for retail sales is above the year-over-year rate that Americans currently have:

![China Retail Sales](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-6-22-china-retail-sales.png)

As Chinese policymakers attempt to stimulate the Chinese economy without re-blowing the housing bubble that they have deliberately deflated, and with industrial production already firing on all cylinders, these non-production non-housing categories (e.g. consumer consumption) seem to be the obvious choice. Overall Chinese consumption for things is still well below North American and European levels, and so a natural release valve for weak housing would be stronger non-housing consumer demand.

As China continues to develop economically, people will buy more non-housing things, will use more fuel, and will travel abroad more. Companies and investments that are positioned for that outcome should do well over the next decade, in my view, with the major caveat that they will need to navigate around geopolitical tail risks. There are ways to invest in that outcome while limiting direct Chinese exposure, such as by investing in many of the energy suppliers to China.
