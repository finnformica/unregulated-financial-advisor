# Deep Dive: Global Energy, AI, and Chevron Analysis

*July 7, 2024*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

The macro section of this report focuses on domestic and global liquidity conditions, the slowing U.S. business cycle, and developing countries’ energy shortages.

The digital asset section covers volatile bitcoin price action, Bolivia’s positive reversal toward bitcoin/crypto, Nubank’s integration of the Lightning network, and the SEC’s lawsuit against Consensys.

The investment analysis section discusses the substantial long-term energy requirements of AI, and reiterates a bullish view on oil and gas producers including Chevron.

## Macro View

**Liquidity Indicators**

Measures of domestic liquidity briefly contracted during the final business days of Q2, which is normal for end-of-quarter periods. We can see this most clearly on the reverse repo chart, with all of the end-of-quarter spikes highlighted in red:

![Reverse Repos](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-reverse-repos.png)

However, reverse repos have been trending sideways for the past couple of months, and that has not been great for liquidity. Liquidity improves as money comes *out* of reverse repos and back into the banking system, which it hasn’t been doing lately.

Partially due to this flatlining of reverse repos, overall domestic liquidity is currently near the bottom end of its recent range:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Treasury could chose to improve the liquidity situation by issuing a higher ratio of T-bills for its funding needs, especially to accommodate the fact that the Congressional Budget Office expects the 2024 fiscal deficit to be $400 billion larger than previously expected. This would pull more money out of reverse repos and back into the banking system. I don’t think the Treasury would have a problem doing that during the second half of 2024 if they were to decide that they need to.

Alternatively, the Fed could ease interest rates a bit and/or slow down its balance sheet drawdown again. Based on recent economic data, there’s a significantly rising chance that we’ll see at least some mild Fed cuts before the end of the year. With the unemployment rate firmly on the rise, the Fed’s mandate is becoming more balanced between sustaining maximum employment and suppressing price inflation, compared to being heavily slanted toward suppressing price inflation as it was in recent years.

The dollar-yen exchange rate firmly broke well over the previous high of 160, which represents the weakest level for the yen since the mid-1980s. After touching about 162, in recent days it has settled back down a bit to just under 161:

![USDJPY](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-usdjpy.png)

Japan has plenty of reserve ammunition to slow down its devaluation, including selling dollars and Treasuries to buy back yen. They can also raise interest rates slightly, although if they raise interest rates too much it’ll completely blow out their fiscal deficit and potentially be pro-inflationary, since their public sector debt is equal to over 250% of GDP.

Japan’s government is not interested in defending a specific exchange rate level, but rather is focused on managing the rate of devaluation and occasionally breaking up leveraged shorts when they accumulate too much. By intervening from time to time, they punish speculators that get overleveraged on the short side, and prevent disorderly rates of devaluation.

I continue to expect Japan’s currency and other currencies to struggle a bit, but once we move into a regime of Fed interest rate cuts and/or resumed Fed balance sheet expansion later this year or in 2025 (triggered either by soft U.S. economic data or an illiquid U.S. Treasury market), that regime shift would have a very decent chance of catalyzing a significant reversal for the yen and other currencies.

Global liquidity, which in these charts is based on the dollar-denominated broad money supply of major countries, continues to trend sideways and only slightly up. I expect it to start to break out more in late 2024 or in 2025.

**International Spotlight: Egypt’s Energy Shortages**

In general, a significant percentage of financial analysts in the United States and Europe tend to be somewhat blind toward the developing world, despite the fact that developing countries contain the majority of the world’s population and are responsible for the majority of marginal economic growth.

Or, to the extent that analysts do focus on developing countries, it’s often in the context of a spreadsheet and high-level numbers rather than on-the-ground understanding of details. There are some analysts from the United States or Europe that specialize in a region and go live and work there, at which point they pick up a lot of nuance and details, but that’s not particularly common.

So, I usually find that the period during which I live in Egypt as my second home each year is very helpful for broader thinking and context. I experience certain things directly that are not necessarily thought about often, and I use the opportunity to pester family and friends who live here year-round with questions.

For example, I ask a small business owner relative how they approach pricing strategy, and how they deal with costs, given the backdrop of 28% year-over-year inflation. I ask a physician relative how he acquires and holds physical cash dollars on the black market. I ask salaried worker relatives how they approach asking for raises each year, whether they’ve kept up with inflation or not (they haven’t), and what they do if a given raise is insufficient. I ask an advertising executive friend who works with companies across multiple industries how business conditions are in the country.

Although I analyze it myself from a macro perspective and have firm views on the matter, I ask people here why they think high inflation is happening, to see how people who don’t work in finance for a living tend to contextualize and describe it. Many of them understand it quite thoroughly. But of course, just like in the United States people can have different views (right or wrong, or shades of gray) about where broad price inflation comes from. When the number is 28%, inflation is inherently a more interesting topic to discuss than in places where the number is lower.

This is the last report that I’ll be writing from Egypt this year, so I’ll use the opportunity to highlight one more observation in Egypt that is relevant for many other developing countries and global energy markets in general. Egypt has been experiencing a natural gas shortage, which has been contributing to recurring rolling power outages.

Egypt consumes 1,094 TWh of primary energy per year as of 2023, which translates into 9,704 KWh per capita. This mostly includes a mix of coal, oil, and natural gas, but also includes some other sources of energy around the margins including hydroelectric, wind, and solar. Although total nationwide primary energy consumption is gradually increasing, it’s not increasing as fast as population growth, and so per capita energy consumption is *declining* under the current administration:

![Egypt Per Capita Energy Use](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-egypt-per-capita-energy-use-700.png)

For context, Egypt’s 9,704 KWh per capita energy consumption is about 1/8th as much energy as the United States’ 77,028 KWh per capita energy consumption. So, while some wealthy high-energy developed countries have declining per capita energy usage due to greater efficiencies and industrial outsourcing, the fact that Egypt’s per capita energy use is declining from a low level is a more serious economic matter, indicative of a stagnation in genuine per capita development even as the country’s overall economy technically grows in size.

Out of that 9,704 KWh in annual energy consumption per capita, about 1,783 KWh is used for electricity, which has been on a trending decline in Egypt for the first time in modern history:

![Egypt Per Capita Electricity Generation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-egypt-per-capita-electricity-generation-700.png)

The vast majority of that electricity generation comes from burning natural gas. This chart below shows the nationwide levels rather than the per capita levels:

![Egypt Electricity Generation Sources](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-egypt-electricity-source-stacked-700.png)

The monthly chart shows that electricity usage skyrockets during the summer for air conditioning, and then declines during less hot months. Grid operators have to design for those hot periods, and then figure out how to repurpose more of their capacity for the colder months so that it doesn’t go to waste:

![Egypt Electricity Generation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-egypt-electricity-generation.png)

And that’s the current source of the problem: Egypt has had significant gas shortages in summer 2023 and summer 2024. This is despite the fact that Egypt has historically been a gas exporter.

Egypt produces its own gas from large gas discoveries. In addition, it has built some of the only liquified natural gas or “LNG” facilities in the broader region (which are quite complex and expensive), and so if countries like Israel and Cyprus want to export surplus natural gas beyond the regions to which they have a pipeline, they can sell it over to Egypt via pipeline, where it can then be chilled and compressed into LNG and exported globally by Egypt for dollars. That’s a useful value-add for Egypt and for the region. It also makes it so that during the less-hot periods when Egypt is using less natural gas, they have something to do with their surplus domestic gas production: convert it and export it as LNG for dollars.

But the numbers have become imbalanced. Egypt’s gas production has been stagnating, while their population and total demand have been increasing. And so that combination has resulted in acutely insufficient natural gas for electricity during summer months, for two years in a row.

To deal with that, the government imposes scheduled power outages for certain neighborhoods across Egypt staggered throughout the day, since they can’t all have power at once. It used to be one-hour daily outage periods, then it crept up to two hours per day, and now it’s often three hours per day. Many of the upper class areas and coastal resorts are excluded from these outages, whereas many of the poorer rural areas have even more than three hours of daily outages.

A few years ago, Egypt partnered with Russia to build a nuclear power facility on Egypt’s north coast to generate more electricity for the country, but that won’t be operational until 2026 or later. Ideally, they should have started that power plant earlier and phased out the construction of their new cities more slowly, but top-down central planning often results in significant inefficiencies and suboptimal ordering of events like that. They were early to finance and direct the construction of entire new cities which are still mostly vacant, but were late to do the same for their power generation capabilities which are gravely needed. And after the power shortages of 2023, they should have prepared temporary responses for more issues in 2024 ahead of time, but didn’t do so.

As a result, there are people without power, while new buildings sit vacant in the desert, all financed with the accumulation of external debt and repeated currency debasement for the people that are currently without power.

Egypt’s government is playing catch-up and trying to temporarily resolve the issue by *importing* LNG now, but that’s expensive and comes with key limitations. For one issue, Egypt doesn’t have re-gasification infrastructure. If you import LNG, you need to be able to convert that imported LNG back into usable gas, which requires complex and expensive facilities to do. Egypt, being prepared mainly for exporting LNG rather than importing LNG, has liquification capability but doesn’t have re-gasification capability.

But there’s a fix for that. Egypt [contracted with Hoegh Galleon](https://arabfinance.com/News/newdetails/16029), a massive Norwegian vessel that serves as a floating storage regasification unit (“FSRU”). Renting these types of ships allows a country to import LNG if they don’t have the regasification infrastructure in place, although naturally it’s expensive and these types of ships are limited. So Egypt will be importing LNG, and is renting the FSRU for at least the next year to be able to convert that imported LNG to the natural gas they need for their power grid.

That’s good, but the problem is that it’s expensive and requires the expenditure of globally-acceptable external currencies like dollars. Egypt has a structural trade deficit, meaning they import more things overall than they export, so dollars are not so easy to come by at scale. As a result, they need to get dollars mainly from external investment and loans. Thanks to the IMF and UAE injections of dollars earlier this year, Egypt now can afford this LNG and FSRU temporarily, but within a couple of years they are likely to run into more dollar shortages, which ultimately puts their energy security at risk.

For the next year and beyond, Egypt plans to increase production from their gas fields, but it remains to be seen if they’ll be successful at that or not. By 2026 or later, their nuclear facility will come online and could boost their nationwide total electricity generation by up to 15% or more. And in theory, Egypt should be a decent place for large-scale solar installations, since they have long hours of desert sun and their biggest electricity shortages occur on hot and sunny summer days. However, solar panels are energy-intensive to produce, and so China produces the vast majority of them, and thus importing solar panels could further hurt Egypt’s structural trade deficit unless they can find more ways to reliably get their exports up (including tourism revenues).

This example of a natural gas shortage and overall energy shortage represents a challenge that a lot of developing countries face. It shows the importance of establishing a balance of trade and acquiring globally acceptable money. If Egypt is going to be a net energy importer, it needs to export more things, which in Egypt’s case can include better manufacturing numbers or can include better tourism numbers.

It also shows how interconnected energy systems are for both developed and developing countries. Most types of energy are not very fungible; they need to be converted, refined, compressed, transported, and so forth. Countries often need to work together to either monetize a surplus for one of them, or fill a deficit for another one of them, both in terms of the type of energy itself and the infrastructure to make use of it. And as previously described, some countries import raw forms of energy (natural gas via pipeline, or barrels of oil via pipeline or via ship, or uranium via ship), and then refine it, compress it, or do whatever value-add process that the particular type of energy requires, so that they can export it as a more readily transportable or usable form.

The United States faces a similar issue; they produce a lot of oil but still have to import oil from elsewhere because their refineries require that type of external oil that the United States doesn’t produce much of. There are often situations where a country can be both an importer and exporter of oil, or gas, or uranium, since there are different types or states of each.

In my view, when the U.S. economy eventually slows more or runs into Treasury market illiquidity problems, and the Fed starts easing interest rates a bit or providing more balance sheet liquidity, the dollar index can also ease a bit and potentially contribute to a boom in dollar-indebted emerging and frontier markets like Egypt, similar to the 2003-2007 period. However, we’re not quite there yet, and so that’s something that I continue to monitor.

## Digital Assets Note

**Bitcoin Price Action**

Since its breakout earlier this year, bitcoin’s price has been ranging between $58k and $73k, and mostly between $60k and $70k. During the past week, it broke down out of that range to briefly below $54k.

In addition to just broadly weak sentiment, some of the bearish catalysts for this recent downward move seems to be that 1) addresses linked to the U.S. and German government (from seized criminal assets) [sent](https://blockworks.co/news/us-german-governments-selling-bitcoin) hundreds of millions of dollars worth of bitcoin to exchanges to sell, and 2) the trustee for Mt. Gox [began distributing](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/07/05/mt-gox-begins-repaying-bitcoin-to-creditors-a-decade-on-from-collapse.html) initial coins back to creditors. These events spooked a lot of traders.

And while bitcoin’s price has been performing that correction/consolidation, a lot of the recently-launched meme assets that did well over the past year are being washed out with much deeper drawdowns, which is healthy. That’s the inevitable outcome of the financial nihilism that has been the main theme of this crypto cycle.

Philip Swift, the founder of Look Into Bitcoin, provided a great chart contrasting long-term capital vs short-term capital in bitcoin. This cycle has been small and early so far:

![BTC Capital Cycles](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-btc-cycles.jpg)

During bull markets, fast money comes in (red area on the chart above), drives the price up, and then the rising price attracts more fast money until eventually the cycle exhausts itself, including from long-term holders trimming their holdings into that new price strength. Fast money traders then start to sell, and coins rotate toward long-term accumulators (blue area on the chart above) that hold for years. That gradually takes a lot of liquid coins off the market, and helps set the stage for the next bull market.

Bitcoin’s price has spent the last decade correlating rather strongly with measures of global liquidity, which have been rangebound for over two years now. I have rather high conviction that this correlation will continue, and so as a base case I continue to expect bitcoin to chop along in a wide range and then likely break out when global liquidity does:

![Global M2 vs BTC](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-global-m2-vs-btc.png)

Notably, bitcoin’s correlation with liquidity doesn’t tell us much about likely price *level*. It mainly only tells us about likely price *direction*. I have no firm view on what price level bitcoin will reach in this cycle or the next, other than to say that I would be somewhat surprised to see it meaningfully decouple from measures of global liquidity for a sustained period of time, either to the upside or downside.

My general recommendation is to only buy bitcoin with capital that you won’t need for at least four years. It continues to be a promising but volatile asset with a strong structural trend. You can [start](https://www.lynalden.com/swan-bitcoin/) with a lump sum and then dollar-cost average into it to build a position that makes sense relative to the rest of your portfolio and volatility tolerance.

**Bolivia Reversing its Bitcoin and Crypto Ban**

Bolivia began putting a lot of restraints on Bitcoin in 2014, and by 2020 they banned financial entities from interfacing with Bitcoin or other cryptocurrencies.

However, here in 2024, Bolivia has reversed the ban and will allow financial entities to make use of Bitcoin and cryptocurrencies. As [Cointelegraph reports](https://cointelegraph.com/news/bolivia-legalizes-bitcoin-crypto-transactions):

> Bolivia’s central bank, Banco Central de Bolivia, has lifted its ban on Bitcoin and crypto payments, allowing financial entities to conduct transactions with digital assets in a bid to modernize its payment system.
>
> The central bank said the move was made to help Bolivia elevate its struggling economy and align it with Latin American crypto regulations.

Bolivia’s actions matter for their citizens but don’t have enough scale to meaningfully impact global markets. However, over the years a number of countries have introduced and then reversed bans like this, and Bolivia is the latest one. Governments are not monoliths, and incentives matter. Any time a country flip-flops around on Bitcoin-related legislation, that’s basically a win for Bitcoin.

**Nubank is Integrating Lightning**

Nu Holdings, aka Nubank (NU), a massive $60+ billion Brazilian neobank, has [announced](https://international.nubank.com.br/company/nubank-partners-with-lightspark-to-enhance-customer-experience-via-blockchain/) that it is partnering with Lightspark to integrate Bitcoin’s Lightning network for its users.

Nubank has over 100 million users in Brazil, Mexico, and Colombia, with about 90% of them being in Brazil. Nubank offers a diverse array of traditional financial services, along with bitcoin and crypto purchases being available as well. Integrating Bitcoin’s Lightning network would allow Nubank app users to send bitcoin to various recipients domestically and internationally quickly and cheaply.

Lightspark is a Lightning infrastructure company based in the United States that is co-founded and run by the former president of PayPal (PYPL).

The Lightning network is an open-source protocol that serves as Bitcoin’s primary second-layer scaling and payments solution. It allows for instant, cheap payments using custodial or non-custodial wallets. While other scaling solutions exist and more are being developed over time, Lightning is the fastest one and is able to tie the others together.

Various companies, ranging from Cash App and River and Strike and Kraken and Coinbase in the United States, to Nubank in Brazil, to Relai in Switzerland (invested in by [ego death capital](https://egodeath.capital/)), to Neutronpay in southeast Asia, to Bitnob in Nigeria, to Blink and Wallet of Satoshi in many countries, provide their users access to the interoperable Lightning network. And various noncustodial wallets such as Phoenix Wallet, Mutiny Wallet, and others allow power-users to interact with Lightning without relying on custodial companies.

Some people have been critical of Lightning over the past year in particular, and they fall into two primary groups.

-The first group of Lightning critics is generally made up of proponents of competing Bitcoin network scaling solutions, many of which are more centralized and VC-backed, and that require a soft fork in base-layer Bitcoin to work. They have an incentive to highlight flaws with Lightning to promote their ideas. While there are some scaling solutions that I find to be very interesting, they generally would not displace Lightning, and in general would benefit from being tied together to other scaling solutions by Lightning. Lightning is like a glue that can connect to every other relevant scaling solution that comes along so that they don’t become siloed ecosystems.

-The second group of Lightning critics generally consists of people who think of Lightning primarily as a mass market solution for end users. If it’s viewed through that lens, then it would indeed be very flawed. While Lightning can be an end user solution for thousands or millions of power users, it’s not the type of mass market solution that is able to scale to a billion direct end users. Instead, it’s like a set of software roads and highways, and the last mile of small roads and driveways to most end users’ house is best left to other solutions. Various wallet providers such as Cash App, Strike, Nubank, Blink, as well as Chaumian e-cash solutions such as Fedimint and Cashu, are great at serving as the last mile to end users, which is what allows for ease of use and unlimited scaling of working capital usage for the network as a whole. It’s important to have *optionality* within the network; each user can choose the level of convenience, expense, and permissionless-ness that suits their needs.

As evidenced by this Nubank integration, Lightning is alive and well. In the coming months, I’ll be writing articles about Nostr and Chaumian E-Cash and how both of them are greatly enhancing the Lightning network recently. Although Lightning’s ecosystem has been gradually improving for years, these open-source protocols have been coming together in a way over the past several months to provide a major step-change improvement for Lightning in terms of usability, features, and privacy.

**SEC Sues Staking Service Providers**

In my prior report, I highlighted news that the SEC dropped one of its key investigations into Ethereum 2.0, but I pointed out that the battleground will likely instead primarily be around staking services:

> Consensys is a major company in the Ethereum ecosystem, as a provider for the biggest wallet and node infrastructure service, and its CEO was one of the Ethereum co-founders. Along with the Ethereum Foundation, Consensys serves as one of the major development and leadership hubs for Ethereum as a whole.
>
> The Securities and Exchange Commission had been investigating Consensys for a number of securities-related legal issues. Ethereum by most accounts passed the Howey Test at launch and therefore has many of the hallmarks of an investment security (unlike Bitcoin which does not), but kind of got a pass by regulators for a while and subsequently was grandfathered in relative to newer coins that similarly pass the Howey Test. The Commodities Futures Trading Commission has had Ethereum contracts listed for a while, meaning that the CFTC and SEC have had somewhat of a regulatory jurisdiction conflict that is awkward to resolve. Ethereum 2.0 (Ethereum’s switch from proof-of-work to proof-of-stake) somewhat revived that conflict, and renewed SEC scrutiny toward Ethereum as a whole.
>
> From a technical perspective, older types of securities inherently remain securities indefinitely, whereas technically with blockchains there can be a situation where something starts as a security and then loses many of its security properties over time. In other words, a share of stock can never go from being a security to a commodity, but a software protocol potentially can in theory. The creator can eventually lose control over his or her creation such that it operates without their involvement, even if the method of creation met the definition of a sale of securities at inception.
>
> In recent days, Consensys announced that the SEC *dropped* their investigation into whether Consensys’ sales of ether count as securities transactions. This is in line with recent announcements that Ethereum ETFs are likely to be approved. The CFTC has likely won over the SEC on this front.
>
> **However, there is still ongoing investigation into whether staking services on top of Ethereum are securities offerings. The upcoming batch of likely-to-be-approved Ethereum ETFs have stripped staking from their offerings. By most accounts, that’s where it seems the battleground will be for a while longer: staking services potentially being securities.**
>
> By most traditional assessments of securities laws, staking services likely are securities. But advocates within the cryptocurrency industry assert that decades-old securities laws are no longer the best regulatory lenses with which to view the landscape, similar to how decades-old broadcasting laws don’t really apply to podcasts and so forth. As the cost of creating a security trends toward zero due to the existence of blockchains, and anyone can do it from any jurisdiction and reach a global audience, it arguably necessitates reshaping how securities regulations works.
>
> -June 22, 2024 report

Well, a week later on June 28, the SEC came out hard against a bunch of staking services, with a fresh lawsuit. As [Cointelegraph reports](https://cointelegraph.com/news/sec-sues-consensys-metamask-brokerage-staking-services):

> The United States Securities and Exchange Commission has [filed](https://www.docdroid.com/YS1DSF5/sec-v-consensys-pdf) a lawsuit against Consensys, the parent company of MetaMask. According to a complaint on June 28, the company has been operating as an unregistered broker and engaging in the unregistered offer and sale of securities through MetaMask Swaps since 2020.
>
> The complaint claims that Consensys has collected more than $250 million in fees by brokering crypto asset transactions and offering staking services without proper registration, thereby depriving investors of crucial protections. The SEC seeks a permanent injunction, civil penalties and other equitable relief against Consensys for these alleged violations of federal securities laws.
>
> In addition, the regulator claims that by facilitating investments in Lido and Rocket Pool’s staking programs, Consensys has acted as an intermediary in unregistered transactions, denying investors essential protections.
>
> “Consensys has offered and sold tens of thousands of securities for two issuers: Lido and Rocket Pool. By this conduct, Consensys acts as an underwriter of those securities and participates in the key points of their distribution,” reads the filing.

It remains to be seen how that lawsuit will go. While staking services do seem to be rather straightforwardly a security offering based on the Howey Test, the SEC in recent years has had a rather weak track record of winning its cases against large crypto companies. And depending on the presidential election results, it could significantly shake up the SEC’s commissioners and change their legal approach. For the time being, it’s a rather big lawsuit for the crypto industry to keep an eye on.

Meanwhile, the [Fairshake PAC](https://www.opensecrets.org/political-action-committees-pacs/fairshake-pac/C00835959/summary/2024) has raised $177 million to support crypto-friendly political candidates during this election cycle, which makes it one of the biggest single-issue PACs in the country. Fairshake supports both Democrats and Republicans as long as they are crypto-friendly, and [according to CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/06/26/crypto-pac-house-senate-elections.html), “[Fairshake] has backed the winning candidate in 33 of the 35 House and Senate primary races it entered.”

## AI, Energy, and Chevron Update

The United States has had generally flat nationwide electricity generation in recent decades:

![United States Electricity Generation](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-united-states-electricity-generation-700.png)

The country has nameplate capacity of about 1,200 gigawatts of electricity generation, but robust electrical grids need to be designed for the most demanding days of the year, and so it normally doesn’t operate anywhere near that level, and likely wouldn’t have the fuel capacity to sustain that level. Over the course of a given year, the average electrical output of the U.S. grid averages a bit under 500 gigawatts.

On a per capita basis, electricity generation in the country is gradually shrinking, but from a very high base. This is common in developed countries today, and generally is due to improving energy efficiency and a shift from industrial production to a services-focused economy.

There’s a good reason to believe that over the next decade, artificial intelligence in the United States will consume tens of gigawatts of electricity on a sustained basis. And that could be true across many other jurisdictions as well.

Over the past century and a half, humanity outsourced a lot of our physical labor. The amount of energy we consume in the form of machined products, electricity, transportation, climate control, agriculture, and more, is equivalent in terms of energy expenditure to many people working for each of us.

A single farmer on a tractor can do the work that dozens of manual laborers could do a couple centuries ago. The engine power of a typical car is equal to well over a hundred horses. A typical house uses more electricity than Lance Armstrong can generate on a bicycle hooked up to a generator, and for the low cost of several pennies per hour. The examples are endless, and represent enormous productivity gains.

Much like how we outsourced a lot of our physical labor away from our human bodies toward fuel-powered machines, over time we will do similar with a lot of our mental labor. The various services we interact with will increasingly have artificial intelligence built into them, to which we can delegate tasks to. We already have the equivalent of a lot of physical laborers working for each of us thanks to the industrial revolution, and in the future we’ll increasingly also have the equivalent of a lot of mental laborers working for each of us.

I won’t try to predict how far or fast it will go, and none of this outlook necessitates the emergence of artificial general intelligence or true software consciousness. To start with, it just means that an increasing number of automatable white-collar tasks will be automated, similar to how a lot of automatable blue-collar tasks were. This displaces jobs, but makes each remaining worker far more productive, and thus builds wealth for society. A worker overseeing and occasionally intervening for a bunch of factory robots is more productive than a worker assembling parts by hand, and the same can be true for a worker overseeing a lot of mental tasks.

The growth of the internet, and subsequently smartphones and the mobile internet and social media throughout the 1990s, 2000s, and 2010s, was not very energy intensive. If anything, these technologies offset more energy than they consumed. A single internet-connected smartphone replaces multiple pieces of large equipment and consumables. Social media is not very computationally intensive relative to how many hours it takes up in our lives and how it shapes society.

But the growth of AI in the 2020s, 2030s, and beyond will likely be a lot more energy intensive. It’s more computationally intensive, and thus more hardware-focused. It could lead to a mild renaissance in energy demand.

And unlike bitcoin mining, most types of AI cannot readily make use of stranded energy that already exists. Bitcoin mining can operate with low bandwidth, high latency, and plenty of unreliability. This makes bitcoin mining suitable for going into remote areas where stranded energy is (e.g. stranded natural gas from small oil wells that would otherwise be flared into the atmosphere), or suitable for serving as a grid stabilizer that gets paid to immediately shut off in a region whenever grid demand exceeds supply. In contrast, most types of AI require high bandwidth and low latency, so their datacenters need to be closer to population centers. And AI operators generally require high uptime, since the upfront capex costs are so high and electricity expenditure is a smaller share of their total expenses relative to bitcoin mining.

So, AI will need stable energy sources near population centers, and thus will likely compete more with residential and industrial electricity consumers. Some types of AI might be able to be pushed to remote areas like bitcoin mining is, but not all of it. In short, we’ll continue to need a lot of on-demand electricity.

While new solar and wind power generation can increasingly help around the margins, the combination of solar/wind and storage is rather expensive and materially intensive. Natural gas and nuclear power are the more realistic methods for bringing on new electricity capacity at scale that can operate during both day and night reliably.

![United States Electricity Sources](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-united-states-electricity-source-stacked-700.png)

As I described in the prior section about Egypt, the developing world continues to need a lot more energy for basic needs. Meanwhile, the developed world has a more secure energy situation, but the desire for re-industrialization and the general growth of AI could kickstart another structural increase higher in energy demand even for developed countries.

Therefore, I continue to be bullish on global hydrocarbon demand and global uranium demand.

Oil and gas producers on average are currently trading at inexpensive valuations, have strong balance sheets, and return large portions of their profits to shareholders via dividends.

Chevron, for example, has an excellent AA- credit rating and has less net debt than one year’s worth of net income. It trades at a price/book ratio of below 1.8x, and a price/earnings ratio of below 12x. It pays a dividend yield of over 4% and has grown its dividend for 37 consecutive years.

![CVX FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2024-7-7-cvx-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (a 15x price/earnings in this case)
* white line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

I continue to be bullish on energy producers that have long-lasting reserves, and/or that have substantial energy infrastructure. And it makes sense to diversify among oil types (e.g. deep water vs oil sands vs shale) and to diversify among jurisdictions.

The way that I treat the current risk/reward situation for oil and gas producers is that in a rangebound oil and gas scenario they are cheaply-priced and pay you to own them, and in an energy bull market scenario they can go much higher. And so I view them to be like positive carry options on higher energy prices.

## Final Thoughts: Focusing on the Long Term

A lot of investors are very short-term focused, and yet also tend to over-prepare for outlier outcomes.

For example, I get a lot of questions about what bitcoin’s price would do in a given 3-6 month period if the United States has a massive recession and major liquidity event. (Ironically, I actually think we already had a pretty big liquidity event in late 2022 and early 2023, which we bottomed and moved up from).

Or, I get questions about if it’s best to hold off on buying energy producers until some massive clearing event occurs. (Again, I think we already had a massive clearing event in the energy sector from 2015 through 2020 due to the disruptive rise of shale oil and the economic slowdown in emerging markets).

People are always looking for one more lower-low, and when that lower-low comes, they become afraid and think maybe there will be an even *lower*-low.

The famous investor Peter Lynch once said, “*far more money has been lost by investors preparing for corrections or trying to anticipate corrections than has been lost in corrections themselves.”*

And he’s right. When it comes to a big trend that you have conviction in, quarterly movements are generally irrelevant. The questions should be primarily directed toward whether that trend is still structurally on track or not, and whether it’s reasonably priced. Big price swings can provide opportunities for rebalancing, but don’t need to be fully predicted ahead of time.

That doesn’t mean to ignore risk. T-bills pay over 5.3% currently. Two-year Treasury notes pay over 4.6%. An investor can lock in a yield of CPI+2% in ten-year TIPS. There’s certainly a case for having a low-volatility side of a portfolio, and my various portfolios do have cash-equivalents, TIPS, gold, and other conservative investments. I *do* think there’s reasonable recession risk in the United States over the next year, and I am *not* particularly enamored with the forward inflation-adjusted return potential of the S&P 500 over the next few years.

But that’s what diversification and rebalancing are useful for. I’ve been emphasizing the concept of a three-pillar portfolio, which includes one big slice of profitable equities, one big slice of cash-equivalents and short-term notes and TIPS, and one big slice of commodity producers and hard monies.

Depending on how conservative or aggressive an investor is, he or she can dial up or down the cash-equivalents portion, but either way, it represents a rather balanced portfolio that is prepared for a variety of market conditions. We can then lean into major pullbacks, or take chips off the table during major bubbles, but otherwise don’t need to do a lot of frequent activity.
