## Macro View

**US Liquidity Conditions**

Base liquidity in the US continues to be pretty tight here in Q4, which was expected. I outlined this scenario back in the summer:

> Domestic liquidity currently remains rangebound. The overnight reverse repo facility is mostly drained (which puts liquidity back into the system), the Treasury General Account is up to $590 billion as it refills (which pulls liquidity out of the system), and the Fed continues to perform gradual quantitative tightening (which pulls liquidity out of the system.
>
> If the Treasury General Account reaches its $850 billion fully-filled target by the end of September, then it’ll likely drop liquidity to the lower end of its multi-year range.
>
> ![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-domestic-liquidity.png)
>
> Overall bank cash levels remain above prior acute liquidity events, but in Q4 2025 they will likely start to enter somewhat tight territory.
>
> ![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-bank-cash-levels.png)
>
> My expectation continues to be that the Fed will end quantitative tightening in 2026, and possibly move toward gradual balance sheet increases (which they won’t call QE, similar to their balance sheet expansion from October 2019 to March 2020).
>
> So, domestic liquidity looks like it’ll be flat-to-down for a while, potentially running into lower thresholds, and when it does bottom it’ll be a pretty structural event that moves us up out of a multi-year range.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

Indeed here in Q4, base liquidity has become tight. In fact, mostly because of the government shutdown, the Treasury didn’t just fill their cash account to $850 billion- they overfilled it to $950 billion, which pushed liquidity even a bit lower:

![Domestic Liquidity](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-domestic-liquidity.png)

The Treasury cash account is currently off its highs, but still elevated relative to its target level of $850 billion:

![TGA](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-tga.png)

Over the past couple of months here in Q4, the secured overnight financing rate has been elevated, which signals a shortage of liquidity in the banking system. Additionally, some entities have turned to the Fed’s standing repo facility, which provides secured financing when there are shortages of it in the market:

![SOFR IORB Spread](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-sofr-iorb.png)

![Fed Repo](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-repo.png)

Partially as a result of this, the Fed ended QT here in December. This mirrors what happened back in September 2019, when the combination of a refilling Treasury cash account and a lot of Fed balance sheet reduction led to liquidity issues in the repo market and the end of Fed balance sheet reduction.

![Bank Cash Levels](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-large-small-bank-cash.png)

The Fed has an FOMC meeting next week to determine and announce updates to monetary policy. Due to the government shutdown they have less economic data than normal to base decisions off of, but soft private payroll numbers and other factors suggest that they’ll most likely cut interest rates by 25 basis points (0.25%).

It’ll be interesting to see if they announce any expansion of their balance sheet, or if Chairman Powell will face questions about expanding the balance sheet in the press conference.

While I don’t know if it’ll come up this week, my base case continues to be that within 2026, the Fed will resume gradual balance sheet increases. They won’t call it QE, because it’ll primarily be for supporting bank liquidity rather than for the purposes of economic stimulus.

![Fed Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-fed-balance-sheet.png)

**Japan’s Bond Selloff**

Japan continues to experience a rapid rise in its bond yields (i.e. a falling of bond prices). It has been going on for four years, but in recent months it has gone rather vertical:

![Japan 10 Year](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-japan-10yr.png)

Zooming out, Japan is exiting a period of unusually dovish policy that consisted of constant central bank balance sheet expansion and yield curve control:

![Japan 10 Year Long-Term](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-japan-10yr-longterm.png)

On one hand, this is pretty good for many Japanese banks. While rapidly-rising interest rates can put some pressure on those banks that are over-exposed to long-duration assets, it’s important for banks to have a positive yield curve. They can actually earn interest on their lending activities and securities portfolios now. The biggest banks in the country currently have stock charts that look like this:

![SMFG FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-smfg-fastgraph.png)

**F.A.S.T. Graphs 101:**

* black line: the current and historical stock price
* blue line: what the stock price would be if were at its historically average price/earnings ratio
* orange line: a conservative measure of valuation (an 13.92x price/earnings in this case)
* yellow line: dividends paid that year (and the payout ratio is relative to the orange line)
* dark/light green: the transition between historical earnings numbers and consensus analysts’ forecast earnings numbers

However, these higher rates put the government in a tough position. They have over 200% debt-to-GDP, so the ongoing rise in interest rates blows out their interest expense. Additionally, since the yen is a big funding currency for assets globally, this rising bond environment can lead to some deleveraging and put some pressure on foreign asset prices.

Eventually the Bank of Japan will be forced to resume balance sheet growth to support the bond market.

In the meantime, the tight domestic US liquidity situation, and the ongoing tightening in Japan’s system, are serving a one-two-punch against liquidity-fueled assets here in 2025.

## Digital Assets Note

Bitcoin bottomed on November 22nd, and so far has been in a sideways-to-up pattern since then.

There’s no guarantee that the bottom of this correction is fully in, but it at least represents a more interesting entry point than when the momentum was headed down. The weekly chart is showing early signs of stabilization:

![BTC Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-btc-chart.png)

From this correction, the market value to on-chain cost basis ratio has been reset to more average levels:

![Bitcoin MVRV](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-bitcoin-mvrv.png)

And overall, bitcoin’s price remains near the middle of the gradually declining exponential trend it’s been fluctuating around for over a decade:

![BTC Power Law](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-btc-power-law.png)

Many bulls want to see big up-swings up in this trend toward the higher red line, but since bitcoin is a rather large and liquid asset now, those types of moves are less likely to occur unless something *huge* happens in macro.

I remain bullish with a multi-year view as part of a diversified portfolio. I expect that once we’re past this phase of tight domestic US liquidity and sharply rising Japanese bond yields, bitcoin will have more of a tailwind behind it than it has lately.

## Pricing Risk Fairly

Throughout 2024, the US economy was slowing and narrowing by some metrics, and consumer sentiment was rolling over, but fiscal deficits, AI capex, and other factors helped prop up stock indices in a rather concentrated way.

That mix created, somewhat unironically, a bubble in low-risk assets. Investors didn’t want to take much risk, and so they bought mega-cap stocks with stable profit growth at almost any price. The problem is that you can turn a low-risk asset into a high-risk asset if you overpay for it.

The quintessential example I have used for that has been Costco Wholesale (COST). It’s a fantastic company with smooth top-line and bottom-line growth, but at one point last year it traded at over 60x earnings. Lately it has been rolling over, but is still at a rather rich 47x earnings.

![COST FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-cost-fastgraph.png)

Meanwhile, how many investors had “Brazilian equities outperforming” on their 2025 bingo card, especially in the introductory year of large tariffs?

The S&P 500 is up 18% this year, inclusive of dividends. The primary Brazilian equities ETF is up 47%, and that’s in dollar terms. Brazil’s biggest bank, Itau Unibanco, is up an explosive 81% in dollar terms this year:

![ITUB EWZ SPY YChart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-itub-ewz-spy-ychart.png)

It’s not that there was no risk in the Brazilian banking system. It’s that the risk was priced appropriately. Brazilian equities were globally under-owned, and as I explained in multiple premium reports, that set them up for at least a good chance (not a guarantee) of outperformance. The Brazilian economy simply had to not do too badly, and it was like a coiled spring ready to pop:

![ITUB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-itub-fastgraph.png)

There’s a well-known saying that’s (perhaps falsely) attributed to Mark Twain:

> It ain’t what you don’t know that gets you into trouble. It’s what you know for sure that just ain’t so.

In other words, small or medium allocations to moderately risky things do not concern me. What concerns me is when investors are heavily exposed to something that they view as ultra-low risk, that turns out to have substantial risk. Almost anything, when priced at 60x earnings, has risk.

A handful of mega-cap tech stocks and retail stocks exhibit those characteristics today. The underlying businesses are generally great, but it’s a question of valuation.

**Fairly Priced Risk: Super-Regional Banks**

When looking around at rather expensive US equities, one of the pockets that I keep finding value in is super-regional banks.

These are the banks that are smaller than the top-four nationwide systemic banks (JP Morgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo), but larger than all other commercial banks. They each generally have a branch footprint that extends through about 2/3rds of the country rather than the whole country.

These super-regionals consist of US Bancorp (USB), Truist (TFC) and PNC Financial (PNC).

Here’s US Bancorp:

![USB FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-usb-fastgraph.png)

Here’s Truist:

![TFC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-tfc-fastgraph.png)

Here’s PNC:

![PNC FASTGraph](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-pnc-fastgraph.png)

These are some of the larger banks in the country, with quite liquid and diversified asset books, and that offer above-average dividend yields. US Bancorp and Truist are trading below their average price/earnings level, and PNC is trading approximately at its average valuation.

Their dividend growth is pretty slow at the moment because they are building bigger capital buffers with retained earnings.

All of them took a pretty big capital hit during 2023 after the sharp rise in interest rates impaired their securities portfolios. However, they were large and stable enough to weather it (and even help provide liquidity to smaller regional banks), and they’ve since built back that value to new highs:

![Regional Bank Crisis](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-regional-bank-hit.png)

The banking system as a whole, including these super-regional banks, does have risk. Going forward, credit risk is the one to watch most closely for a while (as opposed to duration risk, which they ran into back in 2023). However, my view is that at 11x to 13x earnings, and with moderate position sizes, they’re priced fairly for that risk.

The US banking system as a whole is well-capitalized and has buffers in place to reduce the potential spillover from problems in the private credit market (which you can read more about in my [October 26th report](https://www.lynalden.com/premium-2025-10-26/)). Additionally, next year banks are likely to benefit from a combination of mild interest rate cuts, mild Fed balance sheet expansion, and/or partial bank deregulation (such as updates to their supplementary leverage ratios).

## Final Thoughts: Gold

The best type of correction is one that goes sideways rather than down.

So far, that’s how gold has behaved this year. It rose quickly in the early months, then from April to August it went sideways, then broke out to new highs through October, and since then has trended sideways again:

![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-gold-price.png)

Now that it’s at $4,200/oz, I’ll once again warn a bit about the huge momentum in gold that might be overdone, while reiterating that I don’t think the metal is fundamentally overvalued. In other words, it may not be a “buy” like I used to consider it, but by no means do I view it as a “sell” yet either, and thus keep it in the “hold” category.

![Gold Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-gold-chart.png)

Back in August when it was showing signs of breaking out at around $3,500/oz, I explained why I err toward the long side even when I am concerned about it being tactically overbought:

> We’re now over 5 months into a gold consolidation, since I highlighted the risk of a potential 6-12 month gold consolidation.
>
> ![Gold Price](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-google.png)
>
> The good news lately is that gold is back to the top end of that consolidation range, and has touched above the psychologically important $3,500/oz range. Additionally, the increasing consensus around a dovish Fed is beneficial for the price, all else being equal.
>
> The bad news is that it’s not really a true breakout yet, more like a test, and it’s still structurally overbought on the monthly chart:
>
> ![Gold Longterm](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-gold-stockcharts.png)
>
> Now, because I think we’re going through major long-term changes (the end of a four-decade structurally declining interest rate cycle, a global shift in sovereign interest from US Treasuries to gold around the margins in an increasingly multi-polar world, and persistent US fiscal dominance which fuels the attractiveness of hard assets), gold remaining in overbought status for unusually long periods of time is not unreasonable.
>
> That’s why since autumn 2018 when I added precious metals to the newsletter portfolio, I’ve happily erred on staying long even through periods where I expect intermediate-term weakness.
>
> Money Metals visualizes it well in their [quarterly newsletter](https://www.moneymetals.com/uploads/content/MoneyMetalsInsider_Summer2025.pdf). The dollar is no longer expanding as a share of global reserves, and gold is no longer falling, in what is likely the bottom of a trend that basically began after WWII:
>
> ![Money Metals](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-8-31-money-metals.png)
>
> After all these 5 months of price chop, I am increasingly uncertain as to whether to expect a breakout or more consolidation. Given my prior comments, I continue to be structurally long, and at the very least I am less negative on the intermediate-term prospects for the metal than I was in April.
>
> -August 31, 2025 premium report

I don’t think it’s a coincidence that the gold portion of that Money Metals chart looks quite similar to the Japanese bond yield chart. As a reminder, here’s that yield chart again:

![Japan 10 Year Long-Term](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2025-12-7-japan-10yr-longterm.png)

Overall, the 2010s decade likely represented the generational peak for the bond-vs-gold relationship in multiple ways. That was the peak period where the global market was totally happy to hold low-yielding government debt without major concerns for debasement.

In particular, in 2019 there was $18 trillion worth of negative-yielding bonds in the world, mostly in Japan and Europe, and inflation wasn’t on most investors’ radar. I warned about it at the time:

> I read thousands of emails from my readers, and one of the key themes I see is that people are concerned about the next stock market crash, and perhaps rightly so. By many measures we have high stock valuations in the United States after a decade-long bull market, and we have high corporate debt levels both inside the United States and globally.
>
> But one question I rarely receive is: “Are bonds safe?”
>
> From a historical perspective, the bond market is acting a lot weirder than the stock market at the current time. The stock market looks like it often does at this point in the business cycle, which is not great for the probable range of forward returns, but bonds are doing things they haven’t done *ever before in history*, which should give investors pause.
>
> […]
>
> I would tread carefully and be cautious about taking certain trends for granted, like low inflation.
>
> I don’t know how long yields will stay this low and how low they will ultimately go before things reverse in one way or another. However, the risk-reward for most long-term sovereign bonds, other than perhaps 10-year treasuries, is not attractive. The upside potential for holding negative-yielding debt is not great over the long term, but the downside risk in the event that central banks become more aggressive is high.
>
> Cash, precious metals, blue-chip dividend stocks, and real estate outside of high-priced major cities, all generally offer better risk/return potential at the current time in my view than long-term low/zero/negative-yielding bonds.
>
> [-July 2019: Are We in a Bond Bubble?](https://www.lynalden.com/bond-investing/)

Bonds went on to have their worst five-year performance in modern history, and most other assets outperformed bonds.

While I think the worst part of the bond sell-off is likely behind us, I unfortunately think the overall solvency issues in public systems will continue to be a problem for years to come. The focus will shift from time to time from the US, to Europe, to Japan, to China, and so forth, but the structural issues remain, and the release valve is the currency.

Fiscal dominance in developed markets doesn’t generally take the form of a fast debt crisis like people fear, but rather tends to take the form of a slow-motion continuous problem. Deficits remain high, inflation remains sticky, entitlement systems remain top-heavy, consumer sentiment remains weak, political populism rises, and so forth. Eventually something can snap, but predicting when that will happen far in advance isn’t very productive.

People ask things like, “when will the public debt matter?” and the answer is something like, “it already matters, and has for the past 5-6 years.”
