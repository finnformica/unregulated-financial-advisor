# The Liquidity Crunch, and the Federal Reserve’s Response

*Published March 12, 2020*

![Premium Feature Image](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-feature.jpg)

Here’s another unscheduled update. The next normal deep-dive report is scheduled for this upcoming Sunday evening.

Today was the biggest one-day decline in the Dow and S&P 500, in percentage terms, since 1987. They fell almost 10% today. European stocks had their biggest-ever decline in a day, with a loss greater than 11%.

In addition, the Dow and S&P 500 have never gone from all-time-highs, to down 20% (the arbitrary definition of a “bear market”) this quickly. It took just 16 days to go from all time highs to bear market territory. The previous record was in 1929, which took more than twice as long. The crises in 2008 and 2000 and others were not this fast either.

The market is now 26% down from its all-time high from February:

![SPX Chart](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-12-spx.png)

In addition to the sell-off in equities, the credit markets are crashing as well. Junk bonds have sold off (meaning their yields have spiked higher), and investment-grade bonds have done the same.

The volatility index is at 75, which is around record heights.

With this extreme level of market volatility, the global financial system shakes down to its foundation and gets tested at every level. Even traditionally safe haven assets like like gold and Treasuries can decline in this environment alongside stocks, because leveraged investors receive margin calls and become forced to liquidate whatever they have and go to cash. The Treasury market started to get illiquid this week, with wider than normal bid-ask spreads and a troubled 30-year bond auction.

In response, the Federal Reserve suddenly announced this afternoon that it will expand its Treasury purchasing program from T-bills only (short-duration Treasuries) to the full spectrum of duration, meaning short and long-term Treasuries. In other words, it is now engaging in full “QE4”, expanding the monetary base to purchase all types of Treasuries. The Fed also announced that they are willing to perform multiple $500 billion layered 1-3 month repurchase operations to inject liquidity into the financial system as needed starting this week. It’s an unprecedented amount of liquidity in such a short time-frame. Potentially several trillion dollars of liquidity injection in the month ahead.

Here’s how the Fed [described it](https://www.newyorkfed.org/markets/opolicy/operating_policy_200312a):

> These changes are being made to address highly unusual disruptions in Treasury financing markets associated with the coronavirus outbreak. Reserve management purchases into the second quarter will continue to be conducted with this maturity allocation. The terms of operations will be adjusted as needed to foster smooth Treasury market functioning and efficient and effective policy implementation.

The FOMC of the Federal Reserve has a normally-scheduled meeting next week. The market widely expects them to cut interest rates virtually to zero. By then, the Fed might also have other announcements.

## The Crisis Under the Surface

A lot of investors are confused at why a virus can cause this much market chaos.

It’s not so much the virus itself, it’s the bubble that the virus exposed. Corporate debt as a percentage of GDP is at record heights:

![Corporate Debt to GDP](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-12-debt.png)

*Chart Source: St. Louis Fed*

This means that if the U.S. quarantines itself like China and Italy did, and basically shuts down a significant portion of economic activity, many companies simply don’t have a strong enough balance sheet to get through 3 months of little or no revenue, followed by probably another 6-12 months of inevitable recession.

Airlines, hotel operators, oil producers, restaurant chains, malls, manufacturers- many of them face solvency issues if they go without a significant portion of their revenue for a few months. This is especially true for capital intensive companies like airlines, with a large fixed base of expenses. For some companies they can’t help it, while for other companies, it’s because they took a ton of low interest rate debt and used to buy back their own shares, leaving little capital in reserve.

Many large companies, ranging from Boeing to Hyatt, are tapping into their revolving credit facilities. Businesses maintain revolving credit facilities with banks for unexpected expenses. These are generally at higher interest rates than bonds, and are suitable for immediate need as a layer of defense against a liquidity shock.

However, even most well-capitalized banks are not designed to have such a large portion of their business clients tap into their revolving credit facilities at once. It would be like having a large portion of their depositors want to pull cash out at once.

Many investors think the Federal Reserve is trying to throw liquidity into the system to keep the stock market up, but that’s not their primary concern. The credit market and banking system seizing up is their primary concern. That’s what can turn this from a 3-month biological event that we quickly recover from, to a potential financial crisis on par with 1929 or 2008, depending on how it is handled. This time around, it’s not most banks that are over-levered; it’s many non-bank companies.

In addition, about half of the U.S. population has no savings and can’t go a couple months without a paycheck. So, on both a corporate level and a consumer level, it’s potentially a full-blown debt crisis if the U.S. experiences the same sort of shutdown as other nations have.

Basically, the global economic system has very little resiliency to deal with any sort of shock. It’s going to be a very challenging political issue to solve, and it’s happening all over the world rather than localized to any one country.

Always screen your investments for good balance sheets.

## Gold’s Role

Here is the year-to-date performance of various asset classes, as represented by ETFs:

![YTD Performance](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-12-ytd.png)

Long-term treasuries had a surge. Short-term treasuries and gold held their ground. Silver took it on the chin a bit. REITs were slammed. Equities of all types got crushed. Bitcoin is not on the chart but it did even worse.

However, in this past week, even treasuries and gold started to slide down a bit. Gold is 5-6% from its recent highs, even though it is positive year to date. This is due to sheer liquidity insolvency and disorderly selling; many investors/firms are getting margin calls and selling whatever they can, including safe haven assets if need be.

During the leading edges of the prior two recessions, gold sold off by 15% (2000) and 30% (2008). It then turned around and quickly recovered to new highs well before the S&P 500. This chart shows the percent draw-down from recent highs of both instances:

![Gold Drawdown](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-12-gold.png)

In other words, gold doesn’t necessarily help much during a liquidity crunch. Instead, it helps defend against currency devaluation from the inevitable policy response that comes in response to the liquidity crunch- the massive fiscal or monetary injection to stop the system from collapsing.

During the 2008 crisis, for example, gold started soaring after the Federal Reserve performed QE1, where the Fed quickly doubled the size of its balance sheet to provide emergency liquidity. The red line below is the size of the Federal Reserve balance sheet, and the blue line is the gold price:

![Gold vs Balance Sheet](https://www.lynalden.com/wp-content/uploads/premium-2020-3-12-policy-response.png)

Gold has been holding up better on the leading edge of this crash than it did in 2000 or 2008, down only 6% so far, possibly in part due to new Basel III regulations that make gold a tier 1 asset for financial institutions. Gold wasn’t a tier 1 asset back in 2000 and 2008.  And today’s Fed announcement of multiple layered $500 billion repo lending programs does represent the first part of monetary response- a massive liquidity expansion similar to the chart above.

Gold stocks are selling off just as hard as normal stocks. However, with the price of gold holding up, and the price of oil down, they are in great shape fundamentally at these prices. Especially the ones with solid balance sheets.

In short, gold continues to be my preferred defense asset of choice, as part of a diversified portfolio. Its high level of volatility in recent days is normal for this extreme environment, and it continues to hold up better than equities. Silver too, but with more volatility.

My [precious metals guide](https://www.lynalden.com/precious-metals-investing/) shows various ways to invest in gold and silver. In addition to physical coins and bars, one can invest in ETFs and other funds. However, not all funds are identical. Some of them have more counterparty risk, or use futures and non-allocated accounts to get gold exposure by proxy.

Historically, these differences haven’t mattered other than small differences in expense ratios. Most people understandably don’t care about those differences. However, in these extreme environments with unprecedented fiscal and monetary response coming up shortly, I prefer the funds that have less counterparty risk, and that have fully allocated storage. That means the Sprott funds: PHYS for gold and PSLV for silver. I have these in the Defense section of the M1 Fortress Income portfolio.

My second favorite are the Aberdeen funds, SGOL and SIVR. These have lower expense ratios, but not the same level of allocation and redemption as Sprott. I hold the Aberdeen funds in the M1 Newsletter portfolio. I’m happy to own a bit of both, Sprott and Aberdeen. Aberdeen is slightly better for the longer run due to lower expense ratios, and Sprott is slightly better for extreme environments when you need maximum trust.

The popular ETFs GLD (from SPDR) and SLV (from iShares) are more for trading. They have much higher liquidity, but more counterparty risk and moderately high expense ratios. I prefer Sprott and Aberdeen funds over GLD and SLV, unless one wants to trade options on them. Probably the difference won’t matter, but I’ll emphasize Sprott and Aberdeen at the moment anyway.

My favorite method overall, though, is private storage. Coins and bars.
